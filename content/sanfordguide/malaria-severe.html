Malaria, Severe

Clinical Setting

  • Severe malaria is almost always Plasmodium falciparum.
  • P. vivax and P. knowlesi can less commonly cause severe malaria and the treatment approach is the same. 
    • Severe Plasmodium vivax. Predominantly pulmonary (ARDS) and hypotension.
  • Increased risk of severe disease during pregnancy: N Engl J Med 370:2211, 2014.

  • CDC algorithm for diagnosis and treatment of malaria in the US.

    Any one of the following defines severe malaria (due to any malaria species) requiring parenteral therapy:
    • Clinical: impaired consciousness, shock, pulmonary edema, acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), DIC, seizures.
      • Jaundice requires at least 1 other criteria
    • Laboratory: any of severe anemia (Hct <20% in adults), renal failure, hemoglobinuria, lactic acidosis (bicarbonate <15), hypoglycemia (<40 mg/dL), parasitemia >5% (CDC criteria) defines severe malaria and mandates IV therapy even with no other criteria.
      • WHO severe malaria criteria >4% in a non-immune individual. Many US clinicians use IV artesunate at this threshold or even at 2%.
  • FDA approved Artesunate for Injection (intravenous artesunate; Amivas), is now commercially available in the US through major distributors (Cardinal Health, Americsource Bergen, and McKesson).  
    • See Amivas website for 24/7 emergency numbers; all voice messages will receive a response in <30 minutes.
    • Wholesale cost is $5,000 USD per vial; the average 80 kg person requires 6 vials for the first day and then 2 vials per day for each subsequent day that parasitemia is greater than 1%.
    • CDC no longer distributes no-cost antimalarial drugs.

Etiologies

  • Plasmodium falciparum
  • Plasmodium vivax (infrequently)
  • P. knowlesi (uncommon infection; Asia only)

Primary Regimens

  • IV quinidine gluconate no longer produced or available in the US.
    • IV quinine dihydrochloride still available in many countries outside the U.S. but significantly inferior to IV artesunate (preferred WHO therapy for all countries).
      • In imported malaria in France IV quinine had longer ICU stay but no difference in overall mortality (Clin Infect Dis 70:280, 2020).
  • Exchange transfusion no longer recommended in any situation.
  • Steroids not recommended for cerebral malaria.
  • Adult (preferred regimen)
    • Artesunate 2.4 mg/kg IV per dose at 0, 12, and 24 hours (total 3 doses) THEN
      • After 24h, if parasitemia >1% give 2.4 mg/kg IV qday until the day when parasitemia <1%, then continue with a full course of oral therapy (see below) starting at least 4 hours after the last dose of artesunate. 
        • Measure daily parasitemia at least 4 hr after an artesunate dose 
        • Artesunate IV should not be given for more than 7 days total.
      • After 24h if parasitemia <1%, then continue with a full course of oral therapy (see below) starting at least 4 hours after the last dose of artesunate. 
      • If oral meds not tolerated at time for switch may continue artesunate 2.4 mg/kg IV qday
  • PLUS
      • Oral Therapy (complete the full course no matter how many days IV artesunate used)
        • Artemether-lumefantrine 4 tablets (20 mg/120 mg tabs) as a single dose, then 4 tablets again after 8 hours, then 4 tablets every 12 hours for 2 days (take with food).
          • 1st choice
        • Atovaquone-proguanil 4 adult tabs (250 mg/100 mg tabs) po in a single dose daily x 3 days (take with food; repeat dose if patient vomits within 30 minutes)
        • Quinine sulfate 650 mg po tid x 3 days + Doxycycline 100 mp po tid x 7 days
        • Dihydroartemisinin-piperaquine x 3 days (not available in US)
        • Last resort: Mefloquine
        • In US, due to IV artesunate cost, consider using nasogastric administration if IV artesunate discontinuation criteria met
    • Pregnancy: 
      • Use IV artesunate in all trimesters
        • Follow with oral Quinine x 3 days + Clindamycin 20 mg/kg/day divided tid x 7 days.
          • Note: Doxycycline likely safe in 1st 15 weeks of pregnancy (prior to tooth/bone formation). 
    • Pediatric (preferred regimen)
      • Artesunate 2.4 mg/kg IV at 0, 12, and 24 hours (no minimum/maximum weight). 
      • In children wt <20 kg Artesunate 2.4 mg/kg IV at 0, 12, and 24 hours (per FDA PI May 2020).
        • WHO recommended dose of 3.0 mg/kg is model-based and does not meet GRADE criteria
      • Follow parenteral therapy exactly as with adults above with a complete oral course of anti-malarials
        • See pediatric dosing for Atovaquone-proguanil, Artemether-lumefantrine). WHO recommends to continue IV artesunate q24h if unable to take oral medication if <20 kg
    • If IV artesunate delayed, adult or children should start po (or nasogastric) regimen with artemether-lumefantrine (preferred), atovaquone-proguanil or quinine sulfate/doxycycline (per standard P. falciparum regimens' see Malaria, Uncomplicated P. falciparum)
      • switch to IV artesunate as soon as feasible; discontinue oral drugs.  
      • Consider oral mefloquine if other options are not available.
      • If available (non-US) can start with rectal artemether if IV artesunate not immediately available.

    Alternative Regimens

    • None

    Comments

    • If treating severe P. vivax add Primaquine (see Malaria, Uncomplicated, P. vivax)
    • IV Artesunate:
      • Artesunate IV with high parasitemias associated with delayed hemolysis; may be transfusion requiring but non-life threatening and occurs a mean of 15 days post therapy (Ann Intern Med.163:498; 2015). Monitor hematocrit weekly for 4 weeks.
    • Obesity (WHO caution): As the evidence for an association between dose, pharmacokinetics and treatment outcome in overweight or large adults is limited for oral regimens, and alternative dosing options have not been assessed in treatment trials
    • Atovaquone/proguanil po tabs or Artemether/lumefantrine po tabs are less bioavailable than oral quinine tabs if used as initial therapy for severe malaria.
    • Coartem not widely stocked by hospital or retail pharmacies in the US.
      • Generally available from regional drug distribution warehouses with up to 24-hour delay in initiating therapy for a medical emergency.
        • Do not delay therapy in order to obtain Coartem if another 1st line agent is more readily available.
      • Call 1 855-COARTEM for expedited availability information.
    • Dihydroartemisinin/piperaquine (Eurartesim) licensed and available in Europe.
    • For oral follow on therapy: 
      • Artemisinin resistance is present in the Greater Mekong subregion of Asia Antimicrob Agents Chemother 2021; AAC0112121 and in Eritrea, Rwanda and Uganda.
        • Artemisinin resistance alone rarely leads to treatment failure but resistance to both the artemisinin derivative and the partner ACT drug (Mekong region only) does.
        • ACT remains highly efficacious in Africa.
    • Control measure: in low-transmission areas (not countries with no indigenous transmission, give a single dose of 0.25 mg/kg primaquine with ACT to patients with P. falciparum malaria
      • Except pregnant women, infants aged < 6 months and women breastfeeding infants aged < 6 months) to reduce transmission.
        • G6PD testing is not required.
