Gastroenteritis, Campylobacter fetus

Clinical Setting

  • Food-borne illness.
  • Diarrhea is uncommon, often presents as a non-specific febrile illness.
  • Opportunistic pathogen: systemic disease in debilitated or immunocompromised hosts, rarely in immunocompetent individuals.
  • Gastroenteritis in immunocompetent patients usually resolves without specific therapy
  • Therapy recommended for persistent symptoms, severe disease (e.g., fever, significant abdominal pain, bloody diarrhea) and in patients who are at risk for complications (e.g, elderly, frail, pregnant women, immunocompromised)
  • Endovascular infections, infections associated with prosthetic material, meningitis, and other focal infections can occur.

Etiologies

  • Campylobacter fetus

Primary Regimens

  • Imipenem-cilastatin 500 mg IV q6h or Meropenem 1 gm IV q8h for up to 4-6 weeks (Medicine (Baltimore) 95:e2858, 2016)
  • Ampicillin 100 mg/kg/day IV divided q6h
  • Gentamicin 5 mg/kg IV q24h
  • Ertapenem 1 gm IV q24h

Alternative Regimens

  • None. See Comments for other potentially active agents.

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Antimicrobial therapy not recommended for treatment of gastroenteritis in the immunocompetent host, as the infection is self-limited and antimicrobial therapy shortens the duration of symptoms by only a day or two.
  • Duration of therapy
    • Uncomplicated, but severe gastroenteritis in at risk patient: 3-5 days
    • Complicated, more severe disease in immunocompromised patients:  7-10 days
    • Endovascular infections can occur, optimal therapy not defined but a 4-week course of a fluoroquinolone or beta-lactam is recommended.
    • Meningitis is a rare complication, optimal therapy not defined but prolonged therapy (4-6 weeks) may be required and Meropenem at meningeal doses appears to be effective  (Medicine (Baltimore) 95:e2858, 2016). 

Comments

  • Other potentially active agents include Ampicillin, 3rd generation cephalosporins, carbapenems.
  • Resistance occurs to macrolides and fluoroquinolones: susceptibility testing recommended for more serious infections.
  • Reference: Clin Infect Dis 58:1579, 2014.  
  • Outbreaks uncommon, but cluster of intestinal and extraintestinal cases linked to MSM reported. Clin Infect Dis 2017 65:1751-1753. 
  • IDSA guidelines for infectious diarrhea: Clin Infect Dis 65:e45 2017
