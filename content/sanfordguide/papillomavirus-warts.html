Papillomavirus, Warts

Clinical Setting

  • Warts: external genital, cervix, vaginal, urethral, anal, skin papillomas.
  • Associated with cervical and anal cancers (HPV 16, 18)
  • Associated with head and neck cancers: HPV 16, other serotypes. Reviewed in Head Neck 31:1393, 2009.

Etiologies

  • Papillomaviruses
  • Oncogenic HPV types 16 and 18
    • 66% of all cervical cancers
    • five additional types (6, 11, 31, 33, 45, 52, and 58) targeted by the 9-valent vaccine account for 15%.
  • >90% genital warts HPV types 6 and 11

  • Types 6 and 11 also associated with conjunctival, nasal, oral, and laryngeal warts.

Primary Regimens

  • External genital warts
    • Podofilox (0.5% solution or gel): apply 2x/day x 3 days, 4 days no therapy, repeat cycle 4x
    • Imiquimod 5% cream: apply once daily at bedtime 3x/week on non-consecutive days x up to 16 weeks; or 3.75% cream apply once daily at bedtime up to 8 weeks. May weaken condoms and vaginal diaphragms. Treatment area should be washed with soap and water 6–10 hours after application
    • Sinecatechins: apply to external genital warts only 3x/day until effect or adverse effect (not more than 16 weeks)
    • Cryotherapy with liquid nitrogen; repeat every 1-2 weeks
    • Trichloroacetic acid (TCA) or bichloroacetic acid (BCA) 80%–90% solution: repeat weekly as needed
    • Surgical removal.

    • Persons with external anal or perianal warts might also have intra-anal warts so inspection of the anal canal by digital examination, standard anoscopy, or high-resolution anoscopy should be considered.

    • No one treatment has been shown to be superior so patient-provider shared decision making recommended. 

  • Cervical: Need evaluation for evolving neoplasia. Gynecological exam.
  • Vaginal: Cryotherapy with liquid nitrogen or TCA
  • Urethral: Cryotherapy with liquid nitrogen or surgical
  • Anal: Cryotherapy with liquid nitrogen or TCA or surgical removal. Advise anoscopy to look for rectal warts
  • Skin papillomas: Topical α-lactalbumin. Oleic acid (from human milk) applied once daily x 3 weeks
  • Pregnancy and visible genital warts:  No need for C-section unless warts are obstructing the birth canal

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Prevention

  • See Human Papillomavirus, Vaccine for indications, available products, dosing, and vaccine characteristics for pre-exposure prevention.
    • Only 9vHPV vaccine is available in the United States.
    • now optional consideration in 27-45 yr olds.

Comments

  • Podofilox: Inexpensive and safe (pregnancy safety not established). Mild irritation after treatment.
  • Imiquimod: Mild to moderate redness & irritation. Topical imiquimod effective for treatment of vulvar intraepithelial neoplasms (N Engl J Med 358:1465, 2008). Safety in pregnancy not established.
  • Cryotherapy: blistering and skin necrosis common.
  • Podophyllin resin: Must air dry before treated area contacts clothing. Can irritate adjacent skin. No longer recommended due to other less toxic treatment availability.
  • Sinecatechins: Avoid exposure to mucous membranes (vagina, rectum, mouth, eyes). Appears to work best following cryotherapy (J Drugs Dermatol 13:1400, 2014)
  • TCA: Caustic. Can cause severe pain on adjacent normal skin. Neutralize with soap or sodium bicarbonate.
  • Topical α-lactalbumin decreased lesion size and recurrence vs placebo (p <0.001) (N Engl J Med 350:2663, 2004). Further studies warranted.
  • See 2021 CDC STI guidelines MMWR Recomm Rep 70 (RR-4):1 2021 and 2019 IUSTI European guidelines for anogenital warts
