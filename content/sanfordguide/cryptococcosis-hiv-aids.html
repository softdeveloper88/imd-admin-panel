Cryptococcosis, Meningitis, HIV/AIDS

Clinical Setting

  • Cryptococcemia, Cryptococcal Meningitis in HIV-positive/AIDS patients. Decreased incidence with antiretroviral therapy (ART) but still a common opportunistic infection in newly diagnosed AIDS patients. With ART, symptoms of acute meningitis may return. See Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome (IRIS).
  • Cryptococcal infection may be manifested by positive blood culture or positive serum cryptococcal antigen (CRAG: >95% sens). Serum CRAG is no help in monitoring response to therapy.
  • Management of elevated CSF pressure is ESSENTIAL.
    • Increased CSF pressure (>250 mm H2O) associated with high mortality. Therapeutic lumbar punctures lower acute mortality (Clin Infect Dis 59: 1607, 2014)
    • For CSF pressure >250 mm H2O plus symptoms of increased intracranial pressure, relieve by lumbar puncture and CSF drainage: reduce opening pressure by 50% if it is extremely high or to a normal pressure of <200 mm.
    • For persistent pressure elevation >250 mm of CSF and symptoms, repeat lumbar puncture daily until CSF pressure and symptoms have been stabilized for 2 days.
    • If frequent LPs not possible, ventriculoperitoneal shunts or lumbar drains are an option (Surg Neurol 63:529, 2005).
  • Early diagnosis is essential for improved outcomes (PLOS Medicine 4:e47, 2007).

Etiologies

  • Cryptococcus neoformans

Primary Regimens

  • Induction (2 weeks, longer if CSF cultures fail to sterilize):
    • (Liposomal Amphotericin B 3-4 mg/kg IV q24h or Amphotericin B lipid complex 5 mg/kg IV q24h or Amphotericin B 0.7-1 mg/kg IV q24h) + Flucytosine 25 mg/kg po q6h
  • Consolidation (10 weeks): Fluconazole 400 mg po q24h
  • Secondary prophylaxis: Fluconazole 200 mg po once daily (see criteria below for discontinuation)

Alternative Regimens

  • Induction (duration is regimen specific):
    • (Liposomal Amphotericin B 3-4 mg/kg IV q24h or Amphotericin B lipid complex 5 mg/kg IV q24h or Amphotericin B 0.7-1 mg/kg IV q24h) + Fluconazole 800-1200 mg/day IV/po x 2 weeks (consolidation therapy should be with Fluconazole 800 mg po q24h)
    • Liposomal Amphotericin B 3-4 mg/kg IV q24h or Amphotericin B lipid complex 5 mg/kg IV q24h or Amphotericin B 0.7-1 mg/kg IV q24h x 4-6 weeks
    • Fluconazole 800-1200 mg (higher dose preferred)/day IV/po + Flucytosine 25 mg/kg po q6h x 4–6 weeks
    • Fluconazole 1200-2000 mg po daily x 10-12 weeks
    • Liposomal Amphotericin B 10 mg/kg IV x1 PLUS (flucytosine 100 mg/kg q24h PLUS fluconazole 1200 mg po q24h x 14 days)
  • Consolidation (10 weeks): Fluconazole 400-800 mg po q24h
  • Secondary prophylaxis: Fluconazole 200 mg po once daily (see criteria below for discontinuation)

Comments

  • When to initiate antiretroviral therapy (ART)?
    • Controversial. Randomized trials in Sub-Saharan Africa demonstrated higher mortality when ART was started 1-2 weeks after diagnosis of cryptococcal meningitis when compared to initiation of ART > 5 weeks after diagnosis (45% vs. 30% mortality in earlier vs. later initiation of ART, respectively) (N Engl J Med 370:2487, 2014).  However, mortality rates are much lower (~ 10% in the first 10 weeks of Rx; ~15% overall) in the US and Europe (N Engl J Med 337:15, 1997 and N Eng J Med 326:84, 1992).  A recent cohort study showed no difference in mortality when ART was initiated in the first 2 weeks of therapy for HIV-associated cryptococcal meningitis vs. after 2 weeks (mortality 6.8% vs. 10.5%, early vs later initiation of ART, P > 0.05, in a simulated randomized trial). (Clin Infect Dis Mar 8, 2023, on line ahead of print) Taken together, it seems reasonable to initiate ART ~ 10 - 20 days after initiation of treatment for CM among people with HIV (PWH) in resource-rich countries.
  • Induction therapy:
    • Use of lipid based Amphotericin B is associated with lower mortality compared to Amphotericin B deoxycholate in solid organ transplant recipients (Clin Infect Dis 48:1566, 2009).
    • No data to support use of Liposomal Amphotericin B at doses higher than 3-4 mg/kg/day: efficacy is not improved and toxicity is increased (Clin Infect Dis 51:225, 2010).
    • Monitor Flucytosine levels: peak 70-80 mg/L, trough 30-40 mg/L. Higher levels are associated with bone marrow toxicity. No difference in outcome if given IV or po (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 51:1038, 2007).
    • Amphotericin B 1 mg/kg IV q24h + 5FC 25 mg/kg po q6h imparts a survival advantage over Amphotericin B therapy alone . The addition of Fluconazole 800 mg daily to Amphotericin B likely has benefit over Amphotericin B therapy alone  (N Engl J Med 368:1291, 2013).
    • Fluconazole plus Flucytosine is preferred over Fluconazole alone when Amphotericin cannot be used for induction therapy (N Engl J Med 378:1004, 2018).
    • Induction therapy with Liposomal Amphotericin B 10 mg/kg IV x1 PLUS (flucytosine 100 mg/kg q24h PLUS fluconazole 1200 mg po q24h x 14 days) was noninferior to Amphotericin B deoxycholate 1 mg/kg q24 PLUS flucytosine 100 mg/kg q24h for 7 days, followed by fluconazole 1200 mg/day for 7 days in patients with HIV in Sub-Saharan Africa (N Engl J Med  386: 12, 2022)
    • Role of azoles other than Fluconazole uncertain: successful outcomes were observed in 14/29 (48%) subjects with cryptococcal meningitis treated with Posaconazole (J Antimicrob Chemother 56:745, 2005). Voriconazole also may be effective.
    • Failure of Fluconazole may rarely be due to resistant organism, especially if burden of organism high at initiation of therapy. Although 200 mg qd = 400 mg qd: median survival 76 and 82 days respectively, authors prefer 400 mg po qd (BMC Infect Dis 6:118, 2006).
  • Maintenance Therapy
    • Recurrence rate of 0.4 to 3.9 per 100 patient-years with discontinuation of suppressive therapy in 100 patients on ART with CD4 >100 cells/mm3.
    • Discontinuation of antifungal therapy can be considered among patients who have completed induction therapy and received consolidation for at least 1 year, successful antiretroviral therapy (viral load <50/ml), remains asymptomatic, and CD4 ≥100/mm3 for ≥3 months.
    • Itraconazole less effective than Fluconazole: not recommended because of higher relapse rate (23% vs 4%).
    • No data on Voriconazole for maintenance.
  • Adjunctive use of corticosteroids in HIV patients with cryptococcal meningitis did not reduce mortality and was associated with more adverse events (N Engl J Med 374: 542, 2016).
    • Failure may be due to interference with pro-inflammatory cytokine response (Clin Infect Dis 2019;68:1494).
  • IDSA treatment guidelines: Clin Infect Dis 50:291, 2010.
