Splenic Abscess

Clinical Setting

  • Splenic abscesses are associated with or are a result of:
    • Endocarditis, bacteremia, candidemia
    • Contiguous from an intra-abdominal site, peritonitis
    • Immunocompromised patient immune system
  • Presents with fever and left upper quadrant pain

Diagnosis

  • Diagnosis with CT scan.
    • Abscess can be confused with infarction
    • Microbial etiology determined by blood cultures and/or culture of resected spleen

Etiologies

  • Staphylococcus aureus (associated with bacteremia/endocarditis)
  • Burkholderia (Pseudomonas) pseudomallei (common in SE Asia, northern Australia)
  • Streptococcus sp. (associated with bacteremia/endocarditis)
  • Klebsiella sp. (especially in SE Asia)
  • Can be Polymicrobic (if associated peritonitis)
  • Candida sp. (usually in an immunocompromised patient)
  • Bartonella sp. (cat scratch disease)
  • Many others

Primary Regimens

  • Choice of empiric therapy depends on clinical setting
  • Suspected Staph. aureus
    • MSSA: (Nafcillin or Oxacillin) 2 gm IV q4h or Cefazolin 2 gm  IV q8h
    • MRSA: Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator); alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL
  • Suspected Candida sp. (immunocompromised patient)
    • Lipid-based Amphotericin B 3-5 mg/kg IV daily or Fluconazole or Caspofungin or Micafungin
  • Suspected polymicrobic etiology, Piperacillin-tazobactam (see Secondary peritonitis).

Alternative Regimens

  • Usual treatment is the combination of antibiotics and splenectomy
