Shock, Post-splenectomy

Clinical Setting

  • Overwhelming bacteremia due to encapsulated bacteria in patients with anatomic or functional asplenia
    • Anatomic asplenia due to surgical removal. Rarely, congenitally absent.
    • Functional: e.g., hemolytic anemia due to sickle cell disease; SLE vasculitis.
  • Confirmation of asplenia:
    • Howell-Jolly bodies in peripheral blood smear.
    • Imaging and /or radioisotope scan.
  • Often results in symmetrical peripheral gangrene of digits due to severe Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC).

Etiologies

  • Encapsulated bacteria:
    • S. pneumoniae  (most common)
    • N. meningitidis
    • H. influenzae
    • Capnocytophaga spp. (formerly DF-2)
      • C. canimorsus after a dog bite
    • Bordetella holmesia
  • Severe infection due to RBC parasites:
    • Plasmodium spp.
    • Babesia spp.

Primary Regimens

  • No history of dog bite : Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q24h (increase dose to 2 gm q12h if meningitis)
  • Post-dog bite, need to ensure activity vs Capnocytophaga canimorsus:
    • Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h (see drug page for comment on loading dose)
    • Meropenem 1-2 gm IV q8h
    • If severe allergy to beta-lactams: Clindamycin 900 mg IV q8h
    • See Capnocytophaga spp. for more detail

Alternative Regimens

  • No history of a dog bite: Levofloxacin 750 mg IV q24h or Moxifloxacin 400 mg IV q24h

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Roughly 50% of strains of Capnocytophaga sp. are resistant to fluoroquinolones
  • Poor to inconsistent activity of Aztreonam, fluoroquinolones and TMP-SMX

Comments

  • See Septic Shock for other management recommendations.
  • For prophylaxis with vaccines and antibacterials, see post splenectomy, Infection Prevention.
  • Reference: Chest 2016;150:1394
