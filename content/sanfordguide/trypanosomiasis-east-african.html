Trypanosomiasis, East African

Clinical Setting

  • East African Trypanosomiasis, East African Sleeping Sickness due to T.b. rhodesiense (r-HAT)
  • Early disease, Stage 1 (blood, lymphatic) is very acute, often fulminant (including cardiac involvement) febrile illness that may be accompanied by rash.
  • Late disease, Stage 2 (encephalitis).
    • CSF examination needed to stage.  ≥5 WBC or parasite DNA by PCR indicate Stage 2.
    • Elevated CSF protein and an increase in nonspecific IgM are supportive indicators of Stage 2 disease.
  • Most travel-related cases from Tanzania, Malawi, Zambia (Zambezi valley; Luanga valley) 
    • Non-endemic cases 2011-2020:  PLoS Negl Trop Dis. 2022 Nov 7;16(11):e0010885. 
    • 2024 outbreak in travelers in the Zambezi valley (MMWR 2025, 74(9);158–159)
  • Travel-related cases almost always diagnosed early and in stage 1.
  • Transmitted by Glossina sp. (tsetse fly) with large game animals as the main reservoir.
  • Chancre early after infection, enlarged lymph nodes tend to be submandibular, axillary and inguinal in T. b. rhodesiense.

Etiologies

  • Trypanosoma brucei rhodesiense

Diagnosis

  • Blood smears (concentrated or buffy coat), wet preps of blood (motility), 
  • Lymph node or chancre aspirates
  • LP and CSF examination no longer needed to direct therapy for r-HAT if Fexinidazole, first choice for both Stage 1 or Stage 2 disease is available and indicated
  • Rapid card agglutination test and serology not available for T.b. rhodesiense and CDC does not perform PCR (available in Europe)
    • No morphologic differences between T.b. gambiense and T.b rhodesiense but geographic ranges don't overlap except in Uganda

Primary Regimens

  • Stage 1 and Stage 2: persons who are  ≥ 6 yrs and body weight ≥ 20 kg: 
  • Fexinidazole 
    • Wt ≥ 35 kg: Loading dose 1800 mg (3 tablets) once/day x 4 days then 1200 mg (2 tablets) once/day x 6 days
    • Wt ≥ 20 and < 35 kg:  Loading dose 1200 mg (2 tablets) once/day x 4 days then 600 mg (1 tablet) once/day x 6 days
      • Stage 2: Melarsoprol IV may be preferred if: unable to swallow, fexinidazole contraindication, persistent vomiting, or questionable oral absorption.
    • Since 2024, first-choice treatment for r-HAT based on oral administration and tolerability.
      • 2024 WHO Guidelines
        • In affected areas is available only to government programs (Lancet 391:144, 2018).  
        • In the US, FDA-approved but must be obtained directly from Sanofi.  See Antiparasitic Drugs, Sources
        • Discussion of WHO Guidelines (Lancet Infect Dis. 2024 Oct 7:S1473-3099(24)00581-4)
      • Patient must be able to swallow the 600 mg tablets after a substantial intake of food before each dose, as well as on completing the full 10-day treatment schedule. 
        • FDA suggests avoiding use if eGFR < 30 mL/min.
      • Inpatient therapy for entire oral course.
      • Pregnancy: can use fexinidazole in 2nd and 3rd trimester.
      • Pediatric data: Lancet Glob Health. 2022 Nov;10(11):e1665
  • Children aged < 6 years or body weight < 20 kg
    • Stage 1:  Suramin 5 mg/kg IV (test dose), then 20 mg/kg up to 1 gm (2 mg/kg test dose in children) on days 1, 3, 7, 14 and 21
    • Stage 2: Melarsoprol 2.2 mg/kg/day IV x 10 days; non-inferior to long-standing standard regimen (PLoS NTD 6:e1695, 2012).
      • Prednisone pre-treatment may prevent encephalopathy.
      • Pre-treatment with Suramin as above is used by some clinicians to clear blood stages to minimize reactions to melarsoprol.
  • Pregnancy: untreated acute disease is rapidly fatal
    • Fexinidazole and pentamidine are preferred initially.
    • Suramin and melarsoprol should not be withheld if they are needed as rescue therapy.

Alternative Regimens

  •   ≥ 6 yrs and body weight ≥ 20 kg. 
    • Stage 1: suramin as above or pentamidine 4mg/kg/day IM or IV alone or together with oral fexinidazole (where absorption of oral fexinidazole not assured)
      • and add for Stage 2 add melarsoprol as shown above for young children.
      • Pentamidine [4mg/kg/day IM or IV] may be given for up to 7 days to patients as a temporizing measure and will usually clear peripheral trypanosomes within 24 hours.
  • <6 yrs and <20 kg: Pentamidine [4mg/kg/day IM or IV] 
  • Change to primary drugs immediately when available.

    Comments

    • Tsetse flies are attracted to blue and dark-metallic colored clothing
      • safari travelers to avoid while in endemic areas.
    • There is no test of cure for African trypanosomiasis. After treatment, patients should be closely followed for 24 months and monitored for relapse. Recurrence of symptoms will require examination of body fluids, including CSF, to detect the presence of trypanosomes.
    • Serial LP's every 6 months post treatment for either stage 1 or 2 disease.
    • Large clinical experience with travelers, see Int J  Infect Dis 75:101, 2018.
    • WHO data on fexinidazole for r-HAT.
      • Open-label, single-arm study in adults and children aged ≥ 6 years, treatment success at 12 mos was 100% in Stage 1 and 94.3% in Stage 2 with mortality rates were 0 and 2.9% respectively.
    • The literature on superiority of Suramin over pentamidine for Stage 1 disease is unclear; if Suramin is delayed, 1-2 doses of of Pentamidine [4mg/kg/day IM or IV] may be given for up to 7 days to very ill patients as a temporizing measure and will usually clear peripheral trypanosomes within 24 hours.
    • Suramin and Melarsoprol are available free of charge within the US from the CDC Drug Service under a treatment IND.
    • Drugs may also be obtained free of charge from the WHO. See, Sources for Antiparasitic Drugs.
