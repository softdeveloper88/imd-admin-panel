Sparganosis

Clinical Setting

  • Parasitic infection due to larval cestodes: Spirometra spp (e.g., Spirometra mansonoides) and Sparganum proliferum (can multiply in tissues).
    • Species have been redefined Clin Infect Dis 72: 882,2021
  • Global but most common in East and SE Asia.
  • Definitive hosts are dogs and cats; intermediate hosts are frogs, snakes. Humans can be paratenic hosts, infected with larval forms.
  • Ingestion of minute crustaceans in contaminated water or uncooked intermediate hosts.  Larvae can penetrate skin from poultices of frog or snake flesh.
  • Larvae can migrate to any tissue in body including subcutaneous tissue, breast, orbit, urinary tract, pleural cavity, lungs, abdominal viscera and the central nervous system.
    • May mimic cerebral malignancy on scan, orbital larvae grossly visible of direct ophthalmoscopy.

Etiologies

  • Spirometra mansoni (previous reports of S. ranarum are actually S. Mansoni)
  • Spirometra decipiens
  • Sparganum proliferum
  • Spirometra erinaceieuropaei
  • Spirometra folium

Primary Regimens

  • No antiparasitic therapy
  • Surgical resection, need to remove scolex

Alternative Regimens

  • Ethanol or chymotrypsin injection of subcutaneous masses in S. proliferum
  • Small uncontrolled cases series of Praziquantel in cereberal spaganosis PLoS Negl Trop Dis 14;13(1):e0007018, 2019

Comments

  • Older review (old species names): Lancet Infect Dis. 15:1226, 2015.
  • Large clinical experience in Korea: J Korean Med Sci. 21;33:e273, 2018.
