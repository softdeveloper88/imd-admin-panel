Talaromycosis (Penicilliosis)

Clinical Setting

  • Talaromycosis (formerly penicilliosis) is a common disseminated fungal infection in HIV-positive patients (usually with CD4 < 100 cells/mm3) in Southeast Asia, especially southern China and northern Thailand.
  • Clinical manifestations include prolonged fever, lymphadenopathy, hepatomegaly, pulmonary involvement and umbilicated skin nodules (mimicking cryptococcal infection or molluscum contagiosum).
  • Diagnosis requires isolation of the organism in culture of blood, lymph node, bone marrow, or respiratory specimen.

Etiologies

  • Talaromyces marneffei (formerly Penicillium marneffei)

Primary Regimens

  • Moderate/severe disease:
    • Liposomal Amphotericin B 3-5mg/kg IV daily x 2 weeks followed by Itraconazole 200 mg twice daily po x 10 weeks, then Itraconazole 200 mg/day po until CD4 criteria below are met (see Comments)
  • Mild disease:
    • Itraconazole 200 mg twice daily x 12 weeks, then Itraconazole 200 mg/day po until CD4 criteria below are met (see Comments)

Alternative Regimens

  • Amphotericin B 0.6 mg/kg/day can be used in place of Liposomal Amphotericin B when not available
  • Voriconazole 6 mg/kg IV/po q12h x 2 doses, then 4 mg/kg IV/po q12h can be used when itraconazole is not an option or when there is central nervous system infection (Am J Trop Med Hyg 77:350, 2007)

Comments

  • Amphotericin was superior to itraconazole as initial treatment for talaromycosis with respect to 6-month mortality, clinical response, and fungicidal activity (N Engl J Med 376: 2329, 2017)
  • HIV-positive patients should continue treatment until there is no further clinical evidence of infection and CD4 count >100 cells/μL for >6 months in response to ART.
  • Consider primary prophylaxis for HIV+ patients with CD4 <100 cells/mm3 living in an endemic area with Itraconazole 200 mg po once daily.
  • Treatment guidelines: clinicalinfo.hiv.gov; Lancet Infect Dis. 2021 Aug 5:S1473-3099
