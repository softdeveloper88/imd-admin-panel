Mycobacterium abscessus

Clinical Setting

  • Rapid grower, 3 subspecies.
  • M. abscessus is a cause of skin and soft tissue infection, pulmonary infection, or disseminated disease.
  • Emerging as a cause of pulmonary infection in patients with cystic fibrosis or chronic lung disease.
  • Resistant to multiple antibiotics (J Antimicrob Chemother 67:810, 2012); inducible macrolide resistance.

Etiologies

  • Mycobacterium abscessus with 3 closely related subspecies:
    • M. abscessus subspecies abscessus: typically macrolide resistant [contains inducible erythromycin methylase resistance gene, erm(41)]
    • M. abscessus subspecies bolletii: typically macrolide resistant [contains inducible erm(41)]
    • M. abscessus subspecies massiliense: typically macrolide susceptible [does not contain functional erm(41)]

Primary Regimens

  • Pulmonary or disseminated disease (See Antimicrobial Stewardship)
    • No inducible and no constitutive/mutational macrolide resistance. Confirm macrolide susceptibility in vitro or by molecular testing (See Comments).
      • Initial treatment phase: treat for 1-3 months depending on severity of infection, clinical response, tolerability, and toxicity, ideally with the goal of converting sputum cultures to negative. Duration of this phase is not well defined and depends on achieving a favorable clinical and microbiological response.
      • Choose 1-2 parenteral agents + 2 oral agents; at least 3 agents recommended
        • Parenteral agents (preferred order for Amikacin-susceptible isolates)
          • Amikacin 10-15 mg/kg IV once daily (adjust dose to obtain a peak serum concentration of 35-45 μg/mL in a sample drawn 30 min after a 30 min infusion and a trough of < 5 μg/mL) or 15-25 mg/kg IV thrice weekly (adjust dose to obtain a peak serum concentration of 65-80 μg/mL in a sample drawn 30 min after a 30 min infusion and a trough of < 5 μg/mL)
          • Imipenem 1000 mg IV in q8-12h or Cefoxitin 8-12 gm/day IV in 2-3 divided doses
          • Tigecycline 100 mg loading dose then 50 mg q12h IV
        • Oral agents (preferred order)
          • Azithromycin 250-500 mg once daily or 500 thrice weekly
          • Clofazimine 100-200 mg once daily (difficult to obtain in the US)
          • Omadacycline 300 mg po once daily
          • Linezolid 600 mg once daily or Tedizolid 200 mg once daily
          • Bedaquiline 400 mg once daily for 2 weeks then 200 mg thrice weekly
      • Continuation phase: optimal duration of therapy not well defined, but 12 months after sputum cultures convert to negative is reasonable
        • Choose 2 oral/inhaled agents
        • Inhaled option: Amikacin liposome inhalation suspension 590 mg once daily administered only with Lamira Nebulizer System
    • Any macrolide resistance, inducible or constitutive/mutational. Confirm macrolide resistance in vitro or by molecular testing (See Comments).
      • Initial treatment phase: treat for 1-3 months depending on severity of infection, clinical response, tolerability, and toxicity, ideally with the goal of converting sputum cultures to negative. Duration of this phase is not well defined and depends on achieving a favorable clinical and microbiological response.
      • Choose 1-2 parenteral agents + 2-3 oral or inhaled medications, at least 4 agents recommended
        • Parenteral agents (preferred order for Amikacin-susceptible isolates)
          • Amikacin as above
          • Imipenem or Cefoxitin as above
          • Tigecycline as above
        • Oral agents (preferred order)
          • Clofazimine as above
          • Omadacycline as above
          • Linezolid or Tedizolid as above
          • Bedaquiline as above
          • Azithromcyin as above for its potential immunomodulatory effect but should not count as an active oral medication.
      • Continuation phase: optimal duration of therapy not well defined, but 12 months after sputum cultures convert to negative is reasonable. 
        • Choose 2-3 oral/inhaled agents
        • Inhaled option: Amikacin liposome inhalation suspension 590 mg once daily administered only with Lamira Nebulizer System
        • Azithromcyin as above for its potential immunomodulatory effect but should not count as an active oral medication
  • Cutaneous infection
    • Optimal regimen not well defined
    • Surgical debridement + at least 2 active agents from among those listed above.
    • Azithromycin is a preferred agent unless the isolate is macrolide resistant, either inducible or constitutive/mutational (see Comments) in which case two other agents should be selected.
    • Duration of therapy not well defined but 4-6 months is sufficient in most cases, although deeper or more extensive infections may require longer

Alternative Regimens

  • Azithromycin preferred but can substitute Clarithromycin 500 mg po bid for Azithromycin.
  • Omadacycline: emerging data supporting efficacy and tolerability (Open Forum Infect Dis. 2023 Jul 3;10(7):ofad335 and Antimicrob Agents Chemother. 2023 Oct 18;67(10):e0082423).
  • Other potentially active oral agents include Doxycycline, Minocycline, Moxifloxacin, although clinical experience with these agents is limited.
  • M. abscessus subspecies massiliense
    • Inducible resistance to macrolides not seen due to non-functional macrolide resistance gene.
    • Outcomes of medical therapy are better with infection from this species compared to other members of the complex (Int J Tuberc Lung Dis 18:1141, 2014, Clin Infect Dis 64:301, 2017). 
  • If liposomal formulation is unavailable nebulized Amikacin is an option: 250mg/mL amikacin sulfate diluted with 3 mL of saline administered by jet nebulizer once or twice daily.

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Consultation with an expert in the treatment and management of M. abscessus pulmonary infection is strongly recommended to design the antimicrobial regimen and to assist with determination of duration of therapy and most especially for management of refractory disease or salvage therapy.

Comments

  • Resistance to macrolides is either constitutive due to a mutation(s) in rrl, which encodes 23S ribosomal RNA or inducible due to a functional ribosomal methylase, encoded by erm(41).  Constitutive, also termed mutational resistance, is detected phenotypically in vitro by growth in the presence of macrolide (clarithromycin) after a 3-5 day incubation or molecularly by detection of resistance mutations.  Inducible resistance is detect phenotypically in vitro by growth after a 14 day incubation or molecularly by detection of functional gene sequence. 
  • Pulmonary and disseminated disease are notoriously refractory to therapy and outcomes are poor with half or more of patients remaining culture-positive or relapsing, particularly with infection due to strains with inducible macrolide resistance such as M. abscessus subspecies abscessus (Clin Infect Dis 2017; 64:309, Clin Infect Dis 2017; 64:301 and Antimicrob Agents Chemother. 2017; 24;61: e01206). Toxicity may be limiting; long-term suppressive therapy often needed to control symptoms or disease progression.
  • Disseminated disease associated with auto-antibodies to interferon-γ (Immunobiology 2013; 218:762).

  • ATS/ERS/ESCMID/IDSA Clinical Practice Guidelines: Clin Infect Dis 2020; 71:e1-e36. See for an excellent review: Chest. 2022; 161:64
