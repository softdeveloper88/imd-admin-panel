Peritonsillar Abscess

Clinical Setting

  • Peritonsillar abscess, "quinsy", is one type of deep neck infection manifest as an abscess between the tonsil and capsule of the tonsil.
    • Other deep neck infections: retropharyngeal abscess,  parapharyngeal space abscess
  • It can develop as a complication of acute tonsillitis or spontaneously, in the absence of acute disease, in patients with poor oral hygiene, immunodeficiency, or excessive tobacco use (J Antimicrob Chemother 68:1941, 2013).
  • Diagnosis: physical examination with confirmation by CT scan.
  • Peritonsillar abscess microbiology: Eur J of Microbiol Infect Dis 2015; 34:549

Diagnosis

  • Physical examination with confirmation by CT scan.

Etiologies

  • Streptococcus pyogenes
  • Fusobacterium necrophorum
  • Streptococcus anginosus group
  • Rare isolates:
    • S. aureus
    • Nocardia spp.
    • Haemophilus influenzae
    • Arcanobacterium haemolyticum
    • Streptococcus pneumoniae
  • Multidrug-resistant aerobic gram-negative bacilli are unusual but have been reported
  • Other anaerobic bacteria

Primary Regimens

  • Surgical drainage and debridement often required; obtain surgical consultation
  • Empiric: Ampicillin-sulbactam 3 gm IV q6h
  • See Antimicrobial Stewardship re de-escalation

Alternative Regimens

  • Metronidazole 500 mg IV q6-8 h or 1 gm IV q12h + Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV once daily
  • If severed penicillin allergy: Clindamycin 600 - 900 mg IV q8h

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Avoid Azithromycin as Fusobacterium sp. are resistant

Comments

  • Potential serious complications include: jugular vein suppurative phlebitis (Lemierre's syndrome) and/or erosion into the carotid artery with subsequent hemorrhage.
