Cardiac Implantable Electronic Device Infections

Clinical Setting

  • Presence of cardiac implantable electronic device (CIED). Infection resulting from pacemaker, defibrillator. 
  • Pocket discomfort, erythema, swelling, wound drainage, erosion of device or lead through overlying skin; fever, other systemic signs or symptoms of infection.
  • Positive blood cultures for gram-positive cocci or unexplained bacteremia from other organisms
    • Transeophageal echocardigram indicated to evaluate for possible endocarditis, lead vegetation (see Comments)

Etiologies

  • Staphylococcus aureus (40%)
  • Staphylococcus epidermidis (40%)
  • Other staphylococcal species (10%)
  • Other potential organisms
    • Gram-negative bacilli (5%)
    • Enterococcus sp.(5%)
    • Streptococcus sp (5%)
    • Propionibacterium (now Cutibacterium acnes) (1-5 %)
    • Fungi (5%).

Primary Regimens

  • Methicillin-resistant staphylococci: Device removal + Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h
  • Methicillin-susceptible staphylococci: Device removal + Nafcillin 2 gm IV q4h or Cefazolin 2 gm IV q8h
  • Gram-negative infection: Device removal with antimicrobial therapy is guided by in vitro susceptibility test result
  • See Antimicrobial Stewardship for duration of treatment

Alternative Regimens

  • Staphylococci: Device removal + Daptomycin 8-10 mg/kg IV q24h (not FDA approved)

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Duration of treatment after device removal:
    • For “pocket” or subcutaneous infection, negative blood cultures: 10–14 days
    • Bacteremia, no evidence of endocarditis: at least 14 days and 4-6 weeks for staphylococcal infection
    • If lead-associated endocarditis, up to 4–6 weeks depending on the organism. See CIED Endocarditis for details.
  • For patients in whom device removal is not feasible, e.g., those with major co-morbid illness, high risk for poor outcome from the extraction procedure, limited life expectancy, consider pathogen-directed, life-long oral suppressive antibiotic therapy.

Comments

  • Consider 18F-fluoro-2-deoxyglucose positron emission tomography with CT (FDG-PET/CT) in patients with suspected device infection and negative or inconclusive TEE (JACC Cardiovasc Imaging. 2022;15:891).
  • Device removal (which is key) and absence of valvular vegetation associated with significantly greater survival at 1 year (JAMA 307:1727, 2012).
  • American Heart Association guidelines:  Circulation. 2024;149:e201 and Circulation. 2010;121:458.
  • British guidelines: J Antimicrob Chemother 70:325, 2015.
