Tuberculosis, Latent, Positive Test

Clinical Setting

  • Regimens listed below are recommended regardless of HIV status
  • Latent M. tuberculosis (MTB) infection
    •  Positive test for latent tuberculosis, either positive tuberculin skin test (TST) or interferon-gamma release assay (IGRA) (Quantiferon, T-spot, or Elisppot) PLUS no evidence of active disease (see also Diagnostic Testing).
      • Positive TST is > 5 mm induration for the following high risk groups:
        • HIV-positive: risk of progression to active disease is 10-fold higher in advanced HIV compared to otherwise normal host.
        • Recent close contact to active case.
        • Radiographic evidence of old, healed tuberculosis no prior treatment
        • Immunosuppression
        • Chronic steroid therapy (prednisone >15 mg/day)
      • Positive TST is > 10 mm induration for those with other risk factors including foreign-born from high-prevalence region, injection drug use, chronic renal failure, illicit IV drug use, silicosis; diabetes mellitus, weight >10% below normal or chronic malnutrition, previous gastrectomy; smoker.
  • TNF-α inhibitor therapy significantly increases risk of progression to active tuberculosis and candidates should be pre-screened for latent tuberculosis and treated with latent TB if TST or IGRA is positive (Infect Dis Clin N Am 2020;34:413).
    • See Prevention: Persons starting Biologics for other agents associated with risk of TB.
  • Choice of regimen is determined by
    • Susceptibility or resistance to Isoniazid and/or Rifampin of isolate of index case, if known
    • Age
    • Pregnancy
  • CDC 2020 Guidelines for the Treatment of Latent Tuberculosis Infection here.
  • WHO guidelines here.
  • Review: N Engl J Med 2021; 385: 2271.

Etiologies

  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis

Primary Regimens

  • Isoniazid-susceptible organism likely (shorter course rifamycin-based regimens preferred over 6-9 month Isoniazid regimens):
    • Isoniazid (INH) + Rifapentine in 12 once-weekly doses (MMWR Morb Mortal Wkly Rep. 2018 Jun 29;67(25):723-726).
      • INH (plus pyridoxine 12.5 to 25 mg for children aged 2-11, 50 mg for adults, once weekly po):
        • Adults: 15 mg/kg, round up to nearest 50 or 100 mg to a max dose of 900 mg once weekly po
        • Children aged 2-11 years: 25 mg/kg to a max dose of 900 mg once weekly po
      • Rifapentine, adults and children, dosed once weekly po:
        • 10.0-14.0 kg dose = 300 mg
        • 14.1-25.0 kg = 450 mg
        • 25.1-32.0 kg = 600 mg
        • 32.1-49.9 kg = 750 mg
        • > 50.0 kg = 900 mg
      • Safe and effective for children age 2 years and older.
      • May be used in patients with HIV/AIDS taking into account potential drug interactions with antiretroviral agents.
      • Not recommended for children age < 2 years, suspected latent infection with Isoniazid or Rifampin-resistant organism.
      • Not recommended for pregnant women or women expected to become pregnant within the 12 week regimen
      • May be administered as directly observed or self-administered therapy. 
    • Rifampin x 4 months
      • Adult: 10 mg/kg, max dose 600 mg once daily po
      • Children: 15-20 mg/kg, max dose 600 mg once daily po
    • INH + Rifampin x 3 months
      • Adults: INH 5 mg/kg, max dose 300 mg once daily po + Rifampin 10 mg/kg, max dose 600 mg once daily po + pyridoxine 50 mg once daily po
      • Children: INH 10-20 mg/kg, max dose 300 mg once daily po + Rifampin 15-20 mg/kg, max dose 600 mg once daily po + pyridoxine 6.25 mg (infants) to 25 mg (school-aged children) once daily po
    • Pregnancy
      • For most women, treatment can be delayed until 2–3 months post-partum to avoid administering unnecessary medication during pregnancy.
      • For women who are at high risk for progression from latent TB infection to TB disease, especially those who are a recent contact of someone with infectious TB disease, treatment for latent TB infection should not be delayed on the basis of pregnancy alone, even during the first trimester.
      • Rifampin x 4 months or INH + Rifampin + pyridoxine x 3 months as above
  • Isoniazid-resistant organism, rifampin-susceptible organism likely (also an option for Isoniazid susceptible organism in those unable to tolerate INH)
    • Rifampin x 4 months
      • Adults: 10 mg/kg, max dose 600 mg once daily po
      • Children: 15-20 mg/kg, max dose 600 mg once daily po
      • Pregnancy: 10 mg/kg, max dose 600 mg once daily po
        • For women at risk for progression of latent to active disease, especially those who are HIV+ or who have been recently infected, treatment should not be delayed even during the first trimester. If no risk factor, delay treatment until after delivery.
  • MDR, Isoniazid-resistant and rifampin-resistant organism likely:
    • Levofloxacin once daily po x 24 weeks (See WHO guidelines of TB prevention and treatment and  N Engl J Med 2024; 391:2376)
      • Children: 15-20 mg/kg (max dose 750 mg) po once daily
      • Adults: 10-15 mg/kg (max dose 750 mg( po once daily
    • Pregnancy: expert consultation advised

Alternative Regimens

  • INH x 6-9 months
    • Recommended duration of Isoniazid preventative therapy is 9 months, but Isoniazid 300 mg po qd x 6 months also has some efficacy and may be considered in select patients (not recommended in children, HIV+ persons, or those with fibrotic lesions on chest  film) unable to tolerate or comply with a 9-month treatment duration (Ann Intern Med 2017;167:248).
      • Adults: Isoniazid 5 mg/kg/day, max dose maximum 300 mg once daily po + pyridoxine 50 mg once daily po
      • Children: 10-15 mg/kg/day, max dose 300 mg once daily po + pyridoxine 6.25 mg (infants) to 25 mg (school-aged) once daily po
      • Pregnancy: Izoniazid 5 mg/kg/day, max dose maximum 300 mg once daily po + pyridoxine 50 mg once daily po
  • INH+ Rifapentine x 1 month (1HP (N Engl J Med. 2019;3 80:1001)
    • Age > 13 years old only
    • INH 300 mg once daily for 4 weeks PLUS
    • Rifapentine once daily dose for 4 weeks 
      • Weight < 35 kg: 300 mg
      • Weight 35-45 kg: 450 mg
      • Weight > 45 kg: 600 mg
  • Other alternative regimens (MMWR 49:1-54, 2000)
    • Isoniazid 15 mg/kg (adult), 20-30 mg/kg (child) to max dose of 900 mg twice weekly for 9 months + pyridoxine 50 mg (adults), 6.25 mg (infants) to 25 mg (school-aged children) twice weekly po
    • Isoniazid 15 mg/kg (adult), 20-30 mg/kg (child) to max dose of 900 mg twice weekly for 6 months + pyridoxine 50 mg (adults), 6.25 mg (infants) to 25 mg (school-aged children) twice weekly po
    • NOTE:  Rifampin + Pyrazinamide no longer recommended due to toxicity

Comments

  • CDC treatment regimens for latent TB here.
  • For patients on Isoniazid educate and monitor clinically for signs and symptoms of hepatitis
  • Laboratory monitoring.
    • Not routinely indicated.
    • Baseline liver chemistry blood tests indicated for patients with specific conditions: HIV infection, liver disorders, postpartum period (≤3 months after delivery), regular alcohol usage, injection drug usage, or taking medications with known possible interactions with either Isoniazid or Rifapentine.
      • Consider a baseline liver chemistry blood test for older patients on an individual basis, especially for those taking medications for chronic medical conditions.
      • Conduct blood tests at the next clinical visit for patients whose baseline testing is abnormal and for others at risk for liver disease.
      • Discontinue 12-week INH-Rifapentine regimen if a serum ALT/AST concentration is ≥5 times the upper limit of normal in the absence of symptoms, or ≥3 times the upper limit of normal in the presence of symptoms.
    • Lab monitoring of liver function during therapy is also indicated if baseline liver function tests are abnormal, if other risk factors for hepatic disease are present, or to evaluate for possible adverse effects.
  • Meta-analysis of effectiveness of various treatment regimens for prophylaxis after MDR TB exposure: Clin Infect Dis 2017; 64:1670.
