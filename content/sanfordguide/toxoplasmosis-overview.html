Toxoplasma gondii, Toxoplasmosis, Overview

Overview

  • Toxoplasma gondii is an intracellular protozoan organism. Over 1/3 the world's population is infected (as reflected by seropositivity). Prevalence varies world-wide; overall seroprevalence declining Am J Trop Med Hyg, 98:551, 2018
  • Most people are asymptomatic
  • An important cause of opportunistic infections in immunocompromised individuals (AIDS-defining OI)
  • Brain abscess most common in immunocompromised, ocular disease in immunocompetent.
  • In the Northern Hemisphere, 3 genotypes predominate, but much more genetic diversity in other regions of the world. Genotypes from South America more virulent, symptomatic disease more frequent in individuals from Brazil and Latin America.
  • Outbreaks of more severe disease linked to ingestion of game (venison) reported in US.
  • Humans are infected by:
    • Ingestion of food or water contaminated with oocysts from cat feces (including clams and oysters that can concentrate oocysts)
    • Ingestion of cysts in undercooked meat of infected animals including beef and lamb
    • From infected mother to fetus (congenital toxoplasmosis; the "T" in TORCH)
    • Blood transfusion or organ transplant from infected donor.

Clinical Setting

  • In an immunocompetent host, primary infection is usually asymptomatic
  • Immunocompromised individuals can present with encephalitis, brain abscess, or disseminated infection
  • The most common infectious cause of posterior uveitis (especially in South America), which can involve the retina (retinochoroiditis aka chorioretinitis) or optic nerve
  • Latent infection is thought persist for life.
  • Reactivation of latent infection (bradyzoites in tissue cysts) is thought to be the etiology of clinical toxoplasmosis in immunocompromised individuals.
  • Updated (Sept 2024) Guidelines for Prevention and Treatment of Opportunistic Infections  

Diagnosis

  • Diagnosis is usually made by antibody testing (IgM and IgG). See J Clin Micro 42:941, 2004.
  • PCR is useful, if positive, in ocular, CNS, congenital, or disseminated disease. Specific, but not sensitive.
  • Metagenomics of fluids, including blood can make the diagnosis.
  • PCR can be used to monitor infection in seropositive immunosuppressed individuals (esp hematopoietic stem cell transplants) but is not available in all centers.
