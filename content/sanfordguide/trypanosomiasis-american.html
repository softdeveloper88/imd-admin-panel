Trypanosomiasis, American, Chagas disease

Clinical Setting

  • Chagas disease, American trypanosomiasis. Reviews:  Lancet 2024;403:203-218   Ann Intern Med 2023.176:ITC17-ITC32. Am J Trop Med Hyg 2023;109:1006-1011.
  • Six distinct lineages of T. cruzi are classified into discrete typing units (TcI-VI), which vary in their geographic occurrence, host specificity, and pathogenicity
    • No treatment implications at present
  • Transmission via inoculation of infected feces from triatomine bugs in Latin America.
    • Oral transmission, often high innoculum, >30 outbreaks Trop Med Int Health. 2023 Sep;28(9):689-698. doi: 10.1111/tmi.13915.
  • Acute disease with undifferentiated febrile syndrome with adenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly and chagoma at inoculation site
    • not seen with oral transmission. 
  • US burden reviewed: Emerging Infect Dis 2022 Jul;28(7):1313
    • Only 78 autochthonous infections were documented from 1950-2022 in U.S., last case in Missouri 2018 (MMWR Morb Mortal Wkly Rep 69; 193, 2020). 
    • T. cruzi present in 27 US mammalian species notably the Virginia opossum. PLoS Negl Trop Dis 2022 Dec 19;16(12):e0010974
  • PAHO treatment guidelines here
  • Chagas can be transmitted via organ transplant or transfusions. See Transplants, Trypanosomiasis Prevention.
    • If Chagas disease present in donor or recipient, can contact CDC for treatment suggestions: 770-718-4745 (emergency: 770-488-7100).
    • If infection found in donor, monitor recipient by microscopy and PCR of blood (Clin Transplant 33; e13546, 2019).
      • No treatment of recipient without proven infection
  • Congenital infection
    • Transmission occurs in 1-10% of live births to untreated mothers.
    • Estimated up to 43,000 women of childbearing age in the US have chronic Chagas disease
    • Estimated 22-305 infants with chronic Chagas born in US each year. Emerg Infect Dis. 2022 Jul;28(7):1313

Indications for Treatment

  • Strong indications for a full course of treatment for the following persons with laboratory proof of infection:
    • Acute disease
      • Known or suspected exposure to the triatomine vector in the past 3 mos.
      • Congenital disease (always acute) in infants of infected mothers in the first 3 mos of life
        • Review of congenital Chagas: Ped Rev 2023 Apr 1;44(4):213. Recipient of blood, organ, or tissue from an infected donor.
    • Chronic disease
      • Children ≤18 yrs, including infants diagnosed as infected after age 3 mos.
        • The younger the child at time of treatment, the higher the rate of sero-reversion: Antimicrob Agents Chemother 17:66(5), 2022.
      • Pregnant individuals and those considering pregnancy (treat after pregnancy and lactation).
      • Immunosuppressed patient (HIV, after organ transplant, other iatrogenic) with findings suggestive of reactivation.
    • Treatment is discretionary or controversial (weak evidence) for adults >18 yrs with laboratory evidence of chronic or reactivation infection and the following: PLoS Negl Trop Dis 2022 May 16;16(5):e0010386.
      • Normal ECG and echocardiogram and no symptoms suggestive of GI disease.
      • ECG findings suggestive of early cardiomyopathy and preserved left ventricular function.
      • Persons with HIV during entry to care or later as catch-up.
      • Transplant candidate (pretransplant screening).
      • Transplant recipient known to have chronic T. cruzi infection.
    • Treatment is contraindicated in adults >18 yrs with moderate to severe ventricular dysfunction or GI disease with impaired absorption
    • Treatment of blood-donors found positive by blood bank screens is based on same criteria for infected adults as above once the diagnosis is laboratory confirmed by CDC criteria.
    • See JAMA 2024 Jun 18;331(23):2037 for further details and need for ongoing follow-up of chronic infection.

Diagnosis

  • Screen
    • All persons who have resided for >6 mos in Mexico, Central America, or South America.
    • Infants and children of mothers who lived > 6 months in the above areas.
    • Those reporting contact with a triatomine vector in the above areas.
  • Systematically screening (IgG serology) persons with HIV who have spent ≥6 months in continental Latin America  (eg, during their “entry-to-care” visit) is recommended due to 0.68% positivity rate  (Clin Infect Dis 2024; 78:453).
  • Blood films and PCR in acute disease (including transplants, transfusion), congenital disease, reactivation in immunosuppressed.
    • Two target PCR necessary; only available at CDC not commercial labs.
      • Review of testing: PLoS Negl Trop Dis. 6:e1881, 2012.
  • IgG serology ideally comprises 2 IgG tests run in tandem, with a third IgG assay if discordant.
    • If impractical, screen with one sensitive IgG assay then obtain additional testing if initial result is positive or inconclusive.
    • The 4 FDA-cleared assays show a range of sensitivities (88%-97%) and specificities (92%-100%).
  • In those who screen positive in blood bank testing (Ortho EIA kit):
    • Two separate confirmatory serological tests based on different antigens and/or techniques (EIA and immunoblot (TESA) needed to makes diagnosis  
    • If initial EIA and TESA results are discordant, an IFA is used as a “tie-breaker” test. 
  • Non-blood bank screening:  EIA (Wiener kit) plus confirmatory TESA/IFA; all available via CDC
    • Many US commercial labs use Hemagen or Inbios EIA kits which appear to have high false-positive rates in Central American populations. 
      • Positive commercial EIA tests should be confirmed at CDC before any treatment.
  • To diagnose congenital infection 
    • Assess serologic status on mother or, if unkown, on infant as above
      • Serial serology starts at 8 mos of age
    • If even one serologic test is positive, look for organisms, either through stain of infant blood or PCR.
      • Cord blood can be used if free from maternal blood contamination.  J Infect Dis. 2022 May 4;225(9):1601
  • Various other serologic methods are commercially available worldwide.
  • In chronic disease blood films negative and PCR usually negative or unreliable; use serology. 
  • Markedly decreased IgG titer at 1 year after treatment is a marker of cure.

Etiologies

  • Trypanosoma cruzi

Primary Regimens

  • Standard regimen:  Adult (Age ≥12 years): Benznidazole 5-7 mg/kg/day po divided into 2 doses (q12h) x 60 days
    • Due to adverse effects use no more than 5 mg/kg and some experienced clinicians treat adults with 300 mg per day for 60 days, regardless of body weight or give 300 mg per day but prolong treatment to complete the total dose corresponding to 5 mg/kg per day for 60 days
    • Preliminary trials on shorter duration regimens encouraging.
      • Phase 3 trials of 2 or 4 wks of 100 mg po tid are in progress.  
  • US guidelines: treat chronic infection (often incidentally found) in adults up to 50 years old without advanced Chagas cardiomyopathy and consider if over age 50.
  • Pediatric (Age <12 years): Benznidazole 7.5 mg/kg/day po in 2 doses (q12h) x 60 days.  
    • US tablets approved only for 2-12 years of age, may be used as a slurry.
      • May be used for younger children based on clincal judgement
    • Other pediatric tablet formulations (LAFEPE and ELEA) used in South America but not widely available

Alternative Regimens

  • Adult: Nifurtimox 8–10 mg/kg/day po divided 4x/day x 60 days
  • Pediatric: Nifurtimox
    • >41 kg  8 to 10 mg/kg/day po divided 4x/day x 60 days
    • <41 kg 10 to 20 mg/kg/day 
  • Clear second choice due to severe GI intolerance
    • Adverse effects: Clin Infect Dis 2016, 63:1056.

Comments

  • Benznidazole (U.S.)(available from Exeltis, Inc., Tel: +1 877-303-7181; www.benznidazoletablets.com.
    • Benznidazole 12.5 & 100 mg FDA approved tabs for 2-12 yr olds.
      • CDC advocates off-label use in adults.
    • Fast-access program by contacting Exeltis (Foundation Care) supplies at no charge for under-insured patients. See Antiparasitic Drugs Availability.
  • High rates of intolerance using standard doses of benznidazole (Clin Infect Dis 60:1237, 2015).
    • PK modeling in adults suggests that 5 mg/kg/day of benznidazole divided q12h may be excessive (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 59:3342, 2015).
  • Cure rates:  congenital infection (100%), other acute infection (60-80%), chronic age <12 (60%), chronic <50 yrs (20-40%), advanced cardiac or GI disease (0%).
    • Cure rates unclear but consider treatment for chronic disease >50 yrs of age or in persons with very mild cardiac abnormalities
  • Benznidazole in patients with established Chagas cardiomyopathy reduces serum parasite detection but does not significantly reduce 5 year cardiac clinical deterioration. N Engl J Med 373:1295, 2015.
  • Benznidazole and Nifurtimox:
    • The two drugs have different FDA approved age ranges.
      • Benznidazole 2-12 yrs of age
      • Nifurtimox birth to 18 yrs
        • in both cases CDC suggests treating a patient off-label outside of the FDA-approved age range based on clinical diagnosis and decision by a treating physician under practice of medicine.
    • Both are contraindicated in pregnancy, use adequate contraception during therapy.
      • Ideal to treat infected women of child-bearing age when not pregnant.
    • Take both drugs with meals to alleviate GI side effects. See Am J Trop Med Hyg 63:111, 2000.
    • Both are poorly tolerated, courses often unfinished especially in older patients.
    • Nifurtimox FDA approved in August 2020, and is commercially available.  See Antiparasitic Drugs
  • Posaconazole highly effective in vitro but clear failure in humans, likely has only static effect.
  • Fexinidazole is now drug of choice for T.b gambiense and T.b rhodesiense but trials for T. cruzi disappointing so far due to toxicity of prolonged courses (Clin Infect Dis 2022 Aug 4;ciac579)
