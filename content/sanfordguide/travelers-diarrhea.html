Traveler's Diarrhea

Clinical Setting

  • The incidence of traveler's diarrhea (TD) during a 2-week trip is 10-40%. Most cases are caused by bacterial enteropathogens (J Travel Med 2017;24:S57). Antibiotic chemoprophylaxis for TD is not generally recommended. See CDC 2020 Yellow Book recommendations
  • Criteria for treatment depend on severity of TD, which is now based upon functional categories rather than frequency of stool criteria.
    • Mild TD: tolerable that doesn't interfere with activities (formerly 1-2 unformed stools /24h)
    • Moderate TD: distressing or interferes with activities (formerly 3-5 unformed stools/24h)
    • Severe TD: incapacitating, dysentery (bloody stools) (formerly >6-9 stools/24h)
  • Treatment considerations:
    • Mild TD: Bismuth or loperamide is usually effective for treatment of mild TD.
    • Moderate to severe TD: azithromycin, rifaximin, or FQ can be used.
      • Ciprofloxacin 500 mg po bid x 3 days or Levofloxacin 500 mg po once daily x 3 days
      • Azithromycin is now preferred as first line treatment, particularly in areas where Campylobacter is common (J Travel Med. 2017 24:S57).
      • For reviews see: JAMA 2015 313:71; N Engl J Med 2014 370:1532 ; Clin Microbiol Infect2015 21:744; Am J Gastroenterol 2016 111:602; J Travel Med 2017;24:S57.
  • An undefined but real proportion of patients develop significant post-infectious sequelae (J Trav Med 20:303, 2013) that are associated with more severe and longer illness durations.

Etiologies

  • Acute:
    • Enterotoxigenic (ETEC) or enteroaggregative (EAEC) E. coli (60%)
    • Shigella sp.
    • Salmonella sp.
    • Campylobacter sp.
    • Others: Aeromonas, Plesiomonas, viruses (Norovirus) and parasites (amebiasis)
    • C. difficile
  • Persistent/Chronic (defined as >14 days):
    • Cyclospora sp., Cryptosporidia sp., Giardia sp., Cystoisospora sp. (mainly in HIV+)
    • Post-infectious irritable bowel disease

Primary Regimens

  • Adult:
    • Azithromycin 1000 mg po x 1 (or 500 mg q12 x 2 to mitigate GI upset) or 500 mg q24h x 3 days
  • Pediatric:
    • Azithromycin 10 mg/kg/day single dose or x 3 days
    • Ceftriaxone 50 mg/kg/day IV once daily x 3 days
  • Both adult and pediatric:
    • Add Imodium 4 mg po x 1 dose, then 2 mg after each loose stool (up to a maximum of 16 mg/day).

Alternative Regimens

  • Resistance patterns depending on region of travel should be taken into consideration. Fluoroquinolones are no longer recommended as first line treatment for many regions
    • Ciprofloxacin 500 mg po bid x 3 days
    • Levofloxacin 500 mg q24h x 3 days or Ofloxacin 300 mg po bid x 3 days or Moxifloxacin 400 mgNAI (probably would work)
    • Rifaximin 200 mg po tid x 3 days (not absorbed so only for diarrheagenic E. coli NOT invasive disease) or Rifamycin SV two tablets bid x 3 days (for E. coli, adult only; do not use if fever or bloody diarrhea)

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Due to emerging resistance, particularly in Southeast Asia and India/Nepal where fluoroquinolone-resistant Campylobacter is common (JAMA 313:71, 2015), azithromycin is generally the preferred treatment.
  • Increasing FQ resistance for enteric pathogens, including ETEC and EAEC in India. Self treatment with a fluoroquinolone reported associated with acquisition of multi-drug resistant organisms. Clin Infect Dis 60:837 2015; Clin Infect Dis 60:847 2015.  
  • Because of increased acquisition of MDR organisms, single dose therapy and avoidance of fluoroquinolone is recommended.

Comments

  • Azithromycin first choice for pregnant or possibly pregnant women.
  • Many antimalarials also prolong QT; caution in travel patients with underlying cardiac disease.
  • Pediatric suspensions must be refrigerated after reconstitution and expire quickly so not practical for travel. Only a pharmacist can re-constitute.
  • No loperamide if fever or blood in stool (i.e. dysentery).
  • Ciprofloxacin and Rifaximin have equivalent efficacy vs non-invasive pathogens (Am J Trop Med Hyg 74:1060, 2006).
  • Rifaximin is approved (for treatment) of children age >12 years. Adverse effects are similar to placebo. Pregnancy category C (no adequate trials in pregnant women).
  • Avoid fluoroquinolones in children and pregnant women.
  • Preventative treatment of traveler's diarrhea is not routinely indicated. See CDC 2020 Yellow Book recommendations
    • Preferred approach in the current recommendation is Azithromycin 1000 mg once + Imodium with first loose stool.
    • Consider Ciprofloxacin 500 mg po daily for short trips with vital missions that cannot be disrupted and in immunocompromised patients and those with HIV and CD4 <200.
    • As an alternative during the first 3 weeks (if activities are essential): Rifaximin 200 mg po bid, Ann Intern Med 142:805, 2005; Ann Intern Med 142:861, 2005. Such prophylactic use is not an FDA approved indication.
