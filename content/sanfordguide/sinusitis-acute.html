Sinusitis, Rhinosinusitis, Acute

Clinical Setting

  • Rhinosinusitis, acute, empiric therapy. "Rhinosinusitis" preferred over "sinusitis" as both sites are inflamed.
  • Antibiotics rarely needed as etiology is most often viral:
    • Acute viral rhinosinusitis usually resolves within  7-10 days
      • Bacterial sinusitis occurs in only 0.5 to 2%
      • General ref.: N Engl J Med 2016; 375:962
  • Acute rhinosinusitis is usually a result of  obstruction of sinus ostia from inflammation caused by viral infection or allergens.
    • Treatment is oral fluids and perhaps nasal  sterile saline irrigations.
    • For  discussion of the role of antibiotics in patients with persistent upper respiratory tract symptoms, see Ann Intern Med 2017,166:201.
  •  Clinical features are, at best, suggestive:
    • Common features whether etiology is allergic, viral or bacterial:  
      • Facial pressure and frontal headaches
      • Anosmia, nasal congestion, and postnasal drip
    • More common in allergic sinusitis: chronic clear discharge, afebrile, sneezing, itchy eyes
    • More common in viral sinusitis: gradual improvement over 7-10 days. May be febrile.
    • More common features of a true bacterial sinusitis:
      • Fever and  symptom duration of more than 10 days
      • Maxillary toothache
      • Initial symptom improvement and then worsening of symptoms
      • Cacosmia (sense of bad odor in the nose)
      • Unilateral facial pain
  • Rarely able to make a  bacterial microbiologic diagnosis. Treatment regimens are empiric and focus on pathogens most often found in sinus puncture studies: S. pneumoniae, H.influenzae, Moraxella catarrhalis

Etiologies

  • Sinus puncture results in adult patients with clinical picture of acute bacterial rhinosinusitis:
    • Strep. pneumoniae (33%)
    • H. influenzae (32%)
    • M. catarrhalis (9%)
    • Streptococcus sp. (Group A) (2%)
    • Anaerobes (6%)
    • Staph. aureus (10%)
  • Viral (15%)

Primary Regimens

  • Most often viral infection and management focus is symptom relief (see Comments)
  • Guidelines indicate antibiotic therapy indicated in one of 3 situations:
      • If at initial evaluation: fever (39º∘C or higher in children), intense facial pain, and purulent nasal discharge, syndrome justifies empiric treatment as bacterial  rhino- sinusitis.
      • If despite withholding antibiotics, patient is still symptomatic after 7 days, treat as bacterial sinusitis.
      • If treated as bacterial sinusitis, patient fails therapy, see Sinusitis, Treatment Failure.
  • Pediatric:
    • Amoxicillin 90 mg/kg/day po divided q12h; max. dose is 2 gm per dose
    • Amoxicillin-clavulanate (extra strength) suspension, 90 mg/kg/day (Amox component), po divided q12h ; Max. dose of amoxicillin component is 2 gm per dose
    • Treat for 5-7 days
    • Reference, AAP Guidelines: Pediatrics 2013; 132:e262.
  • Adult:
    • Amoxicillin-clavulanate  875/125 mg po bid x 5-7 days.
      • Note: Higher dose of amoxicillin and extended release formulation increases amoxicillin AUC and thereby increases odds of efficacy vs common bacterial pathogens (Drugs 2005;65:121; Clin Lab Med 2004;24:503).
    • Amoxicillin (500 mg tid or 875 mg bid) po active vs most S. pneumoniae but less desirable due to frequent resistance of beta-lactamase producing H.influenzae and M. catarrhalis
  • For Guidelines,  symptomatic relief therapy, and references, see Comments section below.

Alternative Regimens

  • For symptom relief therapies, see Comments
  • Pediatric patient allergic to penicillin:
    • Age > 6 mo and wt < 50 kg: Levofloxacin 16 mg/kg/day divided q12h to max of 250 mg daily
    • Wt > 50 kg: Levofloxacin 500 mg po q24h
    • Cefuroxime axetil 30 mg/kg/day po divided q12h
      • Note: Cefuroxime susp not available in the US, may be available in other markets.
    • Cefdinir 14 mg/kg/day po divided q12-24h, max of 600 mg/day
    • Cefpodoxime 10 mg/kg/day po divided q12h
    • Type 1 allergy (IgE-mediated anaphylaxis, hives): Clindamycin 30-40 mg/kg/day po divided tid or qid x 10-14 days is the first choice with the understanding that Haemophilus sp. and Moraxella are resistant.
      • If Clindamycin use is not possible or not desired:
      • Non-type 1 allergy (skin rash): 2nd or 3rd generation cephalosporin:
    • Treat for 10-14 days
  • Adult patient with Penicillin allergy:
    • Type 1 allergy (e.g., anaphylaxis)
      • (Levofloxacin 750 mg po once daily or Moxifloxacin 400 mg po once daily ) x 5-7 days
      • Doxycycline 100 mg po bid x 5-7 days
    • Type 2 allergy (e.g., skin rash)
      • Cefdinir 600 mg/day divided q12h or q24h x 5-7 days
      • Cefpodoxime 200 mg po q12h x 5-7 days
      • Cefuroxime axetil 500 mg po q12h x 5-7 days

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Duration of antibiotic therapy: generally 5-7 days, but see specific regimens above.
  • Sinus symptoms are common with viral infections and allergies and are a common cause of overuse of antibiotics.
  • Randomized trial of amoxicillin clavulonate vs placebo in children meeting criteria for treatment showed only modest benefit of treatment: 2 point difference in 40 point symptom score and 2 days faster time to total symptom resolution (7 vs 9 days). Presence of colored nasal secretions did not predict response to antibiotics. JAMA.330(4):349,2023
  • Antibiotic treatment goals:
    • Waiting to initiate therapy is justified by the high rate of spontaneous resolution.
    • Meta-analysis of 9 double-blind trials found no clinical signs/symptoms that justify treatment–even after 7-10 days of symptoms, Lancet 371:908, 2008.
    • In randomized placebo-controlled trial in adults, a 10-day course of Amoxicillin compared to placebo did not reduce symptoms at day 3 of therapy, JAMA 307:685, 2012.
    • Prevent bacterial complications, e.g., subdural empyema, epidural abscess, brain abscess, meningitis and cavernous sinus thrombosis
    • Avoidance of chronic sinus disease
    • Avoidance of unnecessary antibiotic use.
    • See Ann Intern Med 2017,166:201 for discussion of role of antibiotics in patients with persistent upper respiratory tract symptoms.

Comments

  • Due to increasing resistance of S. pneumoniae, avoid Macrolides (azithromycin, clarithromycin, and erythromycin) and monotherapy with 2nd and 3rd generation cephalosporins.
  • Due to resistance of both S. pneumoniae and H. influenzae, avoid TMP-SMX.
  • Clindamycin alone is not suggested due to lack of activity against Haemophilus sp. and Moraxella sp.
  • American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) guidelines considers Amoxicillin (if no daycare attendance or antibiotics in last 30 days) or Amox-Clav for first-line therapy. IDSA guidelines suggest Amox-Clav as first-line for children and adults. AAP suggests that use of fluoroquinolones in children should continue to be limited to treatment of infections for which no safe and effective alternative exists.
  • Symptom relief (adjunct) therapies:
    • Topical decongestant (oxymetazoline) is not generally recommended. If used, use for < 3 days.
    • Nasal steroid: no clear evidence of benefit unless concomitant allergy.
    • Antihistamines: minor role unless definite underlying allergic rhinitis.
    • Saline irrigation: may help. Use only sterile saline
  • CT scans do not help as mucosa  reported as  inflamed in 87% of viral URIs; but only 2% develop bacterial rhinosinusitis.
  • Potential Complications:
    • From acute viral rhinosinusitis–transient hyposmia.
    • From acute bacterial rhinosinusitis–orbital infections, meningitis, epidural abscess, brain abscess.
  • For treatment failure: see Sinusitis, treatment failure.
  • Reference: N Engl J Med 2016; 375:962
