Stomatitis, Aphthous

Clinical Setting

  • Acute oral lesions. Idiopathic ulcerations. Commonly called canker sores.
  • Painful, occasionally large, ulcers in the mouth or oropharynx. Recurrent outbreaks occur in persons with celiac disease and inflammatory bowel disease.
  • Can be presenting manifestation of Bechet's syndrome.
  • Part of PFAPA (Periodic fever, aphthous stomatitis, pharyngitis, and cervical adenitis) syndrome: Clinical Rheumatology 2019; 38: 11 (UI: 30488366)

Etiologies

  • Idiopathic (no known cause)

Primary Regimens

  • Antimicrobial therapy is not indicated
  • Topical steroids (triamcinolone in orobase or in solution)
  • "Magic Mouthwash": a combination of Diphenhydramine, Glucocorticoids  (to reduce inflammation), viscous Lidocaine, and Maalox.

Alternative Regimens

  • Prednisone 60 mg po daily (or divided doses) with taper over 2 - 3 weeks

Comments

  • When using systemic steroids, watch for exacerbation of Herpes simplex (HSV) or oral candida infections.
  • See Dermatol Clin 2020; 38:441
