Mycobacterium fortuitum complex

Clinical Setting

  • Cutaneous, bone, pulmonary infections caused by non-tuberculous mycobacteria: Mycobacterium fortuitum.
  • Rapid grower.
  • Surgical excision of infected areas is an adjunct to treatment.
  • Disseminated disease associated with auto-antibodies to interferon-γ (Intern Med 53:1361, 2014)  

Etiologies

  • Mycobacterium fortuitum
  • Mycobacterium peregrinum and others

Primary Regimens

  • Pulmonary disease: use at least 2 active drugs (3 drugs for more severe disease), confirmed by in vitro susceptibility testing
    • (Amikacin 10 mg/kg IV once daily + Cefoxitin 8-12 gm/day IV in 2-3 divided doses + Levofloxacin 750 mg po qd) x 2–6 weeks, then (TMP-SMX 160/800 mg po bid + [Doxycycline 100 mg po bid or Levofloxacin]) x 6-12 months
    • Imipenem-cilastatin 1 gm IV q8-12h is an alternative to Cefoxitin
    • Moxifloxacin 400 mg po qd is an alternative to Levofloxacin

Alternative Regimens

  • Skin and soft tissue infection:
    • (Minocycline 100 mg po bid or Doxycycline 100 mg po bid) + (Ciprofloxacin 750 mg po bid or TMP-SMX 160/800 mg  po bid) x 4-6 months

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • M. fortuitum complex species are resistant to all standard anti-tuberculosis drugs.
  • Sensitive in vitro to Doxycycline, Minocycline, Cefoxitin, Imipenem, Amikacin, TMP-SMX, Ciprofloxacin, Levofloxacin, Linezolid, Tigecycline, Tedizolid (J Clin Microbiol 2017;55:1747).
  • Macrolide resistance, which may be inducible, in often present and differs among species within the complex, so avoid these agents unless inducible resistance (susceptible on incubation day 3, resistant on day 14) has been excluded.
