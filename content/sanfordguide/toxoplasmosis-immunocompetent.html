Toxoplasmosis, Immunocompetent

Clinical Setting

  • Primary toxoplasmosis in an immunocompetent host is usually asymptomatic. Manifestations include chorioretinitis (retinochoroiditis), lymphadenitis, congenital toxoplasmosis.
  • Humans are infected by:
    • Ingestion of food or water contaminated with oocysts from cat feces
    • Ingestion of cysts with bradyzoites in undercooked meat of infected animals including beef, lamb, game (venison).
    • From newly infected mother to fetus.
    • Blood transfusion or organ transplant from infected donor.
  • Presents as self-limited cervical or generalized lymphadenopathy with mild inflammatory response, e.g., fever, mononucleosis syndrome
  • Primary infection in utero can present clinically later in life (e.g. childhood or adolescence) as  retinochoroiditis (aka chorioretinitis) 
  • Associated with waterborne outbreaks, typically presenting as ocular disease (outbreaks in Vancouver and South America) but disseminated disease possible.
  • Genotypes from South America are more virulent, symptomatic disease more frequent in individuals from Brazil and South/Central America.
  • Associated with outbreaks linked to ingestion of game (venison); disease often more severe (high attack rate, disseminated disease).
  • The most common cause of infectious posterior uveitis in many parts of the world, particularly South America.

Etiologies

  • Toxoplasma gondii

Diagnosis

  • Diagnosis is usually made by antibody testing (IgM and IgG). See J Clin Micro 42:941, 2004.
  • PCR of blood, CSF, posterior chamber, or amniotic fluid may be used to make the diagnosis. Specific but not sensitive.
  • Numerous case reports of diagnosis made by metagenomics of plasma.
  • Suggest consultation with Dr. Jack S. Remington Laboratory for Specialty Diagnostics (remingtonlab@sutterhealth.org; previously called Palo Alto Medical Foundation Toxoplasma Serology Lab): (+1) 650-853-4828 which serves as the CDC reference lab.
  • T. gondii organisms persist in hosts, so positive PCR testing may represent latent parasites rather than active disease.

Primary Regimens

  • If immunocompetent and not pregnant: usually no treatment needed.  Treat severe systemic disease or prolonged fever as for chorioretinitis but without steroids. 
  • See summary of toxoplasmosis treatment (HIV and non HIV) in Guidelines for Prevention and Treatment of Opportunistic Infections in Adults and Adolescents with HIV 
  • Treatment of choice is pyrimethamine/sulfadiazine/folinic acid (leucovorin) but TMP/SMX is used increasingly frequently due to difficulties procuring pyrimethamine.
  • Active chorioretinitis:
    • [Pyrimethamine 200 mg po once on 1st day, then 50–75 mg q24h] + [Sulfadiazine 1–1.5 gm po qid] + [Leucovorin (folinic acid) 5–20 mg 3x/week]
    • Add Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day in 2 divided doses until CSF protein concentration falls or vision-threatening inflammation subsides. (often not administered unless threat of visual loss)
    • Adjust folinic acid (leucovorin) dose by following CBC results.
    • Treat 1–2 weeks beyond resolution of signs/symptoms; continue Leucovorin for 1 week after stopping Pyrimethamine.
    • For prevention of recurrent episodes of chorioretinitis, TMP-SMX DS q12 x 45 days reduced episodes over 1 year, but no effect noted in visual acuity. Am J Ophthalmol 157:762 2014. RCT showed that extending TMP-SMX (DS qod for 311 days after DS q12 x 45 days) to 1 year total decreased recurrences over 6 years of follow-up Am J Ophthalmol 213:195 2020
  • Acute illness in pregnancy (Clin Infect Dis 47:554, 2008; Clin Infect Dis 54:1545, 2012):
    • Spiramycin 1 gm po q8h until week 18. Contact U.S. FDA +1 301-796-1600 for IND paperwork after appropriately positive serology result at Dr. Jack S. Remington Laboratory for Specialty Diagnostics (remingtonlab@sutterhealth.org; previously called Palo Alto Medical Foundation Toxoplasma Serology Lab): (+1) 650-853-4828.  Drug then supplied free by Sanofi-Aventis.
    • (Pyrimethamine 50 mg po q12h x 2 days, then 50 mg/day + Sulfadiazine 75 mg/kg po x 1 dose, then 50 mg/kg q12h (max 4 gm/day) + Folinic acid 10-20 mg po daily) for minimum of 4 weeks or duration of pregnancy if ultrasound evidence of fetal distress (Clin Infect Dis 54:1545, 2012).
    • If  diagnosed <14 weeks of gestation: give Spiramycin until delivery (not all acute infection in pregnancy will be transmitted to the fetus), perform amniocentesis for PCR testing and fetal USG every 4 weeks  
    • If >14 weeks gestation at diagnosis, and documented fetal infection by positive amniotic fluid PCR or imaging: Treat with Pyrimethamine/Sulfa as spiramycin does not cross the placenta.
  • Fetal, congenital:
    • (Pyrimethamine 1mg/kg po q12h x 2 days, then 1mg/kg/day for 2 or 6 mo followed by 1mg/kg/day M/W/F + Sulfadiazine 50 mg/kg q12h (max 4 gm/day) + Folinic acid 10 mg po 3 times weekly) for total 1 year.
    • Treatment regimens vary depending on locale. See Lancet 363:1965, 2004, Clin Infect Dis. 42:1383-94, 2006. Expert consultation advisable.
    • Chicago National Collaborative Congenital Toxoplasmosis Treatment Trial Study +1 312-513-6365 or rmcleod@bsd.uchicago.edu can assist with clinical diagnosis or management in children (or advice on how to procure drugs)

Alternative Regimens

  • If unable to procure pyrimethamine/sulfadiazine, administer TMP-SMX 10 mg/kg/day (IV or PO) based on trimethoprim component divided in 2-3 doses.
  • Add one of the following to pyrimethamine/sulfadiazine for refractory cases.  May substitute one of the following in cases of sulfa allergy.
    • Clindamycin 300 mg po q6h
    • Atovaquone 1500 mg po q12h
    • Azithromycin 900-1200 mg po once daily

Comments

  • General references, Lancet 363:1965, 2004; Clin Infect Dis 44:e62, 2007.
  • Due to the expense and limited availability of pyrimethamine, TMP-SMX DS q12  is emerging as treatment of choice by many clinicians for toxoplasmosis. 
  • Screening practices for toxoplasmosis in pregnant women and in newborns varies by geography, with universal testing in France and other parts of Europe  but not in US PLoS Negl Trop Dis 11:e0005222 2017; more frequent transmission from naive newly infected mother to fetus late in pregnancy, but  consequences for  child more severe early in pregnancy. 
  • Although the drugs used to treat are the same, generally lower doses are used for treatment of immunocompetent individuals, and duration of treatment is guided by clinical response.
  • Occasionally, serious disease in immunocompetent patients: e.g., pneumonia (Clin Infect Dis 44: e62, 2007), severe polymyositis (Infection 41:859, 2013), and Hemophagocytic syndrome.
  • Strains from South America are more virulent and can present with sepsis picture,  pneumonia, disseminated disease, or acute retinochoroiditis so travel history important.
  • Serious disease and outbreaks reported after ingestion of game (venison) Clin Infect Dis 72:1557, 2021 ;
    N Engl J Med 390:1612, 2024
  • Pregnancy: Screen patients with IgG/IgM serology at commercial lab. IgG+/IgM neg = remote past infection; IgG+/IgM+ = possible seroconversion--needs additional evaluation as IgM does not reliably indicate new infection.
