Meningitis, Aseptic

Clinical Setting

  • Aseptic, non-bacterial meningitis, enterovirus. (StatPearls, 2022, Review)
  • Fever, headache, meningismus, nausea, vomiting, photophobia. Focal neurologic signs and altered mental status typically are not present. Short duration of symptoms, less than a week, in most cases.
  • CSF with normal glucose, normal to mildly elevated protein, lymphocytic pleocytosis - usually with a few hundred cells. 
    • Up to 1/3rd of adults with enterovirus meningitis have neutrophilic pleocytosis in CSF. Review: (J Clin Virology, 104: 56, 2018)
  • Clinical Prediction Rule for distinguishing bacterial from 'aseptic' meningitis (Pediatrics 146:e20201126, 2020)
    • Bacterial meningitis associated with:  procalcitonin >1.2 ng/mL, cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) protein >80 mg/dL, CSF absolute neutrophil count >1000 cells per mm3, and C-reactive protein >40 mg/L.
    • If none of these conditions are met, SCORE =0, highly unlikely to be bacterial meningitis. 

Etiologies

  • Presumed viral, without etiologic diagnosis in majority of cases
  • Enteroviruses (most common): e.g., Coxsackie and ECHO viruses
  • Primary HIV
  • West Nile virus
  • HSV-2 (also Mollaret's recurrent aseptic meningitis)
  • Lymphocytic choriomeningitis (LCM) virus
  • Secondary syphilis
  • Lyme disease
  • Drug-induced (JAMA Int Med 174:1511,2014)
    • NSAIDs
    • Antibiotics: especially TMP-SMX, Ampicillin / Amoxicillin
    • Immunosuppressive/immunomodulatory: e.g., detuximab, infliximab, sulfasalazine
    • Antiepileptic drugs: e.g., lamotrigine, carbamazepine
  • Leptospirosis (rare)
  • Non-infectious causes include connective tissue disease (e.g. SLE), neoplasia

Primary Regimens

  • Patients usually admitted due to dehydration from nausea and vomiting and/or need for narcotic analgesia.
  • For the viral etiologies, antibiotic treatment is either not available or generally not required.
    • Acyclovir 5-10 mg/kg q8h IV is sometimes given for HSV-2 meningitis; see Mollaret's recurrent meningitis. (Note: this entity is not the same as HSV encephalitis where early treatment is mandatory).
    • Meningitis of acute HIV infection resolves spontaneously, but should prompt consideration of whether to start antiretroviral therapy (ART).
  • Discontinue drugs that may be etiologic.
  • For Leptospirosis: (Doxycycline 100 mg IV/po q12h) or (Penicillin G 5 million units IV q6h) or (Ampicillin 0.5–1 gm IV q6h).

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • For neurologic manifestations: Lyme disease and Syphilis.
  • Repeat LP if suspect partially-treated bacterial meningitis.
  • Enterovirus:
    • If available, PCR of CSF for enterovirus. Rapid CSF PCR test is accurate; reduces costs and hospital stay for infants (Peds 120:489, 2007).
    • Pleconaril (VP 63843) still under investigation. No clinical benefit demonstrated in double-blind placebo-controlled study in 21 infants with enteroviral aseptic meningitis (Ped Infect Dis J 22:335, 2003).  Failed to ameliorate symptoms but did have some improvement among those with severe headache (Antimicrob Agts Chemother 50:2409, 2006).
  • Reference on viral meningitis: Clin Infect Dis 47:783, 2008.
  • Improved diagnostics (reviewed Curr Opin Infect Dis 30:248, 2017)
  • HSV-2: concurrent or history of prior genital lesions often absent (see J Neurovirol 19:166, 2013).
  • For leptospirosis, look for positive epidemiologic history (travel, exposure to potentially contaminated water) and concomitant hepatitis, conjunctivitis, dermatitis, or nephritis.
