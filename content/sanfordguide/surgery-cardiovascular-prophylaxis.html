Surgical Prophylaxis, Cardiovascular

Clinical Setting

  • Antibiotic prophylaxis in cardiovascular surgery has been proven beneficial in the following procedures:
    • Reconstruction of abdominal aorta
    • Procedures on the leg that involve a groin incision
    • Any vascular procedure that inserts prosthesis/foreign body
    • Lower extremity amputation for ischemia
    • Cardiac surgery
    • Permanent Pacemakers (Circulation 121:458, 2010)
    • Heart Transplantation
  • See also Prevention of Surgical Infections, Overview.
  • 2013 Clinical practice guidelines: Am J Health Syst Pharm 70:195, 2013.

Etiologies

  • Staph. aureus, coagulase-negative staphylococci, and other skin flora

Primary Regimens

  • Cefazolin 2 gm IV as a single dose (3 gm IV if weight ≥120 kg); repeat after 4 hours
  • Cefuroxime 1.5 gm IV as a single dose; repeat after 4 hours
  • Vancomycin 15 mg/kg IV as single dose (max 2 gm IV)
  • Use intranasal Mupirocin evening before, day of surgery and bid x 5 days post-op in patients colonized with Staph. aureus.

Alternative Regimens

  • For beta-lactam allergic patients who cannot tolerate Vancomycin: Clindamycin 900 mg IV as a single dose; repeat after 6 hours

Comments

  • Prophylaxis not needed for cardiac catheterization.
  • Begin prophylaxis within 60 min before incision (120 min for vancomycin or fluoroquinolone).
  • For prolonged surgeries, re-dose cefazolin or cefuroxime at 4 hours from start of initial pre-op dose as above (see Am J Health Syst Pharm 70:195, 2013).
  • ASHP guidelines employ weight-based vancomycin dosing (15 mg/kg IV single dose). If using this regimen, no single dose should exceed 2 gm.
  • For cardiac procedures, some continue prophylactic antibiotics for up to 24 hours; if doing so, seek appropriate guidance for dosing based on renal function and other factors.
  • Vancomycin may be preferable in hospitals with high frequency of MRSA or in high-risk patients; it should be used in patients colonized with MRSA. However, vancomycin does not cover gram-negative bacilli; therefore, when there is risk of gram-negative infection would add cefazolin (if not allergic) or an alternative agent (e.g., single-dose of a fluoroquinolone, Aztreonam or an aminoglycoside) to vancomycin.
  • Intranasal mupirocin decreased sternal wound infections from Staph. aureus in 1850 patients; used historical controls (Ann Thorac Surg 71:1572, 2001). In another trial, it decreased nosocomial Staph. aureus infections only in nasal carriers (N Engl J Med 346:1871, 2002). One study of 0.12% chlorhexidine gluconate gel to nares and chlorhexidine oral rinse showed decrease in deep surgical site and lower respiratory tract infections (JAMA 296:2460, 2006).
  • Mupirocin resistance has been encountered, see Clin Infect Dis 49:935, 2009.
  • Some use mupirocin intranasally twice daily starting up to 5 days before surgery in Staph. aureus-colonized individuals, see Am J Health Syst Pharm 70:195, 2013; Clin Infect Dis 2021; 73:1685.
