Surgical Prophylaxis, Orthopedic

Clinical Setting

  • 2013 Clinical Practice Guidelines: Am J Health-Syst Pharm 70:195, 2013.
  • Antimicrobial prophylaxis for orthopedic surgery procedures: hip and other joint replacement, fractures.
  • For prophylaxis to protect prosthetic joints, see Comments.
  • See also, Prevention of Surgical Infections, Overview.

Etiologies

  • Skin flora: S. epidermidis, S. aureus
  • Less often: aerobic gram-negative bacilli

Primary Regimens

  • Repair of hip fracture, spine surgery, and joint replacements:
    • Cefazolin 2 gm IV as a single dose (3 gm IV if weight ≥ 120 kg)
    • ADD Vancomycin 1 gm IV as single dose (use 1.5 gm IV if >90 kg), if patient is colonized with MRSA
    • If additional doses are preferred, use appropriate therapeutic dosing for no more than 24h
  • Open reduction of closed fracture with internal fixation
    • Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV as a single dose

Alternative Regimens

  • If allergic to cefazolin:
    • Vancomycin 1 gm IV as single dose (use 1.5 gm IV if >90 kg)
    • Clindamycin 900 mg IV as single dose
    • If additional doses are preferred, use appropriate therapeutic dosing for no more than 24h
    • NOTE: When using either of these agents because of intolerance to cefazolin and other cephalosporins, in institutions where gram-negative infections following the orthopedic procedure are a concern, ADD:
      • Aztreonam 2 gm IV as a single dose
      • (Gentamicin or Tobramycin) 5 mg/kg (dosing weight) IV as a single dose
      • Ciprofloxacin 400 mg IV as a single dose

Comments

  • For patients determined to be colonized with S. aureus, use preoperative mupirocin for decolonization.
  • Reduction of closed fracture: 3.6% (ceftriaxone) vs 8.3% (for placebo) infection rate found in Dutch trauma trial (Lancet 347:1133, 1996). Several alternative antimicrobials can reduce risk of infection (Cochrane Database Syst Rev 2001 and update in 2010: CD 000244).
  • Note regarding vancomycin. 2013 Guidelines suggest combination of cefazolin and vancomycin in institutions with high rates of MRSA infection. However, based on more recent studies, this is no longer recommended based on lack of efficacy and increased toxicity. See N Engl J Med 2023, 389:1488 and Clin Orthop Relat Res. 2017 Jul;475:1767. See also BMC Infect Dis 2018, 18:178. 
  • Prosthetic joint, bacteremia risk from unrelated procedures.
    • Prophylaxis to protect prosthetic joints from hematogenous infection related to distant procedures (patients with plates, pins and screws not considered to be at risk).
    • A prospective, case-control study concluded that antibiotic prophylaxis for dental procedures did not decrease the risk of hip/knee prosthesis infection (Clin Infect Dis 50:8, 2010).
      • An expert panel of the American Dental Association concluded that, in general, prophylactic antibiotics are not recommended prior to dental procedures to prevent prosthetic joint infection (J Amer Dental Assoc 146: 11, 2015).
      • Individual circumstances should be considered; when there is planned manipulation of tissues thought to be actively infected, antimicrobial therapy for the infection is likely to be appropriate.
    • Prophylaxis with an anti-staphylococcal β-lactam or Vancomycin (according to susceptibility of the organism) for procedures involving tissues infected with or colonized by staphylococci would be appropriate, as these organisms are common causes of prosthetic joint infections. In other circumstances, decisions must be based on individual judgment.
