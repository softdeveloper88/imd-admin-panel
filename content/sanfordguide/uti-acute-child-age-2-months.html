UTI, Acute, Child, Age < 2 months

Clinical Setting

  • Infants presenting with fever. UTI present in 7-15%. Rates highest in uncircumcised males > females > circumcised males.
  • Diagnosis requires urine culture
    • >50,000 cfu/mL of uropathogen from catheterized or suprapubic specimen considered positive. Abnormal UA findings supportive especially for colony counts 10,000-50,000/mL. UA may be normal in 6-10%. Pediatrics.2018 Feb;141(2).Epub 2018 Jan 16.
    • Cultures of bagged urine specimens are unreliable and should not be performed.
  • Concurrent bacteremia in 4-7%
  • Blood cultures should be performed in infants with UTI.  CSF exam and cultures are recommended if <30 days old, ill appearing or febrile

Etiologies

  • E. coli
  • Klebsiella sp.
  • Enterobacter sp.
  • Enterococcus sp.
  • Group B strep
  • Proteus sp.
  • Citrobacter sp.
  • Staph sp.

Primary Regimens

  • Empiric therapy is the same as for other febrile infants.
  • Begin with IV therapy:
    • Ampicillin and Gentamicin traditionally used alone but based on high rates of ampicillin resistance in E. coli, adding a third generation cephalosporin is recommended. If third generation cephalosporin is used gentamicin may not be necessary
    • Ceftriaxone 75-100 mg/kg/day once daily
    • For premature infants or term infants with hyperbilirubinemia, Cefotaxime 150-200 mg/kg/day div q8h (where available outside) or Ceftazidime 100 mg/kg/day div q8h are preferred to Ceftriaxone because of the theoretical risk of increased bilirubin.

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Patients with positive cultures but without symptoms or pyuria do not require therapy
  • Adjust therapy based on culture results.
  • No clear data on optimal duration and route of therapy.
  • Seven days supported in older children but no data if this applies to infants. Traditional duration 10-14 days.
  • IV therapy usually continued until CSF infection and bacteremia are ruled out and patient is improving. Many clinicians switch to high dose oral therapy after sustained improvement.avoiding need for central line.even with transient bacteremia if CSF is normal

Comments

  • Obtain renal ultrasound on all infants after therapy is initiated.
  • Ceftriaxone is contraindicated in neonates if they require (or are expected to require) treatment with calcium-containing IV solutions, including continuous calcium-containing infusions such as total parenteral nutrition
  • Voiding cystourethrogram (VCUG)  reserved for children with ultrasound suggestive of reflux, obstruction or significant structural abnormality.
  • References: Pediatr Infect Dis J 33:342, 2014, Pediatrics 116:644, 2005.
