Bronchitis, Acute, Age > 5 years

Clinical Setting

  • Acute bronchitis in adolescents and adults is defined as self-limited inflammation of the upper airways due to infection or irritants. It should be differentiated from small airway disease by the presence of airway obstruction.
  • Pertussis should be considered in all patients with prolonged cough.
  • Purulent sputum alone is not an indication for antibiotic therapy.
  • Patients should be counseled that cough may persist for 2-3 weeks.
  • If fever/rigors, get chest x-ray. See also, Mycoplasma pneumonia.

Etiologies

  • Viral (usually)
  • M. pneumoniae (5%)
  • C. pneumoniae (5%)
  • Bordetella pertussis

Primary Regimens

  • Antibiotics are minimally effective and are not indicated 
  • Pertussis is the exception
  • Antitussive (limited evidence) ± inhaled bronchodilators
  • Inhaled corticosteroids are sometimes used but efficacy has not been systematically evaluated

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Antibiotics are not useful for acute bronchitis in patients without COPD.
  • Antibiotic use for acute bronchitis is one of the most common inappropriate uses of antibiotics. Reducing antibiotics in acute bronchitis is an excellent target for stewardship and quality improvement activities.

Comments

  • References, N Engl J Med 355:2125, 2006 and JAMA 312:2678, 2014.
