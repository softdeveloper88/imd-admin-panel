Immunization, Routine, HIV+ Adults

Introduction

  • General principles and guidelines regarding immunization of HIV+ adults.
  • In HIV+ adults, significant (2-36 fold) transient (≤6 weeks) ↑ in plasma viral RNA after pneumococcal, influenza & tetanus immunization (N Engl J Med 334:1222, 1996). A similar phenomenon occurs with acute infections, i.e., influenza. There are no data suggesting that this is clinically relevant (Ann Intern Med 131:430, 1999; Clin Infect Dis 28:548, 1999). Concurrently, antibody responses are ↓ in HIV+ individuals. Recommendations are presented in the following table.
  • Administration of live attenuated vaccines is contraindicated in individuals with HIV infection (MMR and VZ are exceptions).
  • COVID-19 (SARS-CoV-2) vaccines are recommended for HIV+ individuals. 
  • Recommendations for a supplemental shot SARS-CoV-2 vaccine for people with HIV vary according to their age, health status and the COVID-19 vaccine that they received and are evolving as more data become available. 
  • Immunization (when indicated) with killed whole cell vaccines &/or purified antigens should be done as soon as reasonable after HIV infection diagnosed, before CD4 cells ↓ further (J Infect Dis 171:1217, 1995).
  • Extended primary series &/or more frequent boosters often indicated.
  • When specifically indicated, immune globulin (IG) & specific immune globulins can be administered.
  • For recent review see Am J Med. 132:437 2019.

CDC Footnotes to Recommended Immunizations

  • Td
    • "Injection" drug users (IDU) are at ↑ risk of tetanus.
  • HibCV
    • Is of unproven benefit & immune responses ↓; although the risk of H. influenzae type b disease is ↑ & adverse reactions are minimal, it is no longer recommended. Discuss with primary provider if dysfunctional spleen or other potential additional risks.
  • Pneumococcal
    • Risk of bacteremia is ↑ [as high as 9.4/1000/yr (J Inf Dis 162:1012, 1990)]. 
    • PCV13 followed by PPSV23 at least 8 weeks later with final boost with PPSV23 at least 5 years later. See CDC flow sheet for timing of different regimens in different cases
  • Hepatitis A
    • Hep A has ↑ in frequency in MSM and IDU
    • 2 hep A vaccines, inactivated, are licensed in U.S.
    • Combination vaccine with Hep B also available
  • Hepatitis B
    • Series of 3 IM injections in deltoid, using 12-inch needle (not into the buttocks), should be given. 
    • Combination vaccine with Hep A also available
  • Human Papillomavirus: See CDC STD guidelines  MMWR 70(RR-4):1, 2021 
  • Influenza
    • Annual immunization with the current vaccine is recommended regardless of age.
    • Poor immune response in patients with CD4 count <200. Some recommend use of amantadine, rimantadine or neuraminidase inhibitors instead of immunization in this group (Clin Infect Dis 28:548, 1999).
    • Avoid live attenuated vaccine.
  • eIPV (enhanced-potency inactivated polio vaccine)
    • In adults ≥18 yrs, use only if specifically indicated: travel to developing countries (not Central or South America), prior to (~8 weeks, time for initial 2 doses) household exposure to individuals given oral polio vaccine.
  • OPV (oral polio vaccine)
    • Contraindicated, live vaccine
  • Specific immunoglobulins: Hepatitis B (HBIG), human rabies (HRIG), tetanus (TIG), vaccinia (VIG), varicella zoster (VZIG)
    • Can be used for same indications, same dosage as in non-HIV infected individuals
  • MMR
    • Withhold MMR or other measles-containing vaccines from HIV-infected persons with severe immunosuppression (CD4 <200).
  • Meningococcal
    • 2016 change in recommendations MMWR 65:1189, 2016 : All adults with HIV infection who have not been previously vaccinated should receive a 2-dose primary MenACWY vaccination series, with doses administered at least 2 months apart, and be revaccinated every 5 years. Those who previously received 1 dose of MenACWY should receive a second dose at least 2 months after the first dose.
    • MenB is not routinely recommended for adults with HIV infection, because meningococcal disease in this population is caused primarily by serogroups C, W, and Y. May be indicated if splenic dysfunction.
  • Varicella zoster
    • Give 2 doses 3 mo apart if no evidence of immunity and no severe immunocompromise. Withhold from HIV-infected persons with severe immunosuppression (CD4 <200). 
  • Varicella zoster (Shingles) 
    • If 50 or older and have no symptoms of HIV, get the 2-dose series of the Shingrix brand of shingles vaccine, even if you were already vaccinated with Zostavax.
  • Vaccinia (smallpox)
    • Contraindicated: live virus vaccine
  • Rotavirus
    • Contraindicated: live virus vaccine
