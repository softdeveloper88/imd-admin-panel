Severe Immunocompromise and HIV: Travel Considerations

Introduction

  • HIV and travel considerations.
  • Immunocompromised / immunosupressed travelers are at increased risk for both travel-associated and other infections and diseases.
  • Travel may exacerbate the specific person’s underlying condition.
  • Increased risk of adverse reactions to or contraindications to important pre-travel vaccines
    and travel medications.
  • May need to work with the traveler’s specialty care professionals to:
    • Be certain the underlying medical condition is stable.
    • Discuss general fitness for the stress of travel.
    • Verify medications, doses and vaccination history.

General Considerations

  • Risk assessment is traveler specific.
  • Traveler’s current state of immunosuppression:
    • Follow CDC guidance regarding the degree of immunocompromise due to specific medications, immunotherapy, underlying conditions and post-transplant.
    • See CDC Yellowbook - additional considerations for immunocompromised travelers.
  • Duration of trip
  • Day-to day risk at locations within each country.
  • Anticipated risk exposures due to planned activities.
  • Accommodation and dining risk scenarios.
  • Lead time needed to complete extra predeparture interventions and tasks.

Traveler Education

  • Pre-travel: emphasize fastidious adherence to common precautions and health maintenance behaviors compared to traveling companions:
    • Hand hygiene with effective sanitizers
    • Mask use
    • Avoidance of excess sun exposure, especially if taking photosensitizing medications
    • Safe food and water precautions
    • Prevention of arthropod bites
    • Malaria prophylaxis (if relevant)
    • Safer sex, barrier protection, and contraception
    • Scheduling of home maintenance medications with preparation for time zone changes
  • Post-travel: stress importance of reporting to home medical providers any new symptoms and travel history upon returning from travel.

Vaccines

  • Make every effort to ensure that all routine and travel-indicated vaccination series are complete.
  • Check CDC country-specific recommendations.
  • See Vaccines, Overview and to link to  specific immunocompromised / HIV sections on relevant vaccine pages.
  • Discuss with the traveler the wisdom of a planned itinerary in context of forgoing live replicating vaccines, e.g., yellow fever, since all live vaccines are contraindicated in immunocompromise.
    • Note: An ICVP (yellow card) exemption certificate from an authorized Yellow Fever Vaccine Centre may be needed in order to pass through certain ports of entry.

Medications

  • No changes to maintenance medications <2 wks prior to trip.
  • Medications should be carried only in hand-baggage.
  • Malaria prophylaxis (see below) even for very low risk itineraries.
  • Antibiotics and loperamide for travelers’ diarrhea for standby self-treatment.
  • COVID-19 self-treatment:
    • Standby self-treatment strategies for Paxlovid; COVID-19 testing kits should be provided and a supply of Paxlovid should be provided while traveling.  
      • Paxlovid should not be prescribed if the patient will not have access to drug levels for maintenance medications that have strong drug-drug interactions (especially tacrolimus and cyclosporin).
  • Altitude sickness prophylaxis and treatment (acetazolamide) if relevant.
  • Extra quantities of home medications in case of travel delays, damage, or loss.
    • >30 day supply of expensive medications often difficult to obtain
  • Avoid new medications at destination without consulting with home provider.
  • Be aware of potential for poor quality or counterfeit local medication depending on travel destination.

HIV: Malaria prophylaxis

    • Malaria prophylaxis for persons on ART. The severity of malaria is increased in HIV-infected people; malaria infection increases HIV viral load and thus may exacerbate disease progression.
    • Clinically relevant concerns with prophylactic drugs have yet to be shown.
    • Potential interactions at www.hiv-druginteractions.org.
    • Commonly used INSTI/NRTI combinations (e.g., Descovy-Tivicay, Truvada-Tivicay) have no known interactions with chemoprophylactic drugs, although co-formulations with cobicistat-boosted elvitegravir (Stribild, Genvoya) may theoretically increase mefloquine levels.
    • Cabotegravir (oral and depot) has no potential interactions with anti-malarial.
    • The rilpivirine, emtricitabine, TAF/TDF combinations (Odefsey and Complera) similarly have no interactions with antimalarials.
    • Chloroquine, mefloquine, and primaquine may interact with older maintenance regimens for HIV. 
      • Efavirenz lowers serum levels of both atovaquone and proguanil and protease inhibitors have potential interactions.

Respiratory Illness

  • Update COVID-19 vaccine in close proximity to trip.
  • Update monoclonal antibody prophylaxis in close proximity to trip if effective for current variants.
  • Carry adequate supply of well-fitting but comfortable N95 masks for whole trip with priority for use in airports, on airplanes, on ground transportation, and in crowded environments.
  • Review history, course and outcome of previous COVID-19 episodes.
  • Carry multiple non-expired SARS-CoV-2 and influenza rapid antigen tests.
  • Create plans for symptomatic COVID-19 that include early testing, treatment, managing anticipated drug–drug interactions of therapy, access to medical facilities.
  • Create plans for safe quarantine or isolation.

Medical Care Abroad

  • Pre-travel planning:
    • Have online access to copies of insurance and medical contact information, vaccinations, and medical history; an email sent to self is sufficient.
    • Ensure adequate medical insurance as well as medical evacuation coverage along itinerary.
      • Explore pre-existing conditions exclusions and age limitations.
    • Identify locations of appropriately sophisticated destination medical facilities in the event of illness.
    • Procedures for contacting traveler's Embassy if needed.
