Burns, Non-Infected

Clinical Setting

  • Initial care and management of non-infected burn wounds.
  • For overall approach to burn wound management, including step-by-step explanation, see ISBI Guidelines: Burns 2016;42:953 and New Engl J Med. 2019;380:2349
  • Goal is to prevent infection. For review of topical agents see: Clin Plastic Surg 36:597, 2009.
  • Don't forget tetanus prophylaxis.
  • If possible, transport patient to specialized burn unit.

Etiologies

  • Not infected

Primary Regimens

  • Early surgical debridement of necrotic skin often with subsequent application of topical cream and/or a skin graft and/or use of a skin substitute (Adv Skin Wound Care 18:323, 2005).
  • Perhaps shower hydrotherapy (J Burn Care Res 3:393, 2010).
  • Topical antimicrobics used in attempt to reduce incident of life threatening burn wound infection. Topical therapy options are listed under alternative regimens (N Engl J Med 359:1037, 2008; Clin Plastic Surg 36: 597, 2009).
  • Wide spectrum of potential pathogens: e.g., gram-positive cocci, gram-negative bacilli, and fungi.

Alternative Regimens

  • Silver Sulfadiazine cream, 1%, apply 1–2 x/day is most popular.
    • Bactericidal vs. gram-positive bacteria (S. aureus), Gram-negative bacteria including Pseudomonas aeruginosa, some yeast (C. albicans) and viruses.
    • Mixture of silver nitrate and sodium sulfadiazine
    • Minimal pain when applied.
    • Minor adverse effects:
      • Sulfonamide allergy
      • Some believe drug impairs re-epithelialization of the burn wound; silver is toxic to keratinocytes and fibroblasts.
      • Transient leukopenia; probably due to margination of WBCs in the wound rather than bone marrow suppression. Resolves spontaneously.
      • Not facial burns for fear of eye irritation or injury.
  • Mafenide acetate (Sulfamylon) cream, 11.1%.
    • Methylated sulfonamide that blocks folic acid metabolism
    • Active vs. gram-positive and Gram-negative; no activity vs yeast.
    • Penetrates burn eschars but painful to apply
    • Can lead to sulfonamide allergy
  • Silver nitrate 0.5% solution. Messy. Turns skin black.
    • Activity vs Gram-negative bacteria less broad than silver-sulfadiazine cream.
    • Hyponatremia and hypochloremia can occur.
    • Rarely, Methemoglobinemia can occur.

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Concern over emerging resistance of multi-drug resistant aerobic gram-negative bacilli to many topical antibacterial preparations (J Burn Care Res 30:19, 2009).

Comments

  • Interesting observation: In severely burned patients requiring mechanical ventilation mortality was reduced in patients who received up to a week of prophylactic Cefazolin or Ampicillin-sulbactam. Needs confirmation: Clin Infect Dis 62: 60, 2016
  • See also, infected burn wounds.
