Posaconazole
Tradename: Noxafil

Usage and Dosing

  • Posaconazole is a triazole with activity against a wide range of fungi refractory to other antifungal treatment including: aspergillosis, Mucorales, fusariosis, Scedosporium (Pseudallescheria), phaeohyphomycosis, histoplasmosis, refractory candidiasis, refractory coccidioidomycosis, refractory cryptococcosis, and refractory chromoblastomycosis.
  • Indications:
    • Prophylaxis of invasive Aspergillus and Candida, formulations and age groups as follows:
      • Injection: adults and pediatric patients ≥2 years of age.
      • Delayed-release tablets (100 mg): adults and pediatric patients ≥2 years of age (weight ≥40 kg).
      • Oral suspension (40 mg/mL): adults and pediatric patients ≥13 years of age.
      • Delayed-release oral suspension (30 mg/mL): pediatric patients ≥2 years of age (weight ≤40 kg).
    • Treatment of oropharyngeal candidiasis (OPC), including OPC refractory to fluconazole and/or itraconazole, in adults and pediatric patients ≥13 years of age.
  • Oral formulations are not interchangeable. Check usage and dose recommendations carefully. Administer all oral formulations with a full high-fat meal for optimum absorption.
  • Delayed-release tablets should be swallowed whole. Do not divide, crush, or chew. Provide higher plasma exposures than oral suspension under both fed and fasting conditions, so is the preferred oral formulation for prophylaxis.
  • In patients who cannot eat a full meal, administration of the oral suspension with an acidic carbonated beverage improves absorption.
  • Posaconazole is released faster from delayed-release oral suspension in the presence of alcohol. Avoid co-administration.
  • Delayed-release oral suspension contraindicated in patients with hereditary fructose intolerance. See Adverse Effects.
  • Injection should be diluted in D5W or NS to a final concentration of 1-2 mg/mL. Infuse over 90 minutes through a 0.22 micron filter via a central line or PICC.
  • Posaconazole takes 7-10 days to achieve a steady state.
  • Use with caution in patients at risk for QTc prolongation.
  • Monitor trough concentrations, prophylaxis goal to achieve >0.7 μg/mL and treatment >1.0 μg/mL (J Antimicrob Chemother 69:1162, 2014).
  • Caution: numerous drug interactions (see chart below, not all-inclusive).

      Other Adjustment

      • Intravenous posaconazole in obesity: In a PK study, total body weight best predicted changes in drug clearance and Vd. For sufficient exposure, modeling suggests increasing the dose used for treatment to 400 mg IV q24h in patients weighing >140 kg. For prophylaxis, 300 mg IV q24h provides sufficient exposure in patients up to 190 kg (J Antimicrob Chemother 2020;75:1006).
      • Oral posaconazole in obesity: no data.
      • Use during ECMO:
        • No dose adjustment may be necessary.
        • For more information, see ECMO Drug Dosing Adjustment.

      Adverse Effects

      • Posaconazole has similar toxicities as other triazoles: nausea 9%, vomiting 6%, abdominal pain 5%, headache 5%, diarrhea, increased ALT, AST, and rash (3% each).
      • In patients treated for >6 months, serious side effects have included adrenal insufficiency, nephrotoxicity, and QTc interval prolongation.
      • Acquired apparent mineralocorticoid excess (e.g., hypokalemia, hypertension). Dose-dependent. Observed also with itraconazole but not fluconazole, voriconazole, or isavuconazole. Mechanism: inhibition of 11β-hydroxylase and 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 2 (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 2022;66(1):e0166821).
      • Iatrogenic Cushing's syndrome from co-administration with inhaled fluticasone: see Major Interactions chart below.
      • Delayed-release oral suspension is contraindicated in patients with hereditary fructose intolerance. The formulation contains sorbitol and may therefore precipitate a metabolic crisis that includes life-threatening hypoglycemia, hypophosphatemia, lactic acidosis, and hepatic failure.
      • Possible phototoxicity: case report.
