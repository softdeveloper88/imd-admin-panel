Malaria, Resistant, Prophylaxis

Clinical Setting

  • Prophylaxis for travel to areas with Chloroquine-resistant malaria (mostlyPlasmodium falciparum).
  • Review: N Engl J Med. 375:247, 2016.
  • CDC Country-by-Country Risk
  • WHO Consolidated Guidelines for intermittent prophylaxis of pregnant women and children in residents of endemic areas.
    • See Fansidar (Pyrimethamine-sulfadoxine) for details on use in prophylaxis. 
  • Prophylaxis includes anti-malaria drugs + personal protection: screens, nets, 30–35% DEET skin repellent (avoid >50% DEET), permethrin spray on clothing and mosquito nets.
  • Recommended options for HIV patients on ART, see Comments.
  • Tafenoquine FDA approved in 2018. Arakoda (U.S) and Kodatef (Australia) are commercially available although US distribution is variable. 
  • Two malaria vaccines, RTS,S/AS01 (Mosquirix, GSK) and R21/Matrix M are recommended for 5-17 month olds by WHO in areas with moderate to high malaria transmission.
    • RTS,S/AS01 (Mosquirix, GSK) VE of ~40% over 4 yrs in infants but complex 4-dose schedule that must be given before age 18 mos. 
      • In short supply due to proprietary adjuvant.
    • R21/Matrix M VE of ~75% over 3 yrs in infants but complex 4-dose schedule can be produced in large quantities

Etiologies

  • Plasmodium falciparum (outside Central America and Caribbean)
  • Plasmodium vivax (in Papua, New Guinea and Indonesia only)

Primary Regimens

  • Adult: Atovaquone-proguanil 250/100 mg (Malarone) combination tablet, 1/day with food 1–2 days prior to, during and for 7 days post-travel in endemic area
  • Pediatrics: Atovaquone-proguanil by weight in kg, single daily dose using pediatric tab (62.5 mg atovaquone/25 mg proguanil):
    • 5-8 kg: 1/2 tab
    • 9-10 kg: 3/4 tab
    • 11–20 kg: 1 tab
    • 21–30 kg: 2 tabs
    • 31-40 kg: 3 tabs
    • ≥41 kg: 1 adult tab

Alternative Regimens

  • Adult, Traveler:
    • Doxycycline 100 mg po once daily. Take 1-2 days before, during and for 4 weeks after travel.
      • Note: The American Academy of Pediatrics now recommends that doxycycline can safely be administered for short duration (≤21 days) regardless of patient age but this does not apply to malaria prophylaxis due to required duration of dosing.
    • Mefloquine 250 mg (228 mg base) 1 tab po once/week, for 2-3 weeks before, every week during and for 4 weeks after travel.
      • Start 3 weeks ahead, if feasible, to assess for intolerance
    • Tafenoquine (age ≥18 years only): 
      • Before travel to malaria-endemic area (loading regimen): 200 mg once daily x 3 days (starting 3 days before travel).
      • While in endemic area (maintenance regimen): 200 mg once weekly (starting 7 days after last loading regimen dose).
      • After exiting malaria endemic area: 200 mg one-time dose (7 days after last maintenance dose).
      • Must have G6PD activity >70% normal on a quantitative test.
        • G6PD test interpretation is complex; review J Travel Med. 26: pii: taz023, 2019.
      • Point of care rapid tests for G6PD are in advanced development PLOS Negl Trop Dis. 2023 Oct 12;17(10):e0011652.
  • Adult, pregnancy, population of endemic areas.  See WHO Consolidated Guidelines
  • Pediatrics
    • Age 8-12 years: Doxycycline 2.2 mg/kg once/day up to 100 mg/day. Take 1-2 days before, during and for 4 weeks after travel
    • Mefloquine weekly dose by weight in kg:
      • <9 kg = 5 mg/kg weekly
      • 10–19 kg = ¼ adult tab weekly
      • 20–30 kg = ½ adult tab weekly
      • 31–45 kg = ¾ adult tab weekly
      • >45 kg = 1 tab weekly
    • In areas of moderate-to-high malaria transmission where SP is still effective various regimens of SP are recommended by WHO (see WHO Consolidated Guidelines for infants and children including perennial malaria chemoprevention (PMC), seasonal malaria chemoprevention (SMC), intermittent preventive treatment in school-aged children (IPTsc), post-discharge malaria chemoprevention (PDMC) and mass drug administration (MDA)

Comments

  • Atovaquone-proguanil:
    • Preferred for short trips for infrequent travelers due to convenience of dosing.
      • Cost highly variable, two generics now FDA-approved, but expense may preclude use for trips >3 weeks.
  • Mefloquine:
    • Not recommended if cardiac conduction abnormalities, seizures, or psychiatric disorders, e.g., depression, psychosis.
    • If tolerated preferred for trips greater than 6 months
    • Outside the US: Mefloquine 275 mg tab, contains 250 mg of base.
    • Mefloquine and Doxycycline have potential to prolong QT interval.
    • Mefloquine has an FDA "black box" warning, the highest level of warning possible, stating that neuropsychiatric and vestibular adverse reactions may persist indefinitely even after discontinuation of drug.
    • A written Medication Guide and wallet card with this boxed warning must be given to all patients by the dispensing pharmacist.
    • Weekly dosing convenient for long-stays in young children. Only choice for <5kg child; must be compounded for all children under 10 kg.
  • Doxycycline:
    • Generic availability but current US pricing for travelers paying out of pocket may make it more expensive than atovaquone-proguanil for short trips due to 28 days post-exposure requirement.
    • Frequent bothersome adverse effects: Photosensitivity (advise diligent sunblock use), gastritis, vaginal candidiasis (carry fluconazole self-treatment).
    • Some evidence that monohydrate better tolerated than hyclate salt.
    • A room-temperature–stable doxycycline liquid suspension is available and is convenient for children.
  • Tafenoquine:
    • may be desirable for longer trips especially in regular travelers due to weekly dosing and preferred over mefloquine in this scenario.  
    • Must test G6PD status (once lifetime) before prescribing.
      • G6PD testing requirement has slowed uptake of this drug.  Rapid quantitative point of care G6PD tests are in advanced development
    • at present is only approved for administration for up to six months of continuous dosing.
  • Pregnancy:
    • Mefloquine is the current best option. Pregnancy risk category  B.
    • Insufficient data with Malarone but possibly safe in pregnancy but no label change to date (J Travel Med 2020 Jul 14;27(4):taaa074)
    • Avoid Doxycycline and tafenoquine/primaquine (fetal G6PD unknown). Doxycycline tooth stain only a risk after 15 weeks gestation, in rare circumstances may be used in early pregnancy
  • Breastfeeding
    • Because chloroquine and mefloquine may be safely prescribed to infants, it is also safe for infants to be exposed to the small amounts excreted in breast milk. Although data are very limited about the use of doxycycline in lactating women, most experts consider the theoretical possibility of adverse events to the infant to be remote.
    • Because the quantity of antimalarial drugs transferred in breast milk is insufficient to provide adequate protection against malaria, infants who require chemoprophylaxis must receive the recommended dosages of antimalarial drugs listed for infants
  • HIV: Malaria prophylaxis for those on ART
    • The severity of malaria is increased in HIV-infected people; malaria infection increases HIV viral load and thus may exacerbate disease progression.
    • Clinically relevant concerns with prophylactic drugs have yet to be shown.
    • Potential interactions at www.hiv-druginteractions.org.
    • Commonly used INSTI/NRTI combinations (e.g., Descovy-Tivicay, Truvada-Tivicay) have no known interactions with chemoprophylactic drugs, although co-formulations with cobicistat-boosted elvitegravir (Stribild, Genvoya) may theoretically increase mefloquine levels.
    • Cabotegravir (oral and depot) has no potential interactions with anti-malarial.
    • The rilpivirine, emtricitabine, TAF/TDF combinations (Odefsey and Complera) similarly have no interactions with antimalarials.
    • Chloroquine, mefloquine, and primaquine may interact with older maintenance regimens for HIV. 
      • Efavirenz lowers serum levels of both atovaquone and proguanil and protease inhibitors have potential interactions.
