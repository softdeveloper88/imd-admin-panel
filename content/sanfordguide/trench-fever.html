Trench Fever, Bartonella quintana

Clinical Setting

  • Trench fever is caused by Bartonella quintana – a small gram-negative rod, spread by the body louse. Often seen in homeless persons and the poor. Bartonella quintana caused a million cases of trench fever during World War One (1914-1918).
  • Trench fever manifestations:
    • Malaise, headache, bone pain, fever, splenomegaly and, sometimes, rash
    • Variety of fever patterns: single incident, intermittent, or persistent (FUO)
  • Bartonella quintana can also manifest as bacteremia, endocarditis, or bacillary angiomatosis (AIDS patients).
  • If chronic infection, evaluate the patient for complicating endocarditis.
    • Systematic review: Clin. Infect. Dis. 2024; 78: 554

Etiologies

  • Bartonella quintana

Diagnostic Considerations

  • Diagnosis:
    • Blood cultures
    • Serology: IFA but cross reacts with B. henselae
    • PCR on blood or tissue

Primary Regimens

  • Optimal therapy is uncertain due to sparse data.
  • Suggest Doxycycline 100 mg po bid x 4 weeks + Gentamicin 3 mg/kg IV once daily x initial 2 weeks of therapy

Alternative Regimens

  • If Gentamicin not possible, Rifampin 300 mg po bid x 14 days + Doxycycline 100 mg po bid  x 4 weeks

Comments

  • Doxycycline + Gentamicin, randomized trial (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 2003;47:2204).
  • TMP-SMX, fluoroquinolones, 1st generation cephalosporins and penicillins are not predictably active against Bartonella quintana and should not be used.
  • Reference: Intl J Antimicrob Agents 44:16, 2014.
