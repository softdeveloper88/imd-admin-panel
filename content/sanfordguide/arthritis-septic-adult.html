Arthritis, Septic, Adult

Clinical Setting

  • Initial empiric therapy, septic arthritis in adults, acute/chronic monoarticular, polyarticular
  • Start empiric therapy after collection of blood and joint fluid for culture; review Gram stain of joint fluid.
  • Synovial fluid WBC > 50,000 with poly predominance.
  • Three settings:
    • Acute monoarticular
      • Review Gram stain–all empirical treatment are guided by Gram stain.
      • Differentiate between patient's risk for sexually-transmitted diseases (STD).
      • Differential diagnosis includes gout and chondrocalcinosis (pseudogout) (crystals in joint fluid).
    • Chronic monoarticular
    • Polyarticular: suspect disseminated gonococcal infection:
      • Usually acute onset, Gram stain is typically negative for gonococci.
      • If patient is sexually active, culture urethra, cervix, anal canal, throat, blood, joint fluid.
      • If pustular/hemorrhagic skin lesions and tenosynovitis present, suspect N. gonorrhea.
      • Expanded differential includes gout, pseudogout, reactive arthritis (HLA-B27 positive), Lyme.

Etiologies

  • Acute monoarticular
    • If risk of STD:
      • N. gonorrhea
      • Staph. aureus
      • Streptococcus spp.
      • Gram-negative bacilli
    • If no risk of STD:
      • Staph. aureus
      • Streptococcus spp.
      • Gram-negative bacilli
  • Chronic monoarticular
    • Brucella spp.
    • Nocardia spp.
    • Mycobacteria
    • Fungi
  • Polyarticular
    • N. gonorrhea
    • B. burgdorferi (Lyme disease)
    • S. aureus
    • Group A streptococcus, pneumococcus
    • Acute rheumatic fever
    • Post-streptococcal arthritis
    • Reactive arthritis
    • Viral: Hepatitis B, Rubella vaccine, Parvo B19, Chikungunya (travel history)

Primary Regimens

  • Treatment: adequate drainage of purulent joint fluid + systemic antimicrobial therapy
  • Acute monoarticular
    • Patient presents with STD risk:
      • Gram stain negative or Gram stain positive for Gram-negative diplococci (see Comments):
        • (Ceftriaxone 1 gm IV q24h or Cefotaxime 2 gm IV q8h (where available outside the US) or Ceftizoxime 1 gm IV q8h)  (See comments for treatment of concomitant chlamydial infection).
      • Gram stain positive for Gram-positive cocci:
        • Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL)

      • Gram stain positive for gram-negative bacilli: See Arthritis Gram-Negative Bacilli

    • No or low STD risk:
      • Gram-stain negative
        • Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL) + Ceftriaxone 1 gm IV q24h OR
        • Vancomycin as above + Cefepime 2 gm q8h IV q8h (preferred for possible healthcare-associated infection)
      • Gram stain shows Gram-positive cocci:
        • Vancomycin as above
      • Gram-stain positive for Gram-negative bacilli: See Arthritis Gram-Negative Bacilli
  • Chronic monoarticular

    • Empiric therapy not generally recommended, treat for specific etiology

  • Polyarticular
    • Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL) + Ceftriaxone 1 gm IV q24h 

Alternative Regimens

  • Acute monoarticular
    • No STD risk: Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL) + (Ciprofloxacin 400 mg IV q12h or Levofloxacin 750 mg IV q24h)

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Duration of therapy not well studied. One randomized controlled trial of septic arthritis in adults caused by a variety of pathogens found that two weeks of oral therapy was non-inferior to four weeks of oral therapy following surgical drainage and a median of 1-2 days of intravenous therapy; 99 of the 154 patients has arthritis of the hand or wrist (Ann Rheum Dis. 2019; 78:1114-1121).

Comments

  • Approximately 20% of patients with septic arthritis presentation have negative cultures; a proportion of these go on to develop rheumatic diseases (Joint Bone Spine 79:156, 2012)
  • For patients with STD risk, Azithromycin 1 gm po or Doxycycline 100 mg po twice daily for 7 days is for treatment of concomitant chlamydial infection
  • See also, Disseminated Gonorrhea.
  • If polyarticular arthritis, consider Lyme disease if exposure areas known to harbor infected ticks; typically involves large joints.
  • Specific therapy for S. aureus septic arthritis.
  • Reference: Curr Rheumatol Rep 15:332, 2013.
