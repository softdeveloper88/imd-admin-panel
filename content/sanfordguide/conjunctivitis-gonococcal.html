Conjunctivitis: Gonococcal

 

Clinical Setting

  • Adult bacterial conjunctivitis due to Neisseria gonorrhoeae.
  • Treat sex partner
  • Test patient for HIV and Syphilis

Etiologies

  • Neisseria gonorrhoeae

Diagnosis

  • Diagnosis may be possible with gram stain of the exudate. NAAT should be positive but commercial assays not approved for conjunctival exudate. Urine and/or throat NAAT reasonable

Primary Regimens

  • Ceftriaxone 1 gm IM or IV single dose 
  • If chlamydial infection not ruled out with NAAT, treat with Doxycycline 100 mg po bid x 7 days
  • Ophthalmology consult desirable as can progress to corneal perforation

Alternative Regimens

  • For patients intolerant or allergic to cephalosporins recommend ID consult
  • Can consider:
    • Gentamicin 240 mg IM (5mg/kg if wt <45kg) x 1 + Azithromycin 2 gm po x 1
    • Gemifloxicin 320 mg single dose + Azithromycin 2 gm po x single dose (but high incidence of nausea)
  • 2020 European IUSTI gonorrhea guidelines for conjunctivitis:
    • The eye should be irrigated with sterile saline solution "frequently"
    • Ceftriaxone 1 g IM as a single dose + Azithromycin 2 gm po x 1

Comments

  • 2020 revised CDC guidelines for gonococcal infection MMWR 69;1911 2020 and 2021 CDC STD guidelines MMWR Recomm Rep 70 (RR-4):1 2021
    no longer recommend routine dual treatment.
  • Review of evidence for CDC guidelines: Clin Infect Dis 74:S95 2022 (Gonorrhea) and Clin Infect Dis 74:S112 2022 (Chlamydia)
  • Consider one-time saline lavage of eye to prevent corneal perforation.
  • Recent review of diagnosis and treatment of conjunctivitis: JAMA 310:1721, 2013.
