IV Line Infection, Tunneled Catheters

Clinical Setting

  • Unexplained fever in patient with IV access via a tunneled catheter.
  • Evidence of infected IV line with one of the following types of "tunneled" access:
    • Subclavian tunneled catheter (Broviac, Hickman, Groshong, Quinton)
    • Chest-wall port
    • Dual lumen hemodialysis access catheter: e.g. Permacath
  • Special populations (impaired host) with infected IV line:
    • Burn patient, neutropenic patient
    • Chronic hyperalimentation
    • Use of IV lipid emulsions
    • Beware of occult infection in clotted hemodialysis grafts.
      • May be Detected by indium scan
      • For review of arteriovenous access for hemodialysis: JAMA 2024; 331:1307

Diagnosis

  • Diagnosis based on positive blood cultures and/or culture of the tip of the catheter if it is removed.
    • Blood culture drawn through the line is positive 2 or more hours sooner than blood cultures from a peripheral vein.
    • Semi-quantitative culture (roll-tube method) of the tip of a removed catheter grows >15 colonies of a putative pathogen.

Etiologies

  • Tunneled IV Line:
    • Staphylococcus aureus
    • Staphylococcus epidermidis
    • Leuconostoc or Lactobacillus
    • Other pathogens possible: e.g., gram-negative bacilli, Candida sp.
  • Burn or neutropenic patient, etiologies expand to include:
    • Pseudomonas sp.,
    • Enterobacteriaceae
    • Corynebacterium jeikeium
  • Long-term alimentation:
    • Candida sp.

Primary Regimens

  • Empiric therapy:
    • Non-neutropenic and not a burn patient: Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL)
    • Neutropenic or a burn patient: Vancomycin + high dose Piperacillin-tazobactam for possible P. aeruginosa infection
  • Specific therapy (positive blood and /or catheter culture):
    • If Staph. aureus in blood or subcutaneous tunnel is infected, generally must remove the line plus antimicrobial therapy to achieve cure
    • Staph. aureus or Staph. epidermidis: Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL).
    • Lactobacillus or Leuconostoc are intrinsically resistant to vancomycin; use Penicillin G, Ampicillin or Clindamycin

Alternative Regimens

  • Empiric therapy: Burn or neutropenic patient:
    • Vancomycin + (Cefepime or Ceftazidime) OR
    • Vancomycin + Piperacillin-tazobactam OR
    • Vancomycin + (Imipenem or Meropenem or Doripenem)
  • Empiric therapy: Patients requiring long-term alimentation (Candida infection a potential`` problem):
    • Empiric therapy should include  Anidulafungin or Micafungin or Caspofungin
    • Usually must remove the line.

Comments

  • In burn patients, suppurative phlebitis often necessitates surgical excision of the vein.
  • Ophthalmologic consult recommended for patients with Candidemia.
  • IV lipid emulsions (Intralipid) are prone to infection with Staph. epidermidis and occasionally Malassezia furfur (responds to azoles).
  • IDSA Practice Guidelines: Clin Infect Dis 49:1, 2009.
