Urethritis, Cervicitis: Chlamydial

Clinical Setting

  • Chlamydia, non- or post-gonococcal urethritis or cervicitis.
  • Concomitant gonorrhea should be assumed. See Gonococcal urethritis/cervicitis.
  • Evaluate and treat sex partners (and advise to abstain from sex until treatment complete).
  • Re-test for cure in pregnancy.
  • For rectal chlamydia, see Comments

Etiologies

  • Chlamydia trachomatis (50%)
  • Mycoplasma hominis
  • Other known etiologies (10–15%):
    • Trichomonas
    • H. simplex virus
    • Mycoplasma genitalium
    • Ureaplasma urealyticum (controversial)

Diagnosis

  • Nucleic acid amplification tests for C. trachomatis and N. gonorrhoeae on urine, urethral, or endocervical sample.
  • Another alternative is patient collected vaginal swab: New Engl J Med 2017; 367: 765.

Primary Regimens

  • Doxycycline 100 mg bid po x 7 days 
  • Pregnancy: Azithromycin 1 gm po x 1 dose

Alternative Regimens

  • Azithromycin 500 mg po x 1 dose followed by 250 mg po x 4 days (for urethritis)
  • Azithromycin 1 gm po x 1 dose (for cervicitis or urethritis) Some evidence that single dose regimen more likely to select for resistant M. genitalium

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Mycoplasma genitalium is associated with 30% of non-gonococcal urethritis (NGU), cervicitis, and pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) (Clin Infect Dis 56:934, 2013) and may account for treatment failures. Macrolide resistance found in almost 40% of M. genitalium-positive patients tested in STI clinic patients in Denmark (Clin Infect Dis 59:24 (Jul 1), 2014). Resistance increasing world wide, with increasing resistance to fluoroquinolones. See 2021 CDC STI guidelines for explanation of new 2 stage treatment 
  • Macrolide treatment also associated with increased resistance of other pulmonary and GI microbes.

Comments

  • Pregnancy
    • All pregnant women should be screened for chlamydia infection on initial prenatal visit.
    • Doxycycline and Fluoroquinolones are contraindicated for use during pregnancy.
  • If patient fails treatment for Chlamydia trachomatis, possibilities are:
    • Trichomonas vaginalis
    • Mycoplasma genitalium
    • Herpes simplex
  • All patients with chlamydia infection should be tested for HIV.
  • Rectal chlamydia:
    • Prevalence of  6% but up to 68% among women with vaginal chlamydia
    • In cohort study, microbiologic cure with single dose azithromycin 78.5% vs 95.5% with 7 days of doxycycline bid.Sex Transm Dis 2014 41:79
    • In RCT in MSM with rectal Chlamydia doxycycline 100 bid superior to azithromycin (100% vs. 74% microbiological cure). Clin Infect Dis 2021 doi: 10.1093/cid/ciab153.
    • Preponderance of evidence indicates Doxycycline is more efficacious for rectal chlamydia and in men with urogenital symptoms. Clin Infect Dis 74:S112 2022
  • Treat LGV for 3 weeks with doxycycline.
  • See 2021 CDC STI guidelines MMWR Recomm Rep 70 (RR-4):1 2021 

  • Review of evidence for CDC guidelines: Clin Infect Dis 74:S95 2022 (Gonorrhea) and Clin Infect Dis 74:S112 2022 (Chlamydia)
