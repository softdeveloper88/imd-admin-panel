Initial Evaluation of the HIV-Infected Adult

Summary of Initial Evaluation

  • For an updated summary of initial evaluation of suspected HIV infection, see N Engl J Med 384: 2131, 2021 and IAS-USA Guidelines for ARV Therapy JAMA. 2025, 333:609.

Confirm Test Results

  • Insist on documentation of an initial positive HIV antibody test.
  • Confirm with a 2nd diagnostic test:
    • HIV antibody test
    • HIV antigen (part of recommended testing platforms)
    • Plasma viral HIV RNA PCR quantitation

Patient History

General Health Status

  • General well-being and constitutional symptoms, physician history
  • Past infectious diseases (e.g., tuberculosis, leishmaniasis, coccidioidomycosis, histoplasmosis)
  • Chronic conditions (e.g., diabetes, multiple sclerosis)
  • Childhood infections (e.g., chickenpox, measles)
  • Prior hospitalizations (where and for what)
  • Immunization history (e.g., hepatitis A/B, pneumococcal, BCG)

Drug History

  • Prescription and non-prescription medications, including any known drug allergies
  • Alternative remedies
  • "Recreational" or "illicit" drug use (identify partners at risk)
  • Tobacco and alcohol use

Sexual History

  • Sexual practices
  • Past or current sexually transmitted diseases (STD)
  • Contraceptive use
  • Obstetric and gynecological history
  • Identify partners at risk

Opportunistic Infection Risk

  • Travel history
  • Residence history
  • Occupational history
  • Avocational activities
  • Tuberculosis status
    • History of BCG vaccination, family history of tuberculosis, contacts (close) with patients with known tuberculosis, results of previous tuberculin tests &/or chest x-rays if known
  • Pets, Animal interests
    • Cats—Bartonella henselae; cat ownership not associated with toxoplasma antibody seroconversion
    • Fish—M. marinum
  • Past history of viral Hepatitis (type, if known)
  • Past history of Herpes zoster
  • History of Antiretroviral Therapy:
    • Prior treatment regimens
    • CD4 count and viral load data
    • Past side effects
  • Cardiovascular disease risk factors

Physical Examination

  • Perform comprehensive physical exam.
  • Document weight and height.
  • Careful fundoscopic and oral examination.
  • Complete dermatologic examination, including back, buttocks, extremities, hands and feet.
  • Examine all lymph node areas: postoccipital, preauricular, cervical, submental, supraclavicular, axillary, epitrochlear, inguinal (measure and record size if palpable, record as negative if not palpable).
  • Rectal/genital examination, to include pelvic exam with Pap smear in women, inspection for perianal/genital Herpes simplex. Pap smears should be repeated every 12 months. PCR for human papillomavirus
  • Assess mental status for evidence of dementia.

Laboratory Evaluation

Determine Baseline

  • Complete blood cell count with differential.
  • Electrolytes, blood sugar (diabetes may complicate use of protease inhibitors (PI), which cause insulin resistance), renal function tests: BUN, creatinine (abnormal renal function may complicate use of Tenofovir or adjustment in NRTI/NNRTI doses).
  • Liver enzyme tests: serum bilirubin, aspartate aminotransferase (AST, SGOT), alanine aminotransferase (ALT, SGPT), alkaline phosphatase (Indinavir & Atazanavir can elevate indirect bilirubin levels)
  • Creatine kinase (high level may indicate either HIV or drug-induced myopathy)
  • Fasting lipid profile (elevated levels may indicate need for dietary/drug therapy or avoidance of certain PIs)
  • HIV resistance test / assessment

HIV Staging

  • Important for all future care decisions including when to initiate antiretroviral therapy (ART) and prophylaxis
  • CD4 & CD8 T-lymphocyte count
  • Quantitative measurement of plasma HIV RNA: “viral load” or plasma “viral burden”
  • Repeat every 3-6 months

Other

  • PPD intermediate (5TU), or blood assay for M. tuberculosis infection (QuantiFERON-TB GOLD), MMWR 54 (RR-15), 2005.
  • Chest x-ray (baseline important for future care)
  • Treponemal antibody (tests for syphilis) (evidence of past or recent exposure requires treatment unless there is documentation of adequate course of treatment); repeat annually.
  • IgG antibody to toxoplasmosis (if + primary prophylaxis indicated when CD4 <100)
  • Hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg), antibody to Hep B surface Ag (anti-HBsAg), antibody to Hep C (important in consideration of treatment decisions for chronic active infection & ART); IgG antibody to Hepatitis A.
  • CMV antibody, IgG
  • Urine nucleic acid amplification test for C. trachomatis & N. gonorrhea
  • Type-specific Herpes simplex antibody
  • Perhaps serum testosterone level
  • Vitamin D serum level

Initial Health Care Maintenance

  • HIV risk reduction education
  • Drug rehabilitation/safer needle use/needle exchange
  • Smoking cessation (smoking increases risk of thrush, hairy leukoplakia, bacterial pneumonia, coronary artery disease)
  • Partner notification
  • Reproductive counseling
  • Psychosocial support
  • Immunizations transiently increase HIV viral load, clinical significance uncertain
    • Protein conjugate pneumococcal vaccine
    • Influenza vaccine (annually)
    • Hepatitis B vaccine, if sexually active or sharing needles; hepatitis A vaccine
  • Preventive dentistry
  • If CD4 count <100 cells/mm3, baseline ophthalmologic evaluation
  • Cervical Pap smear females; anal Pap smear males;anal HPV PCRs in both males and females

Primary Care Considerations

  • Multiple studies demonstrate that physicians and other health care givers who care for large numbers of HIV-infected persons and who make delivery of this care a major focus of their practice, training and continuing education have better outcomes.
  • A team approach with coordination of services around patients’ needs and integration of acute and long-term care is emerging as the most effective management.
