Lomentospora (Scedosporium) prolificans

Clinical Setting

  • Lomentospora is ubiquitous in the environment and infection occurs most commonly through inhalation or direct inoculation of skin.
  • Immunocompetent hosts most often have cutaneous disease.
  • Immunocompromised hosts can have a wide spectrum of infection ranging from pulmonary colonization to invasive disease in lung, skin, and disseminated disease that can include CNS and bone infections.

Etiologies

  • Lomentospora (Scedosporium) prolificans

Primary Regimens

  • Surgical debridement and reduction of immunosuppression
  • Antifungals are often not active, infectious diseases consultation recommended to assist obtaining investigational treatment (see below)

Alternative Regimens

  • Voriconazole 6 mg/kg IV q12h on day 1, then 4 mg/kg IV q12h, consider addition of other agents as below

Comments

  • Consider antifungal susceptibility testing.
  • Surgical debridement should be considered in most cases since these infections can be difficult to eradicate with antifungal agents alone given that Lomentospora prolificans is resistant to all antifungal agents.
  • Combination therapy: in vitro synergy of voriconazole with terbinafine and echinocandins have been reported although clinical data is limited (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 56:2635, 2012). One case report of L. prolificans osteomyelitis revealed response to miltefosine (Clin Infect Dis 48:1257, 2009).
  • Olorfim is an antifungal with in-vitro activity against Lomentospora.  Studies are being conducted to determine clinical efficacy.
