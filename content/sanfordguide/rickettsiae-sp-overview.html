Rickettsiae sp., Overview

Overview

  • These organisms cause a variety of zoonotic infections transmitted by fleas, ticks, and mites and characterized by non-localizing systemic illness, typically associated with:
    • Rash (macular, purpuric, or vesicular) and/or eschar
    • Fever
    • Thrombocytopenia
    • Suggestive exposure histories
  • Susceptibility to Doxycycline
  • Many species identified:
    • The list below identifies species most frequently encountered
    • For convenience, divided into "spotted fever" and "typhus" groups
    • See Tick-borne Illness, Overview
  • Review: Management of tickborne rickettsial diseases, ehrlichiosis and anaplasmosis (MMWR RR 65(2): 1-44, 2016; many illustrations).

Primary Regimens

  • See specific topic for management and treatment, but in general preferred treatment is:
    • Doxycycline 100 mg IV/po bid

Alternative Regimens

  • See specific topic for management and treatment, but in general:
    • Chloramphenicol 50 mg/kg IV/po divided q6h (especially in pregnancy)
    • If critical illness due to RMSF,  some use doxycycline loading dose:
      • Doxycycline 200 mg IV/po bid for first 72 hrs and then 100 mg IV/po bid (but no data)
      • Doxycycline single 200 mg loading dose and then 100 mg IV/po bid (Lancet Infect Dis 8:143, 2008)

Topics Links

  • Spotted Fever group:
    • R. rickettsii: Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever (RMSF)
    • R. parkeri: American Boutonneuse Fever
    • R. conorii: Mediterranean Boutonneuse Fever
    • R. akari:  Rickettsialpox
    • R. africae: African tick bite fever
    • R. philipiii: Pacific coast tick fever or 364D
  • Typhus group:
    • R. prowazekii: Epidemic typhus (Louse-borne)
    • R.typhi: Murine typhus (rat or cat flea)
    • Orientia tsutsugamushi (formerly Rickettsia): Scrub typhus (mites/chiggers)
