Folliculitis

Clinical Setting

  • Infection of the hair follicle with purulent exudate in the epidermis.
  • Need to distinguish from non-infectious etiologies, e.g., pseudofolliculitis barbae (foreign reaction to ingrown hair in beard).

Etiologies

  • Staph. aureus (most common)
  • P. aeruginosa (from exposure to inadequately chlorinated swimming pools, whirlpools and hot tubs)
  • Aeromonas hydrophila (following water exposure)
  • C. albicans (after antibiotic exposure, glucocorticoid therapy or diabetes mellitus)
  • Reports of folliculitis and subcutaneous abscesses from rapid growing atypical mycobacteria after exposure in nail salons, foot whirlpool baths. Etiology: M. chelonae; M. fortuitum
  • Malassezia furfur

Primary Regimens

  • Usually self-limiting. Hot packs for comfort. Topical therapy sometimes needed.
  • Avoid shaving involved area.
  • See specific therapy for M. fortuitum and M. chelonae.

Alternative Regimens

  • Could use Mupirocin ointment if staphylococcal etiology.
  • Could use antifungal topical cream/ointment if Candida suspected or proven.

Comments

  • In AIDS patients, consider eosinophilic folliculitis.
