Continuous, Extended Infusion Dosing

Continuous or Extended-Infusion Dosing

  • The best predictor of bacterial killing by beta-lactam antibiotics (Penicillins, Cephalosporins, Monobactams (Aztreonam), and Carbapenems) is the time during the dosage interval that the free (non-protein bound) drug concentration exceeds the MIC of the target organism(s) (Nat Rev Microbiol 2:289, 2004). It is possible to determine the probability that a given dosing regimen will achieve the desired drug exposure across a range of organism MICs using a variety of models (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 48:4718, 2004).
    • There is gradual implementation of this approach for clinical use.
    • Implementation of either an extended infusion of each dose or continuous infusion over 24 hrs is, in available publications, efficacious and cost saving. Expense is reduced both by a reduction in the amount of drug needed per day plus less labor expense with simplified regimens.
  • Antibiotic stability is a concern. Factors influencing stability include drug concentration, IV infusion diluent (e.g. NS vs. D5W), type of infusion device, and storage temperature (Pharm & Therap 36:723, 2011). Portable pumps worn close to the body expose antibiotics to temperatures closer to body temperature (37ºC) than room temperature (around 25ºC). Carbapenems are particularly unstable and may require wrapping of infusion pumps in cold packs or frequent changes of infusion bags or cartridges.
  • Based on current and rapidly changing data, it appears that extended infusion or continuous infusion of beta-lactams is at least as successful as intermittent dosing. Hence, this approach can be part of stewardship programs as supported by recent publications.
  • A meta-analysis of observational studies found reduced mortality among patients treated with extended or continuous infusion of Carbapenems or Piperacillin-Tazobactam (pooled data) as compared to standard intermittent therapy regimens. The results were similar for extended and continuous regimens when considered separately. There was a mortality benefit with Piperacillin-Tazobactam but not with Carbapenems (Clin Infect Dis 56:272, 2013). The lower mortality could, at least in part, be due to closer professional supervision engendered by a study environment. On the other hand, a small prospective randomized controlled study of continuous vs. intermittent Piperacillin-Tazobactam and Meropenem found a higher clinical cure rate and a trend toward lower mortality in the continuous infusion patients (Clin Infect Dis 56:236, 2013). An individual patient data meta-analysis of a large critical care population with severe sepsis found decreased mortality and increased clinical cure rate with beta-lactams administered by continuous  infusion vs. intermittent dosing (Am J Respir Crit Care Med 194:681, 2016). In a recent secondary analysis of a prospective multicenter study of cirrhotic patients with bloodstream infection, the administration of continuous/extended infusion of Piperacillin-Tazobactam or Carbapenems vs intermittent infusion was associated with improved survival (Clin Infect Dis 2019;69:1731).
  • This Table summarizes the current state of the art for either extended infusion or continuous infusion of those beta-lactams for which a reasonable database exists. Some fine points are in flux. Some investigators have elected to start with an initial (“loading”) dose and some have not. Further, the interval between an “initial” dose and the first extended infusion or the start of the continuous infusion is, at this time, an educated guess.

Ampicillin-sulbactam
Minimum stability@ 37ºC: Stability (in NS) at 24 hr is ampicillin 77%, sulbactam 93% (Int J Antimicrob Agents 6:S31, 1996)
@ 25ºC: 8 hr (in NS) at a conc of ≤ 30 mg/15 mg (ampicillin/sulbactam) per mL
@ 4ºC: 48 hr (in NS) at a conc of ≤ 30 mg/15 mg (ampicillin/sulbactam) per mL
Recommended Dose by Extended Infusion9 gm (6 gm ampicillin + 3 gm sulbactam) IV over 4 hours q8h
CommentsRecommended dose is for ventilator-associated pneumonia due to Acinetobacter. High dose found to be safe and effective in observational studies (Scand J Infect Dis 39:38, 2007; J Infect 56:432, 2008). The need for a high-dose extended-infusion sulbactam regimen is supported by pharmacodynamic computer simulation studies using serum concentration measurements in healthy volunteers (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 57:3441, 2013; Eur J Pharm Sci 136:104940, 2019.  Recommended dose is for patients with normal renal function.

Cefepime  
Minimum stability@ 37ºC: 8 hr
@ 25ºC: 24 hr
@ 4ºC: ≥24 hr
Recommended Dose by Continuous Infusion
Initial dose: 15 mg/kg over 30 min, then immediately begin:
  • If CrCl > 60: 6 gm (over 24 hr) daily
  • If CrCl 30-60: 4 gm (over 24 hr) daily
  • If CrCl 11-29: 2 gm (over 24 hr) daily
Comments
  • CrCl adjustments extrapolated from prescribing information, not clinical data (J Antimicrob Chemother 57:1017, 2006; Am J Health Syst Pharm 68:319, 2011).
  • One report of mortality benefit from extending infusion time from 30 minutes to 4 hours in patients with Pseudomonas pneumonia and/or bacteremia: Antimicrob Agents Chemother 57:2907, 2013.
Ceftazidime
Minimum Stability
@ 37ºC: 8 hr
@ 25ºC: 24 hr
@ 4ºC: ≥24 hr
Recommended Dose by Continuous Infusion

Initial dose: 15 mg/kg over 30 min, then immediately begin:

  • If CrCl >50: 6 gm (over 24 hr) daily
  • If CrCl 31-50: 4 gm (over 24 hr) daily
  • If CrCl 10-30: 2 gm (over 24 hr) daily
CommentsCrCl adjustments extrapolated from prescribing information, not clinical data (Br J Clin Pharmacol 50:184, 2000; Int J Antimicrob Agents 17:497, 2001; Antimicrob Agents Chemother  49:3550, 2005; Infect 37:418, 2009; J Antimicrob Chemother 68:900, 2013). Pyridine, a byproduct of ceftazidime degradation, is a theoretic toxicity concern; minimize risk by limiting daily dose to 6 gm, using normal saline as the infusion vehicle, keeping ceftazidime concentration ≤3 gm per 100 mL if possible, and maintaining infusion device temperature between 15-22 ºC (Am J Health Syst Pharm 76:200, 2019).

Temocillin 
Minimum Stability@ 37°C: 24 hr
@ 25ºC: 24 hr

Recommended Dose by Continuous Infusion

Initial dose: 2 gm over 30 min, then immediately begin:

Vancomycin
Minimum Stability
@ 37ºC: 48 hr
@ 25ºC: 48 hr
@ 4ºC: 58 days (at conc 10 μg/mL)
Recommended Dose by Continuous Infusion
Loading dose 15-20 mg/kg (infusion rate 10-15 mg/min), then 30-40 mg/kg (max 60 mg/kg) over 24 hours daily.
Begin continuous infusion immediately after the loading dose.
Comments
  • Adjust dose to target steady-state concentration of 20-25 μg/mL. Higher concentrations (30-40 μg/mL) achieved with more aggressive dosing increase the risk of nephrotoxicity (Clin Micro Inf 19:E98, 2013).
  • Calculate AUC24 by multiplying the steady-state concentration by 24. A concentration of 20-25 μg/mL equates to an AUC24 of 480-600 μg/mL x hr.
  • Vancomycin administration by continuous infusion is gaining acceptance as a treatment option. Target concentrations are more rapidly achieved with less variability, and the risk of nephrotoxicity appears to be similar to or lower than intermittent infusion (Am J Health Syst Pharm 2020;77:835).
  • Because of incompatibilities with other drugs used in critically ill patients, it will be necessary to use multiple IV lines when vancomycin is administered by continuous infusion.
