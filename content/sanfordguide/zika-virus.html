Zika Virus

Clinical Setting

  • A mosquito transmitted flavivirus; same family as Hepatitis C, West Nile virus, Japanese encephalitis virus, St. Louis encephalitis, and Yellow fever.
  • Sexual transmission is documented (vaginal, anal, oral and sharing of sex toys).
    • Prevention of transmission: 1) abstinence from sex or 2) condom use (to reduce transmission risk). See CDC Guidance at https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/65/wr/mm6539e1.htm
  • Most often acute infection is asymptomatic (~ 80%); when symptoms occur, usually consists of low grade fever, arthralgias, morbiliform rash, and / or conjunctival redness (non-purulent conjunctivitis).  Duration of symptoms ranges from a few days to one week.  Rare Guillian-Barre syndrome. Hospitalization very infrequent, fatalities are very rare.   
    • Most serious manifestation of infection is congenital birth defect(s): microcephaly and fetal demise; causal association made (N Engl J Med 374:1981, 2016).
    • CDC has updated its interim guidance for US health care providers caring for women of reproductive age with possible Zika virus exposure to include preconception counseling recommendations. This guidance also provides updated recommendations for testing pregnant women with possible Zika exposure (MMWR 65:315, 2016).
  • For current travel notices and information, see https://wwwnc.cdc.gov/travel/page/zika-travel-information
  • 31,780 cases in the Americas in 2023.  No local transmissions reported in the US since 2019) 
  • To report cases to the CDC Registry, email (ZikaMCH@cdc.gov) or call 770-488-7100 and ask for the Zika Pregnancy Hotline.
  • Transmission typically from human-to-mosquito-to-human, although intrauterine transmission from mother to fetus occurs, as well as sexual transmission, blood product transfusion, and in the laboratory setting. Theoretical concern re organ transplant transmission and via beast milk.  Intrauterine transmission can occur at any trimester.
  • Incubation period:  ~ 3 - 7 days (may be as long as 2 weeks)
  • Evaluation of pregnant women who are returning from affected countries (MMWR 65:30, 2016)(CDC Interim Guidelines):

  • CDC Guidance for pregnancy.
    • Screen pregnant women for risk of Zika exposure and symptoms of Zika. Promptly test pregnant women with NAT if they become symptomatic during their pregnancy or if a sexual partner tests positive for Zika virus infection.
    • Consider NAT testing at least once per trimester, unless a previous test has been positive.
    • Consider NAT testing of amniocentesis specimens if amniocentesis is performed for other reasons.
    • Counsel pregnant women each trimester on the limitations of IgM and NAT testing.
    • Consider IgM testing to determine baseline Zika virus IgM levels as part of preconception counseling.
  • For men and women considering conception:
    • Male partner traveling to an area with risk for Zika: Couple should use condoms (or abstain from sex) for at least 3 months after onset of symptoms (if symptomatic) or time when the male partner returns (if asymptomatic).
    • Female partner traveling to an area with risk for Zika: Couple should use condoms (or abstain from sex) for at least 2 months after onset of symptoms (if symptomatic) or time when the female partner returns (if asymptomatic).
    • Both partners traveling to an area with risk for Zika: Couple should use condoms (or abstain from sex) for at least 3 months after onset of symptoms (if symptomatic) or time when the last partner returns (if asymptomatic)
  • Of note, if a symptomatic pregnant woman is IgM positive and NAT negative, and lived in or traveled to an area with a posted CDC Zika Travel Notice , healthcare providers should recognize that the positive IgM result does not necessarily indicate recent infection, owing to the long half-life (> 12 weeks) of the IgM antibody.

Diagnosis

  • Diagnosis by Serology or PCR (if available). Potential antibody cross reactivity with other Flaviviruses: e.g., West Nile, St. Louis encephalitis virus, Yellow Fever virus

Etiology

  • ssRNA flavivirus 
  • Vector. Transmitted by Aedes aegypti and Ae. albopictus mosquitos (found ~ world-wide, including Central and South America and the southern US).  Same mosquitoes that transmit Dengue and Chikungunya viruses)

Primary Regimens

  • No treatment is available
  • Symptomatic support (avoid aspirin and NSAIDs until Dengue is ruled out)

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • No vaccine for Zika virus; prevention is mosquito avoidance (DEET, long-sleeve clothing)
  • First identified in Uganda in 1947; sporadic cases reported mostly in Africa and Asia until 2007 (first epidemic reported in Micronesia and Western Pacific).
  • Comprehensive review: N Engl J Med 2019;381:1444
