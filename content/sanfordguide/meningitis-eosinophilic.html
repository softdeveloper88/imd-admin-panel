Meningitis, Eosinophilic

Clinical Setting

  • Parasitic meningitis (eosinophilia). Travel and exposure history are often positive.
  • 1/3 of patients lack peripheral eosinophilia.
  • Automated CSF count may not correctly identify eosinophils (Clin Infect Dis 48:322, 2009).

Etiologies

  • Angiostrongyliasis. For life cycle see New Engl J Med. 2025;392:699
  • Gnathostomiasis
  • Baylisascariasis
  • Other infectious and non-infectious causes:
    • Other infections: visceral larva migrans, trichinellosis, rarely other parasites, and coccidioidomycosis
    • Non-infectious causes: Neoplasis (Hodgkins lymphoma), Drug reaction (NSAIDs), Ciprofloxacin, TMP-SMX, and Vancomycin), and reaction to radiographic contrast or implanted foreign bodies (shunts).

Diagnosis

  • For reliable detection of eosinophils in CSF, centrifuge fluid and stain pellet with Wright or Giemsa stain.
  • Parasite rarely seen in CSF. PCR and Immunoassays developed but rarely available.

Primary Regimens

  • See specific parasites for treatment options:
    • Angiostrongylus: symptomatic treatment with analgesics.
    • Baylisascariasis (Raccoon roundworm): case reports of successful treatment, but overall results are poor once clinical disease has developed.
    • Gnathostomiasis: Albendazole and Ivermectin have activity in cutaneous disease, but effectiveness in CNS disease is unknown.

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • Corticosteroinds used  to help attenuate inflammatory response and associated increase in intracranial pressure. See individual "worms"
  • Angiostrongylus cantonensis in travelers: Curr Opin Infect Dis 2018;31:399.
  • Reference: J Pediatr 2022; 251:202
