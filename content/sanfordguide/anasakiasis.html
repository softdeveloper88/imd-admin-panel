Anisakiasis

Clinical Setting

  • The "sushi" worm
  • Roundworm larva embedded in gastric or sometimes intestinal mucosa acquired by eating raw fish: herring, salmon, mackerel, cod, squid; other species increasingly implicated with aquaculture.
  • Worldwide distribution in colder waters, mostly Japan, North Atlantic, Pacific
  • Similar illness due to Pseudoterranova sp. acquired from cod, halibut, red snapper.
  • Previously frozen fish (FDA requirement for sushi in restaurants) only have dead anisakis.
  • Acute epigastric syndrome within minutes to hours, most frequently 10-12 hours, of ingestion; occasional intestinal obstruction, some patients develop urticaria, anaphylaxis, angioedema
  • Large descriptive clinical cohort from Spain (Clin Infect Dis 69:69, 2019).
  • Endoscopic images: Korean J Parasitol 57; 411, 2019.

Diagnosis

  • IgE antibody test vs A. simplex may help diagnosis.

Etiologies

  • Anisakis simplex, Anisakis physeteris, Anisakis pegreffii, or other Anisakis sp.
  • Pseudoterranova decipiens, P. azarasi and others
  • Contracaecum sp.

Primary Regimens

  • No antimicrobial therapy indicated
  • Physical removal of worm: gastric endoscopy (usual) or surgery if intestinal infestation with obstruction.

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • Anecdotal reports of possible treatment benefit from Albendazole (Lancet 360:54, 2002; Clin Infect Dis 41:1825, 2005).
  • References: Clin Infect Dis 51:806, 2010; Lancet Inf Dis 4:294, 2004.
  • Possible association of anisakis ingestion with allergic symptoms
