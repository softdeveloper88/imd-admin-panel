Typhus, Murine

Clinical Setting

  • Flea-borne rickettsial infection. Usual hosts are rats.
  • Fever, chills and myalgia in patient with exposure to fleas that feed on infected rats, mice and even house cats.
  • Maculopapular rash in 20-54% of patients; hard to see in patients with dark skin.
  • Thrombocytopenia in 48% of patients.
  • When severe can present as adult respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS): Emerg Inf Dis 20:1375, 2014

Etiologies

  • Rickettsia typhi

Diagnostic Considerations

  • Diagnostic methods:
    • Seroconversion:
      • Antibody by IFA on sera collected at least 2 weeks apart
      • Antibodies cross react with R. prowazekii (epidemic typhus) and spotted fever group of rickettsia: e.g. RMSF
    • Can detect with PCR during acute infection,  but PCR may not be readily available (Check with State Public Health Labs)
    • Reference: Acta Tropica 2021;218:105887

Primary Regimens

  • Spontaneous recovery occurs within 2 or more weeks if untreated; fatalities are rare.
    • Empiric therapy shortens illness to 5 days or less
    • See Pediatr Infect Dis J 2018;37:1071
  • Doxycycline 100 mg po/IV bid x 7 days (Adult or child - see Comment)

Alternative Regimens

  • Pregnancy: Chloramphenicol 500 mg po/IV qid x 7 days (maximum 2 gm daily)

Comments

  • Azithromycin is not an acceptable alternative. In a prospective randomized trial in adults, azithromycin therapy was found inferior to 2 different doxycycline regimens (Clin Infect Dis 2019; 68:738).
  • Use of tetracyclines in pediatric patients has historically been limited because of reports of permanent tooth discoloration in children <8 years of age caused by incorporation of the drugs and their colored degradation products in enamel.
    • Doxycycline binds less readily to calcium compared with other members of the tetracycline class, and recent comparative data suggest that doxycycline is not likely to cause visible teeth staining or enamel hypoplasia in children <8 years of age.
    • Doxycycline can safely be used in children age < 8 years for treatment up to 21 days (AAP Red Book 2018, Section 4; J Pediatr 166:1246, 2015).
