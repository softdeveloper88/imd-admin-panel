Intertrigo

Clinical Setting

  • Inflammation of skin folds. Commonly encountered in morbidly obese (BMI >30) patients. Can be non-infected or infected.
  • Major factors in pathogenesis:
    • Friction due to irritation of skin on skin (chaffing)
    • Increased moisture within the folds due to some combination of obesity, sweating, occlusive clothing
    • Impaired host defenses due to any of several factors: e.g., diabetes mellitus, local/systemic glucocorticoids or other immunosuppressive medication, HIV infection induced immunosuppression
  • Intertriginous dermatitis can occur at any site of redundant skin on skin: Inguinal, Axillae, Inframammary, Abdominal (under pannus)
  • Microbial infection of irritated damaged inflamed skin is common.
  • Strongly suggest determining the microbial etiology with KOH preps, culture and sensitivity of skin scrapings, and, if lesions are vesicular, viral studies

Etiologies

  • Candida species (most common)
  • Other potential pathogens include: other fungi, aerobic and anaerobic bacteria, and any of a variety of viruses

Primary Regimens

  • General management: weight loss, control of diabetes, drying agents (corn starch, calcium acetate (Domeboro's solution)
  • If etiology is Candidia species, topical azole antifungal: e.g.: Clotrimazole 1% cream twice daily for several weeks. See Comments
  • If bacterial, topical antibacterial cream or ointment. Vary depending on specific pathogen

Alternative Regimens

  • If severe Candida infection, may need systemic azole therapy: i.e., Fluconazole 150 mg po once weekly. Some add a topical low potency steroid creame or ointment
  • Ciclopirox is a topical antifungal that is active against both common gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria

Comments

  • Some evidence that topical azoles (ketoconazole,  clotrimazole) are anti-inflammatory and have some antibacterial activity (J Am Acad Dermatol. 1991;25:257).
