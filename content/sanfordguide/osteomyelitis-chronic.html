Osteomyelitis, Chronic

Clinical Setting

  • Chronic osteomyelitis usually occurs in adults following trauma or surgery.
  • Implies a long-standing infection and the presence of dead bone.
  • Valid cultures are necessary to guide treatment options; empiric therapy not recommended.

Etiologies

  • Staphylococcus aureus
  • Enterobacter sp.
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Streptococci

Primary Regimens

  • Base systemic therapy on results of culture and sensitivity testing.
  • If acute exacerbation of chronic osteomyelitis, treat as acute hematogenous osteomyelitis.
  • Surgical debridement is critical.
  • Oral and parenteral therapy appear equally effective (see Comments), provided the organism is susceptible and sufficiently high doses are used to achieve effective bone concentrations.
  • Optimal duration of therapy unknown: prolonged course of therapy is typically recommended but 6 weeks probably adequate if surgical debridement is performed.
  • Consider intermittent therapy or chronic suppressive therapy for relapses if surgical debridement unsuccessful or not feasible.

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • Review article on oral therapy: Antibiotics (Basel). 2023; 13:4.
  • Randomized controlled trial found that 6 weeks of oral therapy selected by an infectious disease specialist was non-inferior (7.5% margin) to intravenous therapy (follow-on oral antibiotics were permitted and used in the majority of cases): N Engl J Med. 2019 Jan 31;380(5):425-436.
  • Important therapeutic adjuncts: Removal of orthopedic hardware, surgical debridement, vascularized muscle flaps, distraction osteogenesis (Ilizarov) techniques.
