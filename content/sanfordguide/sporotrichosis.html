Sporotrichosis

Clinical Setting

  • Cutaneous and/or lymphocutaneous disease is most common and follows direct inoculation from trauma while around decaying vegetation/soil. Local disease may progress to osteoarticular infection and rarely disseminated disease. Inhalation of conidia can result in primary pulmonary infection.

Etiologies

  • Sporothrix schenckii

Primary Regimens

  • Cutaneous/Lymphocutaneous
    • Itraconazole 200 mg po daily for 2-4 weeks after all lesions resolved
  • Osteoarticular
    • Itraconazole 200 mg po bid x 12 months
  • Pulmonary
    • Severe disease: Amphotericin B lipid complex 3-5 mg/kg IV qd until response, then Itraconazole 200 mg po bid; total of 12 months
    • Mild to moderate disease: Itraconazole 200 mg po bid x 12 months
  • Disseminated
    • Amphotericin B lipid complex 3-5 mg/kg IV qd x 4-6 weeks, followed by Itraconazole 200 mg po bid for total of 12 months

Alternative Regimens

  • Cutaneous/Lymphocutaneous
    • Itraconazole 200 mg po bid (if poor response to daily dosing)
    • Terbinafine 500 mg po bid
    • SSKI 5 drops (using standard eye dropper) tid and increase to 40-50 drops tid
    • Pregnant and nursing women who have fixed cutaneous sporotrichosis and who cannot safely take any of the previous regimens can be treated with local hyperthermia
  • Osteoarticular
    • Liposomal Amphotericin B 3-5 mg/kg/day IV or Amphotericin B lipid complex 5 mg/kg/day IV or Amphotericin B deoxycholate 0.7-1 mg/kg/day IV daily; if response, change to Itraconazole 200 mg po bid x total 12 months

Comments

  • Document Itraconazole serum levels after 2 weeks of therapy.
  • Life-long suppressive therapy with Itraconazole 200 mg po daily may be required in patients with AIDS and other immunosuppressed patients if immunosuppression cannot be reversed.
  • Localized pulmonary disease: Consider surgical resection in addition to medical therapy.
  • Global guidelines, Lancet Infect Dis 21:e364, 2021 and IDSA guidelines, Clin Infect Dis 45:1255, 2007.
