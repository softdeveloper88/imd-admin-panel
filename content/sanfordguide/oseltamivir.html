Usage and Dosing

  • Oseltamivir is indicated for the treatment of acute, uncomplicated influenza in patients ≥2 weeks old who have been symptomatic for no more than 2 days. It is also indicated for prophylaxis of influenza in patients ≥1 year old.
  • Oseltamivir is also indicated for patients hospitalized for influenza or at very high risk of complications regardless of duration of symptoms.
  • When influenza is circulating, patients suspected of COVID-19 who meed criteria for influenza treatment should be tested for influenza and antiviral therapy should be started while awaiting results
  • Oseltamivir phosphate is extensively converted by hepatic esterases to the active carboxylate metabolite. At least 75% of an oral dose reaches the systemic circulation as the carboxylate. Exposure to Oseltamivir is <5% of the total exposure after oral dosing.
  • Caution: potential for confusion in dosing oral suspension (N Engl J Med 361:1912, 2009).
  • For patients who are severely ill with influenza, consideration was given to use of Oseltamivir at higher doses (150 mg bid). However, in prospective study over 4 influenza seasons, there was no benefit from the higher dose, and hence, the higher dose is no longer recommended. Further study is needed in patients with influenza B where one study showed a trend toward benefit of the higher dose (Clin Infect Dis 57:1511, 2013).
  • High rates of resistance in 2008 H1N1 strains. Since then, only sporadic and treatment emergent resistance seen in H1N1 2009pdm. 
    • Emergence of resistance on treatment more common with critically ill immunocompromised patients.
    • If severely immunocompromised patients remain critically ill with influenza and are still shedding virus despite oseltamivir treatment,  can check for point mutation that confers resistance (H275Y in HIN1 or H274Y in H3N2). Testing by gene sequencing available commercially at ARUP labs, probably other commercial labs, or CDC.
    • Oseltamivir resistant influenza strains with H275Y mutation remain susceptible to Zanamivir.
    • However, other combinations of mutations can induce resistance to both oseltamivir and zanamivir.

Adverse Effects

  • Diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, headache. Nausea reduced with food.
  • Rare: severe skin reactions (toxic epidermal necrolysis, Stevens Johnson syndrome, erythema multiforme).
  • Delirium and abnormal behavior reported (Clin Infect Dis 48:1003, 2009).
  • 75 mg of oral suspension contains 2 gm sorbitol, which is above the daily maximum limit for sorbitol in patients with hereditary fructose intolerance and may cause dyspepsia and diarrhea.

Comments

  • Severe Influenza:
    • In immunocompromised patients, evidence that clinically relevant flu virus replication beyond 5 days. Hence, clinical judgment guides use of Oseltamivir beyond 5 days. Especially true in critically ill who often have concomitant bacterial pneumonia (Infect Dis Clin N Am 27:157, 2013).
    • For use of neuraminidase inhibitors for treatment of severe influenza see editorial: Clin Infect Dis 2017;64: 368
