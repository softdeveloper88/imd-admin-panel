Furunculosis, Recurrent

Clinical Setting

  • Decolonization strategy to prevent recurrent furunculosis (abscesses, boils) from Staphylococcus aureus infection in an otherwise healthy host with no currently active skin infection.
  • Need to culture multiple sites, i.e., the nose, throat and inguinal area skin; nares only culture misses up to half of colonized individuals (Clin Infect Dis 54:1523, 2012).

Etiologies

  • Staphylococcus aureus (MSSA and MRSA)

Primary Regimens

  • A combined regimen of twice daily 0.12% chlorhexidine mouthwash, once daily 4% chlorhexidine bath or shower, and twice daily intranasal 2% mupirocin ointment for 5 days twice per month for 6 months significantly reduced rates of post-discharge MRSA infection in hospitalized patients colonized with MRSA (N Engl J Med. 2019; 380:638-650)

Alternative Regimens

  • 5-day regimen of 4% chlorhexidine body wash + twice daily intranasal 2% mupirocin ointment comparing decolonization of an index Staph. aureus colonized child with skin and soft tissues infection (SSTI) versus whole household decolonization found no significant difference in decolonization rates (Clin Infect Dis 54:743, 2012), but the rate of recurrent SSTIs in the index child and household members was less in household decolonization group.
  • 5-day regimen of bleach baths (tub of warm water with 1/4 cup of 6% sodium hypochlorite (household bleach) for 15 minutes plus intranasal 2% mupriocin ointment twice daily versus a 5-day regimen of twice daily nasal mupriocin plus daily 4% chlorhexidine body wash eradicated S. aureus colonization at 1 month in 63% and 55% of subjects respectively, compared to 38% in untreated controls; recurrent skin infections occurred in 22% of the bleach bath group, 11% of the chlorhexidine group and 26% of the untreated group (Infect Control Hosp Epidemiol 32:872, 2011). 

Comments

  • No "gold standard" regimen: optimal regimen and duration of treatment are uncertain.
  • Rates of recolonization and recurrent infections remain high even after decolonization.
  • See Infect Dis Clin North Am. 2021;35:107-133.
