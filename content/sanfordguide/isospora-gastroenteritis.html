Cystoisospora belli

Clinical Setting

  • Infection of intestinal epithelial cells in both immunocompetent and immunocompromised patients.
  • Most often seen in HIV patients; HIV testing mandatory after stool detection if not known to have HIV.
    • HIV patients may need IV hydration and nutrition due to severe malabsorption
  • Non-bloody chronic crampy diarrhea and malabsorption
  • May cause systemic eosinophilia.

Diagnosis

  • Not seen on routine O & P staining; need AFB stain of stool, PCR (included in most multiplex PCR panels), UV fluorescence microscopy, duodenal aspirates, or intestinal biopsy.  Extraintestinal infection, such as in the biliary tract, lymph nodes, spleen, and liver, has been documented in postmortem examinations of HIV-infected patients.

Etiologies

  • Cystoisospora belli (formerly Isospora belli)

Primary Regimens

  • Immunocompetent:
    • TMP-SMX-DS 1 tab po bid x 7-10 days
  • HIV/AIDS:
    • It may be necessary to increase the daily dose from 2x to 4x daily and/or increase the duration of therapy for up to 3-4 weeks.
    • Consider IV TMP-SMX if severe malabsorption present
    • Secondary prophylaxis with TMP-SMX may be needed

Alternative Regimens

  • Pyrimethamine 50-75 mg/day po (can be divided into 2 doses) for 7-10 days + folinic acid 10-25 mg/day po
  • Ciprofloxacin 500 mg po bid x 7 days (definitely second line alternative; see Comment)
  • Chronic maintenance therapy in AIDS patients (until CD4 >200 X 6 months):
    • TMP-SMX-DS (1 tab po 3x/week or 1 tab po daily)
      • If intolerant: Pyrimethamine 25 mg/day po + folinic acid 5–10 mg/day po
    • Ciprofloxacin 500 mg po 3x/week

Comments

  • IV TMP-SMX if malabsorption or no response after 10 days
  • There are limited data on use of ciprofloxacin for TMP-SMX failure or TMP-SMX allergy. See Ann Intern Med 132:885, 2000.
  • Clinical evidence to support nitazoxanide use is minimal
  • HIV patients with CD4 <200 may not respond to any therapy until adequate ART is instituted.
  • Opportunistic infection in AIDS patients, see DHHS Guidelines.
