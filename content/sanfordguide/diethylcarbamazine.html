Diethylcarbamazine
Tradename: Hetrazan (no longer made)

Usage and Dosing

  • Made available in the United States from the CDC Drug Service through a CDC-sponsored IND protocol. Supplied to CDC by the WHO; Manufactured by E.I.P.I.C.O.
  • May be used for both prophylaxis and treatment of loiasis.
  • CDC IND has very limited clinical criteria allowing release.

Adult Dose

  • See specific parasites/diseases for dosing:
    • Lymphatic filariasis (+Tropical pulmonary eosinophilia)
    • Loiasis
    • Body cavity filariasis, Mansonella streptocerca

Adverse Effects

  • Headache, dizziness, nausea, fever. Host may experience inflammatory reaction to death of adult worms: fever, urticaria, asthma, GI upset (Mazzotti reaction).
  • When diethylcarbamazine is given in standard doses, adverse reactions are related to the interaction of the drug with the parasite (vs. to the drug itself) and depend on the concentration of microfilariae in the blood. The most common side effects are headache, dizziness, nausea, fever, malaise, myalgia, and arthralgia.
  • Patients with lymphatic filariasis or tropical pulmonary eosinophilia may develop tender nodules on their extremities, lymphangitis, and painful scrotal nodules (secondary to death of adult worms).
  • In patients with loiasis who have high levels of microfilaremia, treatment can produce encephalopathy; patients also can develop urticaria, other skin eruptions, pruritus, and Calabar swellings.
  • Contraindicated in patients with onchocerciasis or in whom onchocerciasis can not be ruled out; dying microfilariae in the eye may cause permanent visual loss.
