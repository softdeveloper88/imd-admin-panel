Shock: Septic, Bacteremic, Endotoxin

Clinical Setting

  • Introduction and Definitions
    • Definition of "Sepsis": life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection. Review: N Engl J Med. 2024; 391:2133.
      • "Septic shock" is sepsis accompanied by circulatory and cellular/metabolic abnormalities sufficient to  substantially increase mortality.
        • Organ dysfunction defined by an increase in SOFA (Sequential Organ Failure Assessment) score ≥2 points.
          • See SOFA Score calculator
            • SOFA is measure of organ dysfunction based on assessment of six organ systems: lung, liver, cardiovascular, hematologic, renal, and neurological;
              • Quick SOFA: Three criteria (1 point each) for:
        • Altered mental status
        • Respiratory rate > 22 per minute
        • Systolic blood pressure < 100 mm Hg
        • Score of 2 or 3 consistent with sepsis        
    • Once again: Septic shock is sepsis accompanied by circulatory and cellular/metabolic abnormalities sufficient to  substantially increase mortality.
      • Patients with septic shock have hypotension requiring vasopressors to maintain MAP ≥65 mm Hg and a serum lactate level >2 mmol/L (18 mg/dL) despite adequate fluid volume resuscitation.
      • Pathogen-induced sepsis and septic shock vs Non-pathogen-induced cytokine storm
        • Cytokine storm clinical picture can mimic that of septic shock but no invading pathogen is identified : (Non-bacterial/non-fungal cytokine storm).
          • Stimuli vary and can occur alone or in combination:
            • Immune therapy of cancer: CAR-T cells, Check-point inhibitors.
          • Hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH) COVID-19
          • Ref: N Engl J Med 2020;383:2255; 
  • Critical Care Society Guidelines: Crit Care Med. 2024, 52:e219.
  •  Pathophysiology dictates therapy:
    • Endothelial damage due to inflammatory cytokines in the capillary beds of all organs manifesting as renal insufficiency, ARDS, hypotension, hyperbilirubinemia, thrombocytopenia, and altered mental status (the SOFA score components).
    • Assessments and Treatment based on pathophysiology. 4 major goals of therapy:
      • Fluid resuscitation in response to leaky capillaries
      • Prompt institution of active empiric antimicrobial therapy (see references below)
      • Aggressive diagnostics to determine source of the sepsis: Need Source Control as quickly as possible
      • Vasopressors to  combat peripheral vascular collapse and reduction in peripheral vascular resistance
      • Based on the pathophysiology, sepsis management "bundles" are utilized. See Guideline from Surviving Sepsis Campaign: Crit Care Med 2021;49:1974.
  • Initial Therapy / Management Bundle
    • Collect blood and other pertinent cultures
    • Administer 30 mL/kg of crystalloid fluids within the first 3 hrs (see Comments)
    • Administer first dose of selected empiric antimicrobial regimen within one hour of making a diagnosis of septic shock
    • Document baseline lactate level
    • Initiate pertinent diagnostics to identify source of the infection
    • Initiate vasopressors without delay to achieve a MAP>65 mm Hg
  • Management bundle issues and references:
    • Ann Int Med.2023 ;176: 253
    • JAMA 2023; 329: 535
    • Clin Infect Dis. 2024, 78:505
  • Antibiotic timing:
    • Crit Care Med.2024, 52:1002; Crit Care Med 2024, 52:1138.

Etiologies

  • Aerobic and/or anaerobic gram-negative bacteria.
  • Gram-positive cocci in clusters or chains.
  • Mycobacteria (e.g., Mycobacterium tuberculosis)
  • Viruses (e.g., Ebola; SARS-CoV-2)
  • Fungi (e.g., Candida species)

Primary Regimens

  • Initial management is packaged as a sepsis bundle, ideally completed within 1 hour for  septic shock patients:
    • Measure baseline serum lactate
    • Obtain blood and other pertinent cultures prior to, or concomitant with, administration of antibiotics. Nasal swab for S. aureus PCR (NAAT)
    • Broad spectrum empiric antimicrobial therapy
  • Antimicrobial Regimens will vary depending on: 
    • The clinical setting (e.g., hospital or community),
    • Suspected source (s) of infection (e.g., pneumonia, urinary tract, skin and soft tissue, intra-abdominal infection, central nervous system, unknown source),
    • Patient factors (e.g., immunosuppressive therapy, other immunocompromising conditions, neutropenia),
    • History of recent prior antimicrobial therapy.
    • Local prevalence of multi-drug resistant gram-negative bacilli (MDR GNB)
    • Suggested empiric therapy for selected possible pathogens: 
      • Empiric  therapy choice based on a variable data base, to include;
        • The patient's clinical syndrome: e.g. community-acquired pneumonia
        • Immune status and  other risk factors/exposures
        • Variable micro data: gram-stain of pertinent body fluid, results of molecular diagnostics.
          • May have detected a pathogen by culture but  in vitro susceptibility results NOT YET available
      • For empiric therapy based on clinical syndrome, see  Sepsis, Adult, Non-neutropenic
    • Empiric therapy suggestions by potential pathogen:
      • If suspect Methicillin-resistant Staph. aureus (MRSA):
        • Vancomycin 25 mg/kg loading dose then 15-20 mg/kg q8-12h IV to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC calculator); alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL
      • For possible extended-spectrum beta-lactamase (ESBL) producing GNB:
        • A carbapenem: Ertapenem 1 gm q24h IV (NOTE: Ertapenem is not active vs.  P. aeruginosa
        • Could start: 
          • Meropenem 2 gm q8h IV or Imipenem-cilastatin 500 mg q6h
        • Ceftolozane-tazobactam 1.5 gm IV q8h
      • If high risk of carbapenemase producing GNB: e.g. Klebsiella producing carbapenemase, treatment options include:
        • Ceftazidime-avibactam 2.5 gm IV q8h, Meropenem-vaborbactam 4 gm IV over 3 hrs q8h, or Imipenem-cilastatin-relebactam 1.25 gm IV over 30 minutes q6h or
        • Plazomicin 15 mg/kg IV  q24h
        • A polymyxin (Polymyxin B for non-urinary source, Colistin for urinary tract source)
      • For suspected Pseudomonas aeruginosa:
        • Ceftazidime 2 gm q8h IV
        • Meropenem 2 gm q8h IV
        • Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h (see drug page for comment on loading dose)
        • Ceftolozane-tazobactam 1.5 gm IV q8h
        • Remember Ertapenem is not active vs. Ps. aeruginosa
      • If polymicrobial mixed aerobic-anaerobic infection:
        • (Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q24h + Metronidazole 500 mg IV q 8h)
        • Ertapenem 1 gm q24h IV or Meropenem 2 gm q8h IV
      • Unknown source:  (Vancomycin + Piperacillin-tazobactam) or (vancomycin + (Meropenem or Imipenem-cilastatin)
    • Crystalloid Fluids : Administer an IV bolus of crystalloid 30 mL/kg over 3 hrs. Does composition of the crystalloid matter?  See Comments
  • Subsequent management within first 6 hours:
    • Repeat measurement of serum lactate, if initial value > 2 mmol/L, as indication of changes in perfusion of tissue
    • If still hypotensive after  fluid resuscitation,  administer nor-epinephrine  to maintain mean arterial pressure (MAP) > 65 mm Hg
    • Can also reassess volume status and tissue perfusion by measurement of central venous O2 saturation
  • Within 24 hours:
    • Goal: source identified and  control achieved
    • Adjunctive corticosteroids, if needed.
      • For patients in septic shock unresponsive to volume resuscitation and requiring on-going vasopressor therapy to maintain systolic BP > 90 mm Hg or MAP > 65 mm Hg consider hydrocortisone 50 mg IV q6h + fludrocortisone 50 mcg once daily via NG tube (Lancet Respir Med. 2024; 12; 366)
      • Review: JAMA. 2024; 332: 318
      •  Society of Critical Care Medicine Guideline. Crit Care Med. 2024; 52: e219
    • Insulin to control blood glucose: Target of 140-180 mg/dL.

Alternative Regimens

  • Initial combination antibacterial therapy
    • Debate continues as to the pros and cons of monotherapy vs. combination therapy: Critical Care Medicine 2017; 45:1930 & 1933;  J Antimicrob Chemother 2017;72:3443.
    • Combination therapy may be used empirically due to high risk/prevalence of MDR GNB with hope that at least one of the drugs ordered would be active
    • If pathogen identified, Data most favorable for directed combination therapy in neutropenic patients  and/or patients suffering P. aeruginosa bacteremia
      •  Data to support this recommendation are generally weak and of low quality

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • De-escalate and narrow coverage once a pathogen(s) has been identified and in vitro susceptibility determined
  • Duration of therapy: 7-10 day course of antibiotics is adequate to treat most infections associated with sepsis and septic shock
    • In patients with uncomplicated Gm-Neg bacteremia (mostly pyelonephritis), 7 days of therapy was non-inferior to 14 days: Clin Infect. Dis. 2019; 69:1091
  • Can customize duration of therapy using Sequential measurements of procalcitonin
  • Discontinue antibiotics within 3-5 days if no infection is identified and patient is clinically stable and improving

Comments

  • Vitamin C:
    • In a retrospective before-after clinical study of patients with sepsis and septic shock, the mortality in patients with "standard care" was 40.4% but fell to  8.5 % in those who also received high dose vitamin C, Thiamine , and Hydrocortisone (Chest 2017;151:1229)
    • In a subsequent prospective randomized open label study, found no difference in time alive, time to cessation of vasopressors and 90 day mortality (JAMA 2020; 323: 423)
    • In another randomized, blinded, multicenter placebo controlled trial, there was no reduction in Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) score during the first 72 hrs after enrollment (JAMA 2020; 324: 642)
  • Crystalloid Fluid Composition:
    • Results of trial comparing balanced salt solution( Ringers Lactate or Plasma-Lyte)  to normal saline  favored the balanced salt solutions  (N Engl J Medicine 2018;378:829  & 862 [editorial]). A more recent randomized trial found no difference in 90 day mortality between normal saline and  a balanced solution (Plasma-lyte 148): JAMA 2021; 326: 818.
  • IVIG: no clear evidence of benefit and not recommended: Intensive Care Med 2017 Mar;43(3):304-377; Crit Care. 2017 Jul 13;21(1):18; Infect Dis Clin North Am. 2017 Sep;31(3):497-511. 
  • Empiric anti-Candida therapy did not improve survival in ICU septic non-neutropenic patients colonized with Candida species (JAMA 2016;316:1549; JAMA 316:1555, 2016).
