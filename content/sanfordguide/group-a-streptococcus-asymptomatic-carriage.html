Pharyngitis, Group A Strept, Asymptomatic Carriage

Clinical Setting

  • Asymptomatic presence (carriage) of Group A Streptococcus (GAS) in the pharynx.
    • Asymptomatic household contacts of patients with GAS pharyngitis.
      • Carriers are at low risk of suppurative complications or subsequent acute rheumatic fever
    • Persons with a positive culture or antigen test or NAAT for S. pyogenes and a clinical picture of a viral pharyngitis.
      • If S. pyogenes superinfection, the antistreptolysin O (ASO) antibody titer should increase
      • The more sensitive Streptozme test can detect presence of ASO plus 4 additional S. pyogenes antibodies
  • Background:
    • During winter and spring, up to 20% of asymptomatic children may be carriers of S. pyogenes and carriage can persist for months
    • S. pyogenes may colonize the throat of patients with active viral pharyngitis without evidence of a host immunologic response

Etiologies

  • Streptococcus pyogenes (Group A)

Primary Regimens

  • In general, no indication to treat asymptomatic carriers
  • The rare indications for attempting to eradicate asymptomatic carriage are listed in Comments and the therapy is under Alternative Regimens

Alternative Regimens

  • If treatment of chronic carrier is indicated (see Comments):
    • Wt < 27 kg: 600,000 units
    • Wt > 27 kg: 1,200,000 units
    • Clindamycin 20-30 mg/kg/d in 3 divided doses (max dose 300 mg) po x 10 days
    • Penicillin VK 50 mg/kg/d in 4 divided doses (max dose 500 mg) x 10 days + Rifampin 20 mg/kg (max dose 600 mg) q24h po for last 4 days of treatment
    • Benzathine Penicillin G single dose (below) IM + Rifampin 20 mg/kg/day in 2 doses (max dose 600 mg/day) po x 4 days

Comments

  • In one study, 12% or higher carriage rate among l children without signs or symptoms of pharyngitis (Pediatrics 126:e557, 2010).
  • Situations in which eradication of carriage may be desirable (IDSA Guidelines, Clin Infect Dis 55:e86, 2012):
    • During a community outbreak of acute rheumatic fever, acute poststreptococcal glomerulonephritis, or invasive GAS infection
    • During an outbreak of GAS pharyngitis in a closed, or partially closed, community;
    • Family or personal history of acute rheumatic fever
    • In a family with excessive anxiety about GAS infections
    • Tonsillectomy is being considered only because of detected carriage
