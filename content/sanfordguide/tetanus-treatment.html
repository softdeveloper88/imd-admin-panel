Tetanus, Treatment

Clinical Setting

  • Spasm of musculature caused by tetanospasmin toxin of the anaerobic bacteria Clostridium tetani.
  • Primary tetanus infection, a clinical diagnosis (cultures often are negative):
    • Generalized and persistent, tonic muscle spasms and paroxysms (risus sardonicus, trismus, lockjaw).
    • Neonatal: generalized weakness, failure to nurse, progression to muscle spasms. May result from rubbing spore-containing dirt on the umbilical stump.
    • 6 treatment steps are outlined below.

Etiologies

  • Clostridium tetani (tetanospasmin toxin)

Primary Regimens

  • 6 Steps:
  1. Urgent endotracheal intubation to protect the airway. Laryngeal spasm is common. Early tracheostomy.
  2. Eliminate reflex spasms with diazepam, 20 mg/kg/day IV or midazolam. Reports of benefit combining diazepam with magnesium sulfate (Ln 368:1436, 2006). Worst cases: need neuromuscular blockade with vecuronium.
  3. Neutralize toxin: Human hyperimmune globulin IM; start tetanus toxoid immunization–no immunity from clinical tetanus.
  4. Surgically debride infected source tissue. Start antibiotic: Metronidazole 500 mg IV q6h or 1000 mg IV q12h OR aqueous Penicillin G 3 million units IV q4h. Treat for 7-10 days (see Comment).
  5. Avoid light as may precipitate muscle spasms.
  6. Use beta blockers, e.g., short acting esmolol, to control sympathetic hyperactivity.

Alternative Regimens

  • Doxycycline 100 mg IV q12h x 7-10 days

Comments

  • Reference: Ann Intern Med 154: 329, 2011 (recommends Metronidazole for 10-14 days); Lancet 393: 1657, 2019.
