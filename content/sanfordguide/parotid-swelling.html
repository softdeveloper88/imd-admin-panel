Parotid Swelling, Parotitis

Clinical Setting

  • Parotitis (swelling of the parotid gland) is caused by a variety of bacteria and viruses. Acute parotitis is most commonly due to Staph. aureus and mixed oral aerobes and/or anaerobic bacteria.
    • The distribution of potential microbial pathogens varies:
      • Community acquired and immunocompetent differs from hospital acquired and/or immunocompromised
      • S. aureus maybe MSSA or MRSA
        • Suggest adding NAAT for S. aureus to culture of exudate from Stensen's
  • Presents as either "Hot" tender or "Cold" non-tender parotid swelling:
    • Predisposing factors: stone(s) in Stensen’s duct, dehydration, poor oral hygiene, postoperative state
    • Therapy depends on identification of specific etiologic organism.
    • Hot/tender
    • Cold/non-tender
      • History/lab results may narrow differential.
  • Many serious potential complications:
    • Extension into parapharyngeal space
    • Lemierre's syndrome
    • Parotid abscess
    • Contiguous osteomyelitis of the mandible
    • Bell's palsy

Etiologies

  • Hot/tender:
    • Staph. aureus: MSSA or MRSA (suggest culture and oral swab for NAAT)
    • Strep. pyogenes
    • Aerobic and anaerobic oral flora. Anaerobes include peptostreptococci and Fusobacterium necrophorum
    • Aerobic gram-negative bacilli (Enterobacterales and P. aeruginosa) more likely in hospital-acquired and/or immunocompromised patients
    • Viral
      • Enteroviruses, Epstein-Barr virus, Mumps, HHV-6B, parainfluenza virus, EBV, CMV and rarely others (J Infect Dis 208:1979, 2013).
      • 15% positive for influenza A(H3N2) in 2014/15 influenza season in UK (Eur Surveill 20:21203, 2015).
      • COVID-19 associated parotitis reported: Amer J Emerg Med 2021; 39:254.e3
  • Cold/non-tender:
    • Granulomatous disease (e.g., mycobacteria, fungi, sarcoidosis, Sjögren’s syndrome)
    • Drugs (iodides, et al.)
    • Diabetes
    • Cirrhosis
    • Tumors

Primary Regimens

  • Remember to get aerobic and anaerobic cultures
  • Hot/tender, empirical therapy
    • If MRSA is not a concern (negative NAAT) and the patient is immunocompetent:
      • Ampicillin-sulbactam 3 gm IV q6h
      • Ceftriaxone 1 gm IV q24h + (Metronidazole 500 mg IV q8h or Clindamycin 600 mg IV q6-8h)
    • If MRSA is a concern (positive NAAT)
      • Add Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL) to one of the above regimens
    • If patient is immunocompromised: Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL) +
    • Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h
  • Cold/non-tender:
    • Due to diverse possibilities need biopsy for culture for mycobacteria and fungi plus histology.
    • No indication for empiric therapy

Alternative Regimens

  • Hot/tender, empirical therapy:
    • If immunocompromised patient: Linezolid 600 mg IV/po q12h + Meropenem 1 gm IV q8h
    • Oral therapies, if appropriate
      • Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 m po bid
      • Moxifloxacin 400 mg po q24h
      • If MRSA is a concern add Linezolid 600 mg po bid to either of the above. Daptomycin 10 mg/kg once daily IV is another alternative

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Duration of therapy varies depending on clinical response; usually 10-14 days
