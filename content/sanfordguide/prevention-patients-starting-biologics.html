Prevention: Persons Starting Biologics

Clinical Setting

  • The development of different biologic agents has been a great advance in the treatment of many different autoimmune diseases and malignancies. These agents act by inhibiting components of the normal immune response and therefore can increase risk for different types of infection.
  • To minimize risk of biologic agent-associated infection, screening for latent or subclinical infection prior to use should be considered. When infection is identified, treatment of infection prior to start of biologic or avoidance of using that biologic may be indicated.

Comments

  • Tuberculosis (TB): Increased risk of reactivation of latent TB most clearly described following use of TNF blockers (N Engl J Med, 345;1098, 2001) however, other biologics may also increase reactivation risk (as listed above).  Latent TB screening should include checking a tuberculin skin test (TST) and/or interferon gamma release assay (IGRA), chest x-ray, and detailed TB exposure  history.  Patients with evidence of latent TB infection by TST/IGRA or with negative or indeterminate result but other concerning findings (exposure history, chest x-ray suggestive of prior TB infection) should be treated for latent TB as well.  See treatment of Latent TB infection.
  • Hepatitis B (HBV): Risk of HBV reactivation has been reported with many different immunosupressive agents.  Among the biologics listed above, Rituximab use has been most closely correlated with HBV reactivation (J Clin Oncol 27:605, 2009) although TNF blockers (Clin Rheumatol 3:931, 2012) have been implicated as well.  Screening for HBV infection via serum HBsAg, HBsAb (IgG), HBcAb (IgG) should be performed prior to use of any of these biologics.  Reactivation has been seen in patients who are HBsAg negative and HBcAb positive (+/- HBsAb positive). Prevention of HBV reactivation with anti-virals versus HBV DNA monitoring should be decided based on serologic results, HBV DNA level, and biologic being used.  In patients on Rituximab or Alemutuzamab, prophylaxis should be continued for at least 12 months after stopping therapy.  Patients taking biologics with shorter half-lives (TNF blockers), continuation for at least 6 months after stopping therapy may be reasonable.
  • JC virus: Risk of progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML) has been correlated with Natalizumab use.  Risk factors include serum JC virus IgG positivity and prior immunosuppression (N Engl J Med, 366:1870, 2012).  Prior to treatment, screening for  JC virus infection via serology IgG is recommended.  Cases have also been reported in patients on Rituximab (J Clin Virol, 48;291, 2010) and Ofatumumab (J Clin Oncol, 28;1749, 2010) although JC virus serologic screening is not routinely recommended, risk/benefits should be considered on case-by-case basis.
  • Endemic mycoses: Screening for active or recent histoplasmosis and coccidioidomycosis infection be accomplished by taking an exposure and symptom history, physical exam, +/- chest x-ray.  Serologic screening for these infections is not routinely recommended for histoplasmosis (Clin Infect Dis. 50;85, 2010) although many experts in coccidioides endemic regions do screen with serology prior and during biologic therapy (Arthritis Care Res (Hoboken) 64;1903, 2012) although new infection (not reactivation) seems to be the main mechanism for coccidioides infection in patients on TNF blockers (Arthritis Rheum, 50; 1959, 2004).  In patients with active or recent infection, resolution of infection is recommended before starting biologic therapy.
  • HSV/VZV: See recommendation on prevention of HSV/VZV infection.
  • CMV: Either a prophylactic or pre-emptive approach to prevent CMV infection is recommended for patients on Alemtuzumab.  See CMV prophylaxis section.
  • PCP: Prophylaxis against PCP is recommended for patients on Alemtuzumab.  See recommendations on prevention of PCP infection.
