Pharyngitis, Viral

Clinical Setting

  • Viral pharyngitis:can be vesicular, diffuse erythema or ulcerative.
  • Suspect viral etiology if concurrent conjunctivitis, coryza, cough, skin rash, or hoarseness.
  • Small vesicles on posterior pharynx, with or without lesions on palms and soles suggests enterovirus (Hand, foot, and mouth disease).
  • Posterior cervical adenopathy, splenomegaly suggest Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV).
  • Vesicles on tip of the tongue or along gum line suggest Herpes simplex.
  • Diffuse erythema and associated conjunctivitis suggests possible adenovirus
  • For distinction from S. pyogenes, see streptococcal pharyngitis
    • For other entities that can mimic streptococcal pharygitis see: J Emerg Med 2018;54: 619

Etiologies

  • Enteroviruses, in particular
    • Coxsackie A9, B1-5
    • ECHO (multiple types)
    • Enterovirus 71
  • Epstein-Barr virus (EBV)
  • Herpes simplex Types 1, 2 (HSV)

Primary Regimens

  • Antibacterial agents are not indicated
  • If H. simplex Types 1, 2 identified/suspected:
    • Acyclovir 400 mg tid po x 10 days
    • Famciclovir 250 mg po tid x 7-10 days
    • Valacyclovir 1000 mg po bid x 7-10 days

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Antibiotics should not be given for viral pharyngitis.
  • Treating pharyngitis with antibiotics in absence of a positive rapid strep test or positive culture is a major cause of antibiotic overuse. Recall that a positive strep test with a clinical syndrome of viral pharyngitis represents colonization by strep and does not require antibiotic therapy.

Comments
