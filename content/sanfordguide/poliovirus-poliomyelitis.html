Poliovirus, Poliomyelitis

Clinical Setting

  • Polio or poliomyelitis is a disabling and life-threatening viral disease which can infect the anterior horn cells of the spinal cord and cause paralysis. It is highly contagious and spread by person-to person contact. More common in infants and young children, especially in situations of poor hygiene.
  • Viral transmission occurs through the mouth and multiplies in the oropharynx and GI tract. It is usually present in nasal secretions for 1-2 weeks and can be shed in stools for longer.
  • Usually asymptomatic. 1 in 4 have flu-like symptoms lasting 2-5 days. Fewer than 1% have weakness or paralysis in the arms, legs or both.
  • The incubation period for nonparalytic symptoms is 3-6 days. Onset of paralysis usually occurs 7-21 days after infection.
  • Probable case definition & reporting:
    • Acute onset of flaccid paralysis of one or more limbs with decreased or absent tendon reflexes in the affected limbs, without other apparent cause, and without sensory or cognitive loss
      • Paralytic polio is classified as immediately notifiable, extremely urgent, within 4 hours
      • Nonparalytic polio is classified as immediately notifiable, urgent, within 24 hours
      • Contact CDC emergency operations at 770-488-7100 for consultation
  • There is a lenghy differential diagnosis of acute flaccid myelitis, to include:
    • Other enteroviruses: D68, A71, Coxsackie A, Parecho
    • More mimics: Guillain-Barre, Myasthenia gravis, Eaton-Lambert myasthenia syndrome, Botulism, Tick Paralysis

Diagnosis

  • Whole stool and throat specimens (OP swab) for isolation in cell cultures. Contact State and local health department to order testing.
  • Can try to detect in CSF using PCR if available and/or isolation in cell culture

Etiologies

  • Enterovirus, family Picornaviridae (3 natural poliovirus serotypes: WPV1, WPV2, WPV3)
    • only WPV1 still exists (30 cases/yr in 5 countries predominantly Pakistan, Afghanistan)
    • Eradication threatened by widespread incidence (1000 cases/yr) of cVDPVs (circulating vaccine-derived polio viruses) in over 50 countries.  Reports of increased cVDPV in wastewater in Gaza during the 2024 war.
    • A single clinical case likely represents 100 community infections and risk exists only for unvaccinated or undervaccinated persons in communities with low vaccination coverage.

Primary Regimens

  • Supportive Care
  • No FDA approved antiviral therapy (see Comment re development pipeline)

Alternative Regimens

  • IVIG. No clear evidence of benefit

Prevention

  • See Polio, Vaccine for indications, available products, dosing, and vaccine characteristics for pre-exposure prevention.
    • Only certain vaccine types accepted by CDC in immigrants and refugees as equivalent to doses administered in the US.

Comments

  • Capsid inhibitors under development:
    • Pocapavir. Only for polio virus infection in B cell deficient patients. Pocapavir is an orally bioavailable inhibitor of poliovirus and some other enteroviruses that is under investigation for the treatment of vaccine-derived and wild type polio (J Infect Dis 215:335, 2017)
  • Case report of polio in an unvaccinated person caused by vaccine-derived poliovirus type 2 in New York State in July 2022; detection of virus widely in wastewater in several counties indicates wider community infection
  • No reported cases in the U.S. since 1979; the last imported case in the U.S. was in 1993.
  • A similar type of acute flaccid myelitis (AFM) can occur after infection with enterovirus D68. All cases of AFM should be reported to local public health or CDC
