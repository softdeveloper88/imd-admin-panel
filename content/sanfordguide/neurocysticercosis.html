Neurocysticercosis

Clinical Setting

  • Larval form of Taenia solium (pork tapeworm).
  • Most common cause of seizures in adults from endemic areas, especially Latin America
  • Transmitted by eggs from tapeworm carrier not by pork ingestion.
  • Several different clinical forms requiring different therapeutic approaches
    • Parenchymal neurocysticercosis (NCC):
      • Viable cysts by CT/MRI: Treatment associated with cyst resolution, fewer seizures, and decreased seizure recurrence. 
      • Degenerating cysts
      • Dead calcified cysts (no antiparasitic treatment indicated) but may cause ongoing and difficult to control seizures
    • Subarachnoid NCC
    • Intraventricular NCC
    • Spinal and ocular cystercerci (uncommon but must be looked for if cysts found elsewhere).
  • Fundoscopic examination by non-opthlamologist prior to drug therapy to rule out intraocular cysticerci.
    • Drug induced death may cause inflammatory visual loss. Visualization of the fundus with nothing visible to a non-ophthalmologist rules out a cysticercus.
  • Anti-parasitic drugs are never urgent; control seizures and intracranial pressure first.
    • Levetiracetam better tolerated and preferable to phenytoin or carbamazepine
  • Guidelines based on limited numbers of randomized controlled trials. 2017 ASTMH/IDSA Diagnosis and Treatment Guidelines (Clin Infect Dis 2018;66:1159) and 2021 WHO Guidelines 
  • Recent review: Curr Opin Infect Dis2024 Oct 1;37(5):313.

Diagnosis

  • Both CT and MRI should be performed.
    • Parenchymal scolex on imaging is diagnostic, serology not necessary. 
  • Enzyme-linked immunoelectrotransfer blot (EITB) preferred; ELISA lacks sensitivity and specificity. 
    • EITB performed at CDC validated in peer-review publication.  
    • Serology insensitive for single lesions, calcified lesions.  For multiple parenchymal lesions, subarachnoid NCC, intraventricular NCC, serology almost 100% sensitive.
    • CSF testing no advantage over serum testing.  
    • Point of care rapid test for antibody under development  Lancet Infect Dis 2023 Aug 31:S1473.
  • In non-endemic areas, check stools of household contacts of confirmed cases for tapeworm eggs.
  • Antigen testing and PCR in development for follow-up in subarachnoid disease.
  • If severe disease likely to need prolonged steroids, screen for TB and strongyloides prior to therapy.

Etiologies

  • Taenia solium

Primary Regimens

  • Parenchymal neurocysticercosis (NCC) (1-20 viable and/or degenerating cysts):
    • [Albendazole 15 mg/kg per day (max. 1200 mg/day) + Praziquantel 50 mg/kg per day + Dexamethasone 0.1 mg/kg/day (or prednisone 1 mg/kg/day) beginning 1 day prior to anti parasitic drugs] x 10 days ± Anti-seizure medication x 2 year minimum if needed (Lancet Infect Dis 14:687, 2014)
      • Slow steroid taper after 10 days increasing back if seizures develop.
      • Retreat after 6 months if any viable cysts remain.
      • Albendazole alone as the anti-parasitic component of the regimen (1200 mg/day max) adequate if only 1-2 cysts on an MRI.
      • Anti-parasitics for >30 days if large number of parenchymal lesions.
      • Anti-seizure meds may be stopped after 6 mos with single lesion and 2 yrs with multiple lesions if seizures controlled.
    • Dead, calcified cysts only: No antiparasitic treatment.
      • If seizures, anti-seizure medication x 1 year minimum. (PLoS Negl Trop Dis. 2021;15(3):e0009193)
    • Massive parenchymal NCC (rare) with hundreds to thousands of inflamed or non-inflamed cysticerci.
      • Steroids only (dexamethasone 0.2-0.4 mg/kg/day), antiparasitics contraindicated in most cases; seek expert advice (PLoS Negl Trop Dis 15(11): e0009883)
  • Intraventricular NCC: Neuroendoscopic removal
  • For subarachnoid (racemose): Albendazole 15 mg/kg per day (max. 1200 mg/day) + Dexamethasone (doses as above with very slow taper) + v-p shunt prior to albendazole therapy.
    • 30 day course, may need to repeat multiple times or give continuously for months according to clinical and MRI evolution (Curr Opin Infect Dis 33; 339, 2020)
    • Continue until radiologic resolution and resoluton of antigen levels (if available)(Am J Trop Med Hyg. 102:78, 2020)
    • Intracranial pressure must be monitored and managed by an experienced clinician. If diffuse cerebral edema or raised ICP, control with steroids or shunting prior to any anti-parasitic therapy.
  • T. solium intestinal tapeworm, if present:
    • Praziquantel 5-10 mg/kg po x 1 dose after steroids initiated.

Alternative Regimens

  • Parenchymal NCC: Albendazole + Dexamethasone (doses as above) without Praziquantel.  
  • Limited data indicates that increasing dexamethasone to 8 mg/kg/day X 28 days with 2-week taper decreases seizures but prolongs steroid exposure (Epilepsia 55:1452, 2014)
  • Intraventricular NCC: Albendazole + Dexamethasone (as above); place v-p shunt prior to therapy when no access to neuroendoscopy.
  • Subarachnoid:  Long courses of combination therapy with Albendazole/praziquantel combination therapy for refractory disease.

Comments

  • Dramatic advantage of combination albendazole + praziquantel therapy over mono therapy for parenchymal disease in RCT (Lancet Infect Dis 14:687, 2014; Clin Infect Dis 62:1375, 2016)
  •  ~38% of parenchymal cysts that resolve after antiparasitic treatment calcify by 1 year resulting in increased risk of residual seizure activity.  Regimens to increase steroid dose or duration under investigation (Clin Infect Dis 2021,73; e2592).
  • First control seizures with anti-epileptics.  Anti-seizure meds must be managed case by case but in patients with only a single enhancing lesion  whose cysts resolved after antiparasitic therapy  can have seizure meds withdrawn in 6-12 months,
  • Symptoms may transiently worsen with initiation of antihelminthic agents. 
    • If multiple highly inflammed edematous parenchymal lesions, control symptoms with weeks to months of steroids and anti-epileptics prior to anti-parasitic agents.
  • If prolonged albendazole used, follow CBC and LFTs every 2 weeks
  • For subarachnoid disease some experts increase Albendazole to 15 mg/day up to 1200 mg/day limit off-label.  No trial data.
  • Praziquantel less cysticidal activity than albendazole. Steroids decrease serum levels of Praziquantel.
  • Methotrexate at ≤20 mg/wk may allow a reduction in steroid use if long-term therapy needed such as with subarachnoid NCC (Clin Infect Dis 44:549, 2007).
  • Cysticerci may sometimes be found in muscle and subcutaneous tissue without CNS involvement; no treatment necessary if no tapeworm present.
  • Albendazole 200 mg tablets available from Expert Compounding Pharmacy (see Antiparasitic Drugs, Sources).
    • Branded Albendazole (Albenza) marketed by Amneal is very costly and is difficult for retail pharmacies to access.
