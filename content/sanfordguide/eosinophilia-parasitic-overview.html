Eosinophilia, Parasitic, Overview

Overview

  • Parasitic eosinophilia is a hematologic reaction to tissue invasive stages of helminth (worm) infection.
  • Two protozoans, Cystoisospora and Sarcocystis, rarely cause a mild eosinophilia in immunocompetent patients.
  • Helminth infections that cause eosinophilia, in travelers, or otherwise are listed below.
  • Review: Immunol Allergy Clin No Amer 2015 Aug;35(3):493

Differential Diagnosis

  • In HIV/AIDS patients, eosinophilia may be caused by:
    • Ectoparasite infection: Norwegian scabies
    • Fungal infection: Coccidioidomycosis
    • Malignancy: Hodgkins disease
    • Parasites: round worms, strongyloides
    • Skin disease: HIV-associated eosinophilic folliculitis

Specific Infections

  • Frequent and Often Intense (>5,000 eos/μL):
    • Strongyloides (eosinophilia absent in compromised hosts)
    • Lymphatic filariasis (especially tropical pulmonary eosinophilia)
    • Toxocara (visceral larva migrans)
    • Trichinella (ingestion of uncooked game meat)
  • Moderate During Larval Migration; Absent or Mild During Chronic Infections:
    • Ascaris (visible large round worms in stool or coughed up)
    • Hookworm
    • Fasciola (right upper quadrant pain)
    • Clonorchis
    • Paragonimus (rusty sputum mistaken for TB)
    • Opisthorchis
  • Occurs at Various Stages of infection:
    • Schistosomiasis
      • Acute disease (Katayama fever) in travelers 4-6 weeks post freshwater exposure mimics other undifferentiated tropical fevers but no eosinophilia seen in malaria, typhoid, arboviral infection, amebiasis or rickettsial disease
    • Cysticercosis (seizures or raised intracranial pressure)
    • Echinococcus (especially if cyst rupture occurs)
    • Trichuris
    • Angiostrongylus
    • Onchocerca
    • Loa loa
    • Gnathostoma
    • Capillaria
    • Fasciolopsis
    • Trichostrongylus
    • Baylisascaris
    • Other human and non-human filarias (dirofilaria, mansonella)
    • Anisakis
