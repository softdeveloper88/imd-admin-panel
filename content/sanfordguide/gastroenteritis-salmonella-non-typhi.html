Gastroenteritis, Salmonella: Non-typhi

Clinical Setting

  • Non-typhoidal, Salmonella gastroenteritis.
  • Food or water borne.
  • Fever in 71–91%, history of bloody stools in 34%.
  • Diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, fever, abdomenal cramping, constitutional symptoms. Clinical presentation cannot be distinguished from other etiologies of gastroenteritis.
  • Fewer than 5% have bacteremia or invasive disease.  

Etiologies

  • Salmonella sp., non-typhi
    • Salmonella Enteritidis
    • Salmonella Newport
    • Salmonella Typhimurium

Primary Regimens

  • Use drugs with good intracellular penetration: e.g., TMP/SMX, fluoroquinolones, 3rd generation cephalosporins
    • Do not use aminoglycosides as they do not penetrate macrophages
  • Primary treatment is fluid and electrolyte replacement, not antibiotics.
  • If infection NOT acquired after international travel:
    • Ciprofloxacin 500 mg po bid or Levofloxacin 500 mg po once daily
    • Treat for 7-10 days (14 days if immunocompromised)
  • If infection acquired after international travel:
    • Azithromycin 500 mg po once daily or Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q24h
    • Treat for 7 days (14 days if immunocompromised)

Alternative Regimens

  • Azithromycin 500 mg po once daily x 7 days (14 days if immunocompromised)
  • TMP-SMX 1 DS po bid. Duration is unclear, but 10-14 days is reasonable. See Comment re TMP-SMX resistance.
  • Cefotaxime 2 gm IV q8h (where available outside the US)
  • Meropenem  1-2 gm IV q8h (or other carbapenem such as imipenem or ertapenem)

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Treatment should be guided by local susceptibility patterns and direct antimicrobial testing.
  • If diagnosis obtained by culture independent diagnostic testing, follow-up reflex culture should be performed to test antimicrobial susceptibility. 
  • Disease is self-limited in immunocompetent host and treatment not generally recommended except in severe disease.
  • Treatment also indicated if patient presents with any of these factors:
    • Age <1 yr or >50 yrs
    • Immunocompromised (HIV, transplant, steroids, cancer, cirrhosis, lymphoproliferative)
    • Hemodialysis
    • Vascular aneurysms or grafts or prosthetic joints
    • Bacteremia
    • Hemoglobinopathy
    • Hospitalized with fever and severe diarrhea (9-10 episodes/day).

Comments

  • Antibiotic therapy may prolong fecal excretion of Salmonella after acute salmonellosis.
  • Current surveillance results as monitored by CDC National Antimicrobial Resistance System (NARMS)
  • Resistance:
    • CLSI interpretive breakpoints for susceptibility to Ciprofloxacin: susceptible strains, MIC < 0.06 μg/mL (Clin Infect Dis 55:1107, 2012).
    • Increased resistance to TMP-SMX and Chloramphenicol in some geographic locations.  
    • CDC MDR Salmonella Newport travel alert and outbreak (MMWR 68:713 2019) in Mexico (most Ceftriaxone sensitive, generally resistant to azithromycin, ciprofloxacin, TMP-SMX)
    • Ceftriaxone and fluoroquinolone resistance reported in Asia (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 53:2696, 2009 and Am J Trop Med Hyg 88:661, 2013): meropenem an option for strains resistant to both ceftriaxone and fluoroquinolones.
  • Comprehensive review of invasive non-typhoidal Salmonella infections: Clin Infect Dis 2022; 75:732.
