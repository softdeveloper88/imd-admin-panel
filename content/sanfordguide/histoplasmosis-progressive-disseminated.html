Histoplasmosis, Disseminated

Clinical Setting

  • Disseminated histoplasmosis most often occurs in immunocompromised patients. Infection commonly disseminates to the skin and gastrointestinal tract, but can occur anywhere. Antigen testing of the serum and urine has a high sensitivity, particularly in immunocompromised patients.

Etiologies

  • Histoplasma capsulatum

Primary Regimens

  • Moderately severe to severe disease:
    • Liposomal Amphotericin B 3 mg/kg/day x 2 weeks followed by Itraconazole 200 mg tid x 3 days, then 200 mg bid for at least 12 months
      • A RCT of patients living with HIV/AIDS and disseminated histoplasmosis showed similar outcomes if induction included a single dose 10 mg/kg of Liposomal Amphotericin B OR 10 mg/kg of Liposomal Amphotericin B on D1, and 5 mg/kg on D3; OR 3 mg/kg of Liposomal Amphotericin B daily for 2 weeks (Clin Infect Dis 2023)
  • Mild to moderate disease:
    • Itraconazole (dosing and duration as above)

Alternative Regimens

  • Amphotericin B 0.7-1 mg/kg IV daily
  • Posaconazole (NOTE: different dosing delayed release tabs/IV vs. suspension, better levels achieved with delayed-release tabs)
    • Delayed-release tabs 300 mg po bid x 2 doses and then 300 mg po daily
    • Suspension 200 mg QID, then 400 mg po bid after stabilization of disease
    • Posaconazole IV 300 mg IV over 90 minutes bid x 1 day, then 300 mg IV daily
  • Voriconazole 6 mg/kg po bid x 2 doses, then 4 mg/kg po bid
  • Isavuconazonium sulfate (prodrug of Isavuconazole) 372 mg po/IV q8h x 6 doses and then 372 mg po/IV daily

Comments

  • Use of Itraconazole solution enables higher serum levels than capsules and is the preferred preparation. Serum levels of Itraconazole should be determined after 2 weeks to ensure adequate drug exposure (goal serum level >1.0 μg/mL).
  • Posaconazole and Voriconazole have been efficacious as salvage therapy (J Infect 54:319, 2007; Antimicrob Agents Chemother 53:1648, 2009).
  • Fluconazole is inferior to Itraconazole in the treatment of histoplasmosis and is not preferred therapy (Clin Infect Dis 23:996, 1996).
  • Urinary antigen levels useful for monitoring response to therapy and relapse.
  • Lifelong suppressive therapy may be needed in chronically immunosuppressed patients and in patients who have relapse despite appropriate therapy.
  • IDSA treatment guidelines:  Clin Infect Dis 45:807, 2007.
