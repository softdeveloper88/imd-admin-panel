Tafenoquine
Tradename: Krintafel, Arakoda

Usage and Dosing

  • Tafenoquine succinate is FDA approved for:
    • Chemoprophylaxis of malaria in patients ≥18 years of age.
    • The radical cure (prevention of relapse) of Plasmodium vivax in patients ≥16 years of age who are receiving chloroquine for acute P. vivax infection.
    • The drug is active against the liver stages including the hypnozoites of P. vivax.
    • CDC advises use off-label for radical cure of P. ovale.
  • Test patients for G6PD deficiency prior to administration of tafenoquine using a quantitative test to ensure >70% normal activity
    • Qualitative (rapid) tests lack sensitivity in females but may be used in males
    • G6PD testing needs to only be performed once during the life of a patient
    • Technical details for G6PD testing for tafenoquine J Travel Med 26: pii: taz023, 2019
      • New (2024) WHO classification system for G6PC deficiency (Bull World Health Organ 2024;102:615)
      • Point of care rapid tests for G6PD are in advanced development PLoS Negl Trop Dis. 2023 Oct 12;17(10):e0011652.
  • Use during pregnancy is not recommended due to risk of hemolytic anemia in a G6PD-deficient fetus. Verify pregnancy status prior to initiating treatment.
  • Avoid breastfeeding for 3 months after the dose of tafenoquine if infant is G6PD-deficient (or status is unknown).
  • Tafenoquine is made in 2 separate dosage forms
    • 100 mg tablets (Arakoda; 60 Degrees Pharmaceuticals in the U.S. & as Kodatef in Australia)
    • 150 mg tablets (Krintafel; GSK in the U.S. and as Kozenis in Australia)
    • Tafenoquine FDA approved but Arakoda (U.S) and Kodatef (Australia) and Krintafel (US, Australia) are commercially available but availability is irregular.
  • References:
    • Toxicity: Expert Opin Drug Saf ;20:349, 2021.
    • Prophylaxis: J Travel Med 2018 Jan 1;25(1). doi: 10.1093/jtm/tay110.
    • Radical cure: New Engl. J. Medicine 2019;380: 215, 229, and 285.  
    • Relapse: Cochrane Database Syst Rev 2020 9: CD010458, 2029

Adult Dose

  • Chemoprophylaxis (age ≥18 years):
    • Before travel to malaria-endemic area (loading regimen): 200 mg once daily x 3 days (starting 3 days before travel)
    • While in endemic area (maintenance regimen): 200 mg once weekly (starting 7 days after last loading regimen dose)
    • After exiting malaria endemic area: 200 mg one-time dose (7 days after last maintenance dose)
  • Tafenoquine is approved for administration for up to six months of continuous dosing
  • Radical cure of P. vivax or P. ovale (age ≥16 years): a single dose of 300 mg co-administered on the first or second day of chloroquine therapy for acute P. vivax malaria.
    • TQ should be co-administered with chloroquine only.
      • This is a change from the original Krintafel labeling that allowed for use in combination with other antimalarials (artemether-lumefantrine, atovaquone-proguanil, quinine plus doxycycline).

Adverse Effects

  • Hemolytic anemia if G6PD deficient
  • Methemoglobinemia
  • Psychiatric effects only in those with previous psychosis: Malar J. 2020 Mar 14;19(1):111. 
  • Hypersensitivity reactions

Pregnancy Risk

FDA Risk Category 1 Not recommended (risk of hemolytic anemia if fetus G6PD-deficient)
Use during Lactation Avoid breastfeeding x3 months after dose if infant G6PD-deficient or status unknown

Antimicrobial Spectrum
  • Active against pre-erythrocytic (liver) and erythrocytic (sexual) forms as well as gametocytes of Plasmodium species that include P. falciparum and P. vivax.
  • Triple therapy with tafenoquine-atovaquone-azithromycin for 10 days vs atovaquone-azithromycin under investigation in severe hospitalized cases. J Infect Dis 2024 May 15;229(5):1599.
  • Weekly tafenoquine maintenance after relapse in immunocompromised patients. Clin Infect Dis 2024 Jul 19;79(1):130.
