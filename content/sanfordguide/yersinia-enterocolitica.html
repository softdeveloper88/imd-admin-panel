Yersinia enterocolitica; Y. pseudotuberculosis

Clinical Setting

  • Yersinia enterocolitica and Yersinia pseudotuberculosis: 
    • Zoonotic infection of domestic and wild animals. Humans incidental hosts as result of ingestion of contaminated food and water.
    • Most common clinical manifestation is acute diarrheal disease (gastroenteritis).
    • Inflammation of the terminal ileum and adjacent lymph nodes can produce clinical picture that mimics acute appendicitis
    • Can cause an acute self-limited febrile diarrhea or severe bacteremia.
    • Y. enterocolitica much more common than Y. pseudotuberculosis.
    • Y. pseudotuberculosis can inflame terminal ileum and mesenteric lymph nodes and mimic acute appendicitis.
    • Diagnosis is primarily by culture from stool or blood.
    • Mild infections require no antibiotic therapy. Severe infection is treated with a fluoroquinolone in adults and TMP/SMX in children.
  • Iron overload diseases, e.g., hemochromatosis, thalessemia, stimulate growth of both Yersinia species and are associated with more severe infections
  • Variety of intestinal and extra-intestinal manifestations can occasionally occur:
    • Can cause pharyngitis in distinction to other bacterial causes of febrile gastroenteritis: e.g., Salmonella species
    • Scarlet fever like syndrome described with Y. pseudotuberculosis
  • Post-infectious immunological sequelae include erythema nodosum, reactive arthritis, conjunctivitis.

Classification

  • Facultative Gram negative bacilli

Diagnosis

  • Culture of stool. Blood cultures if severely ill
  • Molecular, PCR, available. Beware that PCR may not be able to discriminate between Y.pseudotuberculosis and Y. pestis
  • Included in most gastrointestinal multiplex PCR panels.

Primary Regimens

  • Treat only if severe infection, comorbid iron overload condition, or immunocompromised host
  • Enterocolitis
    • Usually self-limited with no documented benefit of treatment. 
    • For severe and/or fragile patient, antibacterial therapy is indicated.
    • Adults: Ciprofloxacin 500 mg po bid (see comment reference resistance)
    • Children: TMP-SMX 8 mg/kg/day of TMP component po in 2 divided doses
    • Usual treatment duration is 5 days but no controlled data
  • Bacteremic patients
    • Adult: Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV once daily
    • Child: Ceftriaxone 100 mg/kg/day in 1 or 2 divided doses to a max. of 4 gm per day
    • Some add Gentamicin 5 mg/kg IV once daily
    • Literature suggests treating severe infection for 3 weeks.

Alternative Regimens

  • Enterocolitis:  TMP-SMX 8 mg/kg/day of TMP component po in 2 divided doses
  • Bacteremic patients (Adult): Ciprofloxacin 400 mg IV bid

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Resistance to ciprofloxacin and nalidixic acid reported sporadically.
  • Resistant to penicillin, ampicillin, first generation cephalosporins and macrolides.
  • Seems reasonable in severely ill patients that respond quickly to down escalate to oral therapy.
  • Treatment duration is not standardized: approximately 5 days for enterocolitis; up to 3 weeks for bacteremia.
