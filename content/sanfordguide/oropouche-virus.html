Oropouche Virus

Clinical Setting

  • Tropical settings; Indigenous in Amazon basin (Brazil) with some spread to other regions of South America and Central America (Peru, Bolivia, Columbia, Panama, Cuba, and Trinidad and Tobago) 
  • Transmitted by midges (small flies); occasionally by mosquitos. Zoonotic: reservoirs in sloths, marsupials, primates, and birds.
  • Originally discovered in 1955; several outbreaks involving > 500,000 cases cumulatively 
  • Signs and symptoms similar to dengue, Chikungunya, and Zika virus infections
    • Fever
    • Headache; pain behind eyes
    • Arthralgias / myalgias
    • Skin rash (rubellaform) 
    • Nausea / vomiting
    • CNS manifestations (severe cases):  meningitis / encephalitis, diplopia, darting of eyes, dystonic movements
    • Occasional bleeding disorders (15%)
  • Usually self-limited disease, resolving within 7 days; can be biphasic with return of fever.  Fatigue can last months in some.
  • Special risk for pregnant women and fetuses (similar to Zika)

Diagnosis

  • Serology or RT-PCR

Etiologies

  • Oropouche virus

Primary Regimens

  • Supportive care; no known effective antiviral therapy

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • Ribavirin ineffective
  • Some outbreaks are explosive; up to 10,000 cases 
  • Surveillance needed; Alert pregnant travelers and recommend avoidance of visiting endemic regions while pregnant
  • References: Lancet Infect Dis. 2024; 24: P935 ; Viruses 10: 175, 2018; MMWR 73: 769, Sept 5, 2024 ; CDC Clinical Overview
