Ulcerated Skin: Venous, Arterial Insufficiency, Pressure Decubuti

Clinical Setting

  • Various settings:
    • Best treatment is prevention
    • ACP evidence based guideline recommends:
      • Advanced static mattress or advanced static overlay
      • Avoid alternating air mattresses or alternating air overlays
      • Reference: Ann Intern Med 162:359, 2015
    • Patient with chronic venous stasis develops an area of ulceration
    • Patient with known arterial insufficiency progresses to skin breakdown
    • Pressure decubuti in immobile patient (e.g., paraplegic)
      • Requires joint management with plastic surgery to achieve debridement and flap closure
      • Ref: Clin Infect Dis 2019; 68: 338
  • All skin ulcers become colonized. Subsequent infection may be local, spread to adjacent bone or progress to bacteremia.
  • Microbiologic evaluation:
    • Swab of ulcer is not recommended.
    • Needle aspiration from the ulcer margin is acceptable.
    • Best method is surgically obtained deep tissue specimen for histology and culture. If osteomyelitis is suspected, also bone biopsy.

Etiologies

  • Polymicrobic, including skin flora, aerobic and anaerobic gram-negative bacilli
  • Commonly encountered pathogens:
    • Streptococcus sp.
    • Staph. aureus
    • Enterobacteriaceae
    • P. aeruginosa
    • Anaerobic Streptococci
    • B. fragilis

Primary Regimens

  • Non-infected venous ulcers: there is moderate evidence as to benefit of keratinocyte therapy; for arterial ulcers, some support for benefit from biological skin equivalent (Ann Intern Med 159:532, 2013).
  • Superficial infection of venous or arterial insufficiency ulcers:
    • Society of Vascular Surgery guidelines recommend against routine use of topical antimicrobials (J Vasc Surg 60 (2 Suppl): 3S, 2014)
    • Silver sulfadiazine 1% cream: insufficient evidence.
    • Do not use povidone iodine or chlorhexadine, both may damage "granulation" tissue and fibroblasts.
    • Debride necrotic tissue and use moist wound dressing.
    • Remove pressure if decubitus ulcer; elevate leg if venous stasis; evaluate for revascularization if arterial insufficiency.
    • Topical treatment options:
  • Severe local infection: e,g, sacral pressure ulcers with possible contiguous osteomyelitis
    • Surgical debridement with:
      • Deep soft tissue cultures of wound margins for aerobic and anaerobic bacteria
      • Bone biopsy for histology and culture as basis of assessment for presence of osteomyelitis. Note: MRI not able to make a specific diagnosis of osteomyelitis
      • Attempt/assess for surgical coverage of wound
    • Empiric therapy pending culture results:
      • Meropenem or Imipenem (but not Ertapenem: no activity vs. P. aeruginosa) or Piperacillin-tazobactam
      • If gram-positive cocci in clusters on gram stain: add Vancomycin
    • Specific therapy depends on culture results
      • If bone culture shows no osteomyelitis: short course therapy
      • If bone culture/biopsy consistent with osteomyelitis, can justify 6 weeks of specific therapy

Alternative Regimens

  • Severe local or possible/proven bacteremia:
    • (Ciprofloxacin or Levofloxacin) + Metronidazole
    • (Cefepime or Ceftazidime) + Metronidazole
    • Aztreonam + Metronidazole
    • If gram-positive cocci in clusters on gram stain: add Vancomycin

Comments

  • Sacral pressure ulcers (Review of antimicrobial treatment efficacy): Clin Infect Dis  2019; 68:338.
  • See Cochrane Data Syst Rev 2017; issue 6: CD011947 for discussion of dressings or topical agents for pressure ulcers.
