Bite: Dog

Clinical Setting

  • Bite wound from a dog.
  • Only 5% of dog bite wounds get infected. Treat only if the bite is severe or patient presents bad co-morbidity, e.g., diabetes.
  • If functional or anatomic splenectomy, See Capnocytophaga sp. and Post-Splenectomy/Septic Shock

Etiologies

  • Pasteurella canis
  • Staphylococcus aureus
  • Bacteroides sp.
  • Fusobacterium sp., EF-4
  • Capnocytophaga sp., especially C. canimorsus in splenectomized patients

Primary Regimens

  • Amoxicillin-Clavulanate (875/125 mg po bid or 500/125 mg po tid)

Alternative Regimens

  • Pediatric: Clindamycin + TMP-SMX) po
  • Adult: Clindamycin 300 mg po qid + a Fluoroquinolone po

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • P. canis is resistant to Dicloxacillin, Cephalexin, Clindamycin and Erythromycin; sensitive to Ceftriaxone, Cefuroxime, Cefpodoxime, Ceftaroline and fluoroquinolones; only ceftaroline would have predictable activity vs. Pasteurella species and MRSA (Antimicrob Agts Chemother 56:6319, 2012).

Comments

  • Rabies considerations for penetrating dog bites:
    • If healthy appearing observe for 10 days, PEP only if animal develops clinical signs of rabies
    • Immediately vaccinate if suspected rabid
    • Escaped; consult local public health authority 
  • See Rabies, Vaccines and PEP for detailed evaluation procedure and recommendations
  • Capnocytophaga canimorsus in splenectomized patients may cause local eschar, sepsis with DIC (see Shock, Post-splenectomy).
  • Reference: Am Fam Physician 90:239, 2014.
