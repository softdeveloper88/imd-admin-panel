Toxocariasis

Clinical Setting

  • Mostly asymptomatic infection
  • Visceral larva migrans syndrome (visceral toxocariasis) and ocular larva migrans syndrome (ocular toxocariasis) but other organs may be involved
  • Ingestion of dog (or other canine) or cat (or other feline) ascarids (roundworm) eggs most commonly by young children crawling on the ground or in sandboxes
  • Fever, anorexia, weight loss, cough, wheezing (frequent), rashes, hepatosplenomegaly, and hypereosinophilia
  • Ocular toxocariasis is unilateral (uveitis, retinitis, or endophthalmitis) and may be mistaken for a retinoblastoma
  • Larval infiltration of viscera (liver, heart, lungs, brain, muscle). While the larvae do not undergo any further development in these sites (no adults; no eggs in stool), they can cause local reactions and mechanical damage without any subsequent maturation to adult worms.
  • Infections and egg excretion are more prevalent among puppies than older dogs
  • T. catis increasing in prevalence in humans

Diagnosis

  • Diagnosis usually clinical
  • TES-ELISA (CDC) on serum with 70% sensitivity and 90% specificity; some data on testing vitreous humor but much less sensitive.
    • Positive ELISA may reflect past inactive non-inflammatory as well as current infection, less sensitivity with ocular disease.
  • Cross reactive with other nematodes especially ascariasis
  • Hepatic lesions may be observed with ultrasonography, CT and MRI
  • Stool examinations are not helpful since the parasite does not complete a full life cycle involving the human gastrointestinal tract.
  • Biopsy rarely indicated

Etiologies

  • Toxocara canis
  • Toxocara catis

Primary Regimens

  • Visceral:
    • Asymptomatic or mild disease: No treatment unless massive eosinophilia.
  • Moderate to severe disease: Albendazole 400 mg po bid x 5 days with or without Prednisone 60 mg/day (especially if CNS involvement). Same dose for children.
  • Ocular:
    • First 4 weeks of illness: (Prednisone 30–60 mg po q24h + subtenon Triamcinolone 40 mg/wk) x 2 weeks
    • No benefit to treatment > 4 weeks
    • Albendazole as above may be considered
    • Surgery sometimes necessary

Alternative Regimens

  • Visceral: Mebendazole 200 mg po bid x 21 days (clear inferiority to Albendazole)

Comments

  • Reviews: Lancet Infect Dis 18:e14, 2018; Adv Parasitol 109;111, 2020. 
  • Steroids: Clin Microbiol Rev 16:265, 2003.
  • Vermox brand Mebendazole 500 mg tablet is FDA-approved but for donation only and not sold in the U.S.
  • Albendazole 200 mg tablets and Mebendazole 100 mg capsules available from Expert Compounding Pharmacy (see Antiparasitic Drugs, Sources).
  • Generic Albendazole (Albenza) marketed by Amneal is very costly and difficult for retail pharmacies to access.
