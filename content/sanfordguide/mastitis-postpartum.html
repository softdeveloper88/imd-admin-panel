Mastitis, Postpartum

Clinical Setting

  • Postpartum mastitis occurs in approximately 10% of nursing mothers.
  • Poor breast-feeding technique, incomplete emptying are contributing factors.
  • Risk factors (same for MRSA and MSSA): mother employed outside the home, primiparity, advanced maternal age, breast-feeding difficulties (Clin Infect Dis 54:71, 2012).
  • May present with or without abscess.
    • If an abscess if present surgical incision and drainage (I&D) or drainage by needle aspiration  indicated
  • Staph. aureus is most common etiology but other pathogens are possible. If possible, obtain culture before starting empiric therapy.

Etiologies

  • Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA or MSSA) (vast majority of infections)
  • Streptococcus pyogenes (Group A or B)
  • Escherichia coli
  • Bacteroides species
  • Corynebacterium sp.
  • Coagulase-negative staphylococci (e.g., Staphylococcus lugdunensis)

Primary Regimens

  • If MRSA is not present or suspected:
    • Outpatient: Dicloxacillin 500 mg po qid or Cephalexin 500 mg po qid
    • Inpatient: Nafcillin 2 gm IV q4h or Oxacillin 2 gm IV q4h or Cefazolin 2 gm IV q8h
  • If MRSA is present or possible:
    • Outpatient: TMP-SMX-DS 1-2 tabs po bid (see Comments) OR Clindamycin 300 mg po tid (depending on susceptibility)

    • Inpatient: Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL)

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Duration of therapy not well established: 10-14 days generally recommended but 5-7 days may be adequate on those with good clinical response.
  • If vancomycin is initiated empirically, can transition to a beta-lactam, IV or oral, if MSSA is isolated.

Comments

  • Role of antibiotics unclear once the abscess is drained.
  • Avoid TMP-SMX in premature infants or neonatal hyperbilirubinemia.
  • If baby can latch on and mother is comfortable, continue breast feeding during the antibiotic therapy. Breast feeding helps drain the breast and reduces risk of progression to abscess.
  • If the breast is too painful for breast feeding, no choice but to discontinue. On occasion, feeding can be temporarily interrupted, e.g., surgical I&D of an abscess, only to resume as soon as pain allows.
  • Continued breast feeding does not pose a risk to the infant; discuss with pediatrician age-specific risks to infant of drug exposure through breast milk.
  • References: StatPearls; 2022 Jan. 2022 Jul 18;  JAMA. 2023;329:588.
