Mycobacterium avium-intracellulare, Immunocompetent

Clinical Setting

  • Non-tuberculous mycobacterial pulmonary infection in immunocompetent patient.
  • M. avium-intracellulare complex (MAC  occurs in both immunocompetent (this topic) and immunocompromised patients.
    • “Classic” pulmonary MAC: Men age 50-75, smokers, COPD.
    • “New” pulmonary MAC: Women 30-70, scoliosis, mitral valve prolapse, (bronchiectasis), pectus excavatum (“Lady Windermere syndrome”). May also be associated with interferon gamma deficiency.
    • May be associated with hot tub use (hypersensitivity pneumonitis).
    • Anti-tumor necrosis factor (TNF)-alpha therapy increases risk of infection with MAC and other non-tuberculous mycobacteria (Emerging Infect Dis 15:1556, 2009).
  • Treatment determined by presentation:
    • Nodular or bronchiectatic disease
    • Cavitary disease

Etiologies

  • Mycobacterium avium-intracellulare complex (MAC, MAI)

Primary Regimens

  • Nodular or bronchiectatic disease:
    • Azithromycin 500 mg 3x/week + Ethambutol 25 mg/kg 3 x/week + Rifampin 600 mg 3x/week
    • For advanced or severe disease, macrolide-resistant disease, or previously treated disease consider use of daily therapy as for Cavitary disease, below. 
    • Duration of therapy is for at least 12 months after cultures convert to negative.
  • Cavitary disease  
    • Azithromycin 250-500 mg/day + Ethambutol 15 mg/kg/day + Rifampin 10 mg/kg/day up to 600 mg + Amikacin 15-25 mg/kg IV or IM 3x weekly (adjust dose to obtain a peak serum concentration of 65-80 μg/mL in a sample drawn 30 min after a 30 min infusion and a trough of < 5 μg/mL) for first 2-3 months.
    • Duration of therapy: at least 12 months after cultures convert to negative.
  • Refractory pulmonary infection unresponsive to above regimens (defined as failure to achieve negative cultures at 6 months):
    • Add Amikacin liposome inhalation suspension (Aricayce®) once daily at a dose of 590 mg/8.4 mL (one vial) with the Lamira Nebulizer System only to the standard oral regimen  (See Comments)
      • In those achieving 3 consecutive monthly negative sputum cultures by 6 months, continue for an additional 12 months after the first negative culture  (Am J Respir Crit Care Med 2018 2018;198:1559).
      • Extending therapy in those with failed to convert sputum cultures to negative by 6 months resulted in negative cultures within an additional 6 months in 9.6% and within an additional 12 months in 13.7% (Ann Am Thorac Soc. 2021;18:1147).

Alternative Regimens

  • Clarithromycin 500 mg twice a day administered daily or 3x a weekly is an alternative to Azithromycin
  • Rifabutin 300 mg daily (150 mg if Clarithromcyin is used) or 300 mg 3x weekly is an alternative to Rifampin
  • Amikacin can be administered daily at a dose of 10-15 mg/kg once daily (adjust dose to obtain a peak serum concentration of 35-45 μg/mL in a sample drawn 30 min after a 30 min infusion and a trough of < 5 μg/mL)
  • Streptomycin 10-15 mg/kg IV or IM once daily or 15-25 mg/kg 3x weekly is an alternative to Amikacin
  • If liposomal formulation of Amikacin is unavailable, nebulized Amikacin is an option: 250mg/mL Amikacin sulfate diluted with 3 mL of saline administered by jet nebulizer once or twice daily.
  • Addition of Moxifloxacin 400 mg po once daily to the combination regimen (above) may improve outcome in refractory disease (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 57:2281, 2013).
  • Clofazimine 100 mg + [Clarithromycin 500-1000 mg/day or Azithromycin 250 mg/day] + Ethambutol 15 mg/kg/day as effective as Rifampin + [Clarithromycin or Azithromycin] + Ethambutol in one study (Chest 149:1285, 2016) in bronchiectatic or cavitary disease.

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Expert consultation is recommended for patients with previously treated disease, for those who fail to convert sputum cultures to negative by 6 months, and for those with extensive disease.
  • Treatment should be based on confirmed in vitro susceptibility to Amikacin and Macrolides. The breakpoint for resistance to Amikacin is MIC ≥ 64 μg/mL for parenteral Amikacin and ≥128 μg/mL for Amikacin liposome inhalation suspension; these agents should not be included in the regimen if the isolate is resistant. 

Comments

  • ATS/ERS/ESCMID/IDSA Clinical Practice Guidelines: Clin Infect Dis 2020; 71:e1-e36.
  • Excellent review of MAC diagnosis and management: J Infect Dis. 2020;222(Supplement_4):S199-S211
  • Intermittent therapy (i.e., 3 x/week) not recommended except with  Amikacin or Streptomycin for the following:
    • Patients with cavitary disease;
    • Patients who have been previously treated or patients with severe disease.
  • Consider surgical resection in refractory cases (see Curr Opin Infect Dis 25:218, 2012 for review of treatment options).
  • Amikacin liposome inhalation suspension (full prescribing information can be found here)
    • Amikacin liposome inhalation suspension added to an multidrug optimized background therapy (OBT) achieved sputum culture conversion (3 consecutive monthly negative sputum cultures) by 6 months in 29% of patients compared to 8.9% of patients receiving OBT alone. (Am J Respir Crit Care Med 2018 Sep 14. doi: 10.1164/rccm.201807-1318OC ).
    • Efficacy for patients with cystic fibrosis unknown as these patients were excluded from the clinical trial.
    • Consider pre-treatment with inhaled bronchodilator in patients with a history of hyperreactive airway disease.
    • Has been associated with a risk of increased respiratory adverse reactions, including, hypersensitivity pneumonitis, hemoptysis, bronchospasm, and exacerbation of underlying pulmonary disease that have led to hospitalizations in some cases.
