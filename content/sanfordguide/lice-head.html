Lice, Head

Clinical Setting

  • Lice infestation of the scalp; presents with pruritis. Most common in children. Occurs worldwide. Do not carry any disease.
  • Adult louse free and crawls on scalp and hair; eggs (nits) appear white and on hair shaft near scalp.
  • Most often asymptomatic; reaction to saliva of louse can cause itching.
  • Diagnosis by visualizing live organism. Suggest use of wet combing: wet hair with hair conditioner, use fine-toothed comb (nit comb), start at scalp level with pressure, comb entire scalp twice (sensitivity 91%).

Etiologies

  • Pediculus humanus capitis (head louse)

Primary Regimens

  • Try topical therapy first. Oral options for refractory infections
  • Topical Therapies:
    • Most drugs kill adults but not the eggs; hence re-treatment in 7-10 days usually necessary to kill newly hatched lice prior to their laying eggs.
    • Regardless of topical treatment, remove nits with nit comb (Lice comb; Lockomb and others)  initially and repeat in 7-10 days.
    • Ivermectin 0.5% lotion (Trade name: Sklice). Available OTC.
      • Kills adults; causes death of newly hatched lice within 48 hrs
      • Resistance rare
      • Approved for infants over 6 months old
      • Convenient: Apply to dry hair and scalp for 10 minutes and then rinse
      • Reported cure rate of 74% at 15 days (N Engl J Med 367:1687, 2012)
      • Over the counter formulation approved in 2023 (Med Lett Drugs Ther 65:103 2023)
        • Apply to dry hair and scalp for 10 min, then rinse
    • Pyrethrins and permethrin were first choice, over the counter and less expensive but widespread clinical resistance:
      • Pyrethrins: natural extract from chrysanthemum flowers and available OTC combined with piperonyl burtoxide as RID.
        • Resistance now widespread
        • Rare allergic reactions in patients allergic to chrysanthemums or ragweed
      • Permethrin is synthetic drug similar to the natural pyrethrins  
        • Available as an OTC 1% cream rinse (Nix or generic); prescription strength is 5% but no more effective than the OTC 1%
        • Estimate 75% of lice are resistant. For both above products: Shampoo, dry hair, and apply. Leave in place 10 minutes, then rinse. Repeat in 7-10 days.
      • For both of the pyrethrins: Apply to dry hair for 10 minutes, shampoo, repeat in 7-10 days. A failure to respond may indicate drug resistance.
    • Prescription Topical Alternatives to pyrethrins:     
      • Malathion lotion 0.5% - includes terpineol (Trade name: Ovide)
        • Kills both adults and eggs
        • Apply to scalp for 8-12 hours before rinsing
        • Second treatment in 7 days sometimes needed
        • One report of 98% efficacy with one or two 20 minute applications: Pediatric Dermatol 2004, 21:670.
        • Malodorous, more expensive, but more efficacious than Permethrin; compliance only 50% largely due to offensive odor
        • Cure rate of 85-98%, increasing resistance reported.
        • Highly flammable. Do not  use curling iron or hair dryer after applying
      • Spinosad 0.9% suspension (Trade name: Natroba)
        • Kills adults and eggs
        • No resistance reported so far
        • Apply to affected area of dry hair for 10 minutes and then wash off; repeat in 7 days if needed
        • Expensive
      • Benzyl alcohol (Trade name: Ulesfia)
        • Does not kill eggs
        • No resistance reported
        • 5% lotion applied to dry hair for 10 minutes & then rinsed off. Repeat in 7 days.
        • Cure rate of 75%
        • Contact dermatitis and eye irritation can occur
    • Oral Therapy if lice are clinically resistant to topical therapy
      • Oral Ivermectin:
        • 93% cure rate; no resistance reported
        • Do not use Ivermectin in pregnancy or in children weighing <15 kg
        • Ivermectin dose: 200-400 μg/kg po (off label as oral not FDA approved for treatment of lice); can repeat after 8 days if live lice persist

    Alternative Regimens

    • Manual removal: only for very young children wherein drugs are not indicated. Use special NIT combs: e.g., Lice Comb, Lockomb are examples
    • In Europe, some success smothering lice with dimethicone (silicone product). In US, a dimethicone gel (LiceMD Pesticide Free) OTC reports 96.5 % eradication of live lice and 81% clearance of eggs after concomitant combing and 1-3 gel treatments.

    Comments

    • Prevention of transmission of lice is key for management
    • Lindane not recommended due to neurotoxicity issues. No longer manufactured in US
    • Items  in contact with the head of a louse-infected patient within 24-48 hrs should be cleaned; Lice not on the scalp generally do not survive beyond 24 hrs
      • Wash hats, scarves, coats and bedding in hot water > 130 degrees F and dry in hot dryer for 20 or more minutes.
      • Place items that cannot be  washed in sealed  plastic bag for 48 hrs
      • Wash combs and brushes and vacuum room of the patient.
      • Finding nits (eggs) without lice does not always mean infection is present. Nits can remain visible for months after eradication of live lice. Children with head lice can return to school after first topical treatment.
    • Concise review: Med Lett Drugs Ther. 2024; 66:89.
