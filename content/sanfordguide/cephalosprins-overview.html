Cephalosporins, Overview

Introduction

  • Cephalosporins share structural similarities with the other beta-lactam antibiotics (penicillins, monobactam, carbapenems) due to the presence of a beta-lactam ring.
  • Like the other beta-lactams, cephalosporins are generally safe with some rare exceptions.

Classification

  • Cephalosporins are grouped according to anticipated in vitro spectrum of antibacterial activity.
  • Patterns of antibacterial resistance vary widely. Check local susceptibility data.
  • Oral cephalosporins are no longer recommended for treatment of gonococcal infections due to high-levels of resistance (MMWR 61:590, 2012).
  • The following classification scheme is based on the original pattern of antibacterial activity for each drug.

1st Generation

  • Spectrum of activity: S. aureus (MSSA), streptococci, limited Gram-negative coverage (e.g., some strains of E. coli, Klebsiella spp., P. mirabilis), poor anaerobic coverage
  • Parenteral
    • Cefazolin
  • Oral
    • Cefadroxil
    • Cephalexin

2nd Generation

  • Spectrum of activity: generally less activity against Gram-positive cocci, improved activity against H. influenzae
  • Oral
    • Cefuroxime axetil
    • Cefaclor
    • Loracarbef (where available)
    • Cefprozil
  • Parenteral
    • Cefmetazole (where available)
    • Cefuroxime
    • Cephamycins: activity against anaerobes, including Bacteroides spp.; may be active in vitro vs. ESBL-producing aerobic gram-negative bacilli but clinical efficacy data lacking
      • Cefoxitin
      • Cefotetan

3rd Generation

  • Expanded activity against gram-negative bacilli; parenteral agents have improved CNS penetration; all are subject to  inactivation by ESBLs and AmpC beta-lactamases produced by Gram-negatives
    • Parenteral:
      • Cefotaxime (where available)
      • Ceftriaxone
      • Ceftizoxime: activity against anaerobes
      • Ceftazidime: activity against Pseudomonas aeruginosa; not recommended for treatment of bacterial meningitis
      • Cefoperazone (where available)
      • Cefoperazone-sulbactam (where available): activity against Pseudomonas aeruginosa and anaerobic bacteria, including Bacteroides spp.; not recommended for treatment of bacterial meningitis
    • Oral:
      • Cefdinir
      • Cefditoren pivoxil
      • Cefixime
      • Cefpodoxime proxetil
      • Ceftibuten

Advanced Generation

  • Cephalosporins with enhanced antibacterial spectrum against selected organisms; poor activity against Gram-negative anaerobes, Bacteroides spp. in particular; parenteral only
    • Cefepime: sometimes referred to as a 4th generation cephalosporin; activity against P. aeruginosa and AmpC-producing organisms; good CNS penetration
    • Cefpirome (where available)
    • Ceftaroline fosamil acetate: activity against MRSA due to its affinity for binding PBP2a, the protein that mediates methicillin resistance
    • Ceftobiprole: activity against MRSA due to its affinity for binding PBP2a, the protein that mediates methicillin resistance
    • Ceftolozane-tazobactam: activity against Pseudomonas aeruginosa, ESBL producers, some AmpC producers
    • Ceftazidime-avibactam: same spectrum of activity as Ceftazidime plus activity against ESBL producers, AmpC producers, serine carbapenemase-producing Gram-negatives
    • Cefiderocol: activity against ESBL producers, AmpC producers, carbapenemase-producing Gram-negatives, including non-fermenters such as Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Acinetobacter spp. and Stenotrophomonas maltophilia; no activity against Gram-positive organisms or anaerobic organisms
    • Cefepime-Enmetazobactam

Distribution and Penetration 

  • Cephalosporins achieve therapeutic concentrations in pericardial, peritoneal, pleural and synovial fluids, and in urine.
  • CSF penetration: Cefotaxime, Ceftizoxime, Ceftriaxone are approved for treatment of meningitis
  • Biliary penetration: Cefazolin, Ceftriaxone achieve the highest levels in bile

Adverse Effects, Allergy

  • Allergy, Cross-allergenicity
    • Most common adverse effect is beta-lactam drug allergy.
    • See Related Topics Links below for link to drug allergy and cross-allergenicity discussion.
  • Other class-wide AEs
    • Increased risk of C. difficile toxin-mediated colitis with use of any cephalosporin, especially the 3rd generation drugs.
    • All beta-lactam antibiotics have potential for increasing risk of inducing or potentiating seizure activity.
    • See each drug for specific adverse effects.
