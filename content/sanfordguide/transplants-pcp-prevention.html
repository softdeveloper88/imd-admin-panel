Toxoplasmosis, Immunocompromised (AIDS, Post-Transplantation)

Clinical Setting

  • After usually asymptomatic primary infection, latent infection persists for the life of the host.
  • Reactivation disease can occur in AIDS patients (CD4 <100 cells/µL), transplant patients, and other immunocompromised individuals.
    • Risk of reactivation 30% if CD4 count <100, toxo-antibody positive, and not on prophylaxis.
    • Individuals may be at higher risk for reactivation after hematopoietic stem cell transplantation, so some centers monitor by PCR of blood.
  • Syndromes
    • Toxoplasma encephalitis presents with headache, confusion and fever.
    • Most patients with encephalitis will be Toxoplasma seropositive (though some with HIV, borderline positive).
    • Because of risk of brain biopsy, treat empirically and monitor for clinical and radiographic improvement (MRI more sensitive than CT). 
    • If no clinical improvement after 7-10 days treatment, need to biopsy. 
    • T. gondii can cause pneumonia, retinochoroiditis, hepatitis, myositis, disseminated sepsis picture, and other non-cerebral infections.
  • In one case series, 60 day mortality rate was 82% in patients who were post transplant or had HIV/AIDS (Clin Infect Dis 57:1535, 2013)

Etiologies

  • Toxoplasma gondii

Diagnosis:

  • Usually based on clinical syndrome plus positive antibody (IgM or IgG). Definitive diagnosis requires identification of organism in tissue.
  • Ring enhancing lesions on CT or MRI of the brain. If no clinical improvement after 7-10 days empiric therapy, need to biopsy. 
  • PCR may be positive in latently infected tissue and not indicate active disease. PCR is useful if positive in blood or CSF (specific though not sensitive).
  • Many centers monitor T. gondii seropositive hematopoetic stem patient recipients regularly by PCR of blood.
  • CDC reference lab: Dr. Jack S. Remington Laboratory for Specialty Diagnostics (remingtonlab@sutterhealth.org; previously called Palo Alto Medical Foundation Toxoplasma Serology Lab) +1 650-853-4828.

Primary Regimens

  • Pyrimethamine 200 mg po x 1 dose, then 75 mg/day po + Sulfadiazine (1 gm if < 60 kg; 1.5 gm if ≥ 60 kg) po q6h + Folinic acid 10-25 mg/day po. Treat for 6 weeks after resolution of symptoms and then suppression (secondary prophylaxis). OR
  • TMP-SMX 10 mg/kg/day (trimethoprim-component) IV po divided q12h x 6 weeks and then secondary suppression (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 42:1346, 1998).

Alternative Regimens

  • Pyrimethamine 200 mg po x 1 dose, then 75 mg/day po + Folinic acid 10-25 mg/day po + one of the following:
    • Clindamycin 600 mg IV po q8h or
    • Atovaquone 750 mg po q6h or
    • Azithromycin 900-1200 mg po once daily
  • Treat for 6 weeks and then secondary suppression
  • Primary Prophylaxis: CD4 < 100/µL & IgG antibody to toxoplasmosis, continue until CD4 count >200 cells/µL for 3 months
    • TMP-SMX-DS or TMP-SMX-SS po once daily (also effective prophylaxis for PJP)
    • Dapsone 50 mg po q24h + Pyrimethamine 50 mg po q/week + Folinic acid 25 mg po q/week
    • Once weekly: Dapsone 200 mg po + Pyrimethamine 75 mg po + Folinic acid 25 mg po (JAIDS 15:104, 1997)
    • Atovaquone 1500 mg po q24h
  • Secondary Prophylaxis: after treatment of cerebral toxoplasmosis, continue suppression until CD4 count > 200 cells/µL for 6 months
    • Sulfadiazine 500-1000 mg po qid + Pyrimethamine 25-50 mg po q24h + Folinic acid 10-25 mg po q24h (prevents PJP and Toxoplasmosis)
    • Clindamycin 300-450 mg po q6-8h + Pyrimethamine 25-50 mg po q24h + Folinic Acid 10-25 mg po q24h (does not prevent PJP)
    • Atovaquone 750 mg po q6-12h
