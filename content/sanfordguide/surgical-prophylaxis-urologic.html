Surgical Prophylaxis, Urologic

Clinical Setting

  • Antimicrobial prophylaxis for urologic procedures: cystoscopy and prostate biopsy.
  • Appropriately timed antimicrobial prophylaxis for transrectal prostate biopsy decreases incidence of bacteremia, bacteriuria, fever and UTI. (Cochrane Database Syst Rev. 2011).
  • See Prevention of Surgical Infections, Overview.

Etiologies

  • E. coli and other aerobic gram-negative bacilli
  • Enterococci

Primary Regimens

  • Cystoscopy
    • Prophylaxis generally not necessary if urine is sterile (however, AUA recommends Fluoroquinolone or TMP-SMX for those with several potentially adverse host factors (e.g., advanced age, immunocompromised state, anatomic abnormalities, etc.))
    • Treat patients with UTI prior to procedure using an antimicrobial active against pathogen isolated.
  • Cystoscopy with manipulation (ureteroscopy, biopsy, fulguration, TURP)
    • Ciprofloxacin 500 mg po
    • TMP-SMX 1 DS tablet po (TMP-SMX may be an alternative in populations with low rates of resistance)
    • Treat patients with UTI prior to procedure using an antimicrobial active against pathogen isolated if possible.
  • Transrectal prostate biopsy
    • Ciprofloxacin 500 mg po
    • TMP-SMX 1 DS tablet po (TMP-SMX may be an alternative in populations with low rates of resistance) one hour prior to biopsy

Alternative Regimens

  • See Comments

Comments

  • Drugs that achieve high concentrations in the prostate: e.g.: FQs and TMP/SMX, preferred. However, increasing reports of infection due to MDR FQs led to recommendation to follow local susceptibility patterns. If FQ resistant E. coli > 20%, consider alternate agent (J Urol. 2017;198(2):329. Epub 2017 Mar 29).
  • Serious bloodstream infection due to fluoroquinolone-resistant organisms have now been encountered in patients receiving FQ prophylaxis. Clinicians should advise patients to immediately report symptoms suggesting infection. Pre-operative prophylaxis should be determined on an institutional basis, taking into account susceptibility profiles of prevailing organisms.
  • For patients at high risk of resistance (eg, prior/recurrent UTI, immunosuppression, recent antibiotic), consider rectal swab culture to direct antibiotic selection (Clin Infect Dis 60: 979, 2015).
  • Single dose IM alternatives include 3rd generation cephalosporin (ceftriaxone) or aminoglycoside, e.g., a single dose of gentamicin or tobramycin, 5-7 mg/kg IV.
  • Although second- or third-generation cephalosporins (BMC Urol 16:51, 2016) or the addition of single-dose gentamicin have been suggested as alternatives, infections due to enteric organisms that are ESBL-producers or are gentamicin-resistant, or both, have also been reported (Urol 74:332, 2009).
  • Meta-analysis found fosfomycin trometamol more effective than fluoroquinolones in preventing infection after transrectal prostate biopsy (World J Urol: 36: 323, 2018). Pharmacological analysis by another group suggested fosfomycin trometamol should be effective for isolates with MICs ≤ 4 μg/mL when administered 1-4 hours prior to prostate biopsy (J Antimicrob Chemother 70: 2068, 2015).
