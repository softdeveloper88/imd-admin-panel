Candida auris

Clinical Setting

  • Candida auris is a candida species of particular concern because of the following factors:
    • Antifungal resistance, particularly to azoles, is common:
      • Reported resistance rates for Fluconazole are  86 % to 100%.
      • 43% of strains are resistant to Amphotericin B
      • Echinocandin resistance is reported in 2% to 5%
      • References: Med Mycol, 2019; 57:1-12; Mycoses 2019; 62:620.
    • It can be difficult to identify by common laboratory techniques.
    • Can be transmitted in the health care setting between patients and is difficult to eradicate.
  • Can cause a wide range of clinical disease from asymptomatic colonization, candidemia, abscesses, etc.
  • Presently there is a worldwide spread of C. auris infections, identified in over 30 countries including the United States.

Classification

  • Candida auris is commonly misidentified. Misidentification depends on testing method used. 
  • Definitive identification can be performed via MALDI-TOF and other molecular methods.
  • For more details, see the table below which has been adapted from the CDC materials on C. auris.

Candida parapsilosis*

  • *C. guilliermondii, C. lusitaniae, and C. parapsilosis generally make pseudohyphae on cornmeal agar. If hyphae or pseudohyphae are not present on cornmeal agar, this should raise suspicion for C. auris as C. auris typically does not make hyphae or pseudohyphae. However, some C. auris isolates have formed hyphae or pseudohyphae. 
  • Therefore, it would be prudent to consider any C. guilliermondii, C. lusitaniae, and C. parapsilosis isolates identified on MicroScan or any C. parapsilosis isolates identified on RapID Yeast Plus as possible C. auris isolates and forward them for further identification.

Primary Regimens

  • Caspofungin 70 mg IV loading dose, then 50 mg IV qd
  • Micafungin 100 mg IV qd
  • Anidulafungin 200 mg IV loading dose, then 100 mg IV qd

Alternative Regimens

  • Use of Lipid-based Amphotericin B should be guided by antifungal MIC testing
    • If amphotericin MIC ≥ 2 mcg/mL then it should not be used
  • Use of azole antifungals should be guided by antifungal MIC testing:
    • If fluconazole MIC ≥ 32 mcg/mL then it should not be used.  Efficacy of other azoles when resistance to fluconazole is unknown and MIC testing should be performed and consultation with an infectious diseases specialist recommended

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Should patients identified to have C. auris colonization be treated with antifungals?  No, this is not recommended and may lead to future antifungal resistance

Comments

  • What are the the CDC recommended infection control measures for patients with Candida auris infection/colonization?
    • Place in a single-patient room and using Standard and Contact Precautions (for duration of hospitalization)
    • Emphasize adherence to hand hygiene
    • Clean and disinfect patient care environment and reusable equipment (daily and terminal cleaning) with recommended products
    • Inter-facility communication about patient’s C. auris status at transfer to another healthcare facility
    • Screen contacts of newly identified case patients to identify C. auris colonization
    • Conduct surveillance for new cases to detect ongoing transmission
  • References: CDC resources on Candida auris ; Review of management: JAMA Jan 22, 2024, ahead of print.
