Balanitis

Clinical Setting

  • There are several categories of balanitis. Only two will be mentioned here:
    • Inflammation of the glans penis in uncircumsized men
    • One of several extra-genital manifestations of reactive arthritis (formerly Reiter's syndrome). The balanitis of reactive arthritis takes a distinctive geographic appearance: i.e., circinate balanitis (N Engl J Med 2017;376:157)
  • Reactive arthritis is typified by arthritis, conjunctivitis, urethritis, and other genitourinary manifestations to include circinate balanitis

Etiologies

  • Under the foreskin inflammed glans penis (balanitis):
    • Candida sp. (40%)
    • Streptococcus sp.
    • Gardnerella sp.
    • Other bacteria (e.g., anaerobes)
  • Reactive Arthritis:  Circinate balanitis after infection  with:
    • Most often: Chlamydia trachomatis
    • Others include: Yersinia, Campylobacter, Shigella, Salmonella, C.pneumoniae
    • Presence of HLA B27 genotype predisposes

Primary Regimens

  • For Candidal balanitis, a topical azole is frequently effective in mild cases.
    • Over the counter preparations include: clotrimazole, butoconazole, miconazole, or tioconazole 
    • Otherwise: Fluconazole 150 mg po x 1 dose or Itraconazole 200 mg po bid x 1 day
  • For anaerobes: Metronidazole 2 gm po x 1 dose
  • For circinate balantis: topical glucocorticoid; treat C. trachomatis if present

Alternative Regimens

  • Treatment of infection due to other etiologies is directed towards organisms recovered.

Comments

  • Primary syphilis can present as erosive balanitis without discrete chancre (Acta Derm Venereol 91:191, 2011).
  • Group A streptococcal balanitis reported after oral sex (Int J STD AIDS 16:644, 2005).
  • Review of infectious balanoposthitis in Int J Dermatol 48:121, 2009.
  • See 2021 CDC STD Guidelines:  MMWR Recomm Rep 70 (RR-4):1 2021
