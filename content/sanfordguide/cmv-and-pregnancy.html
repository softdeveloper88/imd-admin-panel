Cytomegalovirus in Pregnancy

Clinical Setting

  • Cytomegalovirus (CMV) infection in pregnancy. May occur as new infection or re-activation
  • Seronegative women who work in day care, have young children or have a child who is shedding CMV are at increased risk of new infection
  • New infection carries higher risk of transmission to infant than re-activation
  • Contact with children < 2 years increases risk
  • Pregnant women with an acute mononucleosis like syndrome or ultrasound showing anomalies consistent with CMV should be tested for acute infection
  • Routine screening of pregnant women is under investigation

Etiologies

  • Cytomegalovirus (CMV)

Primary Regimens

  • Supportive care
  • Ganciclovir or Valganciclovir can be used for severe end organ disease but have not been shown to reduce transmission.
  • Low quality evidence suggests Valacyclovir 8000 mg/day during acute primary infection in first trimester might reduce congenital infection (Am J Obst Gyn 230;109 2024)

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • Hyperimmune globulin failed as preventative therapy in large study (N Engl J Med 385:436, 2021; N Engl J Med 389:1822 2023).
  • Prevention measures for women with contact with toddlers include avoiding contact with saliva, hand hygiene after contact with saliva or urine, not sharing food or drink, not putting pacifiers in mother's mouth (Lancet Infect Dis. 2017 Jun;17(6):e177)
  • For information on congenital infection see Cytomegalovirus, congenital.
