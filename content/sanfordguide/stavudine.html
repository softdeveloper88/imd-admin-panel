Stavudine
Tradename: Zerit, d4T

Usage and Dosing

  • Stavudine (D4T) is not recommended by DHHS as initial therapy because of adverse reactions. Indicated as an alternative nucleoside as part of a 3-drug regimen, usually paired with another nucleoside agent (e.g., 3TC or FTC) and an NNRTI, a boosted PI, or a INSTI (Strand-transfer Integrase Inhibitor).
  • Highest incidence of lipoatrophy, hyperlipidemia, & lactic acidosis of all NRTIs. Pancreatitis. Peripheral neuropathy. These complications are particularly common when Stavudine is used with Didanosine (ddI).  Most experts recommend that D4T and ddI not be used together. This is especially problematic in pregnancy (contraindicated in pregnancy).

Adult Dose

Adult Wt ≥60 kg:40 mg po q12h
Adult Wt <60 kg:30 mg po q12h

Pediatric Dose

Dose (Age >28 Days) (NOTE: The DHHS Pediatric Guidelines Panel does not recommend d4T in children because of a higher rate of adverse effects compared to other NRTIs)

Age 0-13 days: 0.5 mg/kg q12h
Age ≥14 days, wt <30 kg: 1 mg/kg q12h
Wt ≥30 kg: 30 mg q12h

Adverse Effects

  • Most Significant:
    • Peripheral neuropathy 15–20%. Pancreatitis 1%. Appears to produce lactic acidosis more commonly than other NRTIs.
    • Black Box warning—Fatal & nonfatal pancreatitis with d4T + ddI ± hydroxyurea.
    • Fatal lactic acidosis/steatosis in pregnant women receiving d4T + ddI. Possible ↑ toxicity if used with Ribavirin. Motor weakness in the setting of lactic acidosis mimicking the clinical presentation of Guillain-Barré syndrome (including respiratory failure) (rare).
  • Most Common:
    • Diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, headache
  • HIV
