Lyme Disease, Arthritis

Clinical Setting

  • Arthritis occurs as a late manifestation of Lyme Disease.
  • The reported frequency varies from <10% to up to 60% of untreated patients.
  • Most commonly presents as monoarticular or oligo-articular arthritis of the knees.
  • Synovial fluid: 500 to >100,000 cells/μL; neutrophil predominate.
  • For details on diagnostic methods see: Lyme Disease, Diagnosis, and IDSA Lyme Disease Guidelines (Clin Infect Dis 72:e1, 2020) 
  • May be able to detect organism in joint fluid by PCR;  although antibody tests are preferred method of diagnosis with PCR of joint fluid serving as confirmatory test when uncertain

Etiologies

  • Borrelia burgdorferi

Primary Regimens

  • Adult:
    • Doxycycline 100 mg po bid x 28 days
    • Amoxicillin 500 mg po tid   x 28 days
    • Cefuroxime axetil 500 mg po bid x 28 days.
  • Pediatric:
    • Doxycycline 4.4 mg/kg/day po divided bid x 28 days (max 100 mg bid)
    • Amoxicillin 50 mg/kg/d po divided tid x 28 days
    • Cefuroxime axetil 30 mg/kg /day po divided bid x 28 days
      • Note: Cefuroxime susp not available in the US, may be available in other markets.
  • Add NSAIDs for symptom relief
  • If only partial response, treat for an additional 2 -4 weeks with IV therapy (Ceftriaxone preferred); if this fails, refer to rheumatologist

Alternative Regimens

  • Adult:
    • Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q24h x 14-28 days
    • Penicillin G 20–24 million units IV/day x 14-28 days
  • Child (Age ≥ 8 yrs): Ceftriaxone 50-75 mg/kg/d IV x 14-28 days
  • If only partial response, treat for an additional 30 days

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Pediatrics: Doxycycline safe for up to 21 days regardless of age (AAP Red Book 2018, Section 4; J Pediatr 166:1246, 2015).

Comments

  • Response to treatment of late Lyme Disease may require weeks to months for improvement.
  • 2020 Clinical practice guidelines (Neurology 96:262, 2021).
  • Review: JAMA 315:1767 & 2461, 2016
  • Summary: Med Lett 58:57, 2016
