Brincidofovir
Tradename: Tembexa

Usage and Dosing

  • Brincidofovir is a nucleotide analog indicated for the treatment of human smallpox (variola) disease in adult and pediatric patients, including neonates.
  • Because smallpox is eradicated, the effectiveness of brincidofovir was studied in animals infected with viruses that are closely related to the variola virus. Safety information was derived from clinical trials of the drug for a non-smallpox use.
  • Brincidofovir is a lipid conjugate prodrug of cidofovir. The lipid conjugate mimics a natural lipid and thus uses endogenous uptake pathways. Intracellular hydrolysis of the lipid ester linkage liberates cidofovir, which is then phosphorylated to produce cidofovir diphosphate. Cidofovir diphosphate inhibits orthopoxvirus DNA polymerase.
  • Two formulations: tablets and oral suspension.
    • Tablets can be taken on an empty stomach or with a low-fat meal.
    • The oral suspension should be taken on an empty stomach. Can also be administered by NG or gastrostomy tube.
  • Black-box warning: An increase in mortality was a observed in a randomized, placebo-controlled phase 3 trial for CMV prevention. The dose of brincidofovir was 100 mg twice weekly for up to 14 weeks. All-cause mortality at week 24 was 16% in the brincidofovir group compared to 10% in placebo group.
  • Based on animal data, may cause fetal harm. Perform pregnancy testing in patients of childbearing potential before initiating therapy. Females should use effective contraception during treatment and for at least two months after the second dose. Males should use condoms during treatment and for at least four months after the second dose.
  • Considered a potential human carcinogen. Do not crush or divide tablets. Avoid direct contact with broken or crushed tablets, or with the oral suspension.

Adverse Effects

  • Safety information was derived from clinical trials of the drug for a non-smallpox use.
  • Increased AST (7% of patients), ALT, bilirubin (2%). Generally reversible, did not require discontinuation of therapy.
  • Diarrhea (8%).
  • Other GI adverse effects: nausea 5%, vomiting 4%, abdominal pain 3%
  • Less common: decreased appetite, dysgeusia, muscle weakness, rash.
  • Considered a potential human carcinogen.
  • May irreversibly impair fertility, based on testicular toxicity in animal studies.
