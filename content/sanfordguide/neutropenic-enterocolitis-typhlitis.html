Neutropenic Enterocolitis, Typhlitis

Clinical Setting

  • Pathogenesis: Incompletely understood but involves direct cytotoxicity from chemotherapy on mucosa followed by invasion of gut by intestinal microbes in association with neutropenia. Cecum is almost always involved but can extend to proximal small or large bowel.
  • Epidemiology: Occurs primarily in patients receiving chemotherapy for hematological malignancy or undergoing stem cell transplantation.
  • Clinical manifestations: fever, abdominal cramping, tenderness, pain, distention, diarrhea, and/or intestinal bleeding.

Etiologies

  • Polymicrobial infection often involving gram positives, gram negatives (including Pseudomonas), anaerobes (Bacteroides spp. and Clostridium spp.), and Candida spp.

Primary Regimens

  • Bowel rest (consider TPN) + (Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h or Imipenem-cilastatin 500 mg IV q6h or Meropenem 2 gm IV q8h). See pip-tazo drug page for comment on loading dose.

Alternative Regimens

  • Cefepime 2 gm IV q8h + Metronidazole 500 mg IV q8h

Comments

  • Obtain surgical evaluation in patients with acute abdomen, bowel perforation, bowel wall thickening > 10 mm, and persistent hemorrhage.
  • Consider addition of antifungal therapy (echinocandin) to cover Candida in refractory cases.
  • Always screen for C. difficile colitis, clinical picture can be similar (Clin Infect Dis 27:700, 1998).
  • References: Clin Infect Dis 56:711, 2013; Hematol Oncol Clin North Am 24:577, 2010.
