DDx: Anemia, HIV

Clinical Syndrome

  • Anemia in HIV/AIDS patients is multifactorial, i.e., infection, malignancy, vitamin deficiency, chronic disease, hemolysis, and drugs may play a role

Differential Diagnosis

  • Infection  examples:
    • Disseminated mycobacteria or fungi.
      • Infiltrate bone marrow. Inhibit progenitor cells. Diagnosis by culture and biopsy.
    • Parvo B19 virus (N Engl J Med 350:586, 2004). Infects RBC precursors in marrow with subsequent maturation arrest.
      • Bone marrow shows giant pronormoblasts.
        Diagnosis: serology and blood PCR.
  • Malignancy:
    • Non-Hodgkins lymphoma
    • HHV 8:
      • Castleman's Disease
      • Kaposi's sarcoma (rare)
  • Vitamins and nutrition:
    • B12 & Folate. Due to poor dietary intake and reduced intestinal absorption. Iron deficiency due to bleeding.
  • Chronic Anemia:
    • Decreased serum iron, decreased iron binding capacity but normal or increased serum ferritin. True iron deficiency: Decreased serum iron, increased iron binding capacity and decreased ferritin.
  • Hemolysis:
    • With AIDS, positive direct Coomb’s common (18%) but hemolysis rare. Can be drug-induced: e.g., after dapsone or primaquine in G6PD deficient patient. See Transfusion 46:1237, 2006.
  • Drug-induced bone marrow suppression:
    • Zidovudine (most common: majority develop macrocytosis & roughly 25% develop anemia)
    • Ganciclovir
    • Valganciclovir
    • Amphotericin B
    • TMP-SMX
    • Tenofovir

Management & Treatment

  • Initiate ART
  • Correct reversible causes:
    • Treat infections
    • If indicated, replete folate, vitamin B12, and iron (monitor ferritin)
  • Transfuse if severe
  • If still anemic and symptomatic and serum erythropoietin levels ≤ 500 IU and no evidence of hemolysis or bleeding:
    • Human recombinant erythropoietin 100 units/kg sc 3x/wk
    • Some use darbapentin. Same efficacy and given less often
