Meningitis, Neisseria meningitidis

Clinical Setting

  • Specific/directed therapy for Neisseria meningitidis meningitis.
  • Abnormal CSF with positive culture of CSF or blood for Neisseria meningitidis.
  • PCR may be useful for confirmation of culture-negative cases.
  • Patient presents with fever and headache + meningeal signs, altered mental status, petechial rash.
  • See Comments for chemoprophylaxis
  • Patients at increased risk: post-splenectomy, congenital or iatrogenic (use of eculizumab for paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria) absence of functional terminal complement system components
  • CDC clinical guidance document here.

Etiologies

  • Neisseria meningitidis

Primary Regimens

  • Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q12h x 7 days: preferred regimen
    • Children 50 mg/kg IV max dose 2 gm) q12h
  • Penicillin G 4 million units IV q4h x 7 days: confirm in vitro susceptibility (MIC < 0.1 mcg/ml) if used
    • Children 300,000 units/kg/day in 4-6 divided doses

Alternative Regimens

  • Meropenem 2 gm IV q8h
  • Cefotaxime 2 gm IV q4-6h (where available)
    • Children: Cefotaxime dosed based on weight and gestational age
  • Ampicillin 2 gm IV q4h 
    • Children 150-200 mg/kg/d in 6 divided doses
  • Ciprofloxacin 400 mg IV q8h for serious beta-lactam allergy (limited clinical experience, see Comments)
  • Chloramphenicol 12.5 mg/kg (up to 1 gm) IV q6h (likely less effective than other regimens [J Antimicrob Chemother 70:979, 2015] and not recommended if other options are available).

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Increasing non-susceptibility to penicillin (MMWR 69:735, June 19, 2020; Clin Infect Dis. 2021; 73:1185; J  Infect Dis. 2022; 225:1; Ceftriaxone unaffected, nonsusceptibility to it is extremely rare.

Prevention

  • See Meningococcal ACWY, Vaccines and Meningococcal B, Vaccines for indications, available products, dosing, and vaccine characteristics for pre-exposure prevention.
    • MenB regimens may vary during outbreak events.
  • If an agent other than Ceftriaxone is used, administer Ceftriaxone, Rifampin, or Ciprofloxacin to eradicate nasal carriage in the same doses used for post-exposure chemorophylaxis below.

Comments

  • Ciprofloxacin is highly active in vitro and likely achieves therapeutic levels in CSF, but clinical experience is extremely limited and it should be reserved for use only when there is severe penicillin or beta-lactam allergy.
  • Dexamethasone of no proven benefit in meningococcal meningitis and may be discontinued if initiated empirically pending a microbiological diagnosis (see review in Cochrane Database Syst Rev 6:CD004405, 2013).
  • Chemoprophylaxis
    • Rifampin 10 mg/kg (max dose 600 mg) q12h  x 2 days (adult or child > 1 mo)
    • Ceftriaxone single IM dose of 250 mg (adult) or 125 mg (child age < 15 years
    • Ciprofloxacin single 500 mg dose (age > 18 yrs; not recommended in pregnant or lactating women, or if Ciprofloxacin resistant isolates circulating in the local community)
