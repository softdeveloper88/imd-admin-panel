Fever in Returning Travelers

Clinical Setting

  • Undifferentiated fever (no localizing signs), with or without rash, after international travel.
  • Travelers who become ill during, or any time up to several months after, a foreign trip will frequently associate that illness with a possible travel-specific cause.
    • This may be the case, but often it is not.

Diagnosis

  • First, assess for life-threatening disease, especially if hemorrhagic manifestations are present.
    • Initial diagnostics:  
      • Stat blood films (malaria)
        • Rapid diagnostic test (Binax) less reliable to rule out malaria but result often immediately available and a positive test needs stat treatment.
        • NEW: A negative RDT (US: Binax; HRP based) no longer reliably rules out P. falciparum infection due to the increasing global prevalence of HRP2/3 deletion mutants.
          • A negative RDT should be quickly followed by a malaria blood film. Malar J 2022 21:26, 2022.
      • COVID-19 test, blood cultures (for sepsis, enteric fever), complete blood cell count with differential and platelets
      • Liver function tests, urinalysis, and a chest radiograph
      • LP if indicated
    • Consider treatable causes: e.g.:  malaria, viral hemorrhagic fevers, meningococcemia, rickettsial infection, bacterial sepsis and exotic viral respiratory pathogens.
    • Laboratory findings associated with different diseases:
      • Leukopenia: Dengue, Zika, chikungunya, typhoid, brucellosis, rickettsial disease, or acute HIV infection.
      • Thrombocytopenia: Malaria, dengue, or brucellosis.
      • Eosinophilia: Schistosomiasis or strongyloidiasis.
      • Transaminitis (elevated AST/ALT): Viral hepatitis, toxin damage, leptospirosis, rickettsial disease (Q-fever), relapsing fever, Yellow Fever, amebic abscess, brucellosis, typhoid, hemorrhagic fever, and dengue.
  • The second wave of diagnostic testing:
    • Driven by  abnormalities in the initial test results or emerging physical signs or new history.
      • HIV testing (may point to usual OIs)
      • Rickettsial PCR and serology for other tickborne disease
      • Syphilis testing
      • IGRA or TST for Tuberculosis
      • Commercial multiplex PCR global fever panel FDA approved in 2023 
      • Wait 48 to 72 hours before serology and sophisticated diagnostic studies for rare exotic disease are pursued. 
      • In the absence of enlightening abnormalities, may need serologic studies  based on travel itinerary, incubation periods, and known exposures.
      • At this stage, the differential diagnosis expands (review pertinent exposure history)
    • For etiologies by geographic location or exposure and clinical syndromes, see New Engl J Med 2017; 376: 548 and  Ann Intern Med 158:456, 2013.
    • N>53,000 specimens from returned travelers analyzed at UK national reference lab for imported pathogens 5 most prevalent, in order, were, dengue, chikungunya, Zika, leptospirosis and spotted fever group Rickettsia (J Travel Med, Jan 27, 2023 online ahead of print)

    • Other frequent diseases in travelers:
      • Influenza A (year-round tropics; seasonal temperate zones), acute HIV, mononucleosis, acute histoplamosis, measles, varicella, tuberculosis.
    • Infrequent diseases in travelers to consider:  
      • Avian influenza (H5N1 & H7N9; now extremely rare in humans), brucellosis, MERS coronavirus, African hemorrhagic fevers (Ebola incubation is 8-12 days), CCHF, yellow fever, Japanese encephalitis, monkeypox, relapsing fever, acute toxoplasmosis (raw meat ingestion), arboviruses including Ross River, tickborne encephalitis, West Nile virus (emerging in Europe), hantavirus (both hantavirus  cardiopulmonary syndrome in the Americas and hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome caused by Puumala and Dobravavirus in Europe), tuberculosis.
