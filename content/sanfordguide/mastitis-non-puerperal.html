Mastitis, Non-Puerperal

Clinical Setting

  • Non-puerperal mastitis is uncommon in non-lactating women.
  • Inflammatory breast cancer can mimic non-puerperal mastitis.
  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis a consideration in chronic infections.
  • Granulomatous mastitis (Breast J. 2024 Jan 25:2024:6693720) is an idiopathic inflammatory disease that can mimic inflammatory breast cancer or infection.

Etiologies

  • Staph. aureus
  • Bacteroides sp. (less often)
  • Peptoniphilus sp. (formerly Peptostreptococcus)
  • Coagulase-negative staphylococci (selected)
  • Corynebacterium sp. (rare)

Primary Regimens

  • If MRSA is not present:
    • Outpatient: Dicloxacillin 500 mg po qid or Cephalexin 500 mg po qid
    • Inpatient: Nafcillin 2 gm IV q4h or Oxacillin 2 gm IV q4h
  • If MRSA is present or possible:
    • Outpatient: TMP-SMX-DS 1-2 tabs po bid or, if susceptible, Clindamycin 300 mg po qid

    • Inpatient: Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL)

  • If subareolar & odoriferous, most likely anaerobes; add Metronidazole 500 mg IV/po tid

Alternative Regimens

  • For granulomatous mastitis due to Corynebacterium species: Doxycycline 100 mg po bid for 3-4 weeks

Comments

  • Rarely a chronic mastitis can occur due to a Corynebacterium species. Distinctive granulomatous inflammation. Beta-lactams fail despite in vitro activity (J Clin Micro 53:2895, 2015; Clin Infect Dis 59:410, 2014; Clin Infect DIs 74:1494, 2022).
  • Consider inflammatory carcinoma in older women without clear abscess, especially if microcalcification, or a breast mass is detected radiographically.
  • Drainage, either by ultrasound guided needle aspiration or surgical, indicated for abscess (Radiographics 31:1683, 2011).
