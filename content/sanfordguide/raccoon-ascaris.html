Baylisascaris procyonis, Raccoon roundworm

Clinical Setting

  • Eosinophilic meningoencephalitis; generally fulminant and fatal. Mostly infants and toddlers.
  • Symptoms: weakness, incoordination, ataxia, irritability, weakness, seizures, altered mental status, stupor, and/or coma.
    • with onset of neurologic symptoms and signs, significant pathology generally is already present.
    • may be associated with macular rash, abdominal pain, hepatomegaly, and pneumonitis
  • Visceral larva migrans with a tendency to invade the spinal cord, brain, and eye of humans, resulting in permanent neurologic damage, blindness, or death.
  • Unlike in toxocariasis, larvae do not die in humans and continue to grow and wander through tissue
  • Asymptomatic infection found in 7% of population in a high-risk area (Emerg Infect Dis 23:1397, 2017).  In US mostly in northern states and west coast
  • Cases with survivors reviewed in Pediatrics 129:e806, 2012.
  • Caused by ingestion of eggs in raccoon, (rarely, skunk, dog, or kinkajou) feces.
  • Early treatment when first suspected, including after high-risk exposures to raccoon latrines, presence of Baylisascaris eggs in the feces of the implicated animal or animals, and suspected oral exposure to raccoon feces in an area where the prevalence of raccoon infection is known to be high.

Etiologies

  • Baylisascaris procyonis

Diagnostics

  • No widely available serologic diagnostics outside of CDC in US.
  • Immunoblot for detection of antibodies to recombinant (rBpRAG-1) in serum or CSF
    • Extent of asymptomatic human infection not known
  • Ocular exam may be diagnostic in expert hands

Primary Regimens

  • Immediately post exposure try Albendazole 400 mg bid po x 3-4 weeks minimum
  • Pediatrics (>2 yrs of age): Albendazole 25-50 mg/kg/day po
  • Laser photocoagulation of ocular larvae seen on fundoscopy

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • Albendazole has best CSF penetration of anthelmintic agents, ivermectin should not be used due to poor penetration.
  • is important as symptoms lag behind damage caused by larvae.
    • radiologic CNS findings lag clinical manifestations.
  • If clinical disease present may try steroids (Clin Infect Dis 39:1484, 2004) and always for ocular disease.
  • General review: Clin Microbiol Rev. 2016,29:375
  • Other causes of eosinophilic meningitis include Angiostrongylus cantonensis and Gnathostoma spinigerum.
