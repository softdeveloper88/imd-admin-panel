Chromoblastomycosis

Clinical Setting

  • Chromoblastomycosis is a chronic infection of the skin and subcutaneous tissues, caused by melanized fungi. This is differentiated from eumycetoma (chronic infection of subcutaneous and contiguous tissues due to fungi and/or bacteria), and phaeohyphomycosis (dematiaceous fungi not limited to the skin or subcutaneous tissues and may involve any organ system).
  • Presents as a chronic and indolent mycotic lesion, scaly or verrucous, plaque-like or nodular, evolving from a remote inoculation injury (which may not even be recalled) over a period of years.
  • Lesions usually seen on feet or legs, most common in tropical areas.

Etiologies

  • Most common:
    • Cladophialophora carrionii
    • Fonsecaea pedrosoi
  • Less common:
    • Phialophora verrucosa
    • Rhinocladiella aquaspersa
    • Exophiala sp.

Primary Regimens

  • Small/few lesions:
    • Itraconazole 300 mg po daily or Terbinafine 250 mg po daily given 3 months before surgical excision and for an additional 6-9 months
  • Chronic, extensive, burrowing lesions:
    • Itraconazole 200-400 mg po daily x 6-12 months or until response followed by long-term suppressive therapy

Alternative Regimens

  • Itraconazole 200-400 mg daily + Flucytosine 1500 mg po 4x daily
  • Itraconazole 200-400 mg daily + Terbinafine 250 mg by mouth daily
    Posaconazole (NOTE: different dosing delayed release tabs/IV vs. suspension, better levels achieved with delayed-release tabs)
    • Delayed-release tabs 300 mg po bid x 2 doses and then 300 mg po daily OR
    • Suspension 400 mg po bid
  • Anecdotal report of benefit from adding topical imiquimod, 5%, to Itraconazole or Itraconazole plus Terbinafine. Imiquimod was applied 5x/week. Initial increase in inflammation and then slow healing over months (Clin Infect Dis 58:1734, 2014).
