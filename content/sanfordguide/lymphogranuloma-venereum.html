Lymphogranuloma venereum

Clinical Setting

  • Transient painless genital ulcer with painful inguinal lymphadenopathy with unilateral "groove sign" (Clin Infect Dis 2015; 61:S865)
  • Rectal exposure (men, women) can result in proctocolitis (fever, pain, discharge, rectal ulcer, bleeding) (Dis Colon Rectum 52:507, 2009)
    • Infection may be asymptomatic in up to 25% (Sex Transm Infect 89:548, 2013)
    • Most case series reported in MSM with clinical findings that may be confused with inflammatory bowel disease.
  • Treat presumptively while awaiting diagnosis with NAAT.  

Etiologies

  • Chlamydia trachomatis (Serovars L1, L2, L3)

Diagnosis

  • Definitive diagnosis only with LGV-specific molecular testing (e.g., PCR-based genotyping), but not widely available
  • Nucleic acid amplification tests (NAAT) of rectal sample for C. trachomatis will be positive.
  • Chlamydia serology (complement fixation or microimmunofluorescence) should not be used routinely but may be helpful in NAAT negative cases.
  • Biopsy is contraindicated because sinus tracts develop.

Primary Regimens

  • Doxycycline 100 mg po bid x 21 days
    • Evaluate sexual partners with contact within 60 days before onset of symptoms and treat presumptively for Chlamydia trachomatis with Azithromycin 1 gm po single dose OR Doxycycline 100 mg po bid x 7 days

Alternative Regimens

  • Azithromycin 1 gm po once weekly x 3 weeks (Clin Infect Dis 2021 73:614)
  • Erythromycin 500 mg po qid x 21 days
  • For alternative regimens, test for cure with NAAT (4 weeks after diagnosis)

Comments

  • HIV, Syphilis and other STI testing should be performed on all patients who present with Lymphogranuloma venereum (LGV).
  • All patients treated for LGV should be retested at 3 mo for Chlamydia. 
  • Those whose HIV test results are negative should be offered HIV PrEP.
  • LGV proctitis, if not treated promptly, can result in colorectal fistulas and strictures.
  • Longer treatment (3 weeks) needed because infection is more invasive and more difficult to eradicate than most genital infections.
  • Follow clinically until all signs and symptoms resolved. 
  • See 2021 CDC STD Guidelines: MMWR Recomm Rep 70 (RR-4):1 2021
  • Review non syphilis non HSV genital ulcers: Infect Dis Clin North Am 37:369 2023.
