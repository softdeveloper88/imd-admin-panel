Burns, Infected Wound

Clinical Setting

  • Infected burn wounds. Proposed standardized definition: J Burn Care Res 28:776, 2007.
  • For surgical debridement of infected necrotic tissue, application of skin grafts and/or skin substitutes, and /or topical medications. See JAMA 283:716, 2000; Clin Plastic Surg 36: 597, 2009.
  • Critically ill patient with suspected burn wound sepsis.
  • Ideal care is in dedicated burn unit.
  • Time line for likely pathogens (Clin Infect DIs 2017;65:2130).

Etiologies

  • Streptococcus pyogenes
  • Enterobacter sp.
  • Staphylococcus aureus
  • Staphylococcus epidermidis
  • Enterococcus faecalis
  • Escherichia coli
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Fungi (rare)(Clin.Infect.Dis. 2024; 79:682)
  • Herpes virus (rare).

Primary Regimens

  • Quantitative wound cultures, blood cultures, and then empiric antimicrobial therapy
  • Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator); alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL + (Meropenem 1 gm IV q8h or Cefepime 2 gm IV q8h) + Fluconazole 400 mg IV once daily, if Candida infection suspected
  • Adjust any empirical regimen based on culture results.

Alternative Regimens

  • If allergic or intolerant to Vancomycin, could use Daptomycin, 6-12 mg/kg IV once daily
  • if IgE mediated allergy to beta-lactams, could use Aztreonam 2 gm IV q6h
  • If ESBL or carbapenemase producing MDR gram-negative bacillus: 
    • See Highly-resistant Gram-negative Bacilli for details

Comments

  • Other complications of concern in critically ill burn patient: S. aureus toxic shock syndrome (TSS), suppurative phlebitis, pneumonia.
  • Review: Initial burn management. N Engl J Med.2019l, 380:2349
