Neoehrlichia mikurensis

Clinical Setting

  • Emerging tick-borne illness (caused by Ca. Neoehrlichia mikurensis)
  • Systemic inflammatory syndrome, predominantly occurring among those with underlying hematologic or autoimmune disease on immunosuppressive therapy (e.g. rituximab) and /or are asplenic.  Infliximab treated individuals tend to have asymptomatic or subclinical cases.  Organism invades vascular endothelium. Months-long carriage is common.
  • Under-recognized; often missed, not considered and not tested for.
  • Transmitted by hard ticks (Ixodes); closely associated with rodents as pathogen reservoir.  Possibly transmitted via blood transfusion, though not proven.
  • Geographic distribution mostly in Europe, The Netherlands, and Scandinavia (no known de novo cases in the US thus far); sporadic case reports in Asia (China, S. Korea).
  • Most common symptoms are:  Fever, myalgias, arthritis / arthralgia, headache, night sweats, and vascular / thromboembolic events (deep venous thrombosis, pulmonary embolism. transitory ischemic attacks, and arteritis, mimicking polyarteritis or giant cell arteritis).  

Diagnosis

  • Difficult to diagnose.  Must use PCR genotyping of plasma or Buffy coat; this may explain, in part, the relatively low number of reported or known cases.
  • Lab typically shows leukocytosis with lymphopenia, anemia, and elevated C-reactive protein.

Etiologies

  • Candidatus Neoehrlichia mikurensis (intracellular bacterium);  Family Anaplasmataceae 

Primary Regimens

  • Doxycycline 100 mg orally twice daily for 2 - 3 weeks

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • The disease must be thought of to render a diagnosis.
  • Differential diagnosis includes Lyme disease, tick-borne-encephalitis, Mediterranean Spotted Fever, and human granulocytic anaplasmosis.
  • References: Exp and Applied Acarology 68: 279, 2015; Clin Infect Dis 73: e2364, 2021; Clin Infect Dis 73: e2372, 2021
