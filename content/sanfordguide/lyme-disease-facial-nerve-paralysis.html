Lyme Disease, Facial Nerve Paralysis

Clinical Setting

  • Early neurologic Lyme Disease is commonly manifest as unilateral or bilateral Bell's Palsy.
  • Any cranial nerve can be involved in early Lyme Disease but the 7th cranial nerve is the most common (approximately 80% of all Lyme cranial neuropathies).
  • Usually occurs in the absence of concomitant meningitis.
  • Other presentations of peripheral nervous system disorders include: radiculoneuritis, plexopathies, mononeuropathy, and mononeuropathy multiplex.

  • For details on diagnostic methods see: Lyme Disease, Diagnosis and Emerging Infect Dis 22:1169, 2016.

Etiologies

  • Borrelia burgdorferi

Primary Regimens

  • Negative CSF exam
  • Adult:
    • Doxycycline 100 mg po bid x 14 days
    • Amoxicillin 500 mg po tid x 14 days
  • Child:
    • Doxycycline 4.4 mg/kg/day po divided bid x 21 days (max 100 mg bid) 
    • Amoxicillin 50 mg/kg/d po divided tid x 28 days

Alternative Regimens

  • Adult: Cefuroxime axetil 500 mg po bid x 14 days OR, if abnormal CSF exam, Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q24h x 14 days
  • Child (Age ≥8 yrs): Ceftriaxone 50-75 mg/kg/d IV x 14 days

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Pediatrics: Doxycycline can be safely administered for up to 21 days regardless of age (AAP Red Book 2018, Section 4; J Pediatr 166:1246, 2015).

Comments

  • Suggest CSF evaluation to exclude Lyme meningitis. If CSF exam is negative, use an oral regimen.
  • If CSF exam shows pleocytosis, use IV regimen: Ceftriaxone.
  • For Bell's palsy, corticosteroids may be used if administered within 72 hours of onset of symptoms
  • 2020 Clinical practice guidelines (Neurology 96:262, 2021) and (Clin Infect Dis 72:e1-e48, 2021)
  • See Reviews: N Engl J Med 370:1724, 2014; JAMA 315:1767 & 2461, 2016.
