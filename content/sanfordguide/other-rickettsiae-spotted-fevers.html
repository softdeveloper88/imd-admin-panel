Other Rickettsiae, Spotted Fevers

Clinical Setting

  • Spotted fevers (other than Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever) are caused by various Rickettsiae species and found on 6 continents (Clin Micro Rev 26:657, 2013; MMWR Recomm and Rep 65(2): 1-44, 2016).
  • Clinical diagnosis in patients with exposure to ticks or mites and presenting with rash or localized eschar (Tache noire):
    • Fever
    • Intense myalgia and headache
    • Thrombocytopenia is common
  • Susceptible to doxycycline

Diagnosis

  • Definitive diagnosis requires PCR of blood, skin biopsy or sequential antibody tests.

Primary Regimens

  • Doxycycline 100 mg po bid x 7 days (See Comments)

Alternative Regimens

  • Chloramphenicol 500 mg po/IV qid x 7 days
  • Child age < 8 years: (Azithromycin 10 mg/kg/day x 1 dose) or (Clarithromycin 7.5 mg/kg q12h x 3 days)(if mild disease)

Comment

  • Pediatrics. Use of tetracyclines in pediatric patients has historically been limited because of reports of permanent tooth discoloration in children <8 years of age caused by incorporation of the drugs and their colored degradation products in enamel.
    • Doxycycline binds less readily to calcium compared with other members of the tetracycline class, and recent comparative data suggest that doxycycline is not likely to cause visible teeth staining or enamel hypoplasia in children <8 years of age.
    • Doxycycline can safely be administered for short durations (≤21 days) regardless of patient age (AAP Red Book 2018, Section 4; J Pediatr 166:1246, 2015).
  • African Tick Bite Fever: case report of success with Rifampin 450 mg po bid x 10 days (Doxycycline intolerant patient)(Clin Infect Dis 2017; 65:1582)
  • In randomized trial of 40 patients with Mediterranean Spotted Fever, Clarithromycin 500 mg po q12h x 5 days and Doxycycline 200 mg po q12h x 2 doses equally effective (Antimicrob Ag Chemother 60:1642, 2016).
  • Treatment reference: Expert Rev of Anti-infective Therapy 10:1425, 2012.
