Conjunctivitis, Bacterial

Clinical Setting

  • Suppurative bacterial conjunctivitis, non-chlamydial 
  • Redness and purulent discharge, usually unilateral, but may be bilateral.
  • See Conjunctivitis, Trachoma or Conjunctivitis, Inclusion for recommendations for treatment of infections due to Chlamydia trachomatis

Etiology

  • Bacterial, non-gonococcal/non-chlamydial:
    • Staph. aureus
    • Strep. pneumoniae
    • H. influenzae
    • Viridans streptococci
    • Moraxella catarrhalis
  • Gonococcal: N. gonorrhoeae

Primary Regimens

  • Bacterial, non-gonococcal, non-chlamydial:
    • Ciprofloxacin 0.3% ophthalmic solution 1–2 gtts q2h while awake 1st 2 days, then q4–8h up to 7 days.
    • Polymyxin B + Trimethoprim ophthalmic solution 1–2 gtts q3–6h x 7–10 days.
  • Gonococcal:
    • Pediatric: Ceftriaxone 25-50 mg/kg IV/IM (not to exceed 125 mg) as 1 dose
    • Adult: Ceftriaxone 1 gm IM/IV as 1 dose

Alternative Regimens

  • Bacterial,  non-gonococcal, non-chlamydial:
    • Azithromycin 1% ophthalmic solution 1 drop twice a day for 2 days; then 1 drop daily for 5 days

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Fluoroquinolones offer the best spectrum of activity for empiric therapy. High concentrations increase the likelihood of activity vs Staph. aureus, including MRSA.

Comments

  • Topical corticosteroids are contraindicated.
  • Bacteria, non-gonococcal, non-chlamydial:
    • Numerous other topical ophthalmic antibacterial solutions or ointments are also effective including Gentamicin, Tobramycin, Levofloxacin, Gatifloxacin, Moxifloxacin, Erythromycin, Sulfacetamide. 
    • See JAMA 2013; 310:1721. 
  • For review of eye infections, see New Engl J Med 2023; 389: 2363 and Clin Infect Dis 2024; 79:e48.
