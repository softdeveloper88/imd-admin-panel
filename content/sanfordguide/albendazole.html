Albendazole
Tradename: Albenza

Usage and Dosing

  • Most useful member of benzimidazole class of drugs.
  • Mode of action: prevents formation of microtubules and stops cell division in adults and eggs.
  • Albendazole resistance develops due to reduced drug binding to beta-tubulin of microtubules.
  • Albendazole plasma concentrations are negligible. The drug is rapidly converted to the sulfoxide metabolite prior to reaching the systemic circulation. Systemic anthelmintic activity is due to albendazole sulfoxide.
  • Single dose therapy with albendazole (400 mg) or mebendazole as used in public health programs has lower cure rates and 3-day albendazole superior to 3-day mebendazole, PLoS One 6:e25003, 2011; BMJ. 2017;358:j4307; E Clin Med 2018,1:7.

Adverse Effects

  • Over 10%: CNS (headache, especially if patient with neurocysticercosis), increased liver-associated enzymes
  • 1-10%: Abdominal pain, nausea/vomiting, and alopecia
  • Less than 1%: leukopenia. CBC every 2 weeks if on prolonged therapy.

Pregnancy Risk

  • Large systematic review: no harm in 1st TM use (Int J Parasitol 2019;49:541)
  • Tx of pregnant women ↓neonatal mortality (PloS Negl Trop Dis 2021;15:e0009282)
FDA Risk Category 1 Category C. Limited data in pregnancy. Evidence suggests no difference in congenital abnormalities in the children of women accidentally treated with albendazole during MDA campaigns compared with those who were not. In MDA campaigns in countries where STH are common, WHO allows use in the 2nd and 3rd trimester. Balance the risk of treatment for the fetus with the risk of disease progression in the untreated woman.
Use during Lactation Data limited. Minimally excreted in human milk. WHO: a single oral dose can be given safely to lactating women.
