Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever

Clinical Setting

  • Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever (RMSF) is a tick-borne, potentially life-threatening rickettsial disease. The highest incidence in the United States is in the Southeastern and South-central states.
    • Review: MMWR RR 65(2): 1-44, 2016.
    • Signs and Symptoms:
      • Fever, rash (95%), petechiae 40–50%. Rash spreads from distal extremities to trunk. Rash may not appear during the first 72 hours of illness in over 50% of the patients.
      • Clinical picture in tribal areas of Arizona are more subtle, e.g, many patients present without fever, rash or thrombocytopenia (Clin Infect Dis 60: 1650, 2015; Clin Infect Dis 60:1659, 2015). History of exposure to dog ticks helpful.
    • RMSF can mimic Ehrlichiosis. Pattern of spread of the rash is helpful in making a diagnosis.
  • Early therapy is needed to avoid excess mortality; Untreated, RMSF mortality ranges from 25-70%: MMWR RR 65(2): 1-44, 2016
    • Risk factors for death: age >60, delayed therapy, failure to use doxycycline.
    • In endemic areas and with tick exposure, best to start empiric doxycycline early.
    • Again, absence of skin rash should not delay therapy. Only 3–18% of patients present with fever, rash and history of tick exposure (tick bite is painless); many early deaths, especially in children. MMWR 49:885, 2000.
  • Do not hesitate to use doxycycline in children with suspected RMSF(see primary regimen comment).
  • Clinical diagnosis:
    • Fever in an endemic area
    • Known or possible tick bite
    • With or without rash
  • Diagnosis may be confirmed by sequential antibody titers or immunohistology on skin biopsy section.
  • Meningoencephalitis due to  RMSF in 11 children and 8 adults (Clin Infect Dis 2020;71:188)."Starry sky" sign (multifocal punctate diffusion restricting or T2 hyperintense lisions) was seen on MRI in all children but no adults.

Etiologies

  • Rickettsia rickettsii (Dermacentor ticks) (female D. variabilis tick-above; male-below)

Primary Regimens

  • Doxycycline 100 mg po/IV bid x 7 days or for 2 days after temperature becomes normal (do not use in pregnancy);
    • Critical illness: some experts suggest a loading dose of Doxycycline 200 mg IV/po q12h x 72 hrs and then 100 mg IV/po bid
      • Use of tetracyclines in pediatric patients has historically been limited because of reports of permanent tooth discoloration in children <8 years of age caused by incorporation of the drugs and their colored degradation products in enamel.
        • Doxycycline binds less readily to calcium compared with other members of the tetracycline class, and recent comparative data suggest that doxycycline is not likely to cause visible teeth staining or enamel hypoplasia in children <8 years of age.
          • Doxycycline can safely be administered for short durations (≤21 days) regardless of patient age (AAP Red Book 2018, Section 4; J Pediatr 166:1246, 2015).
  • Pregnancy: Chloramphenicol 50 mg/kg/day in 4 divided doses po x 7 days.
    • May not be possible to obtain chloramphenicol in a reasonable time frame.
      • If so, justified in using Doxycycline despite risk of bone and dental abnormalities in the fetus and possible liver injury in the mother

Alternative Regimens

  • Chloramphenicol 50 mg/kg/day in 4 divided doses po x 7 days

Comments

  • In endemic areas, a high level of suspicion is needed for patient's with fever, rash and history of tick exposure.
  • Empiric Doxycycline is a reasonable measure to avoid excessive mortality, MMWR 49:885, 2000.
  • Prophylaxis after a tick bite alone is not indicated as less than 1% of Dermacentor ticks in endemic areas carry R. rickettsii.
  • RMSF, in general:  MMWR RR 65(2): 1-44, 2016.
