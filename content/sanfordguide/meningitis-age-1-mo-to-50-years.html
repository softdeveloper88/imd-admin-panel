Meningitis, Age 1 mo to 50 years

Clinical Setting

  • Classical signs of meningitis, headache, fever, altered mental status, stiff neck, vomiting, seizures.
  • CSF pleocytosis (white cells often, but not always > 500), predominantly polymorphonuclear leukocytes.
  • NOTE: Do not delay LP and empiric antibacterials while waiting for CT scan of head (Clin Infect Dis 2018;66:321)

Etiologies

  • Strep. pneumoniae
  • Meningococci
  • H. influenzae (now very rare)
  • Listeria monocytogenes (immunosuppressed or immunocompromised patient, pregnancy)
  • S. agalactiae and E. coli (Age 1-23 months)

Primary Regimens

  • Adult:
    • [(Cefotaxime 2 gm IV q4–6h (where available outside the US) or Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q12h)] + Vancomycin 45-60 mg/kg/day IV, divided into q6-8h doses + Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg IV q6h x 2–4 days administered with or just before 1st dose of antibiotic
    • If Listeria is a consideration, add Ampicillin 2 gm IV q4h to the regimen; if CSF becomes culture-positive for Listeria, discontinue the dexamethasone.
  • Pediatric:
    • [(Cefotaxime 225-300 mg/kg/day IV divided q6-8h (where available) or Ceftriaxone 100 mg/kg/day IV divided q12h)] + Vancomycin 60 mg/kg/day div q6h + Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg IV q6h x 2–4 days administered with or just before 1st dose of antibiotic for H. influenzae. Adequate data do not exist yet to support dexamethasone use in children for S. pneumoniae.

Alternative Regimens

  • Adult:
    • Meropenem 2 gm IV q8h + Vancomycin 500-750 mg IV q6h (to a maximum of 2-3 gm/day) + Dexamethasone (see Primary)
    • For patients with severe penicillin allergy: Chloramphenicol 12.5 mg/kg IV q6h (max. 4 gm/day) (to cover meningococcus) + TMP-SMX 5 mg/kg q6–8h (to cover Listeria if patient is immunocompromised) + Vancomycin
  • Pediatric:
    • Meropenem 40 mg/kg IV q8h + Vancomycin 60 mg/kg/day q6h (2x the standard adult dose) +/- Dexamethasone (see Primary Regimen)

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • N. meningitidis: Susceptibility testing recommended before switching from empiric ceftriaxone or cefotaxime to penicillin or ampicillin: reported U.S. increase in incidence of penicillin and/or ciprofloxacin resistance (MMWR 69:735, June 19, 2020).

Comments

  • Repeat LP not routinely recommended.
  • High chloramphenicol failure rate in patients with penicillin-resistant Strep. pneumoniae (Lancet 339:405, 1992; Lancet 342:240, 1993).
  • Dexamethasone: value documented in children with H. influenzae B (but not documented for Strep pneumoniae) and adults with Strep pneumoniae (Neurology 79:2177, 2012, Lancet 380:1693, 2012) and possible benefit in neonates (Cochrane Database Syst Rev 2015 Nov 11;(11):CD010435. Use in children in populations with high rates of Hib vaccination is controversial Eur J Paediatr Neurol. 2013 Jan;17(1):24-8
