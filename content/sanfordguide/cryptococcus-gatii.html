Cryptococcus gattii

Clinical Setting

  • Cryptococcus gattii is a species of Cryptococcus previously identified in tropical and subtropical regions. Outbreaks in the U.S. Pacific northwest and British Columbia have been reported (MMWR Morb Mortal Wkly Rep 59:865, 2010; Expert Rev Anti Infect Ther 10:637, 2012).
  • Immunocompetent patients make up a significant portion of the patients who suffer from clinical disease although immunosuppression is associated with worse prognosis (Clin Infect Dis 20:611, 1995). Subclinical immunoglobulin deficiency may predispose to infection (Clin Infect Dis 54:153, 2012).
  • Clinical manifestations include pulmonary and central nervous system diseases similar to what is seen with Cryptococcus neoformans although large cryptococcomas were more common with C. gattii.
  • Cryptococcal antigen testing has a similar sensitivity for the diagnosis of C. gattii as for C. neoformans infections but can help with speciation. Reference labs may be needed to differentiate C. gattii from C. neoformans. Speciation may be helpful to determine treatment plan and set expectation for recovery.
  • Studies to determine optimal treatment regimens are in progress.

Etiologies

  • Cryptococcus gattii

Primary Regimens

  • CNS disease: Treatment based on IDSA guidelines (see Comment) below but consider a minimum duration of 4-6 weeks of induction therapy (Clin Infect Dis 55:789, 2012). Some patients require years of therapy.
  • Recommend starting with Amphotericin B plus flucytosine:
    • See Cryptococcal Meningitis, HIV/AIDS, for drugs and doses
    • See Cryptococcal Meningitis, Non-HIV, for drugs and doses
    • Remove spinal fluid if CSF pressure exceeds 25 cm.
  • Pulmonary/non-CNS disease: Treatment based on guidelines below
    • See Cryptococcosis, Non-meningeal, for drugs and doses

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • Several studies have demonstrated higher azole MICs when compared to C. neoformans (J Antimicrob Chemother 62:205, 2008; J Clin Microbiol 51:3090, 2013).
  • When large and multiple cryptococcomas are present, consider combination therapy for 4–6 weeks, followed by Fluconazole for 6–18 months. Consider surgery if there is compression of vital structures, failure to reduce size of cryptococcoma after 4 weeks of therapy, or failure to thrive.
  • IDSA treatment guidelines: Clin Infect Dis 50:291, 2010, and recent European guidelines review: Lancet Infect Dis 2024 Aug;24(8):e495-e512
