Amebic meningoencephalitis

Clinical Setting

  • Protozoan meningoencephalitis that ranges from acute meningitis to subacute chronic disease depending on the specific organism involved.
  • Naegleria fowleri and Acanthamoeba spp., are commonly found in lakes, swimming pools, tap water, and heating and air conditioning units.
  • Balamuthia mandrillaris and Acanthameba sp. causes chronic granulomatous meningitis (Clin Infect Dis 51:e7, 2010).
    • Balamuthia often presents with antecedent skin lesions
    • U.S. experience 1974-2016 (Clin Infect Dis 68:1815, 2019)
  • Acanthameba castellani causes contact lens associated keratitis. See Acanthameba Keratitis
  • Naegleria fowleri causes acute purulent primary amebic meningitis with 99% mortality Epidemiol Infect 138:968, 2010.
  • CDC has a free living amoeba program, call CDC 24/7 at +1 770-488-7100 but no longer supplies miltefosine.

Diagnosis

  • Pathologic examination for Naegleria. Motile trophozoites on a wet mount of CSF, and a Giemsa-stained smear will show trophozoites with typical morphology. PCR may be used to detect evidence of N. fowleri in CSF and brain tissue.  
  • B. mandrillaris is most reliably detected via immunofluorescence or immunoperoxidase staining of tissue samples: PCR if available. 
    • Acanthameba trophozoites and cysts on microscopic examination of stained smears of biopsy specimens (brain tissue, skin, cornea) or of corneal scrapings, scant in CSF. 
  • Balamuthia mandrillaris and Acanthameba sp.
    • For both need brain tissue for diagnosis; CSF PCR lacks sensitivity.

Etiologies

  • Acanthamoeba sp.: Nearly all cases in immunocompromised patients
  • Balamuthia mandrillaris: occurs in both immune competent and immunocompromised patients.
  • Naegleria fowleri (>95% mortality, MMWR 57:573, 2008): nearly all cases in immune competent patients
  • Sappinia diploidea (few human cases)

Primary Regimens

  • Acanthamoeba sp.
    • Adult IV therapy: Pentamidine + Fluconazole + Miltefosine 50 mg po tid. May add TMP-SMX, Metronidazole, and azithromycin
      • do not use Amphotericin; no in vitro activity
  • Balamuthia mandrillaris (See Clin Infect Dis 51:e7, 2010).
    • Albendazole + (Fluconazole or Itraconazole) + Miltefosine 50 mg po tid  (bid if <45 kg) +/- Pentamidine (poorly tolerated)
    • See Alternative Regimens
  • Naegleria fowleri
    • Amphotericin B  deoxycholate 1.5 mg/kg/day ±  intrathecal + Rifampin 10 mg/kg/day + Fluconazole 10 mg/kg/day IV/po + Miltefosine 50 mg po tid (bid if <45 kg) + azithromycin 500 mg IV/po. Duration of therapy is empiric as there are few survivors
      • Consider adjunctive dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg/day IV in 4 divided doses
      • 11 U.S. survivors (Clin Infect Dis 62:774, 2016); survival dependent on early diagnosis and early Amphotericin.
      • Liposomal amphotericin B less effective in animal models.
    • See Alternative Regimens and Comments for evolving suggestions

Alternative Regimens

  • Some experts would add TMP-SMX, azithromycin, or flucytosine for Balamuthia and flucytosine for Acanthameba
  • Dramatic recovery of single case of Balamuthia GAE with nitroxoline (Emerg Infect Dis 2023; 29:197)
    • Contact <natasha.spottiswoode@ucsf.edu> for logistics of obtaining nitroxoline from Asieris Pharmaceuticals. 

Comments

  • Accumulating anecdotal reports of long-term survivors of Balamuthia and Acanthameba with miltefosine as part of combination therapy: 
    • Now considered first line therapy by CDC and experts
    • Primary source for miltefosine in U.S. is www.impavido.com
  • Search for better therapy for Naegleria:
    • Liposomal amphotericin B has higher in vitro MICs for Naegleria and perhaps other species.
    • In vitro and in mouse survival studies, posaconazole showed fastest and most potent activity.
    • Posaconazole + Azithromycin had additive survival benefit.
      • Authors suggest future combinations should include Posaconazole (to replace fluconazole) 
    • Reference: J Infect Dis 2019;219: 1095.
  • Pentamidine appears clinically successful in some reports and has good in vitro activity against some ameba species but has poor CSF penetration when the blood-brain barrier is intact and has significant general toxicity
  • Amphotericin B,  miltefosine, paromomycin, pentamidine isethionate,  spiramycin have limited in vitro activity against B. mandrillaris: J Eukaryot Microbiol 60:539, 2013.
  • Isavuconazole active in vitro against Acanthamoeba (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 64; e02223, 2020)
