DDx: Hypogonadism, HIV

Clinical Setting

  • Hypogonadism in men is associated with clinical AIDS, wasting, low CD4 cell counts.
  • Improves with antiretroviral therapy (ART).
  • Result of either testicular disease (primary hypogonadism), or, less often, disease of the pituitary gland or hypothalamus (secondary hypogonadism).

Differential Diagnosis

  • Differential diagnosis:
    • Suppression of hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis by glucocorticoids or megestrol
    • Ketoconazole impairment of synthesis of glucocorticoids
    • Psychotropic drugs that produce hyperprolactinemia and testosterone deficiency
    • Impairment of synthesis of testosterone by alcohol, opiates (includes methadone), and marijuana
    • Hepatic cirrhosis is associated with testosterone deficiency, decrease in luteinizing hormone, and low bone mineral density
  • Diagnosis:
    • Suspect if decreased libido, loss of facial and body hair, decrease in muscle mass, gynecomastia, and testicular atrophy. Decreased libido could be due to concomitant neurologic disease, diabetes mellitus, or psychiatric illness.
    • Low AM total and free serum testosterone level.
    • Diurnal variation. Normal levels based on serum collected at 8 AM.
    • Do not measure serum levels during an acute illness
    • If low serum testosterone documented, then need serum levels of LH and FSH to distinguish primary from secondary hypogonadism

Treatment

Primary Regimens

  • Replacement therapy if low serum testosterone and low libido and/or low bone mineral density and/or low body mass despite successful ART
  • Therapy is not indicated if serum testosterone levels are normal
  • Before starting therapy, perform CBC, multichemistry screen, lipid panel, prostate specific antigen
  • If concomitant erectile dysfunction, can use phosphodiesterase inhibitors (sildenafil, vardenafil, tadalafil). Note: selected ARVs (Darunavir, Atazanavir, and Ritonavir) can prolong serum levels of phosphodiesterase inhibitors
  • Specific therapy: Testosterone transdermal patch: one or two 5 mg patches applied h.s. to the skin of the back, thigh, or upper arm.

Alternative Regimens

  • Testosterone gel: 5-10 gm of a 1% gel applied daily to non-genital skin

Comments

  • Cautions:
    • Do not administer testosterone as an injection
    • Do not administer testosterone if there is a history of prostate carcinoma, prostate nodule, or an increased serum prostate specific antigen (PSA)
    • Testosterone therapy my increase the risk of cardiovascular disease
    • Possible to unintentionally transfer testosterone in patch or gel by skin to skin contact
    • Testosterone patches and gel are not a treatment for male infertility
  • Monitoring after treatment initiation:
    • Check serum testosterone level
    • Check hematocrit as elevations may occur
    • Check PSA after 3 months
