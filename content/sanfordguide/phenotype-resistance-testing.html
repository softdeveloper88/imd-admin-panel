Phenotype Resistance Testing, HIV

Indications for Testing, Methods

  • In general, genotypic resistance testing is preferred over phenotypic testing (see clinicalinfo.hiv.gov for latest DHHS Guidelines)
  • Phenotype resistance testing measures ability of HIV to grow in presence of different concentrations of antiretroviral (ART) drugs.
  • Indications for testing:
    • Multiple treatment failures
    • Multiple, complex mutation patterns on genotypic resistance tests
    • New drug susceptibility evaluation
    • Patient infected with non-subtype-B HIV.
  • Methods and interpretation of phenotype test results are evolving; results reflect: accumulated genetic mutations, variables in assay system, end-point (cutoff) used .
    • Compared to genotype resistance testing, phenotype assays:
      • Take longer (2-8 weeks)
      • Easier to interpret
      • Provide a quantitative assessment of resistance
      • Cost more ($800-1500), and need minimal viral load of 500-1000 copies/mL of plasma
    • If circulating drug-resistant virus represents <10% of plasma viral load, resistant virus probably non-detectable;
    • Only perform while on ART.
    • Only detects resistance to single drug, not combinations.
  • Method: Recombinant (Chimeric) viruses:
    • Pertinent genes (reverse transcriptase, protease, integrase, envelope) from patient's plasma HIV are inserted into lab strains of HIV.
    • HIV replication in various drug concentrations is measured by expression of a reporter gene and the results are compared to replication of the lab strain of HIV.
    • Results are expressed as fold increase (or fold resistance). IC50 = drug concentration that
      inhibits viral replication by 50%. IC50 patient/virus IC50 reference virus = fold increase (or fold resistance).

Phenotype Resistance Defined

  • Expert consultation is recommended.
  • Definition of resistance varies depending on which cutoff value is used. “Cutoff” is dividing point between sensitive and resistant virus. There are 3 levels of cutoff in use: technical/reproducibility; biologic; clinical.
  • Technical/reproducibility cutoffs (though rarely used):
    • Defined as the lowest fold difference for which susceptible isolates are reliably separated from reference HIV strains.
    • Sensitive means <4-fold increase in IC50 patient/virus/IC50 reference virus in presence of test drug; Intermediate means 4-10-fold increase and Resistant means >10-fold increase.
  • Biologic cutoffs:
    • Based on variability of wild-type virus from patients. Determined by study of IC50 concentration of test drug vs. HIV from wild-type virus (treatment-naïve patients). More relevant but still arbitrary; cutoffs vary depending on assay used.
    • Cutoff defined as IC50 above mean + 250 (99th percentile).
  • Clinical cutoffs:
    • Determined by correlation of in vitro IC50 with outcome data (virologic response) from clinical trial data. Need large-scale trials. Does not measure influence of multi-drug ART regimens.
    • Provides the best definition of phenotypic resistance but is the most difficult to obtain. Some examples of validated clinical cutoffs:
