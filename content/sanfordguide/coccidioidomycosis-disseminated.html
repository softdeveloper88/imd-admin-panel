Coccidioidomycosis, Disseminated

Clinical Setting

  • Extrapulmonary, non-meningeal sites of infection, commonly skin, bone, joint, lymph nodes.
  • Risk factors for disseminated disease include dysfunction in cell-mediated immunity (HIV, organ transplantation, etc.), TNF-inhibitor therapy, pregnancy, and Filipino or African ancestry.

Diagnosis

  • Serologic testing methods:
    • Screening: Enzyme-linked immunoassay (EIA) for IgM and IgG or immunodiffusion (ID) test; EIA more sensitive but less specific than ID.
    • Limitations: Negative serology does not exclude infection in early disease in immunocompromised patients.
  • Monitoring response to therapy: Complement fixation (CF) antibody test.

Etiologies

  • Coccidioides immitis

Primary Regimens

  • Mild-moderate disease:
    • Fluconazole 400–1,000 mg po once daily
    • Itraconazole 200-400 mg po twice daily
  • Severe disease (rapid progression and/or infection of critical structures):
    • Liposomal Amphotericin B 3-5 mg/kg IV once daily

Alternative Regimens

  • Voriconazole 6 mg/kg po q12h x2 then 4 mg/kg mg po bid (>40 kg body weight)
  • Posaconazole (NOTE: different dosing delayed release tabs/IV vs. suspension, better levels achieved with delayed-release tabs)
    • Delayed-release tabs 300 mg po bid x 2 doses and then 300 mg po daily
    • Suspension 200 mg qid, then 400 mg po bid after stabilization of disease
    • Posaconazole IV 300 mg IV over 90 minutes bid x 1 day, then 300 mg IV daily
  • Isavuconazonium sulfate (prodrug of Isavuconazole) 372 mg po/IV q8h x 6 doses and then 372 mg po/IV daily
  • Amphotericin B 0.6–1 mg/kg IV once daily

Comments

  • Bone and joint infections: Itraconazole 200 mg po twice daily was superior to Fluconazole 400 mg once daily for the subgroup of patients with bone and joint infections in one randomized control trial (Ann Intern Med 133:676, 2000).
  • Pregnancy: Azoles are contraindicated during the first trimester but are likely safe in second and third trimesters. Recommendation on treatment of coccidioidomycosis during pregnancy can be found at Clin Infect Dis 53:363, 2011.
  • A small retrospective study found that of patients unresponsive to fluconazole or itraconazole, voriconazole or posaconazole was effective as salvage therapy in 67% and 75% of cases respectively  (Clin Infect Dis 53:1060, 2011).
  • A small case series suggested benefit of adding Caspofungin to Voriconazole for severe infection in pediatric patients (Clin Infect Dis 56:1573, 2013).
  • Monitor CF titers on therapy: CF titer ≤ 1:4 associated with a good prognosis, CF titer ≥ 1:16 indicates disseminated disease.
    • Rising CF titer post-treatment indicates relapse: reinstitute therapy.
    • Immunocompromised patients may have dissemination even with low CF titer.
  • Relapse rates in patients with extrapulmonary disease are between 18-28% (Ann Intern Med 133:676, 2000). Consider prolonged suppressive therapy in immunocompromised patients and normal hosts that have relapse or elevation of CF titers after discontinuation of therapy.
  • In the absence of symptoms suggestive of CNS infection, LP is not needed to evaluate for CNS disease in disseminated infection (PLoS one 8:e64269, 2013).
  • IDSA Guidelines: Clin Infect Dis 63:e112, 2016.
