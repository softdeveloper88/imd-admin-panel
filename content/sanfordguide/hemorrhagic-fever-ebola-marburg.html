Ebola Virus, Marburg Virus

Clinical Setting

  • Hemorrhagic fever viruses: Ebola and Marburg. Periodic outbreaks historically occur in Africa.
    • Largest ever documented outbreak of Ebola virus disease (EVD), West Africa, 2014-2015. 10,000 + cases. Cases include persons who developed EVD in the US, Spain, UK and elsewhere in Europe after traveling from the outbreak area.
    • Outbreak of Ebola in Uganda and Sudan (Feb 2025).
    • Outbreak of Marburg virus infections in Kagera region of Tanzania (January - February 2025)
  • The mainstay of therapy for Ebola virus disease is early recognition, effective isolation, and optimal supportive care.  Immune therapy is effective (see below)
    • Use of convalescent serum from persons who have recovered is approved for use in newly infected patients by the WHO (BMJ 349:g5539, 2014).
    • Two new monoclonal antibody treatments, Inmazeb (REGN-EB3; Regeneron)  a cocktail of three humanized monoclonal Abs and Ebanga (ansuvimab-zykl; formerly called mb114, Ridgeback Biotherapeutics), have been approved by the US FDA for use in treatment of PCR confirmed Ebola.
  • See CDC Guidance for current guidance for clinicians, laboratory workers and diagnosis. U.S. specialized care centers: Emory University, University of Nebraska, NIH Clinical Center.
  • Risk factors:
    • Healthcare workers (HCW) caring for Ebola patients and family and friends in close contact with Ebola patients are at highest risk because they may come in contact with the blood or body fluids of sick patients.
    • Removal and handling of contaminated personal protective equipment (PPE) poses potential high risk for HCW.
    • People also can become sick with Ebola after coming in contact with infected wildlife: handling bushmeat (wild animals hunted for food) and contact with infected bats (especially Fruit Bats).
  • Presentation & disease progression:
    • Gastrointestinal symptoms: severe watery diarrhea, nausea, vomiting and abdominal pain.
    • Other: chest pain, shortness of breath, headache or confusion, may also develop. Patients often have conjunctival injection. Hiccups reported. Seizures may occur, and cerebral edema reported. Bleeding is not universally present but can manifest later in the course as petechiae, ecchymosis/bruising, or oozing from venipuncture sites and mucosal hemorrhage. Frank hemorrhage is less common. Pregnant women may experience spontaneous miscarriages.
    • Persons with fatal disease usually develop more severe clinical signs early during infection and die typically between days 6 and 16 of complications including multi-organ failure and septic shock.
    • In non-fatal cases, persons may have fever for several days and improve, typically around day 6-11. Patients that survive can have a prolonged convalescence.
    • Abrupt onset of symptoms typically 8-10 days after exposure (range 2-21 days). Nonspecific symptoms, which may include fever, chills, myalgias, and malaise. Fever, anorexia, asthenia/weakness are the most common signs and symptoms. Patients may develop a diffuse erythematous maculopapular rash (days 5-7) (usually involving the face, neck, trunk, and arms) that can desquamate (summarized in N Engl J Med 372:40, 2015).
    • EVD can often be confused with other more common infectious diseases such as malaria, typhoid fever, meningococcemia, and other bacterial infections (e.g., pneumonia).

Etiologies

  • Ebola virus (filovirus)
  • Marburg virus

Primary Regimens

  • Ebanga /ansuvimab-zykl 50 mg/kg administered as single intravenous infusion
    • Monoclonal antibody
  • Inmazeb 50 mg/kg of each of the 3 mAbs (150 mg/kg total) administered as a single intravenous infusion
    • REGN-EB3 (Regeneron) three fully-human monoclonal antibodies: atoltivimab, maftivimab and  odesivimab

Alternative Regimens

  • Convalescent serum from infected patients approved for use as treatment of newly infected patients by the WHO (BMJ 2014;349:g5539)

Prevention

  • See Ebola, Vaccines for indications, available products, dosing, and vaccine characteristics for pre-exposure and post-exposure prevention.
    • Not commercially available

Comments

  • Ebola treatment: 
    • Ebanga:  Single monoclonal antibody (ansuvimab-zyk) with activity against Zaire ebolavirus.  Similar efficacy as Inmazeb (below). Adverse events difficult to discern, mostly similar to symptoms of Ebola.  Hypersensitivity reactions during or post-infusion noted in ~ 1% of treated patients. 
    • Inmazeb: Cocktail of three monoclonal antibodies (atoltivimab, maftivimab and odesivimab).  As above, similar efficacy as Ebanga and similar AE profile; watch for hypersensitivity reaction during or post- infusion occurring in ~ 1% of subjects.
      • PALM [together save lives] study was a randomized, controlled trial of four investigational agents (ZMapp, Remdesivir, mAb114 and REGN-EB3) for the treatment of patients with Ebola virus disease  (N Engl J Med 381:2293, 2019)
      • In PALM, Inmazeb (REGN-EB3Z) three humanized monoclonal Abs and ansuvimab-zykl (Ebanga;  formerly known as monoclonal Ab114), was found to be effective and both were superior to ZMapp, a combination of three murine monoclonal antibodies expressed in tobacco plants.  
    • BCX-4430 (Biocryst) is under evaluation by the NIH; and GS-5734 (Gilead) active in animal models. Brincidofovir was found not to be effective.
    • Brincidofovir is bioavailable with oral dosing and has activity against Ebola virus (as well as CMV, adenovirus, and smallpox) but did not  work in anecdotal cases.
    • ZMapp (Mapp Biopharmaceutical):
      • A 3 mice monoclonal antibody preparation derived from mice exposed to small fragments of Ebola virus.
      • ZMapp used successfully in several patients during the 2014 Ebola outbreak in West Africa. Inferior to Ebanga and Inmazeb.
  • Epidemiology
    • Study in Gabon revealed 15% seroprevalence among 4349 healthy volunteers from 220 randomly selected villages. None recalled any illness similar to Ebola. Seroprevalence among children rises until age 15, then plateaus. Seropositive status correlates to location of fruit bats, PLoS One 5:e9126, 2010.
    • Prior severe outbreak of Ebola in Angola 308 cases with 277 deaths (N Engl J Med 352:2155, 2005; Lancet Infect Dis 5:331, 2005).
    • Major epidemic of Marburg 1998-2000 in Congo and 2004–5 in Angola (N Engl J Med 355:866, 2006).
    • Can infect gorillas and chimps that come in contact with other dead animal carcasses (Science 303:387, 2004).
    • Marburg reported in African fruit bat, Rousettus aegyptiacus (PLoS One 2:e764, 2007).
    • Octopeptides (nanoparticles) being investigated as Rx for Marburg (OpenNano. Nov-Dec 2022, Vol 8)
  • Review: N Engl J Med 2020; 382: 1832
