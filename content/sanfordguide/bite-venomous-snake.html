Bite: Venomous Snake

Clinical Setting

  • Bite wound from venomous snake (pit viper).
  • Antivenom is the primary therapy for the bite itself.
  • Obtain culture.

Etiologies

  • Enterobacteriaceae
  • Pseudomonas sp.
  • Staphylococcus epidermidis
  • Clostridium sp.
  • See comment for more potential pathogens

Primary Regimens

  • Antivenom
  • Antibiotic:
    • In the past, Amoxicillin-clavulanate + Ciprofloxacin was recommended as prophylactic antibiotic therapy
      • In an analysis of 2748 rattlesnake bites, prophylactic antibiotics were given to 120 patients. The overall infection rate was 27 (0.98%) over 18 yrs. Not clear how many had received prophylactic antibiotics but the low rate is impressive (Am J Med. 2018;131:1367). Reviewers concluded that prophylactic antibiotics are not warranted given low rate of infection.
    • Piperacillin-tazobactam for empiric therapy of infected wound

Alternative Regimens

  • Adjust above regimens based on culture results

Comments

  • Anti-tetanus prophylaxis is indicated.
  • Reference on clinical management of venomous bites, Clin Infect Dis 43:1309, 2006.
  • Snake bites in North America, Emerg Med Clin North Amer 35: 339, 2017.
  • Marine snakes, Emerg Med Clin No Amer 35: 321, 2018.
  • General Reference, N Engl J Med 2022;386:68
  • Approximately two-thirds of infected cobra bites contained enteric bacteria, approx one-quarter had coagulase-negative staphylococci or enterococci; uncommonly peptostreptococcus (now Peptoniphilus spp) or B fragilis (J Microbiol Immunol Infect 44: 456, 2011).
  • Other organisms including Shewanella spp (Int J Infect Dis 20:11, 2014), Mycobacterium spp., or Bacillus spp. have been identified from infected snake bites.
