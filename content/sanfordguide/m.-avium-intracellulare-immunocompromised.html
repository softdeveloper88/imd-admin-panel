M. avium-intracellulare complex (MAC), Disseminated, HIV/AIDS

Clinical Setting

  • Disseminated M. avium-intracellulare complex (MAC) infection defined by compatible clinical signs and symptoms coupled with the isolation of MAC from cultures of blood, lymph fluid, bone marrow, or other normally sterile tissue or body fluids in a person with HIV/AIDS
  • Treatment and maintenance therapy for disseminated infection in the patient with AIDS

Etiology

  • Mycobacterium avium-intracellulare complex (MAC)

Primary Regimens

  • Azithromycin 500 mg po/day + Ethambutol 15 mg/kg/day + Rifabutin 300 mg po q24h (dose adjusted as needed for drug interactions)
    • Addition of Rifabutin (and perhaps a fourth potentially active drug, listed in Comments) recommended for patients with a high organism burden (e.g.,  > 2 log10 CFU/ml of blood), CD4 count <50 cells/mm3 or in absence of an effective antiretroviral regimen.
    • HIV-infected patients should be treated with antiretroviral therapy (see Comments concerning IRIS, immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome).
  • Maintenance therapy, post-treatment suppression (secondary prophylaxis)
    • Same as treatment regimen.
    • If ART does not result in immune reconstitution continue chronic maintenance therapy.

Alternative Regimens

  • Clarithromycin 500 mg po bid + Ethambutol 15 mg/kg/day + Rifabutin 300 mg po q24h (dose adjusted as needed for drug interactions)
    • Addition of Rifabutin (and perhaps a fourth potentially active drug, listed in Comments) recommended for patients with a high organism burden (e.g.,  > 2 log10 CFU/ml of blood), CD4 count <50 cells/mm3 or in absence of an effective antiretroviral regimen.
    • HIV-infected patients should be treated with antiretroviral therapy (see Comments concerning IRIS, immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome).
  • Maintenance therapy, post-treatment suppression (secondary prophylaxis)
    • Same as treatment regimen.
    • If ART does not result in immune reconstitution, continue chronic maintenance therapy.

Comments

  • See HIV MAC treatment guidelines
  • Confirm susceptibility of the clinical isolate to macrolides.
  • Other potentially active agents for use in combination with agents of primary or alternative regimens: Amikacin 10-15 mg/kg/d IV, Ciprofloxacin 750 mg q12h, Streptomycin 1 gm IV daily,  Levofloxacin 500 mg po daily, Moxifloxacin 400 mg po daily.
    • Bedaquiline, tedizolid, linezolid, and omadacycline have demonstrated in vitro activity against clinical isolates of MAC; these might also be considered in people with refractory MAC disease.
  • IRIS (immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome)
    • The lower the CD4 count, the higher the risk of occurrence.
    • Onset weeks to months after institution of antiretroviral therapy in up to a quarter or more of HIV-infected patients.
    • Signs and symptoms include fever, painful lymphadenitis, abdominal pain, focal abscesses.
    • Rule out other causes, treat with non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs for those with mild to moderate symptoms or 40 mg of oral predisone for 4-8 weeks with slow taper.
  • Treatment failure rate is high due to drug toxicity, development of drug resistance, and inadequate serum levels. If patient is not responding to initial regimen after 2–4 weeks, add one or more drugs.
  • Discontinue post-treatment suppression once all of the following criteria are met: 
    • Completion of at least 12 months of therapy.
    • No signs or symptoms of MAC disease.
    • >6 months CD4 >100 cells/mm3. 

  • Indication for restarting chronic maintenance therapy (secondary prophylaxis):

    • If a fully suppressive ART regimen is not possible and CD4 is consistently <100 cells/mm3
