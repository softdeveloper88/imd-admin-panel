AIDS-defining Neoplasms

  • The spectrum of malignancies associated with HIV/AIDS includes the following AIDS-defining neoplasms:
    • Kaposi's sarcoma
    • Primary body cavity lymphoma (primary effusion lymphoma)
    • Multicentric Castleman's disease
    • Diffuse large B Cell lymphoma
    • Primary CNS lymphoma (Epstein-Barr virus)(EBV)
    • Non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma (EBV)
    • Body cavity lymphoma; primary effusion lymphoma (HHV8/EBV)
    • Plasmablastic lymphoma of the oral cavity (Lancet Infect Dis 8:261, 2008)
    • Burkitt's lymphoma
    • Classic Hodgkin's lymphoma (EBV)
    • Anogenital neoplasia & squamous cell carcinoma of the anus (human papillomavirus)
    • Basal cell carcinoma of the skin
    • Hodgkin’s disease
    • Seminoma
    • Pediatric leiomyosarcoma
    • Neoplasms associated herpes virus (KSHV)/human herpes virus 8 (HHV8)
    • HIV-associated lymphoma
    • Cervical carcinoma (human papillomavirus)(HPV)

Kaposi's Sarcoma

  • Epidemiology, pathophysiology & treatment
    • Human herpesvirus type 8 (HHV8); also called Kaposi’s sarcoma-associated herpesvirus (KSHV)
    • HHV8/KSHV
      • Viral DNA found in all KS tumors
      • Infection precedes KS
      • Seropositivity rate predicts KS rate
      • Virus latent in most cells; lytic in <5% of cells
      • Targets spindle cells
    • Clinical appearance & then biopsy
    • HHV8/KSHV serology; can now quantitate HHV8 in plasma by PCR
    • Immune reconstitution (improvement) with effective ART leads to:
      • Reports of clearance of HHV8 from circulating cells; 60–80% response rate
      • In randomized clinical trial, some benefit of concomitant initiation of combination of antiretroviral therapy and chemotherapy with etoposide (Clin Infect Dis 2018;67:251)
    • Suggest oncology consultation for best local and systemic therapy
    • Etiology & pathogenesis
    • Diagnosis
    • Treatment

Multicentric Castleman's Disease

  • Rare lymphoproliferative disorder. Can occur at any CD4 count.
  • Etiology: HHV8/KSHV
  • Clinical: Fever, lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, hypergammaglobulinemia
  • Treatment:  Steroids, chemotherapy, IL6 antagonists, rituximab 
  • Prognosis variable

HIV-associated Lymphoma

  • See AIDS-defining neoplasms, above
  • General
    • Systemic lymphomas—no viral association.
    • CNS lymphoma—EBV DNA present in 100% but predictive value low (Clin Infect Dis 38:1629, 2004).
    • Body-cavity lymphomas—HHV8 genome present in virtually all.
    • Compared to immunocompetent patients, present in advanced stage; median survival only 6 months.
    • HIV-associated non-Hodgkin’s lymphomas virtually all of B-cell origin.
    • Viral association
    • Prognosis improving coincident with effective ART.
  • Primary CNS lymphoma
    • Whole brain irradiation; prolongs survival 1–3 months; longer survival with concomitant ART.
    • Responses to combination of ZDV, ganciclovir, & IL-2 in a few patients (Clin Infect Dis 34:1660, 2002).
    • Usually in patients with very low CD4 counts.
    • Detection of EBV DNA by PCR in CSF in >90% patients but predictive value low (Clin Infect Dis 38:1629, 2004).
    • Treatment:
  • Non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma
    •  Rituximab, cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, vincristine, and prednisone (R-CHOP) 
    • ART during chemotherapy – recommended unless compelling reason to delay Rx
  • Primary body cavity lymphoma; primary effusion lymphoma
    • Etiology: HHV8/KSHV; some cells also positive for EBV
    • Can occur at any CD4 count
    • Diagnosis: Biopsy of tumor masses in pleural space, pericardial space, intraabdominal cavity
    • Treatment:
      • Suggest oncology consult
      • Concomitant ART prolongs survival

    Cervical Carcinoma

    • Epidemiology: More frequent, more severe in HIV patients.
    • Etiology: HPV
    • Recommendations:
      • Previous abnormal Pap smear.
      • History of papilloma (wart) virus infection.
      • Post-treatment for cervical intraepithelial neoplasia.
      • Symptomatic AIDS or CD4 count <200/mm3
      • Pap smears x 2 in year one & then annually if normal initially; every 3 yrs if CD4 >500/mm3 (JAMA 293:1471, 2005).
      • More frequent Pap smears if:
      • If Pap smear abnormal, refer for colposcopy &/or biopsy.
      • If CD4 count >200 and under age 26, suggest HPV vaccine (N Engl J Med 365:1576, 2011).

    Anal Neoplasia

    • Prevalence of risk factors, see J Infect Dis 201:1450, 2010; J Infect Dis 201:1498, 2010.
    • Epidemiology:
      • HIV-infected immunodeficient patients at increased risk of human papillomavirus (HPV)-related anal neoplasia: 47% in one series of patients with anal warts (Clin Infect Dis 51:107, 2010).
      • ART may also slow progression (Clin Infect Dis 52:1174, 2011).
    • Recommendations for HIV-infected patients with history of anal intercourse:
      • Anal Pap smear. 
      • Routine anoscopy with biopsy for any positive PAP smear
      • Wide surgical resection for established neoplasia.
