Pneumonia, Neonates, Age < 1 month

Clinical Setting

  • Tachypnea with or without signs of increased work of breathing (grunting, nasal flaring, retractions) is the key symptom of pneumonia in infants age < 1 month.
  • The bacterial etiologies of pneumonia in neonates mirror those of neonatal sepsis.
  • Febrile neonates <30 days should be evaluated as for neonatal sepsis with blood culture, lumbar puncture and CBC. Tests for RSV, influenza and other respiratory viruses useful especially during fall and winter.
  • Afebrile pneumonia syndrome usually seen after 2-4 weeks of age due to C. trachomatis, Mycoplasma hominis, Ureaplasma urealyticum and occasionally CMV or Pneumocystis jirovecii.
  • This does not address ventilator associated pneumonia in intubated premature infant

Etiologies

  • Early illness:
    • Group B Streptococcus
    • Escherichia coli
    • Streptococcus pneumoniae
    • RSV
    • Less commonly other respiratory virus
    • Staph aureus
    • CMV
    • Treponema pallidum
    • Listeria monocytogenes
    • Toxoplasma gondii
  • Later illness:
    • Streptococcus pneumoniae
    • Group B Streptococcus
    • C. trachomatis
    • Mycoplasma hominis
    • Ureaplasma urealyticum
    • RSV
    • Influenza
    • Human metapneumovirus
    • Pneumocyctis jiroveci

Primary Regimens

  • Ceftriaxone 75-100 mg/kg/day once daily may be used in term infants without hyperbilirubinemia
  • Ampicillin + Gentamicin ± Cefotaxime (where available).
  • If methicillin-resistant Staph. aureus (MRSA) is a concern: add  Vancomycin 60- 80 mg/kg IV divided 3-4 times a day to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 10-15 μg/mL but AUC targeting is preferred).
  • For C. trachomatis: Erythromycin 12.5 mg/kg po or IV qid x 14 days or Azithromycin 10 mg/kg po or IV on day one then 5 mg/kg po or IV once daily for 4 days.
  • Dosing is weight- and/or age-based.

Alternative Regimens

  • Cefepime 150 mg/kg/day div q8h
  • If MRSA is documented, Vancomycin as above, Clindamycin, and Linezolid 10 mg/kg q8h are alternatives.
  • Dosing is weight- and/or age-based. See Comments.

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Limited data suggest low procalcitonin has high negative predictive value for bacterial pneumonia
  • If viral infection is demonstrated and patient is improving, antibiotics can be discontinued
  • Oseltamivir is indicated for treatment of influenza associated pneumonia in this age group

Comments

  • Vancomycin: traditional dosing of 45-60 mg/kg/day frequently does not achieve target AUC in term infants and older children with normal renal function. Use of AUC24  closer to 400 μg/mL adequate for most non-CNS infections. Clin Infect Dis Jul 13 2020
  • Optimal duration of therapy is unknown. Short course therapy effective in adults; no good data in children
  • Ceftriaxone is contraindicated in neonates if they require (or are expected to require) treatment with calcium-containing IV solutions, including continuous calcium-containing infusions such as total parenteral nutrition
