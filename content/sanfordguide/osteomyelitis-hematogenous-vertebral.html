Osteomyelitis, Hematogenous

Clinical Setting

  • Empiric therapy for hematogenous osteomyelitis. Select antibiotics appropriate for patient age. Revise quickly to specific therapy according to culture results.
  • Osteomyelitis of long bones is more common in children. Vertebral osteomyelitis most common site in adults. Other bones less commonly involved.
  • Bone culture essential unless likely pathogen is recovered from blood.
  • Source control is important for children who are critically ill or who have evidence of abscess on MRI
  • If patient is not clinically ill and a diagnostic procedure is planned, antibiotics can be held until after the procedure
  • See also Vertebral osteomyelitis.

Etiologies

  • Age Newborn to < 4 months: Staph. aureus, Group B Streptococcus, enteric gram-negative bacilli
  • Age 4 months to adult: Staph. aureus, Group A Streptococcus; enteric gram-negative bacilli are uncommon
  • Salmonella species consideration in developing countries or among patients with sickle cell disease
  • Increased recognition of infection due to Kingella kingae in children under age 4. 

Primary Regimens

  • Empiric regimens (switch to specific therapy as soon as culture data available):
    • Vancomycin + (Ceftriaxone or Ceftazidime or Cefepime or Cefotaxime (where available))
    • In children Vancomycin or Cefazolin, unless Salmonella is suspected (inclusion of MRSA-directed empiric therapy should be driven by local epidemiology)
  • Duration of pathogen-specific therapy:
    • 6 weeks for vertebral osteomyelitis (IDSA treatment guidelines [Clin Infect Dis  61:e26, 2015]) but ≥ 8 weeks if there is end-stage renal disease, MRSA infection, or undrained paravertebral or psoas abscess (any one of which is a risk factor for recurrence (Clin Infect Dis 62:1262, 2016) or for patients undergoing device implantation (Clin Infect Dis 60:1330, 2015).
    • Outcomes comparable for 6 weeks and 12 weeks for low risk infections (see Lancet 385:875, 2015 and Clin Infect Dis 62:1262, 2016).
    • 4-6 weeks adequate in children; early conversion to high dose oral therapy may be equally effective (JAMA Pediatr 169:120 2014; Orthop Clin North Am 2017 Apr;48(2):199-208). 
    • 3-4 weeks may be adequate for uncomplicated MSSA hematogenous osteomyelits with early response to therapy (J Pediatric Infect Dis Soc. 2021; 10:801-844)

Alternative Regimens

  • If severe allergy or toxicity:
    • In place of Vancomycin, could substitute LinezolidNAI or Clindamycin (in areas where susceptibility permits) or DaptomycinNAI.
    • In place of a third-generation cephalosporin, could substitute Ciprofloxacin or Levofloxacin (avoid both in children) or Aztreonam (in most beta-lactam-allergic patients).
    • In some areas, high rates of resistance to extended-spectrum cephalosporins and fluoroquinolones among prevalent gram-negative bacilli may necessitate use of a carbapenem for empiric coverage.

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • De-escalate and tailor therapy based on results of culture and susceptibility tests.
  • Can transition to oral therapy with an active, bioavailable agent once the patient has stabilized (N Engl J Med. 2019; 380:425-436 and J Pediatric Infect Dis Soc. 2021; 10:801-844).

Comments

  • Review of drug penetration for agents with gram-positive activity: J Clin Pharm Ther 38:89, 2013.
  • Guidelines recommend following CRP to evaluate response to therapy
  • In the primary regimens, use Cefotaxime or Ceftriaxone only when Pseudomonas aeruginosa is deemed unlikely.
  • If bacterial cultures are negative, consider M. tuberculosis or fungus as an etiology.
  • Updated guidelines for acute hematogenous osteomyelitis in children: J Pediatric Infect Dis Soc. 2021; 10:801-844
