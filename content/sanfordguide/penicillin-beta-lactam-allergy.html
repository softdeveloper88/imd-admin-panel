SCARs
  • Chronologic classification of drug allergies
    • Immediate
      • Typically occur within one hour of drug ingestion (up to 6 hours).
      • Usually, but not always, IgE-mediated.
    • Delayed
      • Evolve over days, or even weeks, following drug exposure.
      • Most common: morbilliform drug eruption (benign).
      • Many others, including SCARs (severe cutaneous adverse reactions, i.e., DRESS, AGEP, SJS/TEN).
  • Penicillin allergy
    • The most common drug allergy identified in medical records (6-25% prevalence). Of these, over 90% are skin test negative.
    • The most common type of reactions are benign cutaneous reactions (e.g., urticaria, delayed maculopapular exanthema).
    • Penicillin is the most common cause of drug-induced fatal and nonfatal anaphylaxis. The lowest rate is with oral penicillins. Aminopenicillins are among the highest-risk drugs to cause benign delayed reactions, commonly in the context of acute infection with Epstein-Barr virus infection.
    • IgE antibodies wane over time. It is estimated that up to 80% of patients with a history of immediate clinical symptoms compatible with a penicillin allergy become tolerant of it after a decade if there has been no further exposure. After 20 years, fewer than 1% of patients continue to maintain their sensitivity.
    • Labeling a patient as penicillin-allergic is significant. Such patients are more likely to be treated with less effective, more toxic, or more expensive drugs.

  • Structural features and implications:
    • A penicillin consists of a thiazolidine ring fused with a β-lactam ring. An R-1 side chain is linked via an amide bond to the β-lactam ring. The penicillin β-lactam ring degrades in vivo to form a stable derivative that binds to proteins (known as haptenation). The thiazolidine ring remains intact. Not all antigenic forms of penicillins involve a degraded β-lactam ring.
    • A cephalosporin consists of a dihydrothiazine ring fused with a β-lactam ring. An R-1 side chain is linked via an amide bond to the β-lactam ring, and an R-2 side chain is linked to the dihydrothiazine ring. A cephalosporin undergoes rapid fragmentation of both ring structures in vivo.
    • R-1 side chains drive the specificity of allergic reactions. Beta-lactams with identical R-1 side chains have increased risk of cross-reactivity.
    • Four groups of currently commercially available beta-lactam antibiotics have an identical R-1 side chain:
      • Amoxicillin, cefadroxil, cefprozil
      • Ampicillin, cefaclor, cephalexin
      • Aztreonam, cefiderocol, ceftazidime
      • Cefditoren, cefepime, cefotaxime, cefpirome, cefpodoxime, ceftriaxone, ceftizoxime
      • All of the other commercially available beta-lactams have a unique R-1 side chain, although differences may be subtle.
    • Three groups of cephalosporins have an identical R-2 side chain:
      • Cefadroxil, cephalexin
      • Cefdinir, cefixime
      • Cefoxitin, cefuroxime
    • Cross-reactivity between penicillin and cephalosporins: early data suggested >5%, most likely due to contamination of the early cephalosporin preparations with penicillins. Current data suggest ≤2% of patients who are penicillin skin-test positive will react to a cephalosporin (exception: higher in patients with selective aminopenicillin allergy).
    • Cross-reactivity between cephalosporins due to R-2 side chain similarity is plausible, although it is thought to play a less significant role than R-1 side chain similarity.

Management of Beta-lactam Allergy

  • Reference: Drug Allergy: A 2022 practice parameter update (J Allergy Clin Immunol 2022;150:1333).
  • Listed below are Consensus-Based Statements (CBS) from the 2022 guidelines.
  • Skin testing, drug challenges
    • Skin testing is primarily of most value in patients with a history of drug-induced anaphylaxis.
    • Drug challenges are considered the reference standard for determining drug tolerance.
      • Can be performed for both immediate and delayed allergy phenotypes.
      • Usually performed when the clinical probability of an allergic reaction is low. Two options:
        • 1-step challenge: A therapeutic dose is administered, with observation.
        • 2-step challenge: 10-25% of a therapeutic dose is administered (with observation), followed by the remainder of the dose 20-30 minutes later.
  • Managing penicillin allergy
    • A proactive effort should be made to delabel patients with reported penicillin allergy, if appropriate (CBS 4).
    • In a patient with a history inconsistent with penicillin allergy (e.g., diarrhea, family history of penicillin allergy), no testing is recommended. However, a 1-step amoxicillin challenge may be offered to a patient who requires reassurance before the penicillin allergy label is removed (CBS 5).
    • Perform penicillin skin testing for patients with a history of anaphylaxis, or a recent reaction suspected to be IgE mediated (CBS 6).
    • The routine use of multiple-day challenges in the evaluation of penicillin allergy is not recommended (CBS 7).
    • In pediatric patients with a history of benign cutaneous reaction (e.g., morbilliform drug eruption, urticaria), penicillin skin testing prior to direct amoxicillin challenge is not recommended (CBS 8).
    • In adults with a history of distant (>5 years ago) and benign cutaneous reaction (e.g., morbilliform drug eruption, urticaria), consider direct amoxicillin challenge (CBS 9).
  • Managing cephalosporin allergy
    • In patients with a history of nonanaphylactic cephalosporin allergy, direct challenge using a cephalosporin with dissimilar side chains to determine tolerance is suggested (CBS 10).
    • In patients with a history of anaphylaxis to a cephalosporin, a negative cephalosporin skin test should be confirmed prior to administration of a parenteral cephalosporin with a nonidentical R1 side chain (CBS 11).
  • Using cephalosporins in penicillin-allergic patients
    • In patients with a history of penicillin anaphylaxis, a cephalosporin with a structurally dissimilar R-1 side chain can be administered without testing or additional precautions (CBS 12).
    • In patients with an unconfirmed nonanaphylactic penicillin allergy, a cephalosporin can be administered without testing or additional precautions (CBS 13).
  • Using penicillins in cephalosporin-allergic patients
    • In patients with an unconfirmed or verified nonanaphylactic cephalosporin allergy, a penicillin can be administered without testing or additional precautions (CBS 14, 16).
    • In patients with a history of anaphylaxis to cephalosporins, penicillin skin testing and drug challenge should be performed prior to penicillin therapy (CBS 15).
  • Carbapenems
    • In patients with a history of penicillin or cephalosporin allergy, a carbapenem may be administered without testing or additional precautions (CBS 17).
  • Monobactams
    • In patients with a history of penicillin or cephalosporin allergy, aztreonam may be administered without prior testing unless there is a history of ceftazidime allergy (CBS 18).
