Gastroenteritis, Empiric Therapy

Clinical Setting

  • Empiric therapy for diarrhea, diarrhea treatment, diarrhea differential diagnosis, persistent diarrhea, chronic diarrhea.
    • No laboratory studies, culture, microscopy or toxin results are available.
    • Try to make specific diagnosis, especially in patients with severe diarrhea or systemic symptoms.
  •  General references:
    • N Engl J Med 370:1532, 2014; N Engl J Med 350:38, 2004; Clin Microbiol Infect 21:744, 2015. 
    • IDSA 2017 treatment guidelines for infectious diarrhea: Clin Infect Dis 65:1963, 2017.
  • Severity of the diarrhea:
    • Mild
      • 1-2 unformed stools per day
      • Minimal associated symptomatology with minimal interference with activities
    • Moderate
      • 3-5 unformed stools per day
      • With or without systemic symptoms, interferes with planned activities
    • Severe
      • ≥ 6 unformed stools per day
      • ± temperature ≥ 101ºF, tenesmus, blood or fecal leukocytes
      • Severe afebrile bloody diarrhea should increase suspicion of E. coli 0157:H7 or other STEC infection—causes only 1–3% all cases diarrhea in US—but causes up to 36% cases of bloody diarrhea (Clin Infect Dis 32:573, 2001).

Etiologies

  • Possible etiologies vary with  duration of diarrhea and clinical setting:
    • Acute (<14 days) : most viral, but severe cases often bacterial, can also be protozoal
    • Persistent (14-29 days; review JAMA 315:2712, 2016): differential diagnosis depends on exposures and travel
      • Giardia, Cryptosporidium, and E. histolytica 
      • consider Cyclospora, Cystisospora belli, Strongyloides, Schistosomiasis
      • Giardia, Cryptosporidium in day care centers
      • C. difficile if hospitalized or on antibiotics
      • CMV or microsporidia in immunocompromised especially HIV+
      • Post infectious irritable bowel syndrome
    • Chronic (>29 days): Differential diagnosis varies depending on socioeconomic factors. Seen in immunocompromised esp. HIV; chronic diarrhea is often not infectious
      • Infections: C. difficile, Aeromonas, Plesiomonas, Campylobacter, Giardia, Amebae, Cryptosporidium, Whipple's disease, and Cyclospora
      • Noninfectious: irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), inflammatory bowel disease, malabsorption syndromes (lactose intolerance and celiac disease)
      • Post infectious irritable bowel syndrome
  • Viral
    • Norovirus
      • Norovirus causes over 90% of non-bacterial diarrhea, accompanied with or without nausea and vomiting.
      • Duration is 12-60 hours.
      • Treatment is hydration; there is no effective anti-viral therapy
    • Adenovirus 
      • Several strains of adenovirus (including 40/41) common causes of diarrhea
      • Duration can be prolonged. Up to 7 days in minority
      • Treatment is hydration; there is no effective anti-viral therapy
  • Bacterial
    • Shigella sp.
    • Salmonella sp.
    • C. jejuni
    • C. difficile (Toxin positive)
    • Vibrio sp.
    • E. coli (enterotoxigenic, enteroaggregative, or Shiga-toxin producing)
    • K. oxytoca (Toxin producer)
  • Parasitic
    • Cryptosporidium
    • Cyclospora
    • Cystisospora belli
    • E. histolytica
    • Giardia lamblia
    • Microsporidia 

Diagnostic Considerations

  • Multiplex molecular panels are largely replacing culture and microscopy for initial detection of potential pathogens due to their sensitivity, specificity, ease of use and rapidity of test performance
  • Stool culture may be needed for in vitro antibiotic susceptibility testing and/or public health disease tracking
  • Suggestions:
    • Diagnostic studies should be targeted for those with bloody diarrhea, severe illness, symptom duration > 7 days, or immunocompromised hosts
    • For travelers, multiplex molecular panels should be used when symptoms fail to resolve after empiric trial of azithromycin
    • For hospital onset diarrhea, begin with targeted testing for C. difficile. Multiplex testing can be reserved for severely immunocompromised inpatients
  • Interpret results of multiplex PCR with caution and consideration of clinical context. Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) multiplex panels are very sensitive and  may detect organisms that may, or may not,  be causing symptoms:
    • Detection of eae gene for EPEC has not been shown to correlate with presence of diarrhea and in most situations does not warrant antibiotic therapy
    • Detection of C. difficile DNA by molecular methods may represent disease or asymptomatic carriage

Primary Regimens

  • Mild Diarrhea:
    • Hydration: Fluids + lactose-free diet
    • Avoid caffeine
  • Moderate Diarrhea:
    • Fluids + antimotility agents
      • Loperamide (Imodium) 4 mg po, then 2 mg after each loose stool to max. of 16 mg per day
      • Bismuth subsalicylate (Pepto-Bismol) 2 tablets (262 mg) po qid
      • Do not use if hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS) is suspected.
      • Antimotility agents generally not advised in C. difficile toxin-mediated diarrhea, but some feel they can be used cautiously in mildly ill patients on treatment (Clin Infect Dis 48:598, 2009). Others feel there is insufficient evidence for such use (Clin Infect Dis 48:606, 2009).
  • Severe Diarrhea
    • (Ciprofloxacin 500 mg po q12h or Levofloxacin 500 mg q24h) x 3–5 days
    • Azithromycin 1000 mg po once or 500 mg q 24h x 3 days preferred for Campylobacter and disease acquired in Southeast Asia.
    • If C. difficile is suspected (e.g., recent antibiotic use) add  Fidaxomicin 200 bid x 10 days or Vancomycin 125 mg po qid x 10–14 days. Metronidazole 500 mg po tid no longer treatment of choice for  C. difficile but may be effective in milder cases.

Alternative Regimens

  • Severe Diarrhea
    • TMP-SMX-DS po bid x 3–5 days.
      • Campylobacter resistance to TMP-SMX is common in the tropics
      • Add Fidoxomicin 200 bid x 10 days or Vancomycin 125 mg po qid  x 10–14 days if recent antibiotic therapy and C. difficile colitis is suspected

Antibiotic Stewardship

  • The use of molecular diagnostics to detect potential pathogens can result in fewer days of empiric therapy and more focused antimicrobial therapy which ,in turn should improve the safety and efficacy of treatment regimens.
    • Remember, detection of potential pathogens may represent colonization without invasive disease; interpretation requires assessment of the entire clinical data base.
  • Increasing resistance to FQ has led to revision of guidelines to single dose Azithromycin for severe diarrheal illness.
  • Colonization with extended-spectrum beta-lactamase (ESBL) -producing Enterobacteriaceae (ESBL-PE) associated with overseas travel and treatment or prophylaxis for travelers' diarrhea with antibiotics J Travel Med 2017 24:S29.

Comments

  • Hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS):
    • Risk in children infected with E. coli 0157:H7 is 8–10%.
    • Early treatment with TMP-SMX or Fluoroquinolones increase risk of HUS (N Engl J Med 342:1930, 2000 & N Engl J Med 342:1990, 2000).
    • Controversial meta-analysis: JAMA 288:996, 2002 & JAMA 288:3110, 2002,supported by more robust meta-analysis Clin Infect Dis 62:1251, 2016.
  • Other potential etiologies:
    • Cryptosporidia—Nitazoxanide 500 mg po bid x 3 days.
    • Cyclospora—usually chronic diarrhea, responds to TMP-SMX, Ann Intern Med 123:409, 1995.
  • Klebsiella oxytoca identified as cause of antibiotic-associated hemorrhagic colitis (cytotoxin positive), N Engl J Med 355:2418, 2006.
  • Ischemic colitis and inflammatory bowel disease can present as acute abdominal pain and bloody diarrhea.
