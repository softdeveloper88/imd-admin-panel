HBV Reactivation

Clinical Setting

  • Hepatitis B virus (HBV) can reactivate in patients receiving immunosuppressive therapy, even those who have previously cleared HBsAg and have HBsAb.
    • There have been many cases of severe hepatitis, including deaths reported from HBV reactivation following immunosuppressive therapy including corticosteroids, cancer chemotherapy, biologics, other immunosuppressive drugs, and during stem cell transplantation and solid organ transplantation.
    • Among the biologics, Rituximab and ofatumumab use has been most closely correlated with HBV reactivation (FDA warning: Hepatology 61:703, 2015). TNF blockers (Clin Rheumatol 3:931, 2012) have been implicated as well.
    • In short, even with serologic evidence of resolved infection, the HBV genome may silently persist as miniature closed circular DNA in the nucleus of infected hepatocytes
    • Occult HBV is suspected in patients with HBsAg negative, HBcAb(IgG) positive, HBsAb negative and either a positive or negative HBV DNA NAAT.  It is this panel of tests that is used to screen patients prior to use of immunosuppressives for cancer chemotherapy, transplantation, or severe rheumatologic diseases.
    • References: Ann Int Med 164:30, 2016; Infect Dis Clin N Am 2020;34:341.
  • Screen for HBV:
    • Recommended screening labs: HBsAg, HBsAb, HBcAb
    • If positive for HBsAg or HBcAb or HBV DNA (any one of the three or a combination thereof), start prophylactic HBV therapy

Primary Regimens

  • HBsAg+:
    • HBV DNA < 2000 UI/ml and < 12 mo (of immunosuppression): Lamivudine 100 mg po daily
    • HBV DNA > 2000 UI/ml OR > 12 mo (of immunosuppression): Entecavir 0.5 mg po daily
  • HBsAg-, HBcAb+, HBsAb ±:
    • High risk (see below): Lamivudine 100 mg po daily
    • Lower risk: Preemptive monitoring vs. Lamivudine 100 mg po daily

Alternative Regimens

  • HBsAg+: Tenofovir 300 mg po daily
  • HBsAg-, HBcAb+, HBsAb±: Entecavir 0.5 mg po daily
  • An alternative approach is preemptive antiviral therapy started as soon as HBV DNA becomes detectable (CID 61:719, 2015):
    • Monthly  HBV DNA while receiving (rituximab or ofatumumab) plus corticosteroids
    • HBV reactivation defined as HBV DNA levels of >11 IU/ml
    • If reactivation is documented, immediately start entecavir, 0.5 mg/day

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Duration of prophylaxis: 6-12 months after stopping immunosuppression. At least 12 months is preferred in patients on rituximab, alemtuzumab, other antibody depleting drugs, and stem cell transplantation patients

Comments

  • High risk: Chemotherapy for hematological malignancy, stem cell transplant, organ transplant, rituximab,ofatumumab or other heavily immunosuppressive regimens, or HBV DNA positive.
  • Lower risk: Less immunosuppressive regimens not considered high risk as above.
  • Initiation of prophylaxis should begin before (1 mo) or at minimum the same time as start of immunosuppressive therapy.
  • Monitoring for all patients on prophylaxis should occur every 3 months for the first year and include HBV DNA, LFTs, and HBsAg (when HBsAg negative at start). When preemptive monitoring is preferred over prophylaxis consider every 1-3 mo. Monitoring can be less frequent over time if HBV DNA remains negative.
  • American Gastrointestinal Society Guidelines: Gastroenterology148: 215, 2015
