HIV in the Fetus and Newborn, Overview

Overview

  • An estimated 130,000 children ≤ 15 years were newly infected worldwide in 2022, down from 530,000 in 2020 (UNAIDS.org).  Among children living with HIV, an estimated 54% were on ART
  • In the US, 53 cases of HIV in children younger than 13 years of age were diagnosed in 2021, down from 105 in 2017
  • An increasing number of children living with HIV in the U.S. are foreign born and at increased risk of TB, non-subtype B infection and intrapartum nevirapine resistance.
  • The success of prevention of mother to child transmission (PMTCT) to is due  to HIV testing of pregnant women & intrapartum treatment in developed and less developed countries. Universal testing, counseling and treatment to all pregnant women are critical.
  • "Opt out" testing leads to increased testing 

Transmission

  • Over 90% of HIV+ children in U.S. acquired infection from their mothers perinatally: in utero, during delivery, or postpartum through breastfeeding. Risk of transmission 13–40% (clinicalinfo.HIV.gov).
  • Time of transmission:
    • In utero: HIV has been identified in fetal tissues as early as 8 weeks. Probably in the majority, in utero transmission occurs late in pregnancy (Lancet 345:517, 1995).
    • Intrapartum: 50–70% of transmissions believed to occur through exposure to mother's blood, cervical secretions or amniotic fluid during delivery.
    • Postpartum acquisition rare in developed countries, important in developing countries. Breast-fed infants have a 10-14% additional risk of becoming infected in the absence of antiretroviral therapy (J Acquir Immune Defic Syndr. 2002 Jun 1;30(2):230-9). In mothers seroconverting during lactation, risk is 1/3 (Lancet 342:1437, 1993).

Diagnosis

  • HIV can be diagnosed in most infants by 1 month & all infants by 6 months of age by demonstration of virus by viral RNA PCR or DNA PCR. Viral culture is not used for routine diagnosis.
  • HIV DNA PCR is sensitive and specific by 2-4 weeks of age (>90% sensitive). Quantitative RNA PCR assays are at least as sensitive and may better detect non-subtype B virus and provide viral load data. Low viral loads (< 5000 copies/mL) must be repeated and confirmed (guidelines at clinicalinfo.HIV.gov; J Infect DIs 175:707, 1997; J AIDS 32:192, 2003). Both DNA and RNA assays are recommended to diagnose perinatal infection
  • Maternal anti-HIV IgG crosses the placenta & persists until 9–15 mos, so infants born to HIV-infected mothers may test positive for up to 15 mos regardless of infection. Assays for p24 antigen are less sensitive & less specific than PCR.
  • PCR should be performed:
    • By 48 hrs (not on cord blood) in children with increased risk of perinatal infection (e.g. acute HIV infection during pregnancy, detectable viral load at delivery, no ARV or ARV at delivery only)
    • At 14-21 days
    • At 4-8 weeks
    • Repeat at 4–6 mos if initial tests negative
  • Repeat testing 2-6 weeks after end of prophylaxis for infants at high risk of transmission
  • Any positive test should be repeated immediately along with quantitative HIV RNA PCR (viral load) before treatment begun.
  • Presumptive evidence of in utero infection is PCR positive in 1st 48 hours of life. Intrapartum infection defined by negative test in first 48 hours followed by positive test.
  • Some experts confirm loss of HIV antibody at 12-18 months
  • HIV infection can be presumptively excluded by 2 negative PCRs; one at >14 days and one at >1 month; HIV is definitively excluded (in absence of breast feeding) by at least 2 negative PCR tests; one at >1 month and one at >4 months.
  • See Initial evaluation of children with HIV

Natural History

  • Bimodal distribution. Approximately 20% will be rapid progressors with onset of symptoms by median 8 months & median survival of <2 yrs.
  • Median survival, untreated, for non-rapid progressors was 66 mos. Survival has greatly increased with ART, & many perinatally infected children are reaching adolescence & young adulthood.
