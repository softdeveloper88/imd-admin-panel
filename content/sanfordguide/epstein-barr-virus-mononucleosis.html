Epstein Barr Virus, Mononucleosis

Clinical Setting

  • Mononucleosis.
  • Infections and conditions associated with atypical lymphocytes: Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV), cytomegalovirus (CMV), Hepatitis A, Hepatitis B, toxoplasmosis, measles, roseola, mumps, and drug reactions.
  • Diagnosis is serological (see table below). PCR can be positive intermittently throughout life and both expensive and non-specific for diagnosis of mononucleosis.

  • Considered etiology of roughly half of the post -transplantation lymphoproliferative disorders (PTLDs) in adults (N Engl J Med 2018;378:549)
  • Rare entity called "Chronic Active EBV Disease" is a newly appreciated disorder. Most cases in Asia; more cases now identified world-wide (Reviewed in Front. Pediatr., 05 February 2019). Treatment often requires bone marrow transplant. Diagnosis is based on:
    • > 3 months mononucleosis like symptoms;
    • Elevated EBV genomic DNA in peripheral blood;
    • EBV infection of T-cells or NK cells in tissue or peripheral blood;
    • Exclusion of other causes of chronic disease.

Etiologies

  • Epstein Barr Virus (EBV)

Primary Regimens

  • No antiviral treatment.
  • Corticosteroids for tonsillar obstruction, CNS complications, or threat of splenic rupture.

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • 25% of EBV seroconverters developed infectious mononucleosis; such illness was linked to infection with EBV type 1. Higher rate of seroconversion among those engaged in intercourse. No higher risk of disease with kissing only (Clin Infect Dis 43:276, 2006).
  • Differential diagnosis of  "mononucleosis syndrome":
    • EBV (80% of cases)
    • Cytomegalovirus
    • HIV
    • Toxoplasmosis
    • Rarely: HSV, HHV6, HHV7, Adenovirus, Mycoplasma, and Mumps
