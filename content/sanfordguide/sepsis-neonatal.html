Sepsis, Neonatal

Clinical Setting

  • Onset of fever, neutrophilia and, perhaps, hypotension in infants in the first month of life.
  • All febrile infants < 90 days require evaluation. See Febrile infant for general approach
  • Early Onset: Age < 1 week
  • Use of Early Onset Sepsis risk calculator for early onset sepsis ("Kaiser risk score") decreased laboratory workup, antibiotic exposure without adverse outcomes.JAMA Pediatr. 2017 Apr 1;171(4):365-371; Pediatrics. 2018 Dec;142(6); including meta-analysis of studies 
    JAMA Pediatr. 2019 Nov 1;173(11):1032
  • Calculator available at (http://kp.org/eoscalc)
    • Blood cultures are key but only 5–10% are positive. U/A, Urine culture and CBC standard when workup indicated
    • LP should be performed in ill-appearing infants and < 30 days, many would perform in all
    • Regimens are empiric.
  • Late Onset: Age 1 to 4 weeks
    • Several approaches stratify risk
      • Use risk calculator based on clinical features, WBC, ANC, procalcitonin and UA.JAMA Pediatr. 2019 Apr 1;173(4):342-351.
      • Stratify by appearance, age, WBC, ANC and enterovirus PCR and respiratory viral studies.Pediatrics 130:e16, 2012.
      • Perform blood culture, urine culture; enterovirus PCR and respiratory viral studies for high risk infants. 
    • UTI is the most common bacterial infection.

Etiologies

  • Early Onset:
    • Streptococcus agalactiae (Group B) (less common with negative maternal GBS screen or intrapartum penicillin)
    • Escherichia coli
    • Klebsiella sp.
    • Enterobacter sp.
    • Enterococcus sp.
    • Enterovirus
    • Parechovirus
    • Staphylococcus aureus (uncommon)
    • Listeria sp. (predominant in Spain, rare in US)
    • Salmonella sp. (South America)
  • Late Onset:
    • Same as above PLUS
    • Haemophilus influenzae
    • Staphylococcus epidermidis in those with indwelling lines or devices

Primary Regimens

  • Empiric therapy:
    • Age ≤7 days:  Ampicillin 150 mg/kg/day IV div q8h + Cefotaxime (where available) 100 mg/kg/day div q12h ± Gentamicin 5 mg/kg q 24 h or 2.5 mg/kg q8h IV or IM
      • If meningitis, consider increasing to Ampicillin 200 mg/kg/day IV div q6h and Cefotaxime 100-150 mg/kg/day div q8-12h
    • Age >7 days: Ampicillin 200-300 mg/kg/day IV q6h + Cefotaxime (where available) 150 mg/kg/day div q8h or Ceftriaxone 75 mg/kg/once daily ± Gentamicin 5 mg/kg q24h IV or IM
  • If cefotaxime is unavailable and the infant is has hyperbilirubinemia or is too young to receive ceftriaxone, consider Ceftazidime 100 mg/kg/day div q12h if <7 days or 150 mg/kg/day div q8h if >7 days of age.

Alternative Regimens

  • For Late Onset:
    • Ampicillin 200 mg/kg/day IV div q6h + Ceftriaxone 75-100 mg/kg IV q24h
    • Ampicillin 200 mg/kg/day IV div q6 + Gentamicin 5 mg/kg/day div once daily or q8h IV or IM
    • If MRSA is a concern, add Vancomycin 15 mg/kg IV q12h (Wt < 2000 gm) to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 10-15 μg/mL but AUC targeting is preferred).
    • If severe beta-lactam allergy, alternative for Group B Strep has been Clindamycin or Erythromycin; still recommended but reports of erythromycin resistance in 51% and clindamycin resistance in 38% of Group B Strep isolates: Antimicrob Agents Chemother 56: 739, 2012.

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Roughly 100 infants recieve antibiotics for every culture proven sepsis. Use of risk calculator http://kp.org/eoscalccan reduce this safely JAMA Pediatr. 2019 Nov 1;173(11):1032
  • Discontinue antibiotics after 48 hours if cultures and course do not support diagnosis.
  • Note regarding ampicillin and gentamicin: Enterobacteraciae are more common than Group B Strep in many settings and >50% of E. coli are ampicillin resistant
  • Use of gentamicin is based on activity against Listeria and historical precedent. Some experts omit gentamicin
