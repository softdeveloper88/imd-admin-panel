Molluscum contagiosum

Clinical Setting

  • Viral infection of the skin.
  • Umbilicated pick nodule, sometime confused with cutaneous Cryptococcal Infection
  • Lesions can be asymptomatic or associated with itching or local pain
  • Typically lesions appear on the abdomen, neck, legs, arms, genital region or face
  • Most commonly, lesions appear in young children (aged 4- 8 years).  In a normal host, the lesions usually resolve within a year without scarring.  
  • Lesions are quite contagious; Transmission is direct contact with the skin (e.g., through sports activities) or via fomite.  Auto-inoculation is common, leading to spread to multiple areas.

Etiologies

  • Molluscum contagiousum virus (MCV); a member of the poxvirus family  

Primary Regimens

  • Treatment is often not necessary since the lesions often resolve within 6 months to a year.  For those who desire treatment for cosmetic purposes or other reasons:
    • Cantharidin (Ycanth; 0.7%):  Apply topically once weekly for 3 weeks to affected areas on the skin (Not for oral, ocular, or mucosal surfaces).  Wash off at 24 hours.
      •   Note:  Product is flammable, even after drying.  Use care to avoid exposure to open flame

Alternative Regimens

  • Berdazimer sodium (Nitric acid releasing agent; sold as Zelsuvmi): mix 0.5 mL in equal amounts from Tube A and Tube B and apply thin layer to lesions once daily for up to 12 weeks
  • Surgical Rx 
    • Lasar (pulsed dye) therapy
    • Liquid nitrogen (followed by curette removal)

Comments

  • The efficacy of topical Cantharidin for the treatment of molluscum in patients aged 2 years and older was established in 2 double-blind, randomised, placebo-controlled trials (NCT03377790 and NCT03377803). In the trials, a total of 528 subjects aged 2 to 60 years with molluscum contagiosum were randomised by household to receive either topical cantharidin or a placebo treatment.  At primary endpoint (Day 84), 54 % of cantharidin Rx individuals had completely cleared treated lesions vs 13% of placebo group.   AEs (> 1%) included vesiculation, pain, pruritus, scabbing, erythema, discoloration, application site dryness, edema, and erosion.  Data from Package Insert.
  • Berdazimer was approved in early 2024 by the US FDA.   In a 12-week phase III clinical trial, 891 patients with a mean age of 6.6 years were randomly assigned to treatment with berdazimer gel 10.3% or a placebo (vehicle gel) applied to all lesions once daily. At 12 weeks, 32.4% of patients in the berdazimer group achieved complete clearance of MC lesions compared with 19.7% of those in the placebo group (P < .001).  Package Insert here.
  • Imiquimod does not work (no better than placebo in Phase III studies)
  • Topical potassium hydroxide (marginal benefit)
