Hepatitis C, Overview

Overview

  • A revolution occurred in the treatment of Hepatitis C virus (HCV) infection between 2010 and 2015, similar in magnitude to what was observed in the mid-1990s in the HIV field.  Hepatitis C virus was originally known as 'non-A, non-B hepatitis' through the 1980s.  The hepatitis C virus is a small (50 nm in size), enveloped, single-stranded, positive sense RNA virus. It is in the Flaviviridae family and the only member of the hepacivirus genus.
    • Hepatitis C has six major genotypes that are indicated as genotype 1, genotype 2, genotype 3, and so forth. There are three major and eleven minor genotypes based on genetic determinants in the NS5 region of the virus. The major genotypes diverged over 300 years ago from the ancestor virus, with all of the genotypes appearing to have evolved from genotype 1b.
  • In the 1990s, interferon was discovered to be effective in the treatment of HCV.  A major difference between HIV and HCV therapy is that patients can be cured of hepatitis C infection, owing to its lack of integration into the host genomic DNA as is the case with HIV. However, successful treatment resulting in cure was relatively uncommon with interferon (INF) monotherapy.  In the early 1990s, INF combined with ribavirin (RBV) resulted in modestly higher success rates and became the treatment of choice.
    • This therapy became enhanced with the development of pegylated formulations of interferon, so-called peg-interferon (pegIFN), that resulted in markedly improved pharmacodynamics, which allowed once-weekly dosing, some reduction in toxicities, and improved therapeutic efficacy.
    • Success rates of pegIFN/RBV were quite high (> 70% - 80% cure) for patients with HCV genotype 2, but remained quite low (20% - 40%) for other genotypes, especially genotypes 1a and 1b, the most common genotypes in the United States.
  • Novel therapies that target specific gene products of the HCV genome were developed, termed "Direct Acting Agents" (DAAs), leading to the 'revolution' of therapeutic approaches for HCV. These drugs, when used in combination, confer rates of cure > 95% - 98%, in all genotypes including 1a and 1b.
  • Owing to the evolving revolution in HCV treatment, coverage dedicated to Hepatitis C has been expanded to include its epidemiology, natural history (acute/chronic HCV), diagnosis, clinical presentation and staging, and treatment.  Since cure is the objective of therapy and decisions regarding when to start therapy is dependent upon the clinical stage of disease and currently available options. 
  • The Hepatitis C section is tailored to both providers who are already engaged in hepatitis C care and those who are in the process of making a commitment to begin seeing hepatitis C patients in their practice.
