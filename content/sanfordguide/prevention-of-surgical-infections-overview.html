Prevention of Surgical Site Infections, Overview

Overview

  • Recommendations based on 2013 guidelines in Am J Health Syst Pharm 70:195, 2013 and summary in Medical Letter 58:63, 2016.
  • To be optimally effective and deliver high tissue levels at time of incision, antibiotics must be started within 60 minutes of the surgical incision (N Engl J Med 326:281, 1992). Since Vancomycin and Fluoroquinolones may require 1-2 hr infusion times, start dose 2 hrs before surgical incision.
  • Most applications employ a single preoperative dose or continuation for <24 hours.
  • For procedures lasting >2 half-lives of the prophylactic agent (generally surgeries over 4 hrs), intraoperative supplementary dose(s) may be required.
  • Standard regimens may give relatively low tissue levels in patients with high body mass index (BMI), but implications of this are not clear (see Surgery 136:738, 2004 for Cefazolin; Eur J Clin Pharm 54:621, 1998 for Vancomycin; Clin Infect Dis 38:1706, 2004 for weight-based dosing).
  • Prophylaxis does carry risk, e.g., C. difficile colitis (Clin Infect Dis 46:1838, 2008) and allergic reactions.
  • Active S.aureus screening, decolonization, and customized antimicrobial prophylaxis was demonstrated efficacious in decreasing  infections after hip, knee, and cardiac surgery: JAMA 313: 2131, 2015; JAMA 313:2162, 2015.

  • General reference: Surgical Site Infection Prevention: A Review. JAMA. 2023; 329:244

Use of Vancomycin

  • For many common prophylaxis indications, Vancomycin is considered an alternative to β-lactams in patients allergic to or intolerant of the latter.
  • Vancomycin use may be justifiable in centers where rates of post-operative infection with methicillin-resistant Staph. aureus (MRSA) are high, or in patients at high risk for MRSA.
  • Unlike β-lactams in common use, Vancomycin has no activity against gram-negative organisms. When gram-negative bacteria are a concern following specific procedures, it may be necessary or desirable to add a second agent with appropriate in vitro activity. This can be done using Cefazolin with Vancomycin in the non-allergic patient, or in patients intolerant of β-lactams using Vancomycin with another gram-negative agent (e.g., aminoglycoside, fluoroquinolone, possibly Aztreonam, if patient not allergic; local resistance patterns and patient factors would influence choice).
  • Vancomycin histamine release syndrome:
    • Infusion of Vancomycin, especially too rapidly, may result in hypotension or other manifestations of histamine release from mast cells. Not a true allergy; Can continue vancomycin with a slow infusion. See Hospital Pediatrics 10:623, 2020.
