Quinine sulfate

Usage and Dosing

  • Oral regimen for treatment of malaria and severe babesiosis.
  • Marked tissue accumulation in renal impaired patients.
  • FDA category C but can be used for severe malaria in pregnancy as benefit outweighs risk.
  • Review of dosing pharmacology in children, pregnancy, and elderly (Malaria J 2022 Feb 10;21(1):41).
  • For parenteral form see Quinine dihydrochloride.

Adult Dose

  • 648 mg po tid x3 days as part of 7-day combination therapy, see P. falciparum
  • Babesiosis:
    • Mild to moderate. 542 mg base (which equals 650 mg salt) po q6h–8h x 7 to 10 days.
    • Acute Severe Hospitalized: Clindamycin 600 mg IV q6h + quinine sulfate 542 mg base po q6h–8h until symptoms abate, then convert to all oral step-down therapy.
      • Follow with step-down therapy as per mild/moderate

Pediatric Dose

Pediatric doses:  Clindamycin 7–10 mg/kg po q8h; quinine sulfate 6 mg base/kg (which equals 8 mg salt/kg) according to adult schedules/routes above. Never exceed adult dose.

Adverse Effects

  • Cinchonism; tinnitus, headache, nausea, abdominal pain, blurred vision.
  • Rarely: blood dyscrasias (thrombocytopenia and hemolytic uremic syndrome/thrombocytopenic purpura), drug fever, asthma, hypoglycemia. Transient blindness in <1% of 500 patients (Ann Intern Med 136:339, 2002).
  • Contraindicated if prolonged QTc, G6PD deficiency, myasthenia gravis, optic neuritis. See list of concomitant drugs that prolong QTc.
  • Should not be used for the treatment or prevention of nocturnal leg cramps.

Pregnancy Risk

FDA Risk Category 1 Use only if other effective antimalarials are unavailable
Use during Lactation Probably safe, but avoid if infant G6PD-deficient
