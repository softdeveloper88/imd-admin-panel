Acute Retinal Necrosis

Clinical Setting

  • Vision loss, usually in immunocompetent individuals, which progresses rapidly with retinal necrosis, vasculitis and uveitis; frequently results in retinal detachment.
  • Begins unilaterally but may involve the other eye (up to 50% of cases).
  • Differentiate from Progressive Outer Retinal Necrosis (PORN), observed in immunocompromised patients, including advanced HIV. 

Etiologies

  • Varicella-zoster virus
  • H. simplex
  • Cytomegalovirus (rarely)

Primary Regimens

  • Acyclovir 10–12 mg/kg IV q8h x 7–10 days until disease stabilizes, then oral therapy for a minimum of 6 weeks with
    • Valacyclovir 1000 mg po tid OR
    • Famciclovir 500 mg po tid

Alternative Regimens

  • Intravitreal Ganciclovir or Foscarnet and systemic antiviral therapy may have greater therapeutic efficacy than systemic therapy alone.

Comments

  • Ophthalmologist involvement imperative regarding individual need for corticosteroids, intra-vitreal anti-virals, vitrectomy, or laser therapy.
  • Reviewed in Diagnostics 12: 386, 2021 and  Clinical Ophthalmology 14:1931-43, 2020 
  • Case reports of acute retinal necrosis caused by cytomegalovirus, in which case ganciclovir/valganciclovir would be used (Eur J Ophthalmol 18:813, 2008).
  • Strong association of varicella-zoster virus with atypical necrotizing herpetic retinopathy (Clin Infect Dis 24:603, 1997).
