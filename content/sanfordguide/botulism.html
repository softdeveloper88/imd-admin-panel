Botulism

Clinical Setting

  • Acute onset of paralytic disease from the botulina toxin produced by Clostridium botulinum and, less often, by C. baratii or C. butyricum. All are spore-forming anaerobic gram-positive rods
  • C. botulinum toxin is the world's most deadly toxin: 1 gm could kill 1 million people.
  • Five pathogenetic pathways to clinical botulism:
    1. Ingestion of preformed toxin causes food-borne botulism.
    2. Infant botulism results from ingestion of spores that germinate in the intestinal tract. Although rare, a similar pathogenesis can occur in adults (Emerg Infect Dis 18:1, 2012).
    3. Wound botulism results from spores deposited in wounds. Can be recurrent in injection drug users (Clin Infect Dis 52:862, 2011).
    4. Adult enteric infectious botulism: similar to infant botulism. Toxin produced in the GI tract of the adult.
    5. Inhalational botulism: aerosolization as in an act of bioterrorism.
  • Clinical syndrome is the same whether toxin is ingested, inhaled or injected. Toxin results in acute onset of bilateral cranial nerve palsies and descending symmetrical weakness.
  • Diseases/conditions that are mistaken for botulism: Miller-Fischer variant of Guillain-Barre, Myasthenia gravis, Eaton-Lambert paraneoplastic syndrome, tick paralysis, conversion disorder, organophosphate insecticide poisoning.
  • Comprehensive review of all facets of botulism: Clin Infect Dis 2018;66:S1and 2021 clinical guidelines for the diagnosis and treatment of botulism (MMWR Recomm Rep 2021;70(2):1-30)

Diagnosis

  • Should be clinical diagnosis with subsequent testing for confirmation:
    • Submit patient's serum for mouse bioassay for presence of toxin
    • Submit suspect toxin source food for mouse bioassay
    • If wound debrided, can do toxin bioassay on tissue
  • Should be clinical diagnosis with subsequent testing for confirmation:
  • EMG can be helpful in experienced hands: findings include neuromuscular junction blockage, normal axonal conduction, and potentiation with rapid repetitive stimulation. CSF findings are normal
  • Food-borne: mouse bioassay on blood, emesis, stool and/or food product.
  • Infant: Look for organism and toxin by mouse bioassay in the stool.
  • Wound: Try to isolate organism from wound. Toxin bioassay is negative in 1/3 (Clin Infect Dis 48:1669, 2009).

Etiologies

  • Clostridium botulinum (most common)
  • C. baratii
  • C. butyricum 

Primary Regimens

  • Adult:
    • Heptavalent botulism equine serum antitoxin (adult and child > age 1 year)
      • Obtain from State Health Department or contact CDC, +1 404-639-2206 or +1 770-488-7100.
    • Can give laxatives, enemas or other cathartics if no ileus present. Monitor vital capacity. May require mechanical ventilation
    • Penicillin G 3 million units IV q4h (unproven benefit)
      • If penicillin-allergic, Metronidazole 500 mg IV q8h
    • Surgical debridement of wound, even if the wound appears benign
    • Tetanus toxoid booster
  • Infant:
    • Monitor respiratory function
    • Human-derived botulinum immunoglobulin (Baby BIG); Call +1-510-231-7600
      • Do not use equine antitoxin for infants
      • Obtain from California Infant Botulism Treatment and Prevention Program, http://www.infantbotulism.org/
    • Antibiotics not recommended

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • Clinical review, see Clin Infect Dis 15;41(8):1167, 2005
  • Potential bioterrorism agent, see JAMA 285:1059, 2001.
  • 75% of reported cases are infant botulism.
  • Injection drug users: Increase in wound botulism from use of black tar heroin (Frontiers in Pub Health 2021, 9:744179)
  • Hospitalization lasts 1-3 months.
  • Other source examples: tomato chili sauce, Clin Infect Dis 56:376, 2013; home-canned bamboo shoots, MMWR 55:389, 2006; prison-made illicit alcohol, MMWR 62:88, 2013.
