Lyme Disease, Meningitis, Encephalitis

Clinical Setting

  • Encephalitis or meningitis can be an early or late manifestations of Lyme Disease; Early disease typically presents as meningitis, cranial neuritis, radiculoneuritis, and more rarelyencephalomyelitis, and has a more rapid onset of illness.  Later onset neuroborrelia disease can involve peripheral or central nervous system and the onset is more insidious.
  • Variable clinical syndromes:
    • Cranial nerve palsy; most often 7th cranial nerve
    • Headache and neck pain with CSF picture consistent with a lymphocytic aseptic meningitis
    • Cognitive impairment: poor memory, depression, somnolence
  • May have other associated late-stage manifestations: carditis or arthritis; usually early in course; maybe isolated finding
  • For details on diagnostic methods see: Lyme Disease, Diagnosis.

Etiologies

  • Borrelia burgdorferi

Primary Regimens

  • Hospitalized: Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q24h (adult) or 50-75 mg/kg IV q24h (child) x 14 days
  • Ambulatory: Doxycycline 100 mg po bid or 200 mg po once daily x 14 days (use only if no spinal or parenchymal brain involvement; in those settings, IV therapy is warranted)
  • Pediatric dose ambulatory: Doxycycline 4.4 mg/kg/day po divided bid (max.dose 100 mg bid) x 14 days

Alternative Regimens

  • Penicillin G 5 million units IV q6h x 14 days
  • Cefotaxime 2 gm IV q8h x 14 days (where available)

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Pediatrics: Doxycycline can be safely administered for up to 21 days regardless of age (AAP Red Book 2018, Section 4; J Pediatr 166:1246, 2015).

Comments

  • IV therapy risks: line infection or thrombosis; pseudolithiasis from crystals of ceftriaxone.
  • See also, Post-Lyme Disease Syndrome.
  • 2020 Clinical practice guidelines (Neurology 96:262, 2021) and (Clin Infect Dis 72: e1-e48, 2020)
  • Review in JAMA 315:1767, 2016.
