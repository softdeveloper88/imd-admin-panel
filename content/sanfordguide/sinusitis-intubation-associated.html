Sinusitis, Intubation Associated

Clinical Setting

  • Sinusitis in hospitalized patients as a complication of nasotracheal intubation or nasogastric tube.
  • Remove nasotracheal tube and if fever persists, recommend sinus aspiration (by ENT if available) for culture/sensitivity and nasal PCR for MRSA prior to empiric therapy.

Etiologies

  • Gram-negative bacilli (47%) (Pseudomonas sp., Acinetobacter sp., E. coli are commonly encountered)
  • Gram positives (Staph. aureus) (35%)
  • Yeasts (18%)
  • Polymicrobial (80%)

Primary Regimens

  • Imipenem-cilastatin 0.5 gm IV q6h or Meropenem 1 gm IV q8h.
  • MRSA suggestive on gram-stain: Add Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator); alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL

Alternative Regimens

  • (Ceftazidime 2 gm IV q8h or Cefepime 2 gm IV q12h) +  Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator); alternative target is trough of 15-20 μg/mL

Comments

  • Data supports obstructing tube leading to accumulation of sinus fluid that may ultimately harbor invasive bacteria and /or serve as reservoir of bacteria that are aspirated and cause pneumonia (J Intensive Care Med 2019;34:844)
    • After 7 days of nasotracheal or gastric tubes, 95% of patients have x-ray “sinusitis” (fluid in sinuses), but on transnasal puncture only 38% are culture positive, Am J Respir Crit Care Med 150:776, 1994.
    • For patients requiring mechanical ventilation with nasotracheal tube for ≥1 week, bacterial sinusitis occurs in <10%, Clin Infect Dis 27:851, 1998.
    • May need Fluconazole if yeast on Gram stain of sinus aspirate.
  • Review: Clin Infect Dis 27:463, 1998.
