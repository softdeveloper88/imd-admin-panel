Coxiella burnetii, Q Fever

Clinical Setting

  • Zoonotic infection caused by Coxiella burnetii. Cattle, sheep and goats are common reservoirs.
  • Spread to humans by aerosolization of infected animal birth products or excreta; Coxiella can be airborne miles away from the source.
  • Risk groups: farmers, farm workers, veterinarians, abattoir workers, others with exposure to livestock reservoirs.
  • Acute infection, incubation period of 2-3 weeks
    • Non-specific flu-like illness with fever, chills, night sweats, myalgias, headache, non-productive cough with or without associated atypical pneumonia syndrome, anicteric hepatitis.
    • Usually mild and may resolve spontaneously within 2 weeks.
    • Labs: normal WBC with initial thrombocytopenia and subsequent thrombocytosis; elevated AST/ALT/alkaline phosphatase in 85% of cases.
    • Antibody response to phase II antigen.
    • Risk factors for progression to chronic infection.
      • Abnormal cardiac valve, vascular graft, aortic aneurysm, acute infection during pregnancy or in immunocompromised persons. 
  • Chronic  infection (Medicine 2016;95 (34):e4287; Clin Infect Dis. 2021; 73: 1476)
    • Occurs in less than 5% of the patients; may occur months or years after acute infection.
    • Symptoms are nonspecific and variable and may include fatigue, fever, abdominal or chest pain, weight loss, night sweats, hepatosplenomegaly, evidence of thromboembolic phenomenon.
    • Manifestations of chronic infection. 
      • Most commonly culture-negative endocarditis, which occurs in those with underlying valvular heart disease; or endovascular infection in those with a vascular graft or arterial aneurysm (MMWR 62(RR-03):1-28, 2013; Clin Infect Dis 57:836, 2013).
        • 100% fatal if untreated.
        • Valvular vegetations may be small and easily missed on a transthoracic echocardiogram (TTE); a transesophageal echocardiogram (TEE) should be obtained in those with suspected endocarditis and a non-diagnostic TTE. Due to the relative insensitivity of echocardiography, a negative TTE or TEE does not rule out Q fever endocarditis.
      • Other manifestations of chronic infection include chronic hepatitis, chronic bone and joint infection, chronic pulmonary infection (JAMA Network Open 2018; 1(4): e181580.
    • Antibody response to phase I antigen.

Diagnosis

  • Acute infection
    • Phase II antibody response is predominant and develops about a week following onset of symptoms
    • Serologic evidence of a fourfold rise in titer of phase II IgG on immunoflourescent assay (IFA) between acute and convalescent sera obtained 3 to 6 weeks apart is the gold standard.
    • Positive PCR for C. burnetii in whole blood or serum from a specimen obtained during the first two weeks of illness and before or shortly after antibiotic administration is highly sensitive and specific and precedes antibody response. PCR positivity rapidly decreases after treatment and falls after 2 weeks so a negative PCR cannot be used to exclude infection. 
    • In the absence of a positive PCR and an acute serum sample, a single positive convalescent serum sample, IgG phase II ≥1:128 on IFA, in a patient who has been ill >1 week indicates a probable acute infection. 
    • IgM phase II antibodies develop concurrently with IgG response, but are less specific, and likely falsely positive in the absence of an IgG phase II response and not recommended for diagnosis.
    •  Standard blood culture media will not grow C. burnetii and is rarely if ever used for diagnosis. Metagenomic next-generation sequencing may have utility (BMC Infect Dis. 2025; 25:190). 
  • Chronic infection
    • Phase I antibody response is predominant, develops about a week following onset of symptoms, and titers continue to rise, often exceeding phase II antibody titers.
    • Confirmed by elevated IFA phase I IgG titer ≥1:1024.
    • Phase I IgG titer > 1:800 meets 2023 Duke major criteria for Q fever endocarditis (Clin. Infect. Dis. 2023; 77:518).
    • Positive blood or tissue PCR; negative PCR does not rule out chronic infection.
    • Positive immunohistochemistry of infected tissue.
    • Metagenomic next-generation sequencing may have utility (BMC Infect Dis. 2025; 25:190).
    • PET-CT may have utility in detecting persistent focal infections (JAMA Network Open 2018; 1(4): e181580).
    • Standard blood culture or tissue culture media will not grow C. burnetii and is rarely if ever used for diagnosis.

Classification

  • Coxiella burnetii, pleomorphic gram negative bacilli or coccobacilli, obligate intracellular organism

Primary Regimens

  • Acute infection (initiate therapy as soon as the diagnosis suspected)
    • Adults
      • Doxycycline 100 mg po bid x 2 weeks.
      • Pregnancy: TMP-SMX double strength tablet 1 po bid for duration of pregnancy to prevent premature labor. Discontinue prior to delivery so as to lessen risk of hyperbilirubinemia in the infant. Doxycycline and fluoroquinolones are contraindicated in pregnancy.
      • If known cardiac valvulopathy (history of rheumatic fever, bicuspid aortic valve, prosthetic valve, valvular stenosis or regurgitation), consider Doxycycline 100 mg po bid + hydroxychloroquine 200 mg po tid x 12 months to lower the risk of subsequent endocarditis.
    • Children
      • Doxycycline 2.2 mg/kg/dose IV/po bid x 14 days (max 100 mg/dose). 
  • Chronic infection 
    • Endocarditis or infected aneursym/graft: Doxycycline + Hydroxychloroquine (see Endocarditis, Q fever for specific recommendations and details).
    • Infected bone, joint, or liver: as per Endocarditis until fall in antibody titer is demonstrated.
    • Pregnancy; TMP-SMX 1 double strength bid plus folic acid supplementation, then switch to Doxycycline + Hydroxychloroquine post-partum.
    • Child: limited data, infectious diseases consultation is recommended. Combinations of TMP-SMX, Ciprofloxacin, Rifampin, Doxycycline, Clarithromycin or  Azirhromycin have been used for persistent focal infections (Clin Microbiol Rev. 2017; 30:115).

Alternative Regimens

  • Acute infection, for patients unable to take Doxycycline
    • Adult
      • TMP-SMX 1 double strength table po bid x 14 days. 
      • Moxifloxacin 400 mg po once daily x 14 days.
      • Clarithromycin 500 mg po bid x 14 days.
    • Child
      • TMP-SMX 5 mg/kg/d of TMP in 2 divided doses po  x 14 days.
  • Chronic infection
    • In large retrospective observational cohort study, Doxycycline + Moxifloxacin was as efficacious as Doxycycline + Hydroxychloroquine (Clin Infect Dis 2018;66:719). May be useful in patients unable to tolerate hydroxychloroquine.

Comments

  • Dutch consensus guidelines for chronic Q fever diagnosis (Emerg Infect Dis 2015 Jul;21(7):1183-8):
    • Proven chronic Q fever
      • Positive PCR for C. burnetii in blood or tissue in absence of acute infection OR
      • IFA >1: 800 C. burnetii phase I IgG and definite endocarditis by Duke criteria or proven large vessel or prosthetic infection by PET-CT, CT, MRI, ultrasound.
    • Probable chronic Q fever
      • IFA C. burnetii phase I IgG AND any of the following:  evidence of valvulopathy not meeting major Duke criteria, known aneurysm or prosthetic valve, manifestations of chronic Q fever (osteomyelitis, hepatitis; fever night sweats, weight loss, hepatosplenomegaly; pregnancy or immunocompromised state; granulomatous tissue inflammation).
    • Possible chronic Q fever
      • IFA > 800 C. burnetii phase I IgG.
  • Doxycycline can safely be administered for ≤21 days regardless of patient age (AAP Red Book 2018, Section 4).
  • CDC review of clinical presentations, diagnosis and treatment recommendations: MMWR 62(RR-03):1-28, 2013.
  • Review: StatPearls; 2023 Jan. 2023 May 22.
