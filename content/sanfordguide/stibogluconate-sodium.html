Antimony, pentavalent
Tradename: Pentostam, Glucantime

Usage and Dosing

  • Pentavalent antimonial compound for treatment of leishmaniasis.
  • Glucantime (Meglumine antimoniate) available from Sanofi after obtaining an individual IND from FDA.  See Antiparasitic Drugs, Sources
  • Stibogluconate sodium (Pentostam, GSK) has been discontinued by GSK worldwide and is no longer available from CDC. 
  • Many generics of variable quality widely distributed in the developing world.
  • Administration:
    • For IV administration, dilute the appropriate volume of drug shortly before use in about 50 mL of D5W or NSS (smaller volumes of diluent can be used for small drug doses). Administer over at least 10 minutes via butterfly or heparin lock.
    • Because of possibility of formation of particulate matter over time (in multidose bottles), the manufacturer recommends use of an inline filter for IV administration.
  • Intralesional antimony. Meglumine antimoniate (Glucantime) up to 20 mg/kg intralesionally weekly x 5-10 weeks.  Use undiluted drug. For mild disease and can combine with cryotherapy 5-8 sessions until healing. 

Adult Dose

  • See Leishmaniasis for dosing according to species and clinical form.
    • No matter the brand dosing is in mg pentavalent antimony (SbV)
    • Glucantime:  available as 1.5 gm/5mL solution for injection, with 0.4050 gm of antimony per 5 mL ampule. 
      • 75 mg of meglumine antimoniate contains 20 mg of antimony.
    • Intralesional dosing preferable if few lesions
      • New data on IL dosing in old world CL (PLoS Negl Trop Dis 2022 Jul 8;16(7):e0010569).
      • Review of consideration for IL dosing. Clin Infect Dis 2023;77:583.
      • PAHO Guidelines prefer intralesional dosing if few lesions.
      • IL not advocated by CDC; commonly used in Europe and South America. 
      • Intralesional dosing. Many regimens.
        • Suggest: Meglumine antimoniate (Glucantime) up to 20 mg/kg intralesionally weekly x 5-10 weeks.  Use undiluted drug.
          • For mild disease and can combine with cryotherapy 5-8 sessions until healing.

Adverse Effects

  • Common: fatigue/weakness, malaise, headache, myalgia/arthralgia, anorexia/nausea, abdominal discomfort, and reversible laboratory abnormalities (elevated ALT, AST, amylase, and lipase values; cytopenias; and T-wave changes). Risk for QTc prolongation.
  • Monitoring: Baseline and weekly EKG, serum creatinine, liver function tests, serum amylase, and CBC; patients requiring >20 days of treatment should begin twice-weekly EKG monitoring during week 3 of therapy; twice-weekly or more frequent EKG monitoring may be indicated in certain patients (eg, elderly, underlying cardiac, renal, or hepatic disease)

Pregnancy Risk

FDA Risk Category 1 No data, but highly likely to be fetotoxic. Avoid use in pregnancy.
Use during Lactation Milk concentrations low, adverse effects not expected (particularly if infant is >2 mon old). However, if nursing during therapy is withheld, breastfeeding can be reinstituted 24-48 hr after the last dose.

Major Drug Interactions

  • None reported; however, patients should avoid taking other drugs (including alcohol) that are hepatotoxic or that can cause QT prolongation.
