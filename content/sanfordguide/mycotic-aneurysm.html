Mycotic Aneurysm

Clinical Setting

  • Infection of the arterial wall of the aorta, or less often other arteries, with subsequent dilation and potential rupture due to destruction caused by microorganisms.
  • Classic presentation is painful pulsatile abdominal mass in a febrile patient. See Comments for other presentations.
  • Best diagnostic imaging: CT angiogram.
  • Pathogenesis varies: direct inoculation, bacteremic seeding of atherosclerotic plaque, spread from contiguous infection, septic emboli to vaso vasorum.
  • Diagnosis of microbial etiology:
    • Blood cultures (+ in 50-85%)
    • Surgical site cultures (+ in 76%)
    • Rarely need molecular probe for microbial nucleic acid

Etiologies

  • Staphylococcus aureus (28-71%)—often MRSA
  • Staphylococcus epidermidis
  • Salmonella sp. (15-24 %)
  • Other: Streptococcus pneumoniae, gram-negative enteric bacteria, Treponema pallidum, Mycobacterium tuberculosis, Fungi (Candida sp., Cryptococcus sp., Aspergillus sp.), Clostridia sp., Bacteroides sp. and others

Primary Regimens

  • Combination of antimicrobial therapy plus surgical debridement (resection) with revascularization.
  • Empiric antibiotic therapy:
    • Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL)
      • Plus: either Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV once daily or Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h or Ciprofloxacin 400 mg IV q12h
      • Treatment duration depends on specific etiology and surgical care. In general, 6 weeks of antibiotic therapy from the time of definitive surgical procedure.
  • Specific therapy: Dependent on identification of the specific etiologic organism(s).

Alternative Regimens

  • Empiric therapy:
    • For Vancomycin, consider substituting Daptomycin if active.
    • Report of success with Ceftaroline with surgery for mycotic aneurysm due to VISA S aureus resistant to Daptomycin (BMJ Case Rep 2015: PMID 25833910).
    • For gram-negatives consider Cefepime or a carbapenem (Imipenem-cilastatin, Meropenem, or Ertapenem). In the rare patient with a gram-negative rod resistant to all beta-lactams, fluoroquinolones and aminoglycosides, need ID consult. See specific organisms for recommendations.

Comments

  • Myriad of other presentations:
    • G-I bleeding secondary to aorto-duodenal fistula
    • High output heart failure secondary to an A-V fistula
    • Mesenteric and/or peripheral ischemia
    • Contiguous psoas abscess
    • Contiguous vertebral osteomyelitis
