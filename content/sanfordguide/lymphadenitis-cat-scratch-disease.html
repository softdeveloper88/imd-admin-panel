Cat-scratch Disease (CSD)

Clinical Setting

  • Most common clinical syndrome is self-limited regional lymphadenitis after contact between an immunocompetent patient and a cat, especially a young feral or domestic cat. Occurs with or without disseminated infection.
  • Other clinical manifestations:
    • Disseminated cat-scratch disease:
      • Hepatosplenic cat-scratch infection
      • Neurologic cat-scratch infection
        • Encephalopathy most common
        • Transverse myelitis, radiculitis, cerebellar ataxia all reported
    • Ocular cat scratch infection
      • Parinaud's ocular glandular syndrome
      • Optic neuritis, neuroretinitis
    • Other manifestations: fever of unknown origin (FUO)(rare)(Clin Infect Dis 2020, 71:2818), culture negative endocarditis, arthritis, osteomyelitis
  • Recommend treatment of all patients in order to shorten duration of illness and reduce risk of complications.
  • Distribution of inflamed nodes:
    • Axillary/epitrochlear (46%)
    • Neck (26%)
    • Inguinal (17%)
    • Suppuration (10%)
  • Disseminated disease manifestations occur in 14% of patients.

Diagnosis

  • History of cat or cat-flea contact
  • Positive antibody test for Bartonella henselae. Titer ≥ 1:512
  • Other rarely used diagnostic methods:
    • PCR on node aspirate or biopsy with granulomas 
    • Warthin-Starry silver stain.

Etiology

  • Bartonella henselae:
    • Slow growing fastidious gram-negative bacillus

Primary Regimens

  • Lymphadenitis in immunocompetent patient.
    • Often resolves without treatment.
    • Adult: Azithromycin 500 mg po x 1 dose, then 250 mg po/day x 4 days
    • Child (Weight < 45 kg): Azithromycin oral solution 10 mg/kg po x 1 dose, then 5 mg/kg/day po x 4 days
    • If intolerant to Azithromycin, can treat symptoms and avoid anti-infectives.
    • Pain relief: needle aspiration of purulent exudate from suppurative nodes
    • Hepatosplenic or disseminated infection:
      • Adult, preferred regimen:
        • (Rifampin 300 mg IV/po bid  + Azithromycin 500 mg po x one dose , then 250 mg/day) x 10-14 days.
        • Alternative: (Rifampin 300 mg IV/po bid + Gentamicin 2 mg/kg x one dose and then 1.5 mg/kg q8h ) X 10-14 days
        • Consider adjunctive corticosteroids for severe or persistent infection
    • Neurologic and/or ocular (neuroretinitis) infection: collaborative care with ophthalmology
      • Adult preferred regimen:
        • (Doxycycline 100 mg IV/po bid + Rifampin 300 mg IV/po bid together for 4-6 weeks) (Ref: Intl J Antimicrob Agents 44:16, 2014)
        • Alternatives:
          • Rifampin + Azithromycin (as above) x 4-6 weeks
          • Rifampin (as above) + TMP-SMX -DS, one po bid x 4-6 weeks
      • Child (age < 8 yrs): (Azithromycin 10 mg/kg on day 1, then 5 mg/kg/day OR TMP-SMX 8 mg/kg/day (TMP comp) in 2 divided doses) + Rifampin 10 mg/kg q12h (max 600 mg/day) x 4-6 weeks
      • Recommend adjunctive corticosteroids
        • Prednisone: 1 mg/kg/day po x 2 weeks and then taper over the following 4 weeks

    Alternative Regimens

    • Lymphadentis. Other drugs with in vitro activity:
      • Clarithromycin 500 mg po bid x 7-10 days
      • Rifampin 300 mg po bid x 7-10 days. Remember to check for drug-drug interactions.
      • TMP-SMX DS, one po bid x 7-10 days
      • Ciprofloxacin 500 mg po bid x 7-10 day

    Antimicrobial Stewardship

    • Uncomplicated adenitis in children usually resolves without treatment, although one study showed faster resolution with azithromycin (Ped Infect Dis J 1998; 17:447). Joint decision making about benefits and downsides of treatment is reasonable.

    Comments

    • Jarisch-Herxheimer reactions have occurred after the first few doses of antibiotic.
    • Remember to check for drug-drug interactions in patients given Rifampin.
    • Review: Pediatr Infect Dis J 2021,40:S11.
