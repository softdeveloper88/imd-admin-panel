Antibacterial Spectra Legend

The data shown in the table are intended to serve as a general, comparative overview of antibacterial usefulness based aggregated information from approved package inserts, treatment guidelines and recommendations, reported in vitro activity, clinical trials data, predominant patterns of susceptibility or resistance and/or demonstrated clinical effectiveness. Variability in resistance patterns due to regional differences or as a consequent of clinical setting (e.g., community-onset vs. ICU-acquired infection) should be taken into account when using this table because activity of certain agents can differ geographically from what is shown in the table. Syndrome, pathogen and drug pages should always be consulted when making choices regarding treatment.

  • Preferred (++): Agent is a first line therapy: reliably active in vitro, clinically effective, guideline recommended, recommended as a primary or acceptable alternative agent in the Sanford Guide. See pathogen page for specific recommendations.
  • Alternative (+): Agent is a potential alternative agent (active in vitro, possesses class activity comparable to known effective agents or a therapeutically interchangeable agents and hence likely to be clinically effective) but second line due to overly broad spectrum, less favorable toxicity profile, less robust clinical experience or direct evidence of effectiveness.
  • Limited Utility (±): Agent has variable or limited activity for one or more reasons: 1) although clinically effective in some settings or types of infection it is not reliably effective in others; 2) it should be used in combination with another agent; 3) it has efficacy limited by resistance; 4) there are proven effective alternatives with more favorable properties, e.g., pharmacokinetic profile, adverse events profile; 5) and/or clinical data to support its effectiveness are limited.
  • Not Recommended (0): Agent is a poor alternative to other agents for one or more reasons: 1) lack of in vitro activity or resistance is likely to occur; 2) poor or undefined drug penetration at site of infection; 3) an unfavorable toxicity profile; 4) availability of proven effective or safer alternatives; 5) insufficient data to support dosing recommendations; or 6) lack of clinical data to support effectiveness.
