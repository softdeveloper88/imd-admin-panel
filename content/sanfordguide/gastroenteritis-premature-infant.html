Gastroenteritis, Premature Infant (Necrotizing enterocolitis)

Clinical Setting

  • Necrotizing enterocolitis (NEC) has complex pathophysiology but involves ischemic necrosis of the bowel with invasion of the mucosa by enteric organisms with gas formation. The majority of cases occur among premature infants; the risk increases with the degree of prematurity. Other risk factors include early antibiotic exposure, congenital heart disease, indomethacin use. Changes in intestinal microbiome may be involved. Breast milk is protective (N Engl J Med. 2011 Jan 20;364(3):255-64)
  • Rotavirus and other gastrointestinal viruses can infect premature infants. NICU outbreaks have occurred. Maternal antibody is partially protective in infants > 28 weeks.
  • Spontaneous intestinal perforation (SIP) in the first few days of life in very premature infants is a separate entity from NEC. SIP is often associated with candidemia (Pediatr Surg. 1998 Oct;33(10):1463-7).
  • Bell staging of NEC 
    • Stage 1 suspected NEC. S Non specific symptoms such as apnea, bradycardia, or temperature instability associated with gastric residuals or abdominal distension. Abdominal radiographs are normal or show ileus.
    • Stage 2, definite NEC. Symptoms of stage 1 plus pneumatosis intestinalis, or portal venous gas, slightly decreased platelet count, ileus
    • Stage 3, advanced NEC. Symptoms of stages 1 and 2 plus hyptotension, apnea, bradycardia, lactic acidosis, peritoneal signs, abdominal wall changes. KUB may show free air in abdomen
  • Salmonella and Campylobacter infections acquired perinatally can occur in term and pre-term infants

Etiologies

  • NEC is associated with intestinal flora

Primary Regimens

  • Stage 1. Supportive care, hold enteral feedings, close observation, serial KUBs
  • Stage 2. Supportive care plus antimicrobial therapy. Antimicrobial choices target aerobic and anaerobic bowel flora with agents appropriate to age group (Drugs 68:1227, 2008).
    • Piperacillin-tazobactam 300 mg/kg/day IV div q6h
    • Ampicillin, Gentamicin , and metronidazole traditionally used but may be less appropriate given current resistance to ampicilln among gram negative enteric flora.
  • Stage 3. Antimicrobials and supportive care plus urgent surgical consultation

Alternative Regimens

  • Meropenem 60 mg/kg/day div q8h. Increase to 120 mg/kg/day if meningitis is suspected

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Limiting prolonged antibiotic exposure for "rule out sepsis" may reduce risk of NEC
  • Most patients with Bell Stage 1 can be managed without antibiotics or antibiotics can be discontinued if blood cultures negative at 36-48 hours.
  • Breast milk is protective for NEC (Neonatology. 2014;106(4):289-95)
  • Role of probiotics (other than human milk) is controversial
  • CMV colitis is an occasional mimic of Bell Stage 1 NEC. 
  • Additional research on prevention Clin Perinatol. 2022 Mar;49(1):195

Comments

  • Bacteremia-peritonitis in 20–50%.
  • Differential diagnosis includes spontaneous intestinal perforation and rotavirus or other enteric virus.
