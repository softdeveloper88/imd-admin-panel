Meningitis, Chemoprophylaxis

Clinical Setting

  • Chemoprophylaxis for meningitis, invasive meningococcal disease: H. influenzae, N. meningitidis, contacts
  • Prophylaxis depends on the suspected pathogen.
  • If Haemophilus influenzae (type b) (Hib) meningitis or invasive disease (CDC guidelines)
    • No chemoprophylaxis is indicated for household contacts of a person with invasive Hib disease, if all persons are 48 months of age or older, or if children younger than 48 months of age are fully vaccinated.
    • Chemoprophylaxis is recommended for index case-patients (unless treated with cefotaxime or ceftriaxone) and all household contacts in households with members less than 4 years of age who are not fully vaccinated or members less than 18 years of age who are immunocompromised, regardless of their vaccination status.
    • Chemoprophylaxis indicated for other close contacts, i.e., persons who reside with the patient or a nonresident who has spent 4 hours or more with the index patient for at least 5 of the 7 days preceding the day of hospitalization of the patient as outlined above.
    • Chemoprophylaxis indicated for day care contact when two or more cases of invasive Hib disease have occurred within 60 days and unimmunized or underimmunized children attend the facility.   
  • If Neisseria meningitidis meningitis or invasive meningococcal disease (CDC guidelines)
    • Close contact (e.g., household member, day care contacts, cellmates, shared bedroom); direct, unprotected exposure to droplets, nasopharyngeal secretions of documented case (e.g., intubation, mouth-to-mouth resuscitation, kissing, nasotracheal suctioning).
    • Ideally administer chemoprophylaxis within 24h: risk of secondary disease among close contacts is highest during the first few days after the onset of disease. Chemoprophylaxis given more than 14 days after the onset of disease  is probably of limited or no benefit.

Etiologies

  • Haemophilus influenzae (type b)
  • Neisseria meningitidis

Primary Regimens

  • Haemophilus influenzae (type b):
    • Rifampin
      • Age < 1 month 10 mg/kg po q24h x 4 days
      • Age > 1 month 20 mg/kg po (max daily dose 600 mg) q24h x 4 days 
    • Adults: Rifampin 600 mg po q24h x4 days
  • N. meningitidis:
    • Ciprofloxacin 20 mg/kg (max dose 500 mg) po x1 (not recommend for pregnant women or if fluoroquinolone-resistant strains have been identified in the community)
    • Ceftriaxone 125 mg IM x 1 dose (Age < 15 years) or 250 mg IM x 1 dose (Age > 15 years)
    • Rifampin 5 mg/kg (Age <1 mo) po q12h x 2 days or 10 mg/kg (Age ≥1 mo) po q12h x 2 days (not recommend for pregnant women)

Alternative Regimens

  • Azithromycin 10 mg/kg (max dose 500 mg) x 1: not routinely recommended but an option if there is sustained ciprofloxacin-resistance in the community

Comments

  • H. influenzae:
    • Household: If there is one unvaccinated contact ≤4 years in the household, give Rifampin to all household contacts.
    • Child Care Facilities: Rifampin chemoprophylaxis recommended in child care settings when two or more cases of invasive Hib disease have occurred within 60 days and unimmunized or underimmunized children attend the facility; when prophylaxis is indicated, it should be prescribed for all attendees, regardless of age or vaccine status, and for child care providers.
  • N. meningitidis:
    • If Rifampin-resistant N. meningitidis is circulating in the community (rare),  post-exposure prophylaxis with Ciprofloxacin or Ceftriaxone is preferred (Emerg Infect Dis 11:977, 2005).
    • If Ciprofloxacin-resistant N. meningitidis is ciruculating in the community (rare), post-exposure prophylaxis with Rifampin or Ceftriaxone is preferred
