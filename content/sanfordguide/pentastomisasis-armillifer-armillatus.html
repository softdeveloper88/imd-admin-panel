Pentastomiasis: Armillifer armillatus, Lingulatula serrata

Clinical Setting

  • Pentastomes are closely related to crustaceans (Emerg Infect Dis. 2017, 23:2116).
  • Armillifer armillatus is responsible for visceral pentastomiasis in Western and Central Africa, also reports from China and Asia. 
    • Humans infected by eating infected under-cooked snake meat or by direct contact with feces or respiratory secretions from an infected reptile.
    • Sometimes  a high number of nymphs is observed in human viscera including liver, lungs, mesentery, peritoneum and pleura but most infections are asymptomatic and incidentally discovered. 
    • Larva die and calcify after several years.
  • Linguatula serrata, also known as “tongueworm” as the adults attach to the nasopharynx of many host animals, most frequently occurs in Southeast Asia, South America, and in the Middle East (especially Iran).
    • Herbivorous animals or occasionally humans act as intermediate hosts after ingestion of eggs from the environment and larvae migrate to liver and viscera. 
    • Adult tongueworms inhabit nasopharynx in human infection (called Halzoun) if humans ingest raw liver or other viscera from infected intermediate host animals.
    • Reviews: Parasitol Res 120:3045, 2021; Trends in Parsitol. 2024; 40:P89.

Etiologies

  • Armillifer armillatus (visceral disease, West Africa)
  • Armillifer grandis (visceral)
  • Linguatula serrata (nasopharyngeal disease)
  • A. moniliformis (Southeast Asia), A. agkistrodontis (China), Porocephalus crotali (worldwide, predominantly the Americas), and P. taiwana (China) 

Primary Regimens

  • No therapy if asymptomatic or already calcified; surgical excision if clinically necessary

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • Very few clinical reports; empiric Albendazole, Mebendazole, or Praziquantel is recommended by some experts if surgery not feasible for symptomatic cases.
