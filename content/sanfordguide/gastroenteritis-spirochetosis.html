Gastroenteritis, Intestinal Spirochetosis

Clinical Setting

  • Spirochetosis is usually asymptomatic and an incidental finding on colonic biopsy or appendix and requires no specific therapy. Anaerobic intestinal spirochete colonizing colon.
  • If symptomatic, persistent abdominal pain, diarrhea, rectal bleeding most common presenting symptoms.J Bacteriol 201:e00465 2019
  • Inflammatory changes are absent or minimal on colonoscopy; biopsy typically shows absence of inflammation and fuzzy coating of spirochetes on mucosal surface. (Mayo Clin Proc 95:427 2020) Warthin Starry silver stains confirm findings 
  • Reported to be more common in MSM, who may also have concurrent STI or intestinal protozoan infections (Trop Med Infect Dis 8:250 2023)

Etiologies

  • Brachyspira pilosicoli
  • Brachyspira aalborgi

Primary Regimens

  • Symptomatic individuals usually treated with metronidazole (500 mg qid x 10 days preferred dose).
  • Susceptible in vitro to Metronidazole , Penicillin,  Ceftriaxone and Moxifloxacin (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 47:2354, 2003). 

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • Retrospective case series of 165 consecutive cases with human intestinal spirochetosis on colonoscopy (81% with symptoms, usually diarrhea) confirmed predominance of MSM (86.7%) (Trop Med Infect Dis 8:250 2023). There was high coinfection rate with Neisseria, Chlamydia as well as intestinal protozoa. Symptoms were treated successfully with metronidazole or doxycycline.
  • Whether the organism is a pathogen or merely a commensal is unclear (Dig Dis Sci 58:202, 2013).
  • Some suggestion that the organism is more common in HIV+ individuals (BMC Infect Dis15:13 2015; Trop Med Infect Dis 8:250 2023) who had colon biopsy, but clinical significance unclear.
