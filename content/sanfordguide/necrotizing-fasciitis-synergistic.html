Necrotizing Fasciitis, Synergistic

Clinical Setting

  • Synergistic necrotizing fasciitis occurs in a variety of settings that allow combination of aerobic and anaerobic pathogens access to the fascial plane between subcutaneous fat and the underlying musculature.
  • A rare example is Meleney's synergistic gangrene (Staph.aureus + microaerophilic Strep.) on the abdominal wall.
  • Can complicate GI surgery with any combination of Enterobacteriaceae and anaerobic gram-negative rod bowel flora.
  • Penetration of GI organisms into skin can spread in fascial plane into scrotum, penis and inferior abdominal wall (Fournier's gangrene).
    • Inciting events: Post-coital trauma, UTI, genital piercing, penile implants and rectal foreign body
    • Note: Package insert warning for Sodium glucose co-transporter-2 inhibitor (SGLT2) drugs:
      • Reports of necrotizing fasciitis of the perineum (Fournier's gangrene), a rare but serious and life threatening necrotizing infection requiring urgent surgical intervention, have been  identified in post-marketing surveillance in patients with diabetes mellitus receiving SGLT2 inhibitors (Ann Intern Med 2019; 170:764)
  • See Staphylococcal necrotizing fasciitis for further discussion.
  • Review: New Engl J Med 2017;377:2253

Etiologies

  • Mixture of aerobic and anaerobic bacteria spreading in fascial plane

Primary Regimens

  • Early exploratory surgery to establish diagnosis and resect necrotic tissue
  • Imipenem-cilastatin or Meropenem OR
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Add Vancomycin or Daptomycin if Staph. aureus suspected

Alternative Regimens

  • Early exploratory surgery to establish diagnosis and resect necrotic tissue
  • Penicillin allergy manifest as skin rash only and unable to tolerate carbapenem: Cefepime + Metronidazole (+ Vancomycin or Daptomycin if Staph. aureus suspected)
  • Penicillin allergy manifest as anaphylaxis or angioneurotic edema: Ciprofloxacin + Metronidazole (+ Vancomycin or Daptomycin if Staph. aureus suspected)

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Antibiotic therapy should be adjusted based on culture results.
  • Duration of treatment is not predictable; treat until evidence of healthy granulation tissue at all debridement sites.

Comments

  • Use of hyperbaric oxygen (HBO) for necrotizing soft tissue infection has been debated, see Arch Surgery 139:1339, 2004. Recent study limited to centers which have on-site HBO found survival benefit for those in the most extremely ill category (Surg Infect (Larchmt) 15:328, 2014).
