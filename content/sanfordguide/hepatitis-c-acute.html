HCV, Acute

Clinical Setting

  • Acute Hepatitis C infection (HCV)
    • Up to 3% of world is infected, 4 million in U.S.
    • Co-infection with HIV is common.
  • Usually asymptomatic (>75%). Can detect by PCR within 13 days; antibody in 36+ days (Clin Infect Dis 40:951, 2005).
  • See also Management of exposure to Hepatitis C.

Etiologies

  • Hepatitis C virus (HCV)

Primary Regimens

  • Test and treat upon diagnosis of acute HCV.
  • Treat as indicated for chronic HCV (see HCV Treatment)

Alternative Regimens

  • Some providers will decide to post-pone treatment awaiting spontaneous clearance
  • Arguments against waiting and to treat now include (Clin Infect Dis. 2018;66(9):1360-1365.):
    • HCV transmission prevention (e.g., a surgeon, a person with ongoing intravenous drug use, or an HIV-positive man who engages in sex with other men)
    • Mitigation of clinical consequences (e.g., a patient with cirrhosis who is acutely superinfected with HCV)
    • Reduction in the likelihood of loss to follow-up (e.g., a patient who may not be engaged in care in 3 to 6 months) 

Comments

  • 20-50% clear infection within 6 months (Am J Gastroenterol. 103(5):1283-1297, 2008).
  • Now that effective DAA therapy is available, infection is readily cleared (cured) with standard therapy
