Primary HIV Infection

Clinical Setting

  • Clinical picture is non-specific.
    • Usually described as an acute mononucleosis syndrome: i.e., fever, lymphadenopathy, sore throat, and rash. "Monospot negative Mono"
    • Can present as an aseptic meningitis.
  • Definition: Initial burst of HIV viremia with undetectable anti-HIV antibody.
  • Important to recognize as very high viral load poses significant risk of transmission of HIV to others.
  • Diagnosis:
    • Very high level of circulating virus as measured by RT-PCR, often, >1,000,000 copies/mL; values of <2000 copies/mL suggest a false positive result
    • p24 antigen levels also very high
    • HIV antibody tests are negative
  • The standard recommendation is to treat immediately.
    • Potential benefits: Preservation of functional pool of responding CD4 lymphocytes both in the circulation and in deep tissue reservoirs: e.g., gastrointestinal associated lymphoid tissue
    • Decreased risk of transmission of HIV due to rapid decrease in viral "load".

Etiologies

  • HIV-1

Primary Regimens

  • Use same regimens as recommended for treatment of chronic HIV infection (see Adult ART or ART in Children), though preference is for a strand-transfer integrates inhibitor  (Dolutegravir-, or Bictegravir) -containing regimen, since resistance to these drugs is uncommon. Combine with Descovy (FTC + TAF), since HLA-B5701 testing and HBV infection status not immediately available.
  • Adjustment to this initial (urgent) regimen can be made once the baseline data have returned. Change to single tablet regimen, if desired, can be done later after all information has returned.
  • Perform genotypic resistance testing (estimated 8% - 10% transmission of resistant virus).

Comments

  • Lifelong therapy once ART is initiated.
  • Use of more than 3 active agents (e.g., 5 drugs) has no advantage over the usual 3 active drugs and may have more treatment associated toxicity (J Acquir Immune Defic Syndr 66(2):140-7, 2014).
  • Perform resistance testing to guide design of ARV regimen because of appreciable risks of acquisition of virus with resistance to one or more ARV; however, in the setting of acute seroconversion syndrome, empiric therapy should be started while waiting for the resistance test results. The regimen can be modified if needed once the results of the resistance test return.
