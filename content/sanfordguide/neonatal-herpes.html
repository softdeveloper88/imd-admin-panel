Neonatal Herpes

Clinical Setting

  • Most transmission is peripartum but can occur in utero or post partum
  • 3 distinct syndromes of peripartum infection:
    • Skin, eye and mouth (SEM): typically presents 9-11 days of life (range 4-14 days)
    • Disseminated: presents with septic appearance, LFT abnormalities  9-11 days of life (range 4-14 days)
    • Central nervous system (CNS) but infants can progress from one to the other: presents 16-17 days of life (range 10-21 days)
  • In utero infection presents with vesicles or scarring, eye disease, microcephaly or hydrocephalus
  • For management of asymptomatic infant born to mother with active lesions see Pediatrics. 2013 Feb;131(2):e635-4

Diagnosis

  • PCR of lesion, blood and CSF. Also obtain swabs for PCR of conjunctiva, nasopharynx, mouth and anus in asymptomatic infants born to women with active herpes(or culture if PCR unavailable, but this is slower and not more sensitive)
    • PCR has replaced culture because of faster turnaround
  • Elevated transaminases raise suspicion of disseminated or CNS disease
  • Management of infants born by vaginal delivery or C-section to mothers with active lesions depends on whether it is primary or secondary infection. All infants require at least surface swabs of PCR or culture and serum HSV culture. Start acyclovir pending evaluation. For full algorithm, see AAP Red Book

Etiologies

  • HSV type 1 or 2

Primary Regimens

  • Acyclovir 60 mg/kg/day IV divided q8h
  • SEM disease treated for 14 days
  • Disseminated and CNS disease treated for 21 days
  • For CNS disease perform PCR of CSF at or close to day 21. If positive, treat an additional 7 days and repeat PCR. If positive treat for another 7 days
  • After treatment, suppressive therapy reduces recurrence, improves outcome. Acyclovir 900 mg/m2/day  po divided 3 times daily x 6 months

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Empiric acyclovir while awaiting results of surface swabs, lesion, blood and CSF PCR should be considered for infants with born to mothers with active disease, vesicles, transaminits, DIC, or unexplained neurologic symptoms or CSF pleocytosis.

Comments

  • All infants with neonatal HSV should have ophthalmologic exam and neuro-imaging, preferably MRI
  • Risk score successfully identifies infants at low risk of disseminated and CNS disease but did not evaluate SEM Pediatrics 2021 Sept 148(3)
  • Review Infect Dis Clin North Am. 2015 Sep;29(3):391; Efficacy of suppression: N Engl J Med. 2011 Oct 6;365(14):1284
