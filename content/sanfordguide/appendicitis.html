Appendicitis

Clinical Setting

  • Acute inflammation of the appendix. Manifests as right lower quadrant pain.
  • Severity can vary from focal pain to diffuse abdominal pain with rebound tenderness due to peritonitis if the appendix has perforated.
  • Peritonitis can be complicated by bacteremia and bacteremic shock.
  • Surgical consultation is indicated in virtually all patients.
  • Factors and considerations in choice of antibacterials and whether or not appendectomy is indicated:
    • Antibacterials:
      • Desire antibiotic regimens with predictable activity vs both aerobic and facultative and  anaerobic gram-negative bacilli
    • Appendectomy?
      • Can consider antibiotic only therapy if:
        • Appendix is not perforated as judged by absent signs of peritonitis
        • Abdominal CT is without evidence of free air, abscess or fecolith.
        • Serum procalcitonin level is usually ≤0.25 ng/mL
        • Patient understands there is a 39%  risk of recurrent appendicitis within 5 years
        • More details on study design, see Comments
        • Ref: JAMA 2018; 320:1245 (editorial) & 1259; J Trauma Acute Care Surg 2019;86:722; N Engl J Med 2021; 385:1116.
      • Confirmatory randomized trial comparing a 10 day course of antibiotic therapy only with antibiotic plus appendectomy
        • Antibiotics alone were non-inferior to appendectomy on the basis of a 30-day quality of life scale. In the antibiotics alone group:
          • 29% had undergone an appendectomy by 90 days which included 41% of patients with a demonstrated appendicolith
        • See N Engl J Med 2020; 383:1907 and editorial N Engl J Med 2020; 383:1985; after 2 yrs, 46% had had an appendectomy: N Engl J Med 2021; 385:2395

Etiologies

  • Enterobacteriaceae, e.g., E. coli, Klebsiella sp., aerobic bacteria
  • Bacteroides sp. and other anaerobic bacteria
  • Enterococcus spp., e.g., E. faecalis, E. faecium
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa  (not common: 3-15%)
  • Most often, after a few hrs, infection is due to a combination of an aerobic gram-negative bacillus, E.coli, and an anaerobic gram-negative bacillus, B. fragilis
  • Rarely Actinomyces can lead to a protracted sinus tract forming infection
  • Role of bowel Candida species is unclear when fecal flora contaminate the peritoneum. A prospective randomized placebo-controlled study found no benefit from preemptive antifungal therapy (Clin Infect Dis 2015; 61:1671)

Primary Regimens

  • If decision is to perform appendectomy; antimicrobial therapy can be looked at as empiric or prophylaxis. If no perforation found at surgery, antimicrobial therapy is prophylaxis; If evidence of perforation or fecolith found at surgery, then pre-surgery antibiotics are initial empiric therapy
    • Adult: Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q24h + Metronidazole 500 mg IV q8h
    • Pediatric: Ceftriaxone 75 mg/kg IV q24h (max daily 2 gm) + Metronidazole 10 mg/kg IV q8h (max dose 500 mg; max daily 1500 mg)
      • With this approach, most patients were able to be discharged with no antibiotic therapy within a mean (±SD) of 23.5 (20) hrs (J Ped Surgery 2015;50:1566)
      • Can further streamline: Ceftriaxone 50 mg/kg/dose q24h (max 2 gm/day) + Metronidazole 30 mg/kg/ dose q24h (max 1500 mg/dose if ≥ to 80 kg or max of 1000 mg /dose if < 80 kg). Efficacious and cost-saving (J Ped Infect Dis 2017;6:57-64)
      • PK/PD justification of once daily metronidazole; studies in human volunteers: Antimicrob Agents Chemother 2004;48:4597
  • If decision is not to perform appendectomy but treat with antibiotics.  CT scan shows no evidence of appendicolith, perforation or abscess. Suggested antibacterial therapy:
    • Adult: Ertapenem 1 gm IV q24h
    • Pediatric: Ertapenem 15 mg/kg IV bid (max 1 gm/day)
  • Appendix is perforated; evidence of peritonitis and /or septic shock
    • Surgical consultation and likely appendectomy plus antibacterial therapy: 
    • Empiric antibiotic  therapy options for adults:
      • Meropenem 1 gm IV q8h
      • Imipenem 500mg to 1 gm IV q6h
      • Doripenem 500 mg IV q8h infused over one hr
      • Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h (see drug page for comment on loading dose)
      • Ciprofloxacin 400 mg IV q12h + Metronidazole 500 mg IV q8h
    • Duration of therapy
      • Treat until normalization of temperature and WBC, GI tract functioning
      • Can trend serum procalcitonin levels every 2-4 days. Stop antibiotics when serum level is < 0.25 ng/mL or has fallen 80% from peak concentration      

Alternative Regimens

  • Appendix at surgery is not perforated: want as narrow a spectrum as is reasonable (Pediatrics 2016;138:e20154547)
    • (Ciprofloxacin 400 mg IV q12h or Levofloxacin 750 mg IV q24h) + Metronidazole 1 gm IV q12h  or
    • Moxifloxacin 400 mg IV q24h
    • Usually only need 24 hrs of therapy
  • Appendix is perforated. Evidence of peritonitis and /or septic shock
    • Ceftolozane-tazobactam 3.0 gm IV over three hrs and repeat q8h + Metronidazole 500 mg IV over 30 min q8h.
      • Report of decreased efficacy if CrCl 30- 50 mL/min (Clin Infect Dis 2015;60:1462)
    • Ceftazidime-avibactam 2.5 gm IV over 2 hr and repeat q8h + Metronidazole 500 mg IV over 60 minutes and repeat q8h.
      • Report of decreased efficacy if CrCl 30-50 mL/min (Clin Infect Dis 2016;62:1380)
    • Ampicillin 2 gm IV q6h + Metronidazole 1 gm IV q12h + an aminoglycoside (Gentamicin or Tobramycin)
      • Rarely used due to risk of aminoglycoside toxicity
    • Aztreonam 1 gm IV q8h or 2 gm IV q6h + Metronidazole 1 gm IV q12h
    • Eravacycline 1 mg/kg IV over 60 minutes q12h x 4-14 days.
      • Efficacy based on non-inferiority to Ertapenem or Meropenem (see Clin Infect Dis 2019;69: 921)
  • See Primary Regimens for duration of therapy

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • General principal for empiric therapy of appendicitis/peritonitis due to bowel flora
    • Want regimen that includes drugs with predictable activity vs both aerobic and anaerobic gram negative bacilli
      • Drugs active vs only aerobic gram-negative bacilli: Aminoglycosides, second-third-fourth generation cephalosporins, Aztreonam, antipseudomonal penicillins, Ciprofloxacin, Levofloxacin
      • Drugs active vs only anaerobic gram negative bacilli (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 2012;56:1247):
        • Metronidazole
        • Ceftolozane-tazobactam and Ceftazidime-avibactam have some, but incomplete activity vs anaerobes and hence need to use with metronidazole
        • Drugs no longer recommended for gram-negative anaerobes due to increasing resistance:
          • Cefoxitin/Cefotetan (Clin Infect Dis 2014;59:698)
          • Clindamycin (Clin Infect Dis 2010;50:133)
      • Drugs active vs both aerobic and anaerobic gram negative bacilli
        • Piperacillin-tazobactam
        • Ampicillin-sulbactam no longer recommended for empiric therapy due to increasing resistance of E. coli and other Enterobacterales (Clin Infect Dis 2010;50:133)
          • 45% of E. coli resistant in patients with UTIs (Current Urol. 2022;16:94)
        • Carbapenems (meropenem, imipenem, doripenem)
        • Moxifloxacin: Limited experience with severe disease. Maybe OK for non-perforated appendicitis
        • Eravacycline: Limited experience due to recent FDA approval
  • Common areas of uncertainty
    • Need for empiric activity vs. MRSA?  No clear need.
    • Need for empiric activity vs. enterococci?  Usually not necessary. Exception is patient with valvular heart disease who is at risk of infective endocarditis
    • Empiric therapy of Candida species?  Prospective randomized placebo-controlled trial found no benefit of pre-emptive (empiric) echinocandin antifungal therapy (Clin Infect Dis 2015;61:1671)
    • When is specific therapy for Candida indicated?
      • Pure culture of Candida species from the abdominal cavity or blood
      • In a patient not responding to antibacterial therapy, positive serum marker of fungal infection: e.g., serum beta-D-glucan
      • Presence of acute necrotizing pancreatitis

Comments

  • Treating appendicitis without surgery. Clinical trials:

    • 530 patients with CT confirmed appendicitis were randomized to antibiotics alone or antibiotics plus surgery. Excluded if appendix perforated, presence fecolith, or abscess.
      • All were given Ertapenem.
      • Success rate with surgery plus antibiotics: 99.6%. Success rate with antibiotics alone: 72.7% success.  Of those who chose no appendectomy, there was a 39% chance of developing recurrent appendicitis within 5 years of initial presentation
      • Reference: JAMA 2018; 320:1245 (editorial) & 1259; Review: N Engl J Med 2021; 385:1116.
    • Randomized trial of antibiotic therapy and no surgery in adult patients. Asked whether all oral therapy would be as efficacious as IV therapy followed by oral therapy.
      • Compared Moxifloxacin 400 mg po once daily  x 7 days vs Ertapenem 1 gm IV once daily for 2 days and then combination of Levofloxacin 500 mg po once daily + Metronidazole 500 mg po tid
      • Result:
        • Discharge from the hospital without need for surgery and no recurrent appendicitis within 1 year occurred in 70.2% of patients who received oral antibiotics alone vs 73.8% who received IV followed by oral antibiotics
        • However, the difference exceeded the non-inferiority margin of 6%, p = 0.26
      • Reference: JAMA 2021;325: 351 (editorial) & 353
  • Reviews on diagnosis and management: JAMA 2021;326:2299; Amer. Surgeon 2023; 89:2644; Advances in Pediatrics. 2023; 70:105
