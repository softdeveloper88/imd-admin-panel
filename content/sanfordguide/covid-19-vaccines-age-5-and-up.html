COVID-19, mRNA Vaccines (≥5 yrs)

Introduction

  • CDC recommends that everyone >6 mos. of age, whether or not they have ever previously been vaccinated with a COVID-19 vaccine, receive an updated 2024-2025 vaccine this fall.  
    • Antigenic composition: 
      • KP.2 for Moderna and Pfizer mRNA vaccine
      • JN.1 for Novavax recombinant protein vaccine. Option for for certain persons >12 yrs.
  • Summary of guidelines for people >5 yrs who are not moderately or severely immunocompromised
    • Initial vaccination
      • 1 dose of 2024–25 Moderna or Pfizer
    • Received previous doses of a COVID-19 vaccine
      • 1 dose of 2024–­25 Moderna or Pfizer
    • No additional doses of 2024–­25 vaccine for people <65 yrs who are not moderately or severely immunocompromised are recommended at this time.
    • Persons >65 yrs should receive 2 doses of 2024–2025 mRNA vaccine separated by 6 mos (minimum interval 2 mos) regardless of previous vaccination history, 
  • Dosing, including additional discretionary doses for the moderately and severely immunocompromised remains complex (see table below).

Vaccine

  • Comirnaty (Pfizer) COVID-19 Vaccine, mRNA (2024-25 Formula)
  • Spikevax (Moderna) COVID-19 Vaccine, mRNA (2024-25 Formula)
    • CDC acronym for both: 1vCOV-mRNA
  • Novavax COVID-19 Vaccine, Adjuvanted (2024–25 Formula) is an option for certain persons >12 yrs.

Indications for Vaccination (US)

  • All US persons age >6 mos should be up-to-date on vaccination regardless of
    • a history of symptomatic or asymptomatic SARS-CoV-2 infection,
    • presence of long COVID, or
    • a history of a SARS-Cov2 breakthrough infection. 
  • In short, for normal hosts:
    • Every normal host aged >5 yrs is
      • Up-to-date after receipt of 1 updated COVID-19 vaccine.
        • Regardless of 0 or number of previous doses of mRNA, or Janssen/J&J vaccine
        • Exception: unvaccinated persons need 2 doses of updated Novavax vaccine to be up-to-date
  • Persons with prior infection still benefit from vaccination as “hybrid immunity” is associated with increased T and B cell responses and better protection against severe disease than either infection or vaccination alone.
  • Defer vaccination until recovery from acute episode and discontinuation of isolation.
    • After SARS-CoV-2 infection, persons may consider delaying next dose by 3 mos from symptom onset or positive test.

U.S. CDC COVID-19 Vaccination Schedules

  • Vaccine schedule is based on the age on the day of vaccination.

Efficacy, Duration of Protection

  • Time since most recent COVID-19 vaccine dose is more important than cumulative number of COVID-19 vaccine doses (no matter what antigenic content) that have been given.
  • In adults, compared to no receipt of updated vaccine, multiple surveillance networks have shown that the 2023-24 formulation had a relative VE of 40-50% (vs no 2023-24 booster) against any of symptomatic infection, ER visits or hospitalization for up to 4 mos but as before waning with time is expected.

Choice, Interchangeability

  • CDC guidelines refer to "2024-25 COVID vaccine" with no preferred product (Moderna, Pfizer, Novavax) regardless of past vaccine history.
  • Reproducible data indicate that the original Moderna vaccine has both a modest VE and durability advantage over the Pfizer vaccine (higher mRNA dose in monovalent doses).
  • Pemivibart is not a substitute for COVID-19 vaccination.
    • People who are moderately or severely immunocompromised should receive vaccine according to the recommended schedule.
    • Administration of pemivibart should be deferred for at least 2 wks after a dose of COVID-19 vaccine.

Toxicities

Contraindications

  • History of a severe allergic reaction (e.g., anaphylaxis) after a previous vaccine dose or to a component of the COVID-19 vaccine.
    • Do not vaccinate with the same COVID-19 vaccine type but with allergist consultation may administer the alternate COVID-19 vaccine type.
  • Recent exposure to SARS-CoV-2 is not a contraindication or precaution to COVID-19 vaccination.

Precautions

  • History of a diagnosed non-severe allergy to a component of the COVID-19 vaccine.
    • Non-severe allergic reactions include but are not limited to: urticaria beyond the injection site; angioedema involving lips, facial skin, or skin in other locations.
    • Any angioedema affecting the airway (i.e., tongue, uvula, or larynx) is considered a severe allergic reaction. 
    • may administer the alternate vaccine
  • History of a non-severe, immediate (onset less than 4 hours) allergic reaction after administration of a previous dose of one COVID-19 vaccine type.
    • may administer the alternate vaccine
  • Defer vaccination until resolution of a moderate or severe acute illness, with or without fever
  • History of MIS-C or MIS-A.
    • seek expert consultation
  • History of myocarditis or pericarditis within 3 wks after a dose of any COVID-19 vaccine
    • A subsequent dose of any COVID-19 vaccine should generally be avoided.
  • Recent SARS-CoV-2 infection may allow consideration of delaying a primary series dose or booster dose by 3 mos
    • Increased interval likely improves immune response to vaccination.
  • Administration of an antiviral drug pre- or post-vaccination unlikely to impair response

Adverse Effects

  • Comprehensive overview of U.S. government’s COVID-19 vaccine safety monitoring systems (Vaccine 2024, Apr 16:S0264-410X).
  • Local: Pain at the injection site, sometimes severe
  • Systemic: Fatigue, headache, and myalgia
    • Symptoms more frequent and severe following dose 2 especially in younger adults
    • Symptoms begin 1–2 days after vaccination and resolved after 1–2 days.
  • Estimated anaphylaxis incidence is 5 per million doses of mRNA COVID-19 vaccines administered.
  • Cases of myocarditis and pericarditis causally related to COVID vaccination have rarely been observed in the US.
    • Cases have occurred most frequently in adolescent and young adult males within 7 days after receiving the second dose of an mRNA vaccine.
    • Cases among young males have occurred after booster doses
    • The risk of adverse cardiac outcomes is 1.8–5.6 times higher after SARS-CoV-2 infection than after mRNA COVID-19 vaccination among males age 12 through 17 years.  
  • Development of myocarditis or pericarditis within 3 wks after a dose of any COVID vaccine is a precaution to a subsequent dose of any COVID vaccine, and subsequent doses should generally be avoided.
    • If, after a risk assessment, the decision is made to administer a subsequent dose, wait until after the episode of myocarditis or pericarditis has resolved 
  • Available data do not provide clear and consistent evidence of a safety problem for ischemic stroke with bivalent mRNA COVID-19 vaccines when given alone or given simultaneously with influenza vaccines.
  • Persons who have a history of other heart disease, including congenital heart disease and Kawasaki disease, may receive COVID vaccine.

Drug Interactions

  • Influenza (including HD or adjuvanted) and COVID-19 vaccines should be given at the same visit, if eligible.
    • Coadministration of bivalent Pfizer vaccine and influenza vaccine was associated with generally similar effectiveness in the community setting against COVID-19-related and influenza-related outcomes compared with giving each vaccine alone (JAMA Net Open 2023 Nov 1;6(11):e2342151). 
  • COVID-19 vaccines may be administered without regard to timing of other vaccines.
  • Dose 2 administered no more than 4 days before the minimum (21 or 28 days) interval is valid; however, do not repeat an inadvertent dose administered earlier.
  • No minimum interval between COVID-19 vaccination and mPox vaccine
  • Consider waiting 4 wks after mPox vaccination before COVID-19 vaccination to minimize myocarditis.
  • Simultaneous administration of COVID vaccine and nirsevimab (a long-acting monoclonal antibody for certain infants and young children for prevention of RSV) is recommended.

Special Populations

Pregnancy, Breastfeeding

  • Vaccinate and keep up-to-date according to standard recommendations if pregnant, breastfeeding, attempting or contemplating conception.
    • Pregnant and recently pregnant persons at increased risk for severe illness and the fetus is at increased risk
    • No increase in birth defects have been detected among infants whose mothers received vaccine in the first trimester (JAMA Pediatr. 2024, Jul1:e241917).
  • Benefits outweighs risks of vaccination.
  • Vaccine antibodies are transferred to the newborn and likely are effective for up to 6 mos.
  • No contraindications to breastfeeding.

Serologic Testing

  • Antibody testing is not recommended to assess for immunity following COVID-19 vaccination or to assess the need for vaccination in an unvaccinated person.
  • No correlates of protection are available and assays vary widely.
  • +ve IgG only minimally reassuring, -ve IgG unhelpful in patients whose ability to mount a B-cell response is uncertain.
  • If antibody testing is done, vaccination should be completed as recommended regardless of the antibody test result.
  • Prior receipt of a COVID-19 vaccine will not affect the results of SARS-CoV-2 viral tests (nucleic acid amplification or antigen tests).

Comments

  • Consider 15 minute observation period post-administration especially for adolescents.
  • Consider 30 minute observation period post-administration if:
    • Allergy related contraindication to another COVID vaccine
    • Non-severe allergic reaction (within 4 hrs) to any previous COVID vaccine
    • Anaphylaxis after non-COVID-19 vaccines or injectable therapies
  • mRNA vaccines do not have a risk of modifying the vaccine recipient’s genetic makeup as mRNA does not enter cell nucleus where host DNA is located
