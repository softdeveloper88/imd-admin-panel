Influenza, Prevention

Clinical Setting

  • Give influenza vaccine: see Influenza, Vaccines
  • Influenza vaccine can be co-administered with COVID-19 vaccines 
    • What about patients with allergy to eggs ?
      • Persons with a history of egg allergy who have experienced only urticaria (hives) after exposure to egg can receive any icensed, recommended influenza vaccine (i.e., any IIV4, RIV4, or LAIV4) that is otherwise appropriate for the recipient’s age and health status
      • People who have severe egg allergies (e.g., angioedema or swelling, respiratory distress, lightheadedness, or recurrent vomiting) should be vaccinated in a medical setting and be supervised by a health care provider who is able to recognize and manage severe allergic conditions.
      • Recombinant influenza vaccine (RIV4, Flubloc) and cell culture-based vaccine (Flucelvax, ccIIV4) can be given without precautions to people with severe egg allergy.
    • What about people with allergies to flu vaccines?
      • A history of severe allergic reaction (e.g., anaphylaxis) to any influenza vaccine (i.e., any egg-based IIV, ccIIV, RIV, or LAIV of any valency) is a contraindication to future receipt of all egg-based IIV4s and LAIV4.
      • A history of severe allergic reaction (e.g., anaphylaxis) to any influenza vaccine (i.e., any egg-based IIV, ccIIV, RIV, or LAIV of any valency) is a precaution to the use of RIV4 or ccIIV4. The should be given in a medical setting and be supervised by a health care provider who is able to recognize and manage severe allergic conditions.
      • For more detail on allergy see MMWR 2022 Aug 26;71(1):1-28
    • Children <8 years old should get two doses of flu vaccine initially. Once they have had two doses they only need a single dose each year thereafter.
    • New types of influenza vaccine have become available and led to changes in names and indications. See Influenza, Vaccines.
    • Two vaccines are available that are more immunogenic in persons >65: High dose IIV4 (HD-IIV4) and MF-59 adjuvanted IIV4 (aIIV4). RCT demonstrated improved efficacy for HDIIV4 and observational studies demonstrated increased efficacy for aIIV4.
  • Chemoprophylaxis with antivirals (see CDC recommendations and 2018 IDSA Guidelines (published online Clin Infect Dis 2018) 
    • CDC and IDSA do not recommend widespread or routine use of antiviral medications for chemoprophylaxis except all nursing home residents should receive prophylaxis as one of multiple interventions to control institutional influenza outbreaks.
    • Chemoprophylaxis be considered  for persons at very high risk for complications(such as those with severe immune compromise)and for whom influenza vaccination is contraindicated, unavailable, or expected to have low effectiveness, after household exposure to influenza.
    • Test for influenza and switch to antiviral treatment dosing in persons receiving postexposure antiviral chemoprophylaxis who become symptomatic, preferably with an antiviral drug with a different resistance profile 
    • Decision should consider the exposed person's risk for influenza complications, the type and duration of contact, recommendations from local or public health authorities, and clinical judgment.
    • Postexposure prophylaxis should be used in to control influenza in nursing home outbreaks
    • Postexposure chemoprophylaxis (that is once daily dosing) should be only used when antivirals can be started within 48 hours of the most recent exposure.
    • In many settings, early empiric therapy initiated by the patient is an effective alternative to chemoprophylaxis.
  • Persons at higher risk of complications of influenza include:
    • Children age < 2 years
    • Adults age > 65 years
    • Persons with chronic pulmonary, cardiovascular, renal, hepatic, hematologic, metabolic, or neurologic conditions; severe developmental delay; mental retardation
    • Persons with immunosuppression, transplant, HIV
    • Women who are pregnant or 2 weeks post-partum
    • Persons age <18 years who are receiving long-term aspirin therapy
    • American Indians/Alaskan natives
    • Persons with morbid obesity (i.e., BMI > 40)
    • Residents of nursing homes and other chronic care facilities
    • Highest risk of complications in HSCT and solid organ transplant patients

Etiologies

  • Influenza virus

Primary Regimens

  • Age 5 years - adult:
    • Oseltamivir 75 mg po q24h or Zanamivir 2 inhalations (5 mg each) once daily x 7 days after last known exposure
    • Baloxavir 40 mg po once (80 mg if >80 kg) for those ≥5 years

Alternative Regimens

  • Laninamivir 40 mg inhalation single dose (age ≥ 10 yrs to adult) or 20 mg (age < 10 yrs), is approved in Japan. Trials showed non-inferiority to oseltamivir (Clin Infect Dis 51:1167, 2010)

Comments

  • Baloxavir was 86% effective as postexposure prophlyaxis after confirmed family exposure (attack rate 1.9% vs 13.6%)similar to older studies with oseltamivir and zanamivir
  • Chemoprophylaxis with Oseltamivir or Zanamivir can be considered for the two weeks following vaccination in high risk individuals, or for the duration of peak influenza in community, or for outbreak control in high-risk populations if vaccination cannot be administered (Clin Infect Dis online).
