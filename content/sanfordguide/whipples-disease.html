Whipple's Disease

Clinical Setting

  • Infection of the small intestine, CNS, or endocardium: Whipple's disease.
  • Also manifests as a multiorgan, chronic infectious disorder.
  • Caused by the rod-shaped intracellular bacterium Tropheryma whipplei (N Engl J Med 356:55, 2007; Lancet Infect Dis 8:179, 2008).
  • In vitro:
    • Ceftriaxone had high MICs for intracellular organisms (Antimicrob Agts Chemother 48: 747, 2004)
    • TMP is not active: absent dihydrofolate reductase (trimethoprim target)
    • Acquired resistance to sulfonamides and failure of TMP-SMX described (Int J Antimicrob Agents 43: 383, 2014)
    • Doxycycline + hydroxychloroquine bactericidal in vitro (Antimicrob Agts Chemother 48: 747, 2004)
  • Clinically:
    • 35 reports of acquired resistance and relapses in recipients of TMP/SMX
    • High failure rates of TMP-SMX therapy in France (J Antimicrob Chemother 69:219, 2014)
    • Evidence of genetic predisposition to infection and relapse, hence some recommend lifelong suppression

Etiologies

  • Tropheryma whipplei

Primary Regimens

  • Doxycycline 100 mg bid + Hydroxychloroquine 200 mg tid for 12 months, followed by Doxycycline long-term (possibly life-long) suppression (J Infect 69:103, 2014; Curr Opin Infect Dis 31(6):463-470, 2018)
  • Treatment may be complicated by Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome (IRIS). Successful treatment of IRIS with steroids (Ann Internal Med 153: 710, 2010) or Thalidomide (J Infect 60:79, 2010).

Alternative Regimens

  • Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV daily for 14 days then TMP-SMX 160-800 mg twice daily for 1-2 years (Clin Micro Rev 30(2): 529, 2017)
  • Meropenem 1 g IV q8h x 14 days, then TMP-SMX 160/800 mg po bid (alternative Doxycycline 100 mg po bid + Hydroxychloroquine 200 mg po tid) for at least a year (Lancet Inf Dis 16: e13, 2016)
  • Both Ceftriaxone and Meropenem for initial IV therapy x 14 days (followed by TMP-SMX for a year) were effective in a non-blinded study of Whipple's disease (Gastroenterol 138: 478, 2010).

Comments

  • Late relapses with resistance to TMP-SMX have been described (J Antimicrob Chemother 65:2005, 2010).
  • French group recommends consideration of life-long doxycycline suppression after initial treatment course (J Infect 69: 103, 2014).
  • Some extended IV therapy to 4 weeks for CNS disease.
  • Cultivated from CSF in patients with intestinal disease and no neurologic findings (J Infect Dis 188:797, 2003 & J Infect Dis 188:801, 2003).
  • Case report of successful treatment of refractory CNS infection with Chloroquine + Minocycline.
  • Tropheryma endocarditis experience (Emerg Infect Dis 19: 1721, 2013)
  • Review: Clin Micro Rev 30(2): 529, 2017; Curr Opin Infect Dis 31(6): 463, 2018.
