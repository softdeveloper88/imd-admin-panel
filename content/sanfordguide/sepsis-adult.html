Sepsis, Adult

Clinical Setting

  • Sepsis in adult, non-neutropenic patient. Review: N Engl J Med. 2024; 391:2133.
  • Introduction and Definitions
    • Sepsis definition: Syndrome of acute organ dysfunction due to dysregulated response to infection: JAMA 2016;315:801
      • Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) Score used to assess liklihood  and severity of invasive infectiion
        • See SOFA Score calculator.
          • SOFA measures organ dysfunction  based on assessment of six organ systems: lung, liver, cardiovascular hematologic, renal and neurologic
          • Points for oxygenation, platelet count, bilirubin, blood pressure, renal function and mental status.
        •  Alternatively, "quick" SOFA score (qSOFA) works. At least 2 of:
          • Respiratory rate ≥ 22/min
          • Altered mental status
          • Systolic blood pressure ≤ 100 mm Hg
    • Septic shock definition:
      • Meets sepsis definition plus life-threatening organ dysfunction
      • Need for vasopressor therapy to increase/maintain MAP at > 65 mm Hg
      • Lactate > 2 mmol/L (18 mg/dL) after adequate fluid resuscitation
    • References: Third international Consensus Definitions of Sepsis and Septic Shock (Sepsis 3): Crit Care Med 2021;49: e1063; Crit Care Med 2021; 49:1974.
      • SIRS and Severe Sepsis are no  longer considered part of the spectrum of invasive microbial infection
  • Principal modalities of therapy summarized as Sepsis Management Bundles. 
    • Fluid resuscitation: current data support preference for balanced crystalloid (e.g., lactated ringers) as opposed to normal saline: 30 ml/kg within the first 3 hrs. See N Engl J Med. 2018;378:829
    • Empiric antimicrobial therapy:
      • Targets suspected or possible etiologic organisms; focus therapy as dictated by culture results
      • Initiation of therapy within one hour of arrival at Emergency Department; some say 3 hrs (controversial)
      • Refs.: Chest 2019; 155:938; Chest 2019; 156:645; New Engl J Med 2019; 380:1369; Clin Infect Dis 2021; 72:541and 553.
    • Diagnostics to detect presence of potential pathogen(s) to help ascertain source of the sepsis
    • Pressors if necessary to maintain mean arterial pressure >/= 65 mm Hg
    • Measure serum lactate concentration
  • References
    • Surviving Sepsis Campaign Bundle (Crit Care Med 2021; 49:1974).
    • Timing of empiric antibiotics (Crit Care Med. 2024; 52: 1138)
    • Assessment of the sepsis/septic shock bundle (Clin Infect Dis. 2024; 78:505)

Etiologies

  • If suspect intra-abdominal source, need treatment active vs:
    • Aerobic gram-negative bacilli, e.g., E. coli
    • Anaerobic gram-negative bacilli, e.g., Bacteroides fragilis
  • If associated petechial rash
    • Neisseria meningitidis
    • Consider Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever
  • If suspect pyelonephritis
    • Aerobic gram-negative bacilli
    • Enterococcus sp.
  • Community-acquired pneumonia
    • Strep. pneumoniae
    • Staph. aureus (MSSA, MRSA)
    • Legionella sp.
    • Gram-negative bacilli
  • Illicit IV drug use
    • Staph. aureus (MSSA and MRSA)
  • Urinary
    • Aerobic gram-negative bacilli
    • Enterococcus sp.

Primary Regimens

  • Empiric therapy when source is unclear and patient is unstable and/or immunocompromised:
    •  Meropenem 1-2 gm IV q8h + Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator); alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL
    • If low prevalence of ESBL and/or carbapenemase producing gram-negative bacilli: Vancomycin + Piperacillin-tazobactam pending clarification of clinical syndrome and culture results.
    • If high prevalence of ESBL producing and/or serine (KPC) carbapenemase producing aerobic gram-negative bacilli: Vancomycin + Ceftazidime-avibactam 2.5 gm IV q8h, infused over 3h, or Meropenem-vaborbactam  2gm IV q8h, infused over 3h, or Imipenem-relebactam 1.25 gm IV q6h, infused over 30 min. 
      • Adjust based on culture results.
      • NOTE: the latter drugs lack activity against bacteria producing metallo-carbapenemases.
      • Cohort observational study comparing standard short infusion vs extended infusion of beta-lactams in the treatment of hospitalized adults with gram negative bacteremia (JAMA Network Open. 2024;7:e2418234).
  • Suspect source is biliary:
    • Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h (see drug page for comment on loading dose)
  • Suspect source is community-acquired pneumonia:
    • Levofloxacin 750 mg IV q24h or Moxifloxacin 400 mg IV q24h + Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h + Vancomycin (see drug page for comment on pip-tazo loading dose)
    • For use of low dose hydrocortisone see Pneumonia, Community-Acquired, Adult Inpatient and JAMA. 2024; 332:318.
  • Suspect source is illicit IV drug use:
    • If high prevalence of MRSA:
      • Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator); alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL
      • Use nasal swab for rapid detection of S.aureus using NAAT
  • Suspect meningococcemia manifest as petechial rash:
    • Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q12h

Alternative Regimens

  • Alternative empiric therapy when source is unclear:
    • Daptomycin 8-12 mg/kg IV q24h + Cefepime 2 gm IV q8h, infused over 3h or
    • Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h (see drug page for comment on loading dose)
  • Alternatives if suspected source is biliary:
    • Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q24h + Metronidazole 1 gm loading dose then 0.5 gm q6h or 1 gm IV q12h or
    • (Ciprofloxacin 400 mg IV q12h or Levofloxacin 750 mg IV q24h) + Metronidazole 1 gm loading dose then 0.5 gm q6h or 1 gm IV q12h
  • Alternatives for suspect community-acquired pneumonia:

    • Aztreonam 2 gm IV q8h + (Levofloxacin 750 mg IV q24h or Moxifloxacin 400 mg IV q24h) + Linezolid 600 mg IV q12h

Comments

  • Source unclear: Linezolid 600 mg IV q12h could be substituted for Vancomycin or Daptomycin, but Linezolid is bacteriostatic against Staph. aureus.
