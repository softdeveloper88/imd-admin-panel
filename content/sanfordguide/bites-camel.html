Bite: Camel

Clinical Setting

  • Bite wound from camel
  • Upper extremity most frequent site, osteomyelitis reported as late complication

Etiologies

  • Staph. aureus
  • Streptococcus pyogenes
  • Bacillus spp.
  • P. aeruginosa
  • Other gram-negative bacilli, including Aeromonas sp., Pasteurella aerogenes, and Actinobacillus sp.

Primary Regimens

  • Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h

Alternative Regimens

  • Cephalexin 500 mg po qid + Ciprofloxacin 750 mg po bid

Comments

  • Administer tetanus toxoid or Tdap (preferred) if > 10 years since last dose
  • Consider rabies as appropriate, rabies can occur in camels (PLoS Negl Trop Dis 10: e0004890, 2016).
    • Bites caused by livestock, uncommonly require initiation of PEP.
    • Consultation with local public health is advised.
  • See Rabies, Vaccines and PEP for detailed evaluation procedure and recommendations
  • See Wilderness & Environmental Med 29: 380, 2018
  • Eur J Clin Micro Infect Dis 18:918, 1999. Same publication lists organisms recovered from buccal swabs of recently slaughtered camels in Nigeria, including: S. aureus, group A streptococci, coagulase-negative staphylococci, Bacillus spp, Moraxella catarrhalis, Klebsiella pneumoniae and Pseudomonas aeruginosa.
  • MERS-CoV has been isolated from dromedary camels (Lancet Infect Dis 14:140, 2014).
