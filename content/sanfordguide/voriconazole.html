Voriconazole
Tradename: Vfend

Usage and Dosing

  • Voriconazole is a triazole (trifluorinated) with activity against Aspergillus sp., including Amphotericin B resistant strains of A. terreus. Active vs. Candida sp. (including C. krusei), Fusarium sp., and various molds.
  • Voriconazole pharmacokinetics are non-linear, and interindividual variability of PK is high.
  • Monitor trough concentrations to achieve the therapeutic range of 1-5.5 μg/mL (Pharmacother 31:214, 2011; Clin Infect Dis 55:1080, 2012 and Antimicrob Agents Chemother 53:24, 2009).
  • Up to 20% of patients have subtherapeutic levels with oral administration: check levels for suspected treatment failure, life threatening infections. 300 mg bid po dose or 8 mg/kg/d IV dose may be required to achieve target steady-state drug concentrations of 1-5.5 μg/mL.
  • Less than 2% of a dose is excreted unchanged in the urine.
  • Voriconazole has no activity vs. agents of mucormycosis.
  • Caution: numerous drug interactions (see table below, not all-inclusive). Voriconazole is metabolized by CYP2C19, CYP2C9, and CYP3A4. The affinity of voriconazole is highest for CYP2C19, followed by CYP2C9, and is appreciably lower for CYP3A4. The major metabolite is the N-oxide (inactive).

NOTES

  • For invasive aspergillosis, scedosporiosis, and fusariosis, IV therapy for at least one week is suggested before considering switch to oral therapy. Treatment is prolonged.
  • For serious Candida infection, treatment for at least 14 days following resolution of symptoms or following last positive culture, whichever is longer, is suggested.
  • For esophageal candidiasis, treatment for a minimum of 14 days, and for at least 7 days following resolution of symptoms, is suggested.
  • Adults weighing less than 40 kg should receive 50% of the oral maintenance dose (i.e., 100 mg po q12h).
  • Oral suspension dosing is the same as tablet dosing.

    Adverse Effects

    • Toxicity similar to other azoles/triazoles including uncommon serious hepatic toxicity (hepatitis, cholestasis & fulminant hepatic failure). Liver function tests should be monitored during treatment & drug discontinued if abnormalities develop.
    • Rash reported in up to 20%, photosensitivity is common and rare Stevens-Johnson, hallucinations and anaphylactoid infusion reactions with fever and hypertension. 1 case of QT prolongation with ventricular tachycardia in a 15 y/o patient with ALL reported.
    • Voriconazole use is an independent risk factor for skin cancer in lung transplant patients and may increase the risk for skin cancer in other population as well (Clin Infect Dis 2014; Am J Transplant 2018;18:5).
    • Approx. 21% experience a transient visual disturbance following IV or po (“altered/enhanced visual perception”, blurred or colored visual change or photophobia) within 30–60 minutes. Visual changes resolve within 30–60 min. after administration & are attenuated with repeated doses (do not drive at night for outpatient treatment). Persistent visual changes occur rarely. Cause unknown.
    • In patients with CrCl <50 mL/min., the drug should be given orally, not IV, since the intravenous vehicle SBECD (sulfobutylether-β cyclodextrin) may accumulate. Hallucinations, hypoglycemia, electrolyte disturbance and pneumonitis attributed to increased drug concentrations. Potential for drug-drug interactions high.
    • With prolonged use, fluoride in drug can cause a painful periostitis. Correlates with elevated serum fluoride levels (Clin Infect Dis 59: 1237, 2014). Resolves with discontinuation of drug.

    Pregnancy Risk

    FDA Risk Category 1 Can cause fetal harm. No data on voriconazole use in pregnancy are available. Advise use of effective contraception during treatment. Evidence of embryomortality, fetotoxicity, and teratogenicity in animals exist.
    Use during Lactation No data are available. If voriconazole is required by the mother, it is not a reason to discontinue breastfeeding. An alternate drug might be preferable.

    Comments
