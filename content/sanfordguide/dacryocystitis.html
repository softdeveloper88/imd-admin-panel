Dacryocystitis

Clinical Setting

  • Acute or chronic lacrimal sac infection often stemming from obstruction of lacrimal duct.
  • Acute infection often accompanied by preseptal cellulitis.
  • Culture to detect resistant Staph. aureus (MRSA). May be possible to collect exudate from punctum for Gram stain and culture.
  • Empiric therapy is based on Gram stain of aspirate.
  • CT-scan indicated for more serious infection extending beyond the lid or if lacrimal gland abscess is suspected.

Etiologies

  • Strep. pneumoniae
  • Staph. aureus
  • H. influenzae
  • S. pyogenes
  • Enterobacteriaceae
  • P. aeruginosa  (rare)

Primary Regimens

  • Ophthalmologic consultation is needed, treatment is primarily surgical.
  • Empirical therapy of mild infection limited to lacrimal sac and lid
    • Cephalexin 500 mg po qid
    • Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875 mg po bid
    • TMP-SMX 2 double strength tablets po bid
  • Empiric therapy of acute infection with systemic signs or symptoms of infection, see Orbital cellulitis

    Alternative Regimens

    • [Nafcillin or Oxacillin] 2 gm IV q6h or Cefazolin 2 gm q8h for documented MSSA infection
    • In cephalosporin-allergic patient, can substitute Levofloxacin 750 mg IV/po q24h for the cephalosporin.

    Antimicrobial Stewardship

    • Duration of therapy not well defined, but treat for 7-14 days.

    Comments

    • Cultures recommended to establish microbiologic diagnosis.
    • IV therapy and hospitalization in cases of suppurative bacterial infection with associated lacrimal gland abscess or possible periorbital infection.
