Arthritis, Post-Streptococcal

Clinical Setting

  • Immunologic reaction after streptococcal pharyngitis, usually in children.
  • Post-streptococcal reactive arthritis (PSRA) is a distinct entity from Acute Rheumatic Fever (ARF)
    • Onset of PSRA  occurs about 10 days after pharyngitis (14-21 days in ARF)
    • Arthritis of PSRA is cumulative, persistent, involves large, small, and axial joints (arthritis of ARF is migratory, transient, involves large joints)
    • PSRA responds less well to aspirin, whereas arthritis of ARF responds rapidly
    • Increased  HLA DRB1*01 expression
  • Aseptic arthritis with evidence of prior streptococcal infection.
    • Elevated ESR, CRP
    • Synovial fluid WBC < 50,000
    • Blood and synovial fluid cultures sterile
    • May be polyarticular, involve large, small, and axial joints
    • Jones criteria for ARF not met and other causes are excluded (e.g., Lyme disease, rheumatoid arthritis)
  • Symptoms may last for months.

Etiologies

  • Group A Streptococcus (S. pyogenes) (throat culture positive in about half of cases).

Primary Regimens

  • Treat as for Streptococcal pharyngitis
  • Then, manage with NSAIDs ± prednisone (may be required in some patients)

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • Secondary prophylaxis
    • Cardiac involvement rare: Clin Exp Rheumatol 33:578, 2015. 
    • The patient should be followed for several months for evidence of carditis, which if observed the patient should be reclassified as having ARF and should receive secondary prophylaxis.
    • Although one year of secondary prophylaxis is recommended by some, its utility are not well established: Circulation 2009; 119:154.
  • Reference: Pediatr Emerg Care 28:1185, 2012; BMJ Case Reports 2016; Curr Rheumatol Rep. 2021 Feb 10;23(3):19
