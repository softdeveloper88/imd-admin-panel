RSV, Vaccine

Introduction

  • Three RSV vaccines are FDA approved with ACIP guidelines in place.
    • In clinical trials VE of a single vaccine dose in the first year and VE in the second year against severe outpatient disease were: Abrysvo (Pfizer) 86% and 74%,  Arexvy (GSK) 79% and 59%, and MRESVIA (Moderna) 55% and 36%
    • Excess GBS cases are seen with Pfizer vaccine and to a lesser extent the GSK but so far not with Moderna vaccine.
      • The rates appear in line with other vaccines where the risk-benefit was deemed acceptable
    • Risk benefit analysis is vastly in favor or vaccination per indications below.
  • In May 2025, indicated age range for persons with increased risk of severe RSV expanded to 50-74 yrs of age 

Indications for Vaccination

Routine

  • Recommended for all adults age >75 yrs.

  • Recommended for adults age 50–74 yrs with certain chronic medical conditions or other factors that increase risk of severe RSV disease

    • NOT recommended for adults age 50–74 years who are not at increased risk of severe RSV.
    • Definition of increased RSV risk:

      • Chronic cardiovascular disease (e.g., heart failure, coronary artery disease, or congenital heart disease [excluding isolated hypertension])

      • Chronic lung or respiratory disease (e.g., chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, emphysema, asthma, interstitial lung disease, or cystic fibrosis)

      • End-stage renal disease or dependence on hemodialysis or other renal replacement therapy

      • Complicated diabetes mellitus  by chronic kidney disease, neuropathy, retinopathy, or other end-organ damage, or requiring treatment with insulin or sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 (SGLT2) inhibitor

      • Neurologic or neuromuscular conditions causing impaired airway clearance or respiratory muscle weakness (e.g., poststroke dysphagia, amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, or muscular dystrophy [excluding history of stroke without impaired airway clearance])

      • Chronic liver disease (e.g., cirrhosis)

      • Chronic hematologic conditions (e.g., sickle cell disease or thalassemia)

      • Severe obesity (body mass index ≥40 kg/m2)

      • Moderate or severe immune compromise

        • Less severe stages of diabetes mellitus, obesity, and chronic kidney disease are excluded given limited evidence of association with severe RSV disease.

      • Healthcare providers have discretion to vaccinate persons with other chronic medical conditions or risk factors that they determine might increase the risk of severe disease due to respiratory infection.  

        • The decision may be based on clinical assessment even in the absence of medical documentation of a named risk condition.

        • Patient self-attestation is sufficient evidence of the presence of a risk factor

      • Frailty

      • Residence in a nursing home or long-term care facility

        • immunocompromised persons >18 yrs of age

          • However, clinicians may consider use in severe CHF and advanced lung disease

  • Only Abrysvo is FDA approved for maternal pre-natal vaccination at 32 wks and zero days to 36 wks and 6 days gestation to protect infants with passive antibody.
    • ACIP recommends seasonal administration of a single dose of Abrysvo for pregnant persons who are between 32-36 wks gestation during the period of September to January to passively protect babies born between October and March as an equal option to nirsevimab (Beyfortus) given to infants for their first RSV season but both are not needed for most infants. 
      • In tropical areas of the US  Alaska, parts of Florida, Hawaii, Puerto Rico, the U.S. Virgin Islands, and Guam - RSV can circulate year round. The seasonal rule for use of the vaccine would not apply in these areas.

Travel

  • Travelers are at increased risk of all respiratory infections and vaccination including RSV
  • Vaccination should be considered during shared clinical decision-making in travelers  >60 yrs as persons having greater risk of increased risk for severe RSV disease.
  • The destination RSV season may occur
    • during the winter months in temperate climates.
      • October/November to April/May in the northern hemisphere and May to September in the southern hemisphere.
    • year round in tropical climates with outbreaks during the destination rainy season
    • earlier or later than the US season in some temperate countries.

Dose and Schedule

  • See Pregnancy, Breastfeeding for consideration of pre-natal vaccination at 32-36 wks gestation.
    • Additional vaccine doses are not currently recommended for this indication.
      • Infants of mothers who received Abrysvo during a previous pregnancy should receive nirsevimab (Beyfortus)

Efficacy, Duration of Protection

  • Precise direct comparison of the vaccines is not possible.
    • Trials for each of the 3 vaccines had different clinical endpoints, different age groupings, different periods of follow up after vaccination, and were conducted during different seasons.
  • In clinical trials against the more severe outpatient clinical outcome defined for that vaccine:
    • VE in the first year vs VE in the second year were: Abrysvo (Pfizer) 86% and 74%,  Arexvy (GSK) 79% and 59%, and MRESVIA (Moderna) 55% and 36%
    • Initially MRESVIA showed a good short-term (3.7 month) VE of 80%
  • Real-world observational data for hospitalization from US hospitals during the 2023-24 winter showed:
    • For both Arexvy and Abrysvo VE was approximately 75% against RSV-associated hospitalization, ED visits and critical illness in persons s>60 yrs
      • Study subjects were not cohorted by co-morbidities (JAMA published online Sep 4, 2024, doi:10.1001/jama.2024.15775)
      • VE was similar among adults aged 60–74 years and ≥75 years
  • For 2023-4 MRSEVIA hospitalization data is unavailable
  • With each vaccine, neutralizing antibody titers boosted notably, in persons who were re-vaccinated after 12 months but in no older adults >60 years of age did the GMT rise to the level seen after the initial vaccine dose. 
  • The conclusion, at least based on this limited current data, is that the VE after an annual booster might not be as high as after the initial dose. 

Choice, Interchangeability

  • ACIP recommendations refer to RSV vaccination generically without differentiating one product from the other.
    • Clinical trial data show similar VE results over 2 seasons for the 2 protein vaccines and more disappointing results for either season for MRESVIA
    • For inpatient outcomes both protein vaccines show similar 80% VE rates
    • Adjuvanted Arexvy (same adjuvant as Shingrix) appears more reactogenic than Abrysvo based on separate placebo-controlled trials but no head-to-head comparison data available.
      • Arexvy has a safety advantage due to markedly lower GBS rates

    • For MRESVIA the limited number of subjects in trials has shown an excellent safety profile - no deaths, no severe adverse events, no GBS, and no cardiomyopathy at day 42  in 20,000 subjects who received actual vaccine in the trials.

      • for MRESVIA, post-licensure safety surveillance has yet to occur.

Toxicities

Contraindications

  • History of severe allergic reaction (e.g., anaphylaxis) to any vaccine component. 

Precautions

  • None

Adverse Effects

Protein-based Vaccines

  • Local AE (≥10%): injection site pain 
  • Systemic AE (≥10%): fatigue, myalgia, and headache. Arthralgia (Arexvy only).
    • Higher rates of all noted AE's with Arexvy vs placebo.
    • Arexvy uses same reactogenic adjuvant as Shingrix (Zoster vaccine). 
  • Severe AE's not different than placebo

  • Early V-safe and VAERS data during 1st season of use

    • Estimated GBS rates of 16 and 3 per million doses with an incidence rate ratio of of 4.48 and 2.30 (vs control)  for Abrysvo and Arexvy vaccine respectively among U.S. adults ≥60 yrs

    • Definitive risk estimates are pending 2nd season data and full analysis of hospitalization records.

mRNA Vaccine

  • Injection-site pain (55.9%), fatigue (30.8%), headache (26.7%), myalgia (25.6%), arthralgia (21.7%), axillary (underarm) swelling or tenderness (15.2%), and chills (11.6%).
  • No severe AE's in trials
  • No GBS in trials (N=20,000 vaccinees).

Drug Interactions

  • Coadministration of RSV vaccines with any other adult vaccines is acceptable.

  • Co-administration of Abrysvo and adjuvanted flu vaccine showed non-inferior immune responses when RSVpreF co-administered with influenza vaccine (Clin Infect Dis 2023 Nov 22:ciad707 ahead of print)

  • Mixed evidence on increased reactogenicity with co-administration of RSV and influenza vaccines.
    • In general, co-administration (simultaneous or sequential) of RSV seasonal influenza vaccines met non-inferiority criteria for immunogenicity.
    • However, both RSV and influenza antibody titers were generally somewhat lower with co-administration.
  • Data are lacking on co-administration of other vaccines that might be recommended for persons in this age group, such as COVID-19 vaccines, pneumococcal vaccines, Td/Tdap, and the recombinant zoster vaccine (Shingrix by GSK) which contains the same adjuvant as Arexvy.

Special Populations

Pregnancy, Breastfeeding

  • Only Abrysvo is FDA approved (August 2023) for maternal pre-natal vaccination at 32 wks and 0 d to 36 wks and 6 d gestation to protect infants.
  • Nirsevimab (long-acting anti-RSV monoclonal) received FDA approval in July 2023 and is recommended by ACIP for all infants prior to their first RSV season and high risk infants in their second season and appears more effective for RSV prevention than maternal vaccination
    • No data are available directly comparing the efficacy of nirsevimab and maternal RSVpreF vaccine in preventing RSV-associated LRTI in infants.
    • Protection conferred through maternal vaccination will likely wane after 3 months,
  • When applicable, ACIP recommends seasonal administration of Abrysvo as an equal option to post-natal nirsevimab.
    • At other times of year, outside the maternal indications for Abyrsvo, nirsevimab (Beyfortus) given to infants would be the only option.
      • For choice of RSV vaccine vs nirsevimab, see Respiratory Syncytial Virus, RSV (table).
  • Administration of both nirsevimab to an infant and Abrysvo to the mother provides no benefit over administration of 1 or the other but may be considered if the mother was immunocompromised or the infant has especially high RSV risk. 
  • Infants born <34 weeks gestation or if the mother was vaccinated but the infant born <14 days after vaccination are recommended to receive nirsevimab.
  • Abrysvo is about half the cost of nirsevimab but the effect does not appear to last as long (3 mos for Abrsyvo) as nirsevimab.

  • If an infant mistakenly receives RSV vaccine, nirsevimab (should be administered as recommended as soon as feasible.

  • With Abyrsvo, no increased risk of preterm birth based on maternal vaccination status in real-world use.

    • Vaccinated 5.9% rate compared to unvaccinated 6.7% (JAMA Netw Open. 2024;7(7):e2419268). 

    • See RSV and Nirsevimab-alip.

  • A pregnant person who receives Abrysvo during one pregnancy is not recommended to receive Abrysvo during a subsequent pregnancy:

    • in subsequent pregnancies, the baby should receive nirsevimab after delivery. 

Immunocompromised / HIV

  • In clinical trials, vaccine effectiveness was demonstrated among adults aged ≥50 years with certain immunocompromising conditions and those with end-stage renal disease.
  • All persons >50 yrs. with moderate or severe immunocompromising conditions are recommended to receive the RSV vaccine given the potential for significant benefit.

Serologic Testing

  • Not recommended under any circumstances.
