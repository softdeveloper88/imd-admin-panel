Pancreatitis

Clinical Setting

  • Acute alcoholic pancreatitis: alcoholism, without necrosis, idiopathic pancreatitis
  • Prophylaxis to prevent infection in patients with pancreatitis is not considered justified based on well-designed trials (Ann Surg 2007;245:674).
  • Patients with necrotizing pancreatitis who develop gas in the area of necrosis, rising inflammatory markers or persistent fever may be suspected of having infected pancreatic necrosis and would be candidates for antibiotic therapy.
  • Post-Necrotizing pancreatitis infected pseudocyst or pancreatic abscess:
    • Need culture of abscess/infected pseudocyst to direct specific therapy.
    • May be able to  get specimen by fine-needle aspiration.
    • See below for delayed intervention strategy

Etiologies

  • Acute Alcoholic
    • Non-bacterial
  • Abscess, Pseudocyst, Post-Necrotizing
    • Enterobacteriaceae
    • Enterococcus sp.
    • Staphylococcus aureus
    • Staphylococcus epidermidis
    • Anaerobes
    • Candida sp.

Primary Regimens

  • Acute Alcoholic pancreatitis without evidence of tissue necrosis:
    • No indication for an antimicrobial agent.
  • Empirical antibiotic therapy for suspicion of infected pancreatic necrosis based on considerations outlined above:
    • Imipenem-cilastatin 0.5-1 gm IV q6h; or Meropenem 1 gm IV q8h; or Moxifloxacin 400 mg IV q24h.
    • If patient worsens, CT-guided fine-needle aspiration for culture and sensitivity testing to re-direct therapy.
  • Post-Necrotizing pancreatitis infected pseudocyst or pancreatic abscess:
    • Randomized superiority trial compared immediate drainage versus waiting until the necrosis was walled off (NEJM 2021;385:1372)
      • There was no detectable benefit to immediate drainage plus empiric antibiotic therapy.
      • Fewer invasive interventions in the postponed-drainage patients
    • Need culture of walled off  abscess to guide specific  therapy;
    • Can use Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h or Moxifloxacin 400 mg IV once daily while waiting for culture result.
  • Prophylaxis to reduce risk of secondary infection of necrotic pancreatic tissue
    • Hard to study as studies often lack culture confirmation
    • Nonetheless, in meta-analysis of trials where prophylactic antibiotic or placebo was initiated within 72 hrs of onset of symptoms, incidence of infected pancreatic pancreatic necrosis fell from 25.1% to 16.3 % (OR 0.55) with concomitant decrease in mortality: J Hepatobiliary Pancreat Sci 2015;22:316

Alternative Regimens

  • (Ciprofloxacin or Levofloxacin) + Metronidazole

Comments

  • General review of pancreatitis: N Engl J Med 375:1972, 2016.
  • Patients with necrosis involving 30% or more of the pancreas are at greatest risk of developing secondary infection.
  • AGA guidelines recommend against prophylactic antibiotics in acute non-necrotizing pancreatitis (Gastroenterology 154: 1096, 2018).
  • General references (acute pancreatitis, necrotizing pancreatitis): Curr Opin Crit Care 2021; 27:193; Lancet 2020; 396:726; Gastroenterology 2020; 158:67
