Pelvic Vein Thrombophlebitis, Suppurative (Septic)

Clinical Setting

  • Usual clinical syndrome: Unexplained fever within a few days postpartum (especially following C-section), post-abortion or post-pelvic surgery; often fever while on appropriate antibiotics for endometritis/pelvic infection.
  • Complicated by small pulmonary emboli in 2% of cases.
  • Diagnosis by exclusion; modern imaging may help.

Etiologies

  • Streptococcus sp.
  • Bacteroides sp.
  • Enterobacteriaceae
  • Other anaerobic organisms

Primary Regimens

  • Metronidazole (1 gm loading dose then 0.5 gm q6h or 1 gm IV q12h) + Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q24h
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h
  • Add heparin anticoagulation to antibiotic regimen; optimal duration is unclear (See Comment)

Alternative Regimens

  • Imipenem-cilastatin 0.5 gm IV q6h or Meropenem 1 gm IV q8h, or Ertapenem 1 gm IV q24h OR
  • [Clindamycin 900 mg IV q8h + (Aztreonam 2 gm IV q8h or Gentamicin)]
  • Add heparin anticoagulation to antibiotic regimen; optimal duration is unclear (See Comment)

Comments

  • In one study, addition of anticoagulant to antibiotic therapy did not improve outcome (Am J Obstet Gyn 181:143, 1999).
  • Increasing resistance of B. fragilis to Cefoxitin and Cefotetan.
  • Uncommon presentation of septic abdominal/pelvic vein thrombophlebitis associated with S. aureus osteomyelitis in children (Pediatrics 106: e87, 2000).
