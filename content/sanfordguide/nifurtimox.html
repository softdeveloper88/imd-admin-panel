Nifurtimox
Tradename: Lampit

Usage and Dosing

  • Nitrofuran antitrypanosomal agent.
  • An alternative drug for the treatment of Chagas Disease (American trypanosomiasis) and certain clinical scenarios for T. b. gambiense
  • Fexinidazole now preferred by WHO and CDC where available with several clinical exceptions. See African Trypanosomiasis, West African
  • NECT may have lower fatality and fewer relapses but oral fexinidazole much higher adherence (Cochrane Database System Rev 2021 Dec 9;12(12):CD015374).
    • Large clinical series using NECT (PLoS Negl Trop Dis 2021 Nov 8;15(11):e0009903).
  • FDA approved (Lampit, Bayer) in August 2020 only for T. cruzi and only for those under 18 years of age. Off-label use in adults recommended by CDC (www.lampit.com).
    • Lampit tablets (30 mg, 120 mg) are functionally scored tablets which can be split in half at the scored lines by hand. Do not break the tablets mechanically with a tablet splitting device.
    • For patients unable to swallow whole or half tablets, a slurry can be prepared and taken immediately with food. Disperse in approximately 2.5 mL of water in a spoon. Tablet(s) should disintegrate in 30 seconds or less.
  • Obtain pregnancy test in females of reproductive potential prior to initiation of therapy. Advise use of effective contraception during treatment and for six months after final dose.
  • Good review of recent pharmacological and clinical data: PLoS Negl Trop Dis 2025;19:e0012849.

Adult Dose

  • T. cruzi (Chagas disease): 8-10 mg/kg/day po divided 4x/day for 90-120 days
  • West African Trypanosomiasis: 15 mg/kg/day po, divided q8h x 10 days in combination with Eflornithine (abbreviated NECT)

Pediatric Dose

Dose (Age >28 Days)

Chagas: Newborn (wt ≥2.5 kg) to <18 years:
Wt ≥40 kg: 8-10 mg/kg/day (divided tid) x60 days
Wt <40 kg: 10-20 mg/kg/day (divided tid) x60 days

West African Trypanosomiasis: same as adult dosing

      Adverse Effects

      • Most patients experience at least 1 side effect, and side effects tend to be more frequent and bothersome in adults vs. children.
      • GI: abdominal pain, nausea/vomiting, diarrhea, anorexia, weight loss (all very common).
      • Neurologic: irritability, restlessness/excitement, nervousness/anxiety, insomnia/drowsiness (frequent), depression or mood swings/changes, disorientation/confusion/forgetfulness, vertigo/dizziness, blurred vision, loss of balance, paresthesia, tremors, and, rarely, seizures. Peripheral neuropathy is a relatively uncommon, dose-dependent side effect that may appear late in the course of treatment and should prompt discontinuation; the condition is reversible, but complete resolution may require months.
      • Constitutional and/or musculoskeletal symptoms: myalgia, arthralgia, fatigue, weakness, fever, chills, sweats.
      • Dermatologic: skin eruptions, erythema, pruritus.
      • Monitoring:  Laboratory testing (CBC, hepatic enzymes, bilirubin, serum creatinine, and blood urea nitrogen) at baseline, after 4-6 weeks, and at end of course.  Symptoms and signs of peripheral neuropathy should be assessed every two weeks.
      • Adverse effects in those treated in the US under CDC IND for Chagas 2001-2021: gastrointestinal (68.7%), neurologic (60.5%), and constitutional (46.5%).  Adverse events reported were nausea (50.6%), anorexia (46.1%), weight loss (35.0%), headache (33.3%), and abdominal pain (23.1%)  Serious adverse events in 10%. Summary of AEs in MMWR 71(10):371, 2022.

      Major Drug Interactions

      • Alcohol may increase the risk for adverse effects and should be avoided.
      • Use caution when administering with other drugs that have neurotoxic side effects.

      Comments

      • The patient’s weight should be monitored during therapy; if indicated, the dose of nifurtimox should be adjusted.
