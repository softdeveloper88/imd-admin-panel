COVID-19, Transmission, Exposure, Isolation

Introduction

  • SARS-CoV-2 is a highly infectious viral infection transmitted primarily through small droplets (airborne)
  • The CDC issued updated Guidelines for Respiratory Viral Infections (1 Mar 2024, found here).  
  • While COVID-19 still poses a significant health threat to people at higher risk, its health impacts are now similar to other respiratory viruses, like influenza, which are also important causes of illness and death, especially for people at higher risk.  Therefore, CDC issued their updated Guidelines to create uniform recommendations and avoid issuance of additional guidance for each specific virus. 
  • Risk factors for severe illness from Respiratory Viral infections, including SARS-CoV-2 (COVID-19), include:
    • Older Adults (> age 65)
    • Young Children (< 2 years of age)
    • People with weakened immune systems (chronic corticosteroids, use of biologic agents for inflammatory disorders (e.g., Rituximab), recipients of cancer chemotherapy, transplant patients)
    • People with Disabilities 
    • Pregnant People

Transmission

  • Predominantly droplet, less commonly airborne; asymptomatic persons can transmit infection
    • Transmission through contact with contaminated surfaces or objects (fomites) is low (generally 1:10,000).
  • Maximum viral shedding begins prior to onset of symptoms, see figure below (He et al, Nature on line, 15 Apr 2020 (Figure 1c excerpt used with permission). Although recent study indicates a delay in time to peak viremia with Omicron variant infection (Clin Infect Dis: 28 Sept 2023)

  • Mean incubation time is estimated to be ~5 days after exposure (range 4.1 - 7.0 days, but as short as 36 hours. 
  • Viral shedding (References: Nature. 2020;581(7809):465-469; Lancet Infect Dis. 2020;20(5):565-574; Nat Commun. 2021 Jan 11;12(1):267, N Engl J Med . 2021 Jan 27. doi: 10.1056/NEJMc2027040):
    • Infectious virus unlikely to be isolated after the first week from onset of symptoms, falling to below 5% after 2 weeks. 
    • Shedding of viral RNA assayed by RT-PCR from saliva and nasopharyngeal secretions remains high for approximately 6 days, declines significantly in the second week of illness, and usually ceases after 2-3 weeks.
  • Emerging SARS CoV-2 variants
    • Alpha and Beta variants are ~ 50 - 60% more infectious than the original wide-type strain
    • Delta variant is ~60% more infectious and  transmissible than the alpha strain (and by extension, ~ 90% more infectious than the original wild-type strain)
    • Omicron ~ 2x as transmissible as Delta variant.  Transmission efficiency approaches that of Measles (highly infectious).
  • Re-infection
    • Re-infection accounts for an increasing number of COVID-19 cases.  The protective effect of prior infection is estimated to be ~ 6 months and longer.
    • Vaccination can extend the time of protection against symptomatic infection
    • COVID-19 vaccines reduce risk of severe disease by > 50% among those at high risk, and > 90% among those who are not at high risk. 
  • Treatment as Prevention
    • Treatment, especially when administered early in the course of infection (< 48 hrs after the onset of symptoms) reduces the time of viral shedding and, as a result, the risk of transmission to others.

Precautions

  • Use same procedures for all viral respiratory pathogens (e.g., SARS-CoV-2, influenza, RSV)
  • Updated CDC Guidance (03/01/2024) on exposure, isolation and quarantine.
    • Well-fitted mask means KN-95, N-95 (not cloth)
    • Masks Work !  (JAMA  2023;6(10):e2339443)
  • Vaccination status based on CDC Guidance (01/16/2022):
    • Up-to-date means primary series completed + booster (if eligible)
    • Fully vaccinated means primary series completed
  • Antigen tests can be used for screening before gathering with others, especially to help protect people in your life who have risk factors for severe illness. 

Exposure

  • Start precautions immediately if you or the people around you were recently exposed to a respiratory virus, are sick, or are recovering.
    • Avoid contact with immunocompromised persons and people with underlying cardiopulmonary disease.
    • Mask wearing is prudent and pre-exposure testing is recommended if contact with groups of people (some will likely be at high risk for severe disease) indoors, or any at risk people is anticipated 
  • Continue these precautions and monitor for symptoms for at least 5 days.  
  • If asymptomatic but a positive test done as a pre-exposure measure (see above), isolation is unnecessary, continue normal activities.
    • Follow precautions per below for return to normal activities.
  • If symptoms develop test immediately (PCR / RNA testing preferred if available; otherwise use rapid (antigen) home test).  
    • Avoid contact with others if symptoms develop while waiting for test results.
    • If positive, isolate immediately and follow guidance below.

Isolation

  • Isolate from others if COVID-19 positive (regardless of vaccination status)
  • Symptomatic infection: 
    • Stay home and away from others (including people you live with who are not sick) if you have respiratory virus symptoms that aren't better explained by another cause. These symptoms can include fever, chills, fatigue, cough, runny nose, and headache, among others
    • Return to normal activities when, for at least 24 hours, both are true:
      • Overall symptom improvement
      • No fever.
    • For the next 5 days after return to normal activities:
      • Take additional steps to:
        • improve air quality around you (e.g., open windows, increase ventilation, use air purifier);
        • improve hygiene (hand washing);
        • wear masks when around others;
        • physical distancing (keep > 6 feet away from others); 
        • avoid large crowds
        • avoid travel (if possible)
        • and/or pre-exposure testing when around other people indoors (especially high risk people).
  • Asymptomatic infection. For the next 5 days: take precautions / steps as above.
