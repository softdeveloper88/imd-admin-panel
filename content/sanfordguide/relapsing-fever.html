Relapsing Fever

Clinical Setting

  • Relapsing Fever is caused by Borrelia spirochetes that are spread by arthropods. There are two forms of relapsing fever: Louse-borne and Tick-borne.
    • Louse-borne relapsing fever is caused by the spirochete B. recurrentis; the vector is the body louse Pediculus humanus and humans are the only reservoir.
    • Tick-borne relapsing fever is caused by 8 or more species of Borrelia:
      • North America
        • B. hermsii (mountains of the US west)
        • B miyamotoii (northeastern and northern midwest of US)
        • B. tunicatel (southwestern and south central US)
      • Africa: B. hispanica, B. crocidurae, B. duttonii
      • Russia: B. miyamotoi (See Comment)
      • The reservoir/vector of most tick-borne relapsing fever are soft-bodied ticks that remain close to the habitats of animals and/or humans. B. miyamotoii is transmitted by Ixodes ticks.
        • Fever, headache, myalgia and/or arthralgia occur after exposure to infected Ornithodoros tick.
        • Rarely: acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), liver dysfunction, encephalopathy.
        • In pregnancy:
          • More severe and more prolonged illness
          • Associated with increased risk of abortion and stillbirths
  • Can relapse up to 10 times.

Diagnosis

  • Examine stained peripheral blood smear during fever for presence of spirochetes.
  • If negative, a more sensitive  blood PCR is available

Etiologies

  • North America: Borrelia hermsii, Borrelia turicata (Image is the tick vector of Borrelia turicata: O. turicatae)

  • Africa:
    • Borrelia hispanica
    • Borrelia crocidurae
    • Borrelia duttonii (O. moubata)(image above)
  • Russia: Borrelia miyamotoi

Primary Regimens

  • For Louse-borne: Tetracycline 500 mg po/IV x one dose
  • For Tick-borne:
    • Adult:
      • Aqueous Penicillin G 5 million  units IV q6h x 7-10 days
      • Doxycycline 100 mg po/IV bid x 7-10 days
    • Child: Doxycyline 4.4 mg/kg/day divided bid (max 200 mg /day)(See Comments)
    • Prophylaxis, see Comments

Alternative Regimens

  • For Louse-borne: Erythromycin 500 mg po/IV x one dose
  • For Tick-borne: Erythromycin 500 mg po/IV qid x 7-10 days
  • Note relative resistance of Borrelia species to aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones, and sulfonamidess

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • There is reported in vitro  susceptibility to doxycycline, azithromycin, and ceftriaxone but resistance of B. miyamotoi to Amoxicillin (Antimicrob Agts Chemother 2018; 62:e00419-18 and  Antimicrob Agts and Chemother. 2017; 61: e00535-17).

Comments

  • Doxycycline can be safely administered for up to 21 days regardless of age (AAP Red Book 2018, Section 4; J Pediatr 166:1246, 2015).
  • Note: Jarisch-Herxheimer reaction (fever, increased pulse, increased respiration, decreased blood pressure) may occur within the first 1-4 hours after the first dose of an effective antibiotic.
    • Not prevented by prior steroids.
  • Prophylaxis: Post-exposure Doxycycline pre-emptive therapy is highly effective, N Engl J Med 355:148, 2006; up to 72 hrs  after exposure, Clin Infect Dis 2020; 71:1768
  • Borrelia miyamotoi:
    • First identified in Japan, prevalent in Russia; Increasing incidence in the U.S Northeast and northern Midwest
    • Clinically non-specific: fever, headache, and thrombocytopenia common. A few "relapses" can occur.
    • Diagnosis: reference lab serum PCR.
    • Susceptible to doxycycline, azithromycin and ceftriaxone (Ann Intern Med 163: 91, 2015; Ann Intern Med 163:141, 2015).
    • References: Am J Medicine 2019; 132:136. Pathogens Apr 3;12(4):553,2023
