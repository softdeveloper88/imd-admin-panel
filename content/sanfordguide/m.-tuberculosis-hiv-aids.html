Tuberculosis, HIV/AIDS

Clinical Setting

  • Treatment considerations for Mycobacterium tuberculosis in HIV/AIDS patients
  • 60–70% of HIV-positive patients with pulmonary TB have extrapulmonary disease.
  • See Comments concerning TB IRIS (immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome)

Etiologies

  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis

Primary Regimens

  • NOTE: Always check for drug interactions between rifampin or rifabutin and drugs used in the antiretroviral regimen
  • Regimens used for treatment of drug-susceptible TB in persons without HIV are also recommended for persons who are HIV infected with the following modifications (see Updated CDC guidelines)
    • Isoniazid: no modifications, may be used with all antiretroviral agents
    • Pyrazinamide: no modifications, may be used with all antiretroviral agents
    • Ethambutol: no modifications, may be used with all antiretroviral agents
    • Rifampin
      • Not recommended for use with bictegravir, doravirine, cabotegravir/rilpevirine (IM/PO), elvitegravir/cobicistat, efavirenz 400 mg, etravirine, fostemsavir, lenacapavir (SC/PO), maraviroc, nevirapine, protease inhibitors (PIs), raltegrivir (daily), rilpivirine (PO)
      • Tenofovir alafenamide (TAF): use with caution as rifampin decreases TAF levels; tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF) okay to use.
      • All other antiretroviral agents may be used
    • Rifabutin
      • Not recommended: bictegravir, cabotegravir/rilpivirine (IM/PO), elvitegravir/cobicistat, etravirine with boosted PIs, lenacapavir (SC/PO), PIs with cobicistat
        • 5 mg/kg dose (usual dose 300 mg: dolutegravir (increase dose to 100 mg twice daily), doravirine (increase dose to 100 mg twice daily), raltegravir, or rilpivirine (increase dose to 50 mg, po only)
        • 150 mg once daily dose: protease inhibitors with ritonavir
        • 450-600 mg dose:  efavirenz 
      • All other antiretroviral agents may be used
    • Rifapentine
      • Drug interactions not well worked out
        • May be co-administered with Efavirenz or nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (TAF with caution); avoid coadministration with other antivirals.
        • When used as a once weekly regimen with Isoniazid as a 3-month regimen (3HP) for treatment of latent tuberculosis infection (LTBI), may be co-administered with Efavirenz, Raltegravir, or once daily Dolutegravir.
      • Not recommended for use as intermittent therapy for HIV-positive patients with active tuberculosis.
  • NOTE: Usual total duration of therapy is 6 months provided that Pyrazinamide was included in the initial 2-month intensive treatment phase.  Extend total duration of therapy to 9 months in patients with delayed response, e.g., culture-positive at 2 months, especially if cavitary lesions are present.
  • Intermittent therapy: because of possibility of emergence of resistance, administration of rifabutin or rifampin weekly or twice weekly is not recommended. May use partially intermittent therapy: Daily (5-7 doses per week) x 2 months followed by 3 doses/week to complete a total of 6 months of therapy

Alternative Regimens

  • Resistant TB:
    • Mono-resistant organism
    • Multi-resistant (MDR-TB)
    • Extensively-resistant (XDR-TB)

Comments

  • When to initiate antiretroviral therapy (ART) 
    • For patients with pulmonary TB and CD4 < 50 cells/mm3 initiate  ART within 2 weeks; although associated with 2-fold increase risk or immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome, it improves survival (Ann Intern Med 163:32, 2015).
    • For patients with pulmonary TB and CD4 > 50 mm3, no demonstrated survival benefit with early ART (Ann Intern Med 163:32, 2015).  For patients with TB of moderate or greater severity, initiate ARTs within 2-4 weeks of TB therapy; for those with less severe disease ART should be initiated by 8-12 weeks.
    • For patients with CNS tuberculosis, early therapy not associated with improved survival and ART should be deferred for 2 months after starting TB therapy; adjunctive therapy with dexamethasone should be administered.
  • Immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (IRIS)
    • IRIS is a paradoxical inflammatory reaction to mycobacteria or other pathogens precipitated by ART treatment and reconstitution of host immune response.
    • Incidence of 20-50% in HIV-TB co-infected patients upon initiation of ART;  onset of symptoms is 2 -4 weeks after initiation of ART, but may occur at anytime during treatment.
    • Risk factors include initiation of ART within 6 weeks of TB treatment, disseminated/extrapulmonary disease, CD4 count < 50 per mm3, rapid fall in viral load, rapid rise in CD4 count.
    • Suspect TB-IRIS in patients with new fevers without obvious source, worsening adenopathy or abscess, worsening of other tuberculous lesions.
    • Treatment is based on severity of symptoms and may include anti-emetics an non-steroidal anti-inflammatory agents or corticosteroid therapy (e.g., 1.5 mg/kg/d oral prednisone for 2 weeks followed by 0.75 mg/kg/d for 2 weeks [AIDS 2010 Sep 24;24(15):2381]).
      • A randomized placebo-control trial of prednisone 40 mg once daily for 2 weeks then 20 mg once daily for two weeks administered prophylactically to patients with CD4 < 100 per mm3 reduced to occurrence of IRIS from 46.7% in placebo-treated patients to 32.5% in prednisone-recipients (N Engl J Med. 2018;379:1915).
