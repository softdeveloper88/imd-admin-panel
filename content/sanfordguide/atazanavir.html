Atazanavir
Tradename: Reyataz

Usage and Dosing

  • Atazanavir (ATV) is indicated for use as part of a 3-drug combination regimen, usually combined with 2 nucleos(t)ide agents (e.g., TDF-FTC or ABC-3TC). Ritonavir (RTV)-boosting markedly increases bioavailability and efficacy; boosting with Ritonavir is required whenever using Atazanavir in combination with Tenofovir (owing to lower ATV drug levels).
  • Atazanavir has lower potential for increased lipids. Asymptomatic unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia common; jaundice especially likely in Gilbert syndrome (J Infect Dis 192:1381, 2005).
  • Headache, rash, GI symptoms. Prolongation of PR interval (1st degree AV block) reported. Caution in pre-existing conduction system disease.
  • Efavirenz & Tenofovir decrease Atazanavir exposure: use Atazanavir/Ritonavir regimen; also, Atazanavir increase Tenofovir concentrations—watch for adverse events.
  • In treatment experienced patients taking TDF and needing H2 blockers, Atazanavir 400 mg with Ritonavir 100 mg can be given; do not use PPIs.
  • High incidence of renal stones in HIV-Infected patients receiving Ritonavir-boosted Atazanavir compared to other protease inhibitors, P < 0.001 (Clin Infect Dis 55:1262, 2012; Clin Infect Dis 56:1186, 2013); perhaps increased risk of cholelithiasis as well (Clin Infect Dis 55:1270, 2012).
  • Generic formulations available: 150 mg, 200 mg, 300 mg capsules.
  • Caution: numerous drug interactions (see chart below, not all-inclusive).
  • See ARV Drugs, Overview for class-wide adverse effects and ARV Drug-Drug Interactions.

Adverse Effects

  • Most Significant:
    • Prolongation of PR interval (1st degree AV block) reported; rarely 2° AV block. QTc increase and torsades reported (Clin Infect Dis 44:e67, 2007).
    • Acute interstitial nephritis (Am J Kidney Dis 44:E81, 2004) and urolithiasis (Atazanavir stones) reported (Clin Infect Dis 55:1262, 2012 and Clin Infect Dis 56:1186, 2013).
    • Potential increased transaminases in patients co-infected with HBV or HCV.
    • Severe hyperbilirubinemia (> 5 mg/dl) associated with UGT1A1*28 allele and MDR G2677T/A variation, Clin Infect Dis 51:101, 2010. Similar to Gilbert's syndrome. Is not harmful; aesthetic complaints from patients.
  • Most Common:
    • Asymptomatic unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia in up to 60% of patients, jaundice in 7–9% [especially with Gilbert syndrome (J Infect Dis 192:1381, 2005)].
    • Moderate to severe events: Diarrhea 1–3%, nausea 6–14%, abdominal pain 4%, headache 6%, rash 5–7%.
