Bacteria: Normal Flora, Non-pathogenic, Contaminants, Rare Pathogens

Introduction

  • This table lists less common bacteria that may be identified in human specimens, including blood cultures, and reported by increasingly specific laboratory methods. The listed organisms share several characteristics in that they tend to be:
    • Rare pathogens
    • Normal human flora
    • Environmental contaminants
    • Often non-pathogenic and of low virulence
    • Present in mixed cultures
  • While lab diagnostic methods may identify these organisms, they are rarely reported as an etiology of infection. Thus, it is important to differentiate presence of the organism from infection caused by such.
  • When such organisms do present as an infection, it is often in special populations such as immunocompromised hosts or indwelling foreign material (central catheters, prosthetic materials, etc).

Anaerococcus spp.

Gram-positive anaerobic cocci, formerly classified as a peptostreptococcus, but reclassified into its own genus

Commensal, normal skin, vaginal, oral and gut flora; often present in mixed infections; susceptible to penicillins, glycopeptides, linezolid, and metronidazole and most other agents used to treat anaerobic infections

Microorganisms. 2021; 9:1665

Granulicatella spp.

Facultative Gram-positive coccus

Normal human oral, gut, genitourinary flora, formerly referred to as "nutritionally deficient strep" due to fastidious growth in culture. Susceptible to vancomycin and linezolid; due to variable susceptibility to beta-lactams combination therapy with penicillin, ampicillin, or ceftriaxone plus gentamicin is recommended for treatment of endocarditis,

Pathogens. 2022;11:1431; Antimicrob Agents Chemother. 2015;60:1411;

Micrococcus spp.

Gram-positive coccus

Normal human flora, environmental organism, blood culture contaminant; rare pathogen; susceptible to vancomycin, beta-lactams

Clin Microbiol Infect. 2018; 24:964.

Ochrobactrum anthropi

Aerobic Gram-negative rod, oxidase-positive, motile, non-lactose-fermenter formerly known as Achromobacter group Vd

Environmental organism, possibly a component of normal gut flora, rare opportunistic pathogen that can cause a variety of infections; an AmpC beta-lactamase producer that is resistant in vitro to penicillins and cephalosporins and generally susceptible to imipenem, fluoroquinolones (variable susceptibility), TMP-SMX, and tetracyclines, and aminoglycosides

Int J Med Microbiol. 2009; 299:209; J Clin Microbiol. 2013; 51:1330.

Pantoea agglomerans

 

Facultative anerobic Gram-negative rod, formerly Enterobacter agglomerans

Plant pathogen, rare cause of human infection; scant susceptibility data but likely similar in susceptibility to other Enterobacter spp. 

Microorganisms. 2022;10 2072.

Raoultella spp.

Gram-negative enteric, formerly classified as Klebsiella spp.

Rarely pathogenic opportunist found in soil, water, on plants and gastrointestinal tract of humans; susceptible to most antibiotics except for aminopenicillins due to production of a class A beta-lactamase

Infect Drug Resist. 2020; 13:1091; Folia Microbiol (Praha). 2017; 62:221.

Rothia dentocariosa

Gram-positive coccus

Normal oral flora, rare pathogen; susceptible to vancomycin, most beta-lactams; cause of periodontal disease and rare cause of bacteremia and endocarditis; susceptible to vancomycin, penicillin, ampicillin, ceftriaxone

Am J Med Sci. 2015; 350:239.

Rothia mucilaginosus

Normal oral flora, rare pathogen; susceptible to vancomycin, most beta-lactams.
