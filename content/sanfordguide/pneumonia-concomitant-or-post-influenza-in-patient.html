Pneumonia, Concomitant or Post Influenza

Clinical Setting

  • Signs or symptoms of pneumonia or sepsis concomitant with or following influenza.
  • Sputum gram stain useful for identifying Staph. aureus pneumonia post-influenza.
  • Complicates 0.5 to 2.5% of influenza cases.

Etiologies

  • Staph. aureus
  • Strep. pneumoniae
  • Haemophilus influenzae
  • Strep. pyogenes

Primary Regimens

  • In addition to coverage for both influenza and community-acquired pneumonia cover for MRSA: Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL) or Linezolid 600 mg IV q12h)
    • For patients with MRSA risk factor (e.g., IVDU, prior positive culture for MRSA, inpatient parenteral antimicrobial therapy within 90 days) or with evidence of necrotizing pneumonia, hemoptysis, severe disease. 
    • Obtain sputum and blood cultures, which if negative MRSA coverage can be stopped. 

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • CDC surveillance data during the 2009 H1N1 influenza outbreak showed that pediatric deaths were associated with a documented bacterial infection in about a third of cases, 33% of which were Staph. aureus (both MSSA and MRSA) and 22% Strep. pneumoniae.
  • About a third of patients admitted to the ICU and requiring mechanical ventilation had microbiological evidence of a concomitant bacterial pneumonia, predominantly due to Gram-positive cocci (Am J Respir Crit Care Med. 2021; 204:546).
