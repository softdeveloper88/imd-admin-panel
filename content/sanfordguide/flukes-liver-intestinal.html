Flukes: Liver, Lung, Intestinal

Clinical Setting

  • Infection caused by presence of liver, lung, or intestinal flukes.
    • All have snail intermediate hosts and are transmitted by ingestion of metacercariae on or in plants (notably watercress), contaminated water, fish, or crustaceans.
  • Liver:
    • Fasciola hepatica has an acute phase with RUQ pain, fever, and eosinophilia.
      • Chronic phase with biliary symptoms
      • Uncommon ectopic location of F. hepatica in intestinal wall, lungs, subcutaneous tissue, and perhaps pharyngeal mucosa.
      • Detailed epidemiology in Asia (PLoS Negl Trop Dis 2024 Jan 17;18(1):e0011904).
      • Opisthorchis felineus (present in Europe as well as Asia) may present an acute phase with fever, facial edema, lymphadenopathy, arthralgias, rash, and eosinophilia.
    • In long-standing Clonorchis or Opisthorchis sp. infections, cholangitis, cholelithiasis, pancreatitis, and cholangiocarcinoma can develop. 
      • Cholangiocarcinoma in non-endemic countries not related to liver flukes
      • Fasciola gigantica: a related species that primarily affects domestic and wild animals but can also infect people.
  • Lung:
    • Uncommon ectopic location of Paragonimus sp. subcutaneously, in the abdominal viscera or in brain. 
    • P. skrjabini often produces skin nodules, subcutaneous abscesses, or a type of creeping eruption known as “trematode larva migrans.”
    •  Paragonimus species cause pulmonary cavities with rusty sputum.
  • Intestinal flukes usually asymptomatic; in heavier infections, diarrhea, abdominal pain, fever, ascites, anasarca and intestinal obstruction may occur.

Diagnosis

  • Liver flukes: stool exams negative during acute migratory phase.
  • Adult liver flukes seen on ERCP in biliary tree.
  • Lung flukes, sputum for O & P
  • EIA, immunoblot for F. hepatica and for paragonimus.
    • Serology for Clonorchis in the US at Kephera Diagnostics
    • Serology does not detect current vs past infection

Etiologies

  • Liver Flukes:
    • Clonorchis sinensis
    • Opisthorchis viverrini
    • Opisthorchis felineus
    • Fasciola hepatica
    • Fasciola gigantica
    • Metorchis conjunctus
  • Lung Fluke
    • Paragonimus sp. (includes westermani, skrjabini, africanus, mexicanus, kellicotti).  P. kellicotti is endemic to North America
  • Intestinal Flukes
    • Fasciolopsis buski
    • Heterophyes heterophyes
    • Echinostoma sp.
    • Metagonimus yokogawai
    • Nanophyetus salmincola

Primary Regimens

  • Liver Flukes
    • C. sinensis, O. viverrini, O. felineus, M. conjunctus:
      • Praziquantel 25 mg/kg po tid x 2 days (pediatric dose same) 
    • F. hepatica and F. gigantica:
      • Triclabendazole 10 mg/kg po and repeat in 12-24 hours; may use 20 mg/kg for treatment failures. 
        • FDA approval only for age ≥ 6 years.
        • Only flukes where Praziquantel not effective. 
      • In some patients physical removal from biliary tree via ERCP beneficial.
  • Lung Flukes
    • Paragonimus sp.:
      • Praziquantel 25 mg/kg po tid x 2 days
  • Intestinal Flukes
    • F. buski, H. heterophyes, Echinostoma sp., M. yokogawai, N. salmincola:
      • Praziquantel 25 mg/kg po tid x 1 day

Alternative Regimens

  • Clonorchis sinensis and Opisthorchis sp.: Albendazole 400 mg bid po x 7 days
  • O. viverrini: Praziquantel 50 mg/kg once also effective
  • Paragonimus sp: Triclabendazole 10 mg/kg po x 2 doses over 12-24 hrs (Trans R Soc Trop Med Hyg 97:451, 2003). 

Comments

  • Many minor species of intestinal flukes not listed here.
  • False fascioliasis (pseudofascioliasis) refers to the presence of Fasciola eggs in the stool because of recent ingestion of contaminated liver (containing noninfective eggs). 
  • Triclabendazole resistance of increasing concern for F. hepatica in affected animal hosts (Trends Parasitol 2016, 32:458) and recently humans Emerg Infect Dis 27:1850, 2021.
    • Consider multiple triclabendazole courses for treatment failures.  J Glob Antimicrob Resist 25:264, 2021.
    • Some efficacy of nitazoxanide in F. hepatica treatment failures.  PLoS Negl Trop Dis 2019 Sep 25;13(9):e0007779. doi: 10.1371/journal.pntd.0007779. eCollection 2019 Sep.
  • Triclabendazole FDA approved in early 2019. Review: Trans R Soc Trop Med Hyg. 2019 Oct 22. pii: trz093. PMID: 31638149; Trans R Soc Trop Med Hyg 113;797, 2019
    • Novartis will provide at no charge. Tel: +1 888-669-6682. Available and shipped for free to any pharmacy having a valid prescription on hand. Novartis will request patient name, DOB, and pharmacy address/phone. 
    • Also available from Victoria Pharmacy, Zurich, Switzerland: (+41) 43-344-6060.
  • 78% cure rate for F. hepatica with 10 mg/kg triclabendazole.  Repeat stool exam at 3 months and re-treat if positive (PLoS Negl Trop Dis 6(8):e1720, 2012.)
  • Add steroids if CNS involvement with Paragonimus sp.
  • Pregnancy: WHO allows use of triclabendazole in pregnant women only in clinical settings where medical staff can monitor for complications.
  • Breastfeeding: Drug may be present in human breastmilk
    • Consider the mother's clinical need for triclabendazole and any potential adverse effects on the breastfed infant.
  • Uncontrolled study showing efficacy of nitazoxanide against F. hepatica.  PLoS Negl Trop Dis. 7:e2553, 2013.
