HBV Epidemiology

Caseload

  • ~300,000,000 cases of Hepatitis B virus infection (HBV) reported worldwide, at least 1.25 million in US; numbers are likely higher due to under-reporting (Nature Reviews GI and Hepatology 20: 524, 2023)
  • HBV infection is defined as persons Hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg) positive for more than 6 months.
  • 15- 40% of Carriers will develop complications, including cirrhosis, hepatic decompensation, and hepatocellular carcinoma.
  • Co-infection with Hepatitis D occurs in an estimated 60,000,000 to 70,000,000 worldwide.

Risk of Transmission

  • Hepatitis B virus (HBV) replicates to high titres in the blood, especially during initial (acute) infection.  Any parenteral or mucosal exposure can transmit the virus.
  • HBV can 'reactivate' at anytime, leading to higher viral titers at unpredictable times, with resultant increased risk of transmission
  • HBV is transmitted via perinatal, percutaneous, sexual exposure, and close person-to-person contact (e.g., open cuts or sores). It is ~ 100-fold more efficiently transmitted than HIV.
  • HBV is present in most tissues and body fluids (serum, saliva, semen, vaginal secretions) and can survive for long periods of time on environmental surfaces.

Virology and Life Cycle

  • DNA virus: HBV is a partially double stranded hepadnavirus.  Once within the cell nucleus, the HBV DNA causes the liver cell to produce surface (HBs) proteins, the core (HBc) protein, DNA polymerase, the HBe protein, HBx protein and other as yet undetected proteins and enzymes.
  • HBV has a unique mechanism of replication via reverse transcription of pregenomic RNA by its DNA polymerase. The lack of proofreading function leads mutant virions that can fuel antiviral resistance. Produces full virion (42 nm complete virus; Dane Particle) and smaller (22 nm) HBsAg particles in large quantities.
  • Primarily infects hepatocytes, although believed to infect lymphocytes as well. HBV amplifies via reverse transcription of an RNA intermediate. Enters nucleus; forms covalently closed circular (CCC) DNA . May, but does not always, integrate into host DNA.

Genotypes

  • There are 8 genotypes:
    • A (N. America / Europe / Parts of Africa): More responsive to Interferon therapy
    • B (Asia):  More responsive to Interferon therapy
    • C (Asia):  More severe liver disease; slower HBsAg clearance; more HCC; Less responsive to Interferon therapy
    • D (India / Middle East / Mediterranean / Africa): Less responsive to Interferon therapy
    • E (Africa)
    • F (S. America)
    • G (Undetermined)
    • H (Undetermined)
