Mediastinitis

Clinical Setting

  • Predisposing causes of acute mediastinitis
    • Post-sternotomy, cardiothoracic surgery
    • Extension of head and neck, odontogenic infections
    • Esophageal perforation
    • Contiguous (e.g., pneumonia, empyema, osteomyelitis) or hematogenous spread from another site  (uncommon)
  • Clinical features: fever, chest pain, sternal wound drainage or infection, presence of mediastinal fluid or pneumomediastinum on CT scan
  • Empirical therapy pending results of microbiological studies

Etiologies

  • Post-sternotomy, cardiothoracic surgery (in order of prevalence)
    • Staphylococcus aureus
    • Coagulase-negative staphylococci
    • Enterobacteriaceae
    • Other Gram-negatives, e.g., Pseudomonas aeruginosa
    • Candida albicans (<1%)
    • Polymicrobial
  • Extension from head and neck, odontogenic infections; esophageal perforation
    • Oral anaerobes and facultative organisms (most common)
      • Bacteroides spp.
      • Fusobacterium spp.
      • Peptostreptococcus spp.(now Peptoniphilus spp)
      • Prevotella spp.
      • Streptococcus spp.
      • Polymicrobial
    • Other facultative or aerobic organisms (less common)
      • Eikenella corrodens
      • Enterobacteriaceae
      • Staphylococcus aureus
      • Streptococcus species

Primary Regimens

  • Post-sternotomy, cardiothoracic surgery
    • Surgical debridement, drainage
    • Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL) + (Cefepime 2 gm IV q12h or Meropenem 1 gm IV q8h)
  • Extension from head and neck, odontogenic infections; esophageal perforation
    • Surgical debridement, drainage
    • Ampicillin-sulbactam 3 gm IV q6h + Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg q8-12h IV (add if suspected MRSA)

Alternative Regimens

  • Post-sternotomy, cardiothoracic surgery
    • (Daptomycin 6 mg/kg q24h IV or Linezolid 600 mg q12h IV) + (Cefepime 2 gm IV q12h or Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h or Meropenem 1 gm IV q8h) (see comments for serious allergy to beta-lactams)
  • Extension from head and neck, odontogenic infections; esophageal perforation
    • Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q12h + Metronidazole 500 mg q8h + (Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg q8-12h IV  or Daptomycin 6 mg/kg IV 24h or Linezolid 600 mg IV q12h, add if suspected MRSA) (see comments for serious allergy to beta-lactams)
    • Ertapenem 1 gm IV q24h + (Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr [see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL] or Daptomycin 6 mg/kg IV 24h or Linezolid 600 mg IV q12h, add if suspected MRSA)(see Comments for serious allergy to beta-lactams)

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Adjust and de-escalate based on results of culture and susceptibility testing
  • Anaerobic coverage should be continued in cases of infection due to extension from head and neck, odontogenic infections, esophageal perforation

Comments

  • Duration of therapy based on clinical response and effective surgical drainage and debridement, typically 2-3 weeks and up to 6-8 weeks in cases of sternal osteomyelitis
  • For the patient with a serious allergy to beta-lactams
    • Aztreonam or a fluoroquinolone (Ciprofloxacin or Levofloxacin) is an option instead of a beta-lactam to cover Gram-negative aerobes, but should be paired with Vancomycin or Daptomycin or Linezolid to cover Gram-positives and Metronidazole to cover anaerobes
  • Surgical drainage and debridement are mainstays of therapy
