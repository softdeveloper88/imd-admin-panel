Tuberculosis, CNS

Clinical Setting

  • CNS tuberculosis
    • Meningitis
      • Basilar meningitis, may be accompanied by focal cranial nerve or other neurological deficits, altered mental status, hydrocephalus
      • Lymphocytic pleocytosis, high protein, and/or low glucose of CSF
    • Tuberculoma: granulomatous lesions in the parenchyma of the brain, calcifications in healing lesions
    • Brain abscess
    • Spinal arachnoiditis, presentation similar to meningitis but findings of cord compression, nerve root irritation predominate

Diagnosis

  • See J Clin Microbiol 2021; 59: e01771021
    • Culture: 50-60% sensitivity (should be obtained in all suspected cases)
    • Method of choice: nucleic acid amplification tests: 60-80% sensitivity
      • In addition to culture WHO recommends Cepheid Xpert MTB/RIF Ultra cartridge (not available in the US) rather than older version Xpert MTB/RIF for diagnosis of tuberculous meningitis because of its superior sensitivity over Xpert MTB/RIF or culture (Lancet Infect Dis. 2018;18:6-8). 
    • Acid-fast stain: ~10-30% sensitivity
    • Adenosine deaminase (ADA): ~30% sensitivity

Etiologies

  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis

Primary Regimens

  • Treatment of meningitis for adults and children with drug-susceptible strains
    • Expert consultation strongly advised.
    • WHO standard-of-care (SOC) regimen: Isoniazid + Rifampin + Ethambutol + Pyrazinamide
      • For dosing see First-line TB Drug Dosing Table. (See Comments section below for important considerations)
      • Discontinue Ethambutol and Pyrazinamide after 2 months.
      • Treat for a total of 9-12 months.
  • Treatment of meningitis caused by multi-drug resistant (MDR) or rifampin-resistant strains or for those intolerant drugs of the primary regimen for drug-susceptible TB
    • Expert consultation strongly advised, no consensus standard-of-care regimen.
    • See MDR tuberculosis for treatment of meningitis caused by MDR strains (those resistant to both INH and Rifampin) 
    • To the extent possible select agents with good CSF penetration (Curr Treat Options Neurol. 2018; 20:5):  Isoniazid (80-90%), Rifampin (10-20%), Pyrazinamide (90-100%), Ethambutol (20-30%), Levofloxacin (70-80%), Moxifloxacin (70-80%), Ethionamide (80-90%), Cycloserine (80-90%), Linezolid (30-70%); aminoglycosides have poor penetration (10-20%) and an unfavorable therapeutic window.
  • Adjunctive corticosteriods strongly recommended for patients with tuberculous meningitis (See Comments)
    • Adult:  Dexamethasone 0.4 mg/kg/day week 1, 0.3 mg/kg/day week 2, 0.2 mg/kg/day week 3, 0.1 mg/kg/day week 4, then tapered to stop over 3-4 weeks.
    • Child: Dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg/day or Prednisolone 4 mg/kg/day for 4 weeks then tapered to stop over 4 weeks. 
  • Obtain cerebral MRI to look for evidence of ischemia or infarction and need for adjunctive aspirin therapy.

Alternative Regimens

  • WHO alternative regimen for children and adolescents without suspicion or evidence of MDR or rifampin-resistant TB (See Comments)
    • Isoniazid 20 mg/kg/d + Rifampin 20 mg/kg/day + Pyrazinamide 40 mg/kg/d + Ethionamide 20 mg/kg/day for 6 months

Comments

  • Concerning WHO SOC regimen for drug-susceptible TB, some experts recommend using higher doses of rifampin.
    • The currently recommended dose of rifampin is 10 mg/kg/d for adults and 10-20 mg/kg/d for children.  Due to its relatively poor CNS penetration, it has been speculated that CNS levels at these doses may be subtherapeutic and that higher doses of rifampin (e.g., 20-35 mg/kg/d) might improve outcomes.  Higher rifampin exposures have been associated with improved survival in a meta-analysis of three dose-ranging studies (Clin Infect Dis. 2020; 71:1817). However, a randomized controlled trial (N Engl J Med. 2016;374:124) comparing an intensified regimen that included higher-dose rifampin (15 mg/kg/d) and levofloxacin (20 mg/kg/d) for the first 8 weeks of therapy to the standard regimen showed no improved survival with the intensified regimen. Randomized controlled trials (Lancet Infect Dis. 2025; 25: e47–e58) are planned or underway comparing intensified regimens that include high-dose rifampin, 35 mg/kg/d, and linezolid to the standard WHO regimen.
    • For patients with severe infection (e.g., altered mental status, focal neurological deficits): consider pushing rifampin dose to 20 mg/kg/d and if available, use intravenous medications until clinical improvement.
  • The WHO 6-month alternative regimen using higher than standard doses of isoniazid, rifampin, and pyrazinamide and substituting ethionamide for ethambutol for TB meningitis in children and adolescents (with no risk for drug resistance) instead of the conventional 12-mo regimen is  a conditional recommendation, very low certainty of evidence based on a meta-analysis (Open Forum Infect Dis. 2022 Apr 9;9(6):ofac108) that showed potentially better outcomes with the 6-month compared to the standard 12-month regimen. Caution is warranted however given that these are observational data from non-randomized studies conducted in different clinical setting and study populations.
  • Steroids reduce mortality from tuberculous meningitis (Cochrane Database Syst Rev, Apr 28;4:CD002244, 2016): also see British Infection Society guidelines (J Infect 59:167, 2009) and ATS/CDC/IDSA guidelines (Clin Infect Dis 63:e147, 2016).  Rifampin enhances metabolism of steroids: consider increasing dose in those not responding.
  • Aspirin (dose range 75-81 mg once daily up to 100 mg/kg/d in 3 divided doses, typically for 1-2 months), may lower mortality and reduce risk of stroke in adults with tuberculous meningitis (Curr Treat Options Neurol. 2018; 20:5; Neurol India. 2019; 67:993–1002; Acta Neurologica Belgica. 2021;121:11); benefit in children not established.
  • Antiretroviral therapy should be delayed by 8 weeks in HIV infected patients with TB meningitis: immediate (i.e., within 1-2 weeks of initiation of tuberculosis therapy) compared to deferred (after 2 months of tuberculosis therapy) associated with increased rates of paradoxical worsening (immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome) of CNS tuberculosis and does not improve outcome (Clin Infect Dis. 2011;52:1374; Clin Infect Dis 2013; 56:450).
  • Although therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM) is not routinely recommended, if there is a delayed or poor clinical response, especially in those with severe disease, assay serum or plasma drug concentrations to document adequate drug exposure, which may be impaired due to poor drug absorption due to gastrointestinal disease, drug-drug interactions, non-adherence, or other factors that may affect drug clearance.
