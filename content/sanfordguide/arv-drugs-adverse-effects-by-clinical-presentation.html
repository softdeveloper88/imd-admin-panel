ARV Drugs: Adverse Effects By Clinical Presentation

Introduction

  • This is a summary of adverse effects of antiretroviral therapy (ART) organized by clinical presentation. For specific adverse effects for ARV drugs, see the relevant drug page.
  • Topics are divided as follows:
    • Life Threatening
    • Serious
    • Long Term Consequences (HIV & Aging: JAIDS 60 (Suppl 1):S1, 2012)
    • Quality of Life related
  • S&S = clinical signs and symptoms.
  • See also, Overlapping Toxicities Between ARVs and Other Commonly Used Drugs.

Life Threatening Adverse Effects

Drug-induced Hepatitis

  • Nevirapine (Viramune) hypersensitivity reaction most important. Onset in 1st 6-18 weeks of ART. S&S: Nausea/vomiting/icterus. Skin rash in 50%. Frequency: 2.5–11% in clinical trials.
  • Risk factors: Females with CD4 >250; males with CD4 >400.
  • Monitor ALT/AST every 2 weeks x 1 month, then every month x 3, then every 3 months.
  • Discontinue all ARVs + other potential hepatotoxic drugs.

Lactic acidosis, hepatic steatosis ± pancreatitis

  • NRTIs: stavudine (Zerit), didanosine (Videx), zidovudine (Retrovir). Onset: Months after starting therapy. S&S: Nausea, vomiting, fatigue, dyspnea, icterus. Lab: Metabolic acidosis with anion gap & elevated lactate. Frequency: Rare: 0.85 cases/1000 pt yrs, but mortality up to 50%.
  • Risk factors: Didanosine + stavudine. Female gender, pregnancy, obesity.
  • Monitor lactic acid levels if suggestive symptoms and low serum HCO3 and/or high anion gap. Routine lactate levels not recommended.
  • Discontinue all ARVs. IV thiamine &/or riboflavin reported helpful. If needed, use NRTIs with low potential for mitochondrial toxicity: i.e., abacavir, tenofovir, lamivudine, emtricitabine.
  • Mitochondrial toxicity reference: J Antimicrob Chemother 61:8, 2008.

Lactic acidosis, rapid progressive ascending neuromuscular weakness

  • Stavudine (Zerit) and other nucleoside analogues, esp. didanosine (Videx) & zidovudine (Retrovir). Onset: After months of ART. S&S: Rapid progressive ascending polyneuropathy that mimics Guillain-Barré. Lab: Metabolic acidosis with anion gap & elevated lactate + high CPK. Occurrence is rare.
  • Risk factors: Prolonged stavudine use; female gender, obesity.
  • Prevent by early recognition.
  • Discontinue all ARVs, mechanical ventilation. Unclear benefit from plasmapheresis, IVIG, corticosteroids, carnitine. Do not rechallenge with stavudine.

Stevens-Johnson syndrome, toxic epidermal necrolysis

  • NNRTIs. Rare case reports with other classes of ARVs and TMP-SMX. Onset in 1st few days to weeks of ART. S&S: Skin eruption with mucosal ulcers ± epidermal detachment. Frequency: Nevirapine (Viramune) 0.3–1%, Efavirenz (Sustiva) 0.1%.
  • Risk factors: Nevirapine – female, African-American, Asian, Hispanic.
  • Prevention: Educate patients for early recognition.
  • Discontinue all ARVs + other possible drug etiology, e.g., TMP-SMX. Usually requires ICU care.
  • See also, drug-induced hepatitis, above.

Systemic hypersensitivity reaction

  • Abacavir (Ziagen) hypersensitivity reaction. DO NOT rechallenge with abacavir. Onset: Median 9 days; 90% within 1st 6 weeks of ART. S&S: Fever, diffuse rash, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, arthralgia, dyspnea, cough, or pharyngitis. Frequency: 8% in clinical trials (range 2–9%).
    • Risk factors: HLA-DR7 or HLAB*5701 positive.
    • Prevention: Screen for HLA-B*5701.
    • Discontinue all ARVs; resolution within 48 hours; do not rechallenge with abacavir.
  • Nevirapine (Viramune). Severe skin reactions in 2%. Do not restart Nevirapine. See drug-induced hepatitis, above.
  • Systemic reaction also reported during therapy with etravirine.

Serious Adverse Effects

Bleeding events: Hemophiliac patients

  • Protease inhibitors. S&S: spontaneous bleeding. Frequency is unknown. Risk factors: PI use. Try to avoid PIs in hemophiliacs.
  • Management: Increased use of Factor VIII.

Intracranial hemorrhage

  • Tipranavir boosted with ritonavir (TPV/r). Median time to hemorrhage: 525 days of TPV/r therapy. 24 cases with TPV/r and 2 deaths reported.
  • Risk factors: CNS disease, injury or surgery, anti-coagulants, Vitamin E.
  • Avoid Vitamin E supplements and discontinue TPV/r.

Bone marrow suppression

  • Zidovudine (Retrovir). Onset after weeks or months of ART. S&S: Fatigue. Lab: Anemia (1.1-4%) &/or neutropenia (1.8-8%).
  • Risk factors: AIDS; high-dose concomitant marrow suppressive drug(s).
  • Avoid marrow suppressive drugs. CBC & differential at least every 3 months. If severe, could use G-CSF &/or erythropoietin.

Hepatotoxicity: steatosis to NASH

  • All NNRTIs, all PIs, most NRTIs, maraviroc (Selzentry). Steatosis: most common with zidovudine (Retrovir)(ZDV), stavudine (Zerit)(d4T), didanosine (Videx)(ddI). PIs: Clinical hepatitis reported with tipranavir boosted with ritonavir (TPV/r) Onset is variable. With NRTIs: Asymptomatic increased AST/ALT + lactic acidosis (ZDV, ddI, d4T). Occurrence varies depending on drug.
  • Risk factors: HBV or HCV co-infection, alcoholism, other hepatotoxic drugs.
  • Nevirapine: Monitor LFTs frequently; especially females. TPV/r: Avoid in patients with hepatic insufficiency. Test for Hep B&C. If symptomatic, discontinue all ARVs. Vitamin E may improve steatosis. L-carnitine (2 gm/day) may help (Dig Dis Sci 53:1114, 2008).

Nephrolithiasis, urolithiasis, crystalluria

  • Indinavir (Crixivan) most often, rarely atazanavir (Reyataz). Onset at any time during ART. S&S: Flank pain & dysuria.
  • Lab: Hematuria, crystalluria, pyuria. Occurrence: 4.7–34.4% range in clinical trials.
  • Risk factors: Dehydration; history of nephrolithiasis.
  • Hydration is key: Intake of 1.5–2 liters water/day.

Nephrotoxicity

  • Tenofovir (Viread) tubular injury. Onset with tenofovir is weeks to months after starting ART. S&S: Tenofovir: nephrogenic diabetes insipidus, Fanconi syndrome. Lab: Non-anion gap metabolic acidosis, glycosuria, hypokalemia, hypophosphatemia. References: Top Antivir Med 22:655, 2014; J Infect Dis 210:363, 2014; N Engl J Med 370:959, 2014).
    • Cobicistat (along with tenofovir is part of Stribild) inhibits creatinine secretion by the proximal tubule. Results in 0.1-0.4 mg/dl increase in serum creatinine and an apparent decrease in eGFR; however, this is NOT due to any change in actual GFR (as determined by iohexol clearance).
    • Unknown frequency of tenofovir toxicity; severe toxicity is rare. Based on meta-analysis, severity of toxicity is modest (CID 51:496, 2010).
    • Tenofovir toxicity risk factors: Other nephrotoxic drugs; other ARV drugs handled by proximal tubular cells; report of predisposing human genetic variant (JID 194:1471, 2006; JID 194:1481, 2006).
    • Monitor for tenofovir tubular toxicity:
      • Periodically check serum levels of phosphate, potassium, and creatinine.
      • Small increases in serum creatinine maybe due to secretion competition from other drugs: i.e., cobicistat, ritonavir, dolutegravir, rilpivirine, or trimethoprim.
      • Decrease dosing frequency of tenofovir if estimated CrCl <50 cc/min.
      • Tenofovir renal injury is reversible if caught early.
      • Risk with tenofovir offset by potent antiretroviral effect (JAC 63:374, 2009).
  • For Indinavir (Crixivan) induced crystalluria, see above.

Pancreatitis

  • Didanosine (Videx); didanosine + stavudine (Zerit); didanosine + ribavirin or tenofovir (Viread). Onset in weeks to months after starting ART. S&S: Abdominal and/or back pain, nausea/vomiting. Lab: Elevated amylase/lipase levels. Frequency: Didanosine alone 1–7%. Increased frequency if didanosine with stavudine, tenofovir or ribavirin.
  • Risk factors: High serum/cell didanosine levels; alcoholism; hypertriglyceridemia. Failure to reduce dose of didanosine if given with tenofovir.
  • Do not use didanosine if history of pancreatitis. Adjust dose of didanosine if tenofovir used. Avoid use of didanosine with stavudine, tenofovir or ribavirin. Discontinue ARV drugs.

Polyneuropathy

  • Stavudine (Zerit), also didanosine (Videx). Stavudine induced S&S: Rapidly progressive ascending polyneuropathy mimics Guillain-Barré syndrome. Occurrence is rare.
  • Risk factors: Prolonged use of stavudine.
  • Early recognition is key. Discontinue all ARVs. Variable success with plasmapheresis, steroids, IVIG, carnitine.

Adverse Effects With Long Term Complications

  • HIV and Aging: General reference: JAIDS 60 (Suppl 1):S1, 2012.

Atherosclerotic MIs & CVAs

  • May be driven by chronic infection and immune reactivation. Can visualize arterial inflammation with PET scans (JAMA 308:379, 2012; JAMA 308:405, 2012). Also,  see Ann Intern Med 160:458, 2014; Ann Intern Med 160:509, 2014. Traditional risk factors and uncontrolled HIV infection are most important. Selected HIV drugs are suspected but risk factors are unproven. Abacavir was considered and has been rejected as a risk factor (Clin Infect DIs 52:929, 2011; Clin Infect DIs 53:84, 2011; Clin Infect DIs 53:92, 2011). Onset: Months to years after starting ART. S&S: Premature or accelerated atherosclerotic vascular disease (e.g., MI, stroke). Data on frequency is controversial.
  • Risk factors: Tobacco use, age, hyperlipidemia, hypertension, diabetes, obesity, HIV infection +, low CD4 count (Clin Infect DIs 51:435, 2010).
  • Address risk factors; suppress HIV viremia. Review: Topics HIV Med 18:164, 2010. Check Vitamin D level: correlation with silent coronary artery disease (Clin Infect Dis 54:1747, 2012).

Hyperlipidemia

  • All protease inhibitors (PIs) are implicated except atazanavir. Stavudine and Efavirenz are also implicated. Ritonavir boosting elevates triglyceride levels. Associated with elevation of TG, LDL, HDL: Stavudine > zidovudine > abacavir; Efavirenz and all RTV-boosted PIs (Lopinavir/r > Darunavir/r > Atazanavir/r). 1.7-2.3-fold increase in frequency with PIs other than atazanavir.
  • Risk factors: PIs: ritonavir boosted lopinavir; NNRTI: Efavirenz; NRTI: Stavudine.
  • ART causes modest increase in serum lipids; manage with statins. Check fasting lipid panel at baseline at 3-6 months, then annually. Check for drug-drug interactions: statins and PIs. In general, prefer treatment with atorvastatin or rosuvastatin (AIDS 24:77, 2010; Clin Infect Dis 52:387, 2011).
  • References: J Infect DIs 205(Suppl 3):S383, 2012; Clin Infect Dis 52:387, 2011.

Insulin resistance, Diabetes mellitus

  • Zidovudine, Stavudine, didanosine. Relation to PIs is unclear. Onset in weeks to months after starting ART. S&S: Polydipsia, polyuria, polyphagia. Frequency: Diabetes in 3-5%.
  • Risk factors: Obesity, genetics, dyslipidemia.
  • Avoid Zidovudine & Stavudine. Diet & exercise, metformin, “glitazones,” sulfonylureas, insulin.
  • Reference: J Infect DIs 205(Suppl 3):S383, 2012.

Osteonecrosis

  • Also described as: Avascular necrosis: Clin Infect Dis 2017;64:1413
  • HIV itself, tenofovir (Clin Infect Dis 51:963, 2010), ritonavir boosted atazanavir & efavirenz are suspect.
    • Onset is insidious or can be abrupt.
    • S&S: Periarticular pain.
    • 85% involve one or both femoral heads. Described in humeral heads and proximal tibia.
    • Symptomatic in 0.08–1.3%; asymptomatic by MRI in 4%.
  • Risk factors: Diabetes; prior steroid use; alcohol use; Vitamin D deficiency.
  • Avoid steroids. Periodic MRI to assess disease progression. Check Vitamin D levels. Remove risk factors; less weight-bearing; some require total joint arthroplasty. Vitamin D supplement if levels low.

Adverse Effects Influencing Quality of Life

Lipoatrophy

  • NRTIs, especially stavudine, then zidovudine, tenofovir, abacavir, lamivudine, emtricitabine. Also when combined with efavirenz. Loss of subcutaneous fat on face, buttocks & extremities. Note: HLA-B*4001 presence risk factor for stavudine-associated lipodystrophy (Clin Infect DIs 50:597, 2010). Precise frequency is unknown.
  • Risk factors: Low nadir CD4 count, older age. Low baseline body mass index (BMI).
  • Management:
    • Change NRTI to abacavir
    • Two temporary dermal fillers approved by the FDA: poly-lactic acid (Sculpts) and calcium hydroxyapatite (Radiesse) of limited help.
    • If concomitant insulin-resistant type 2 diabetes mellitus: Pioglitazone (J Infect Dis 195:1731, 2007).
    • If possible, avoid stavudine and zidovudine.
    • What makes it worse? Exercise, testosterone, metformin, recombinant human growth hormone.
    • What helps?
  • References: J Infect Dis 205(Suppl 3):S383, 2012; Clin Infect Dis 51:591, 2010 and Clin Infect Dis 64:1422, 2017

Fat Accumulation: Lipodystrophy

  • Implicated Drugs: PI or NNRTI combined with Stavudine or Zidovudine. Excess adipose tissue in abdominal viscera, breast size, dorsocervical fat pad. Management: Tesamorelin responders had decrease in triglycerides & adiponectin levels (Clin Infect DIs 54:1642, 2012). Precise frequency is unknown.
  • Risk factors and comments:
    • Obesity prior to HIV infection; low CD4 count prior to therapy; older age; low baseline BMI.
    • Avoid combination of PI or NNRTI with Stavudine or Zidovudine. Switching from Lopinavir-ritonavir to boosted Atazanavir reduced visceral fat (AIDS 23:1349, 2009).
  • What helps?
    • Growth hormone releasing factor (tesamorelin - EGRIFTA) reduced visceral fat by 18% (JAIDS 53:311, 2010, Med Lett 53:33, 2011). FDA approved 2 mg sc once daily; (http://egrifta.com/#/). Tesamorelin responders had decrease in triglycerides and adiponectin levels (Clin Infect Dis 54:1642, 2012).
    • Metformin helps patients with type 2 diabetes mellitus
  • Reference: J Infect Dis 205(Suppl 3):S383, 2012; Clin Infect Dis 64:1422, 2017

Gastrointestinal: Diarrhea

  • All PIs and didanosine. Onset is after 1st dose. Symptoms: Perhaps worst with lopinavir/ritonavir, nelfinavir, & buffered didanosine. Frequency varies.
  • Risk factors: all patients.
  • Antidiarrheals. Loperamide, diphenoxylate/atropine, calcium tabs, psyllium products, pancreatic enzymes, L-glutamate.

Osteopenia & Osteoporosis

  • Bone loss: Tenofovir, ritonavir-boosted atazanavir, efavirenz. Occurs months to years after starting ART; increased risk of fractures. Frequency is unclear but higher than in the general population.
  • Risk factors: Low Vitamin D levels (Clin Infect Dis 52:396, 2011).
  • Give Vitamin D supplements. Perhaps increased risk with stavudine & tenofovir; consider bisphosphonates.
  • Review: J Infect Dis 205(Suppl. 3):S391, 2012; Clin Infect Dis 2017;64:1413

Peripheral neuropathy

  • Didanosine (12-34%) and stavudine (52%). Onset in weeks or months after starting ART. S&S: Usually legs: Numbness & paresthesias. Often irreversible even if drug(s) are stopped.
  • Risk factors: Pre-existing neuropathy; advanced HIV; concomitant drugs that increase intracellular didanosine, e.g., ribavirin, tenofovir.
  • Avoid didanosine and stavudine, especially in combination. If painful can try gabapentin or tricyclic antidepressants; lamotrigine, carbamazepine (watch for drug interactions), topiramate, tramadol, narcotics, topical capsaicin.
