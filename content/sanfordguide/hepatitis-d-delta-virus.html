Hepatitis D (Delta) Virus

Clinical Setting

  • Hepatitis D virus (HDV) is also known as “Delta” hepatitis
  • Small circular RNA virus; replication defective.  Requires hepatitis B virus co-infection in order to survive and propagate in humans
  • Transmitted by blood and blood products or sexual transmission; Risk factors similar to those for HBV infection
  • Infection with HDV can occur at the same time infection (leads to persistent infection < 10%) with HBV or can occur as a ‘super-infection’ of HBV at a later time (persistent infection occurs in ~80%)
  • Among 292 million people with chronic HBV infection worldwide, between 4.5% and 14.6% also carry HDV
  • Underdiagnosed; Guidelines recommend testing for HDV antibodies and, if antibody test is positive, check HDV RNA
  • Super-infection of HBV can lead to dramatic deterioration of clinical status, including rapid liver failure and death in patients with pre-existing HBV.

Diagnosis

  • Hepatitis D Antibody tests.  Presence of IgM antibodies indicates acute infection.
  • Hepatitis D virus RNA PCR

Etiology

  • Hepatitis D virus (incomplete RNA virus; missing envelop proteins)

Treatment

  • Acute infection / illness:  Supportive care.  Liver transplant may be required in cases of fulminant hepatitis
  • Chronic infection:  Alpha interferon-2b (5 million units/m2  3x/week x 4 months, then 3 million units/m2  x an additional 8 months) can be used.  Most effective when used early in the course of therapy
    • Bulevirtide a novel entry inhibitor is conditionally approved in the EU and under review by the US FDA

Comments

  • Phase III study of Bulevirtide demonstrated 45% and 48% reduction of HDV RNA levels by at least 2 log combined with normalization of ALT levels in the 2 mg and 10 mg SQ daily groups , respectively, by 48 weeks.  Efficacy remained during extension of daily SQ therapy out to 144 weeks.  It is unclear whether HDV virus rebounds upon cessation of Rx (being assessed in study extension phase).  AEs (up to 10 - 14 %)include headache, pruritus, fatigue, eosinophilia, injection site reactions, RUQ abdominal pain, and asthenia.  (N Engl J Med 389: 22, 2023)
  • Prevention:  HBV vaccination also protects against infection with HDV, owing to the requirement of HBV co-infection for Hepatitis D virus to survive and propagate.
