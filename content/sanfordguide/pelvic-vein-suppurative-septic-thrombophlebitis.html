Pelvic Vein Suppurative (Septic) Thrombophlebitis

Clinical Setting

  • Infection of clotted ovarian or deep pelvic veins
    • Usually postpartum (either vaginal or C-section delivery). Can complicate postpartum endometritis (endomyometritis) or pelvic inflammatory disease.
    • Much less common associations: post-hysterectomy, underlying malignancy, hormonal stimulation
  • Infection of ovarian vein:
    • One week postpartum with fever and focal pain;
    • Infection of deep pelvic veins presents 3-5 days postpartum with fever but no localizing pain.
  • Deep pelvic vein infection complicated by pulmonary emboli in 2% (usually small).
  • For post-operative infections in obstetrics and gynecological setting, see Clin Obst Gyn 2012; 55:904.

Diagnosis

  • CT scan or MRI

Etiologies

  • Bacteroides sp., Prevotella bivia, other anaerobes (e.g., Clostridium species)
  • Streptococcus sp. (Group A, B)
  • Enterobacteriaceae: E. coli, Klebsiella species and others

Primary Regimens

  • Treatment is a combination of effective antibiotics and anticoagulation
  • No clear role for surgery on infected veins
  • If low prevalence of multi-drug resistant gram negative bacilli (MDR GNB):
    • Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h
    • Ceftriaxone 1-2 gm IV q24h + Metronidazole 500 mg IV q8h
    • Heparin
    • Discontinue antibiotic(s) when WBC and differential normal and afebrile for 48 hrs
    • Then switch from heparin to warfarin and continue for 6 weeks.
  • If high prevalence (≥ 20%) of MDR GNB:
    • Imipenem-cilastatin 0.5 gm IV q6h or Meropenem 1 gm IV q8h to ensure adequate therapy vs ESBL producing aerobic GNB

Alternative Regimens

  • If severe IgE-mediated beta-lactam antibiotic allergy, could use:
    • Ciprofloxacin 400 mg IV q12h + Metronidazole 500 mg IV q8h
    • Heparin
    • Discontinue antibiotics after WBC and differential normal and afebrile for 48 hrs
    • Then switch from heparin to warfarin and continue for 6 weeks.
