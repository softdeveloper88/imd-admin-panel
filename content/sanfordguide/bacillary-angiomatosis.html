Bacillary Angiomatosis, Peliosis

Clinical Setting

  • Immunocompromised:
    • HIV-positive patients with AIDS (CD4 count < 100 cells/µL)
    • Cancer and/or organ transplant patients.
  • For Bartonella infection in immunocompetent patient, see Cat Scratch Disease.
  • Presentation:
    • Uncomplicated: Fever, skin lesions (often mistaken for Kaposi's Sarcoma) with variety of forms (papules, nodules, pedunculated) but always vascular.
    • Complicated: Disseminated disease can manifest as bone, mucosal, or central nervous system infection. Can present as an FUO or endocarditis.
  • Bacillary peliosis: cystic vascular lesions in liver (in differential diagnosis of liver abscesses) or spleen containing colonies of bacteria.
  • Diagnosis:
    • Elevated antibody titer
    • Histopathology or PCR of affected tissue / biopsy.   Histopathology:  use a modified silver stain (such as Warthin-Starry stain)  numerous bacilli are typically seen. Tissue Gram staining and acid-fast staining are negative
  • Brief Review (StatPearls)

Etiologies

  • Bartonella henselae
  • Bartonella quintana

Primary Regimens

  • Uncomplicated (not disseminated) infection:
    • Doxycycline 100 mg IV/po bid x 3 months or more
    • Erythromycin 500 mg po q6h x 3 months or more
    • Azithromycin 500 mg po daily x 3 months or more
  • Complicated (Dissemminated ) infection, e.g., CNS involvement:
    • (Doxycycline 100 mg IV/po bid + Rifampin 300 mg po bid) x minimum of 4 months
  • See Comment for relapsing disease and possible chronic suppressive therapy if CD4 count < 200 cells/μL.

Alternative Regimens

  • For uncomplicated (not disseminated) infection: Clarithromycin 500 mg po twice daily for 3 months or more
  • See Comment regarding relapsing disease.

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Note drugs that should not be used: TMP-SMX, Ciprofloxacin, penicillins, cephalosporins.

Comments

  • Jarisch-Herxheimer reaction may occur in the first 48 hrs of therapy.
  • After the initial 3-4 months of therapy, regardless of CD4 count, discontinue therapy and observe the patient for relapse. If no relapse, no need for suppressive therapy.
  • If the patient relapses, re-institute therapy with Erythromycin or Doxycycline as above. Treat for at least 3 months and then, if CD4 count <200, continue as suppressive therapy. Safe to stop suppression when CD4 count >200 for 6 months.
