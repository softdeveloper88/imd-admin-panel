Malaria, Uncomplicated, P. falciparum (or species unknown)

Clinical Setting

  • Uncomplicated malaria (Plasmodium falciparum identified, presumed, or species unknown and any possibility of P. falciparum):
  • Chloroquine-sensitive
    • All malaria even if P. falciparum in Central America west of the Panama canal, the Caribbean, Korea and most middle eastern countries is chloroquine sensitive.
  • For uncomplicated malaria the oral ACT Artemether/lumefantrine is recommended as first choice by the US CDC for all species of malaria unless assuredly chloroquine sensitive.
    • P. falciparum is rarely chloroquine sensitive and other species uncommonly chloroquine resistant.
  • Consolidated WHO guidelines GRADE give a strong recommendation to preferentially treat adults and children with all of uncomplicated P. vivax, P. ovale, P. malariae or P. knowlesi malaria with either an oral ACT (including all pregnant women including in the first trimester)
    • However, chloroquine remains an option unless there is known chloroquine resistance in which case an ACT should be used.

  • CDC algorithm for diagnosis and treatment of malaria in the US.

  • Important to know where malaria was acquired.
    • If in SE Asia, recommendations differ due to resistance, don't use mefloquine and extend quinine duration to 7d.
  • Treat parasitemia >4% (>2% in non-immune traveler) or Hct <20 as severe malaria even if clinically mild.
    • CDC cutoff 5% to define severe malaria based solely on parasitemia.

Diagnosis

  • Rapid antigen detection test (Binax NOW) (Clin Infect Dis 49:908, 2009; Clin Infect Dis 54:1637, 2012) used in many hospital laboratories, not approved as a point of care test.
    • Original trials: >96% sensitive for clinically significant P. falciparum parasitemias.
    • Caution: A negative RDT (US: Binax; HRP based) no longer reliably rules out P. falciparum infection due to the increasing global prevalence of HRP2/3 deletion mutants.
      • A negative RDT should be quickly followed by a malaria blood film. Malar J 2022 21:26, 2022. 
    • 50% sensitivity for low non-faciparum parasitemias which is the usual case.
    • High specificity; treat a positive test.
    • Need microscopy to speciate.
    • Can stay positive for over a month after successful treatment.
  • Blood film (ideally finger-stick) read by properly trained personnel.
  • PCR is available from commercial labs and is the gold standard.
  • Serology of limited value.

Etiologies

  • Plasmodium falciparum
  • Plasmodium species unknown but P. falciparum possible

Primary Regimens

  • Chloroquine-resistant/resistance unknown:
    • Adult:
      • Atovaquone-proguanil 4 adult tabs (250 mg/100 mg tabs) po in a single dose daily x 3 days (take with food; repeat dose if patient vomits within 30 minutes) or
      • Artemether-lumefantrine 4 tablets (20 mg/120 mg tabs) as a single dose, then 4 tablets again after 8 hours, then 4 tablets every 12 hours for 2 days (take with food) ) or
      • [(Quinine sulfate 650 mg po tid x 3 days (7 days if SE Asia)] + [(Doxycycline 100 mg po bid) or (Tetracycline 250 mg po qid)]
      • Pyronaridine/artesunate 4 tablets (180mg/60mg tabs) as a single dose each day for 3 days for >65 kg or 3 tablets if 45 to <65 kg (not available in US)
    • Pediatric:
      • Artemether-lumefantrine (20 mg/120 mg tablets): Wt-based dosing (see below) or
      • Atovaquone-proguanil (pediatric 62.5 mg/25 mg tabs):
        • <5 kg:  Not recommended
        • 5-8 kg: 2 pediatric tablets in a single dose daily for 3 days
        • 9-10 kg: 3 pediatric tablets in a single dose daily for 3 days
        • 11–20 kg: 1 adult tablet in a single dose daily for 3 days
        • 21–30 kg: 2 adult tablets in a single dose daily for 3 days
        • 31–40 kg: 3 adult tablets in a single dose daily for 3 days
        • >40 kg: as per adult dose  OR
      • (Quinine sulfate 10 mg/kg po tid x 3 days (7 days if SE Asia)) + (Doxycycline 2.2 mg/kg/ bid) for 7 days (See Comment re doxycycline in children)
      • Pyronaridine/artesunate 2 tablets (180mg/60mg) as a single dose each day for 3 days for 24 to >45 kg or 1 tablet if 20 to <24 kg.  
        • Pediatric granule formulation for oral suspension (60mg/20mg)
          • Wt 5 - < 8 kg 1 sachet daily for 3 days
          • Wt 8 - < 15 kg 2 sachets daily for 3 days
          • Wt 15 - < 20 kg 3 sachets daily for 3 days
  • Chloroquine-sensitive:
    • Adult:
      • Chloroquine phosphate 1 gm (600 mg base) po, then 0.5 gm at 6, 24, and 48 hours. Total: 2500 mg (Entire course is 5 x 500 mg tablets) or
      • Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil and generics) 800 mg salt (620 mg base) po, followed by 400 mg salt (=310 mg base) po at 6, 24, and 48 hours. Total: 2000 mg salt (=1,550 mg salt) or
      • Artemether-lumefantrine (20 mg/120 mg tabs), 4 tablets (80 mg/480 mg) as a single dose, then 4 tablets again after 8 hours, then 4 tablets every 12 hours for 2 days (take with food). Note: ACT not mentioned by CDC for CQ-sensitive P. falciparum
      • Pyronaridine/artesunate 4 tablets (180mg/60mg tabs) as a single dose each day for 3 days for >65 kg or 3 tablets if 45 to <65 kg (not available in US)
    • Pediatric:
      • Chloroquine phosphate 10 mg/kg of base po; then 5 mg/kg of base at 6, 24, 48 hrs. Total: 25 mg/kg base (never exceed adult dose) or
      • Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil and generics) 10 mg base/kg po immediately, followed by 5 mg base/kg po at 6, 24, and 48 hours. Total: 25 mg base/kg 
      • Artemether-lumefantrine (20 mg/120 mg tablets): Wt-based dosing:
        • <5 kg:  Not recommended for these infants nor for mothers breastfeeding these infants.
        • 5 kg to < 15 kg: 1 tablet (20 mg/120 mg) as a single dose, then 1 tablet again after 8 hours, then 1 tablet every 12 hours for 2 days
        • 15 kg to < 25 kg: 2 tablets (40 mg/240 mg) as a single dose, then 2 tablets again after 8 hours, then 2 tablets every 12 hours for 2 days
        • 25 kg to < 35 kg: 3 tablets (60 mg/360 mg) as a single dose, then 3 tablets again after 8 hours, then 3 tablets every 12 hours for 2 days
        • ≥ 35 kg: as per adult dose
        • ACT not mentioned by CDC for CQ-sensitive P. falciparum
      • Pyronaridine/artesunate 2 tablets (180 mg/60 mg tabs) as a single dose each day for 3 days for 24 to >45 kg or 1 tablet if 20 to <24 kg.  
        • Pediatric granule formulation for oral suspension (60 mg/20 mg sachets)
          • 5 - < 8 kg 1 sachet daily for 3 days
          • 8 - < 15 kg 2 sachets daily for 3 days
          • 15 - < 20 kg 3 sachets daily for 3 days

Alternative Regimens

  • Chloroquine-sensitive
    • Atovaquone-proguanil 4 adult tabs (250 mg/100 mg tabs) po in a single dose daily x 3 days (take with food; repeat dose if patient vomits within 30 minutes) or
    • [(Quinine sulfate 650 mg po tid x 3 days (7 days if acquired in SE Asia)] + [(Doxycycline 100 mg po bid) or (Tetracycline 250 mg po qid)]
  • Chloroquine-resistant/resistance unknown
    • Mefloquine 750 mg po x 1 dose, then 500 mg po x 1 dose 6-12 hr later.
      • Pediatric:  13.7 mg base/kg (15 mg salt/kg) x 1 then 9.1 mg base/kg (10 mg salt/kg) po 6-12 hr later.
    • Use only if no other options
    • Not effective against strains of P. falciparum from SE Asia
  • Pregnancy:
    • Artemether-lumefantrine
      • Artemether-lumefantrine can be used in all trimesters in pregnancy per WHO and CDC evidence review and policy. Safety of ACT in 1st trimester (WHO analysis).
      • Do  not use in women breastfeeding infants <5 kg. 
    • Quinine sulfate 10 mg/kg po tid x 3 days (7 days if SE Asia)) + Clindamycin 20 mg/kg/day divided tid x 7 days.  
      • Do not delay therapy if quinine available and Artemether-lumefantrine is not.
    • Note contraindications:
      • Atovaquone-proguanil contraindicated
      • Pyramax and ACTs containing sulfadoxine/pyrimethamine contraindicated  

Comments

  • Avoid same drug for treatment for chemoprophylaxis failures with that same drug.
  • Artemisinin combination therapy (ACT) in the US. Only Artemether/lumefantrine is FDA-approved.
    • Not widely stocked by hospital or retail pharmacies in the US.
      • Generally available from regional drug distribution warehouses with up to 24-hour delay in initiating therapy for a medical emergency.
        • Do not delay therapy in order to obtain Coartem if another 1st line agent is more readily available.
      • Call 1 855-COARTEM for expedited availability information.
  • An ACT dihydroartemisinin/piperaquine (Eurartesim) is licensed and available in Europe and recommended by WHO.
    • WHO 2022 revision: Children weighing <25kg  should receive a minimum of 2.5 mg/kg/day of dihydroartemisinin and 20 mg/ kg/day of piperaquine daily for 3 days. 
  • Artesunate/mefloquine, artesunate/amodiaquine,  artesunate+sulfadoxine/pyrimethamine, and artesunate/pyronaridine (Pyramax) (do not use in setting of local S/P resistance) are additional ACTs recommended by WHO and available in some settings.
  • Artemisinin resistance is present in the Greater Mekong subregion of Asia Antimicrob Agents Chemother 2021; AAC0112121 and in Eritrea, Rwanda and Uganda.
    • Artemisinin resistance alone rarely leads to treatment failure but resistance to both the artemisinin derivative and the partner ACT drug (Mekong region only) does.
  • Obesity (WHO caution): As the evidence for an association between dose, pharmacokinetics and treatment outcome in overweight or large adults is limited, and alternative dosing options have not been assessed in treatment trials
    • In the absence of data, treatment providers should attempt to follow up the treatment outcomes of large adults whenever possible.
    • Limited pharmacokinetic data suggest no dosage adjustment required in adults (Int J Antimicrob Agents 2022;59:106482).
    • Two small clinical studies showing occasional failures in non-immune adults >65 kg (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2008, 78:241 and Clin Infect Dis 2017, 64:199). 
  • Although they usually occur together, any one of the following defines severe malaria (due to any malaria species) requiring parenteral therapy:
    • Clinical: Impaired consciousness, shock, pulmonary edema, acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), DIC, seizures.
      • Jaundice requires at least 1 other criteria
    • Laboratory:  any of severe anemia (Hct <20% in adults), renal failure, hemoglobinuria, lactic acidosis (bicarbonate <15), hypoglycemia (<40 mg/dL), parasitemia >5% (CDC criteria) defines severe malaria and mandates IV therapy even with no other criteria.
      • WHO severe malaria criteria >4% in a non-immune individual. Many US clinicians use IV artesunate at this threshold.
  • Note: Doxycycline can safely be administered for short durations (≤21 days) regardless of age.
  • Few data on clindamycin for malaria so avoid if possible outside of pregnancy 
  • Quinine: The only formulation of quinine in the U.S. is a 324 mg capsule. Thus, it is very difficult to treat children with quinine.
  • Mefloquine is a clear second-line alternative due to neuropsychiatric reactions in treatment doses.
    • Avoid if malaria acquired in SE Asia due to resistance.
  • Mefloquine has an FDA "black box" warning stating that neuropsychiatric and vestibular adverse reactions may persist indefinitely even after discontinuation of drug.
    • A written Medication Guide and wallet card with this boxed warning must be given to all patients by the dispensing pharmacist.
  • HIV: avoid artesunate + SP if on co-trimoxazole, and avoid artesunate + amodiaquine if on efavirenz or zidovudine. 
  • Chloroquine and mefloquine have long half-lives and prolong QTc intervals. Minor overdosing or too many doses over several days may be toxic or fatal.
  • In endemic areas with low-transmission areas, a single dose of 0.25 mg/kg primaquine should be given with an ACT to patients with P. falciparum malaria (except pregnant women, infants aged < 6 months and women breastfeeding infants aged < 6 months) to reduce transmission.
    • G6PD testing is not required.
