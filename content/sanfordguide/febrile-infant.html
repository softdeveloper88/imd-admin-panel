Febrile Infant, Neonatal Sepsis

Clinical Setting

  • Fever > 38.0 C in infants 1-29 days and children 30-89 days
  • Serious bacterial infection (SBI), e.g., UTI, bacteremia, or meningitis present in about 7-11%
    • Most SBI are UTI (7-9%); bacteremia (1-2%), meningitis (0.5%) Risk decreases with each week of life
  • Low risk criteria (age >29 days, WBC <15,000, Urine WBC < 10/hpf, CSF WBC < 10) identify group with ~3% chance of SBI
  • Diagnosis & Evaluation
    • Several approaches stratify risk
      • Use risk calculator based on clinical features, WBC, ANC, procalcitonin and UA. JAMA Pediatr. 2019 Apr 1;173(4):342-351.
      • Stratify by appearance, age, WBC, ANC and enterovirus PCR and respiratory viral studies.Pediatrics 130:e16, 2012.
      • Perform blood culture, urine culture; enterovirus PCR and respiratory viral studies for high risk infants. 
      • Inflammatory markers CRP and procalcitonin further stratify risk. Procalcitonin is the most accurate for risk stratification if available
    • UTI is the most common bacterial infection.
  • Detecting viral cause, e.g., enterovirus, respiratory virus other than rhinovirus, further lowers risk of SBI in high risk: ~ 5% if high risk and virus positive; ~17% if HR and virus negative. Detection of rhinovirus does not lower risk to a similar degree (Pediatrics 113:1662, 2004, Pediatrics. 2018 Feb;141(2).).
  • The role of viral testing is uncertain when procalcitonin is available
  • Consider risk factors and clues for HSV: Genital lesions in mother 48 hours before to 48 hours after delivery, vessicles, mucosal ulcers, seizures, transaminitis, hypothermia in infant
    • Recommended HSV studies:CSF PCR; HSV blood PCR; HSV surface swabs of the mouth, nasopharynx, conjunctivae, and anus for an HSV culture (if available) or PCR assay; alanine aminotransferase.

Etiologies

  • Etiologies have changed over time due to maternal screening, vaccinations and food safety
  • Viral (most common):
    • Enterovirus
    • RSV
    • Influenza
    • hMPV
    • Adenovirus
    • Parainfluenza virus
    • HSV rare but extremely serious. Consider especially if vesicular skin lesions, transaminitis, seizures, or CSF pleocytosis or failure to improve
  • Bacterial causes:
    • UTI (most common): E. coli, Group B Strep (GBS), Klebisella, Enterococcus, other Enterobacteraciae
    • Bacteremia: E. coli, GBS, Enterococcus, other Enterobacteraciae, rarely Listeria
    • Meningitis: GBS, E. coli, pneumococcus, meningococcus, rarely Listeria

Primary Regimens

  • For bacterial causes:
    • Age <29 days: (Ceftriaxone 75-100 mg/kg/day once daily in term infants without hyperbilirubinemia or Cefotaxime 200 mg/kg/d div q6h (where available outside the US) or Ampicillin 200 mg/kg/day div q6h) ± Gentamicin 5 mg/kg/day once daily
    • Age >29 days: Ceftriaxone 75-100 mg/kg/day once daily until cultures negative at 24-36 hours if low risk
    • Observation alone can be considered for low risk infants >29 days with normal UA, normal inflammatory markers +/- normal LP
  • If risk factors for HSV, initiate acyclovir 60 mg/kg/day div q 8h and send HSV studies. Discontinue if studies are negative
  • Above adapted from Pediatrics. 2021 Aug;148(2):e2021052228

Alternative Regimens

  • Ampicillin and Gentamicin. Once considered gold standard but may not be adequate for E. coli, Klebsiella or other Enterobacteraciae given emergence of resistance. Need susceptibility data.

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Consider observation without antibiotics for patients at low risk given that viral causes are the most common etiologies.
  • Using evidence-based algorithm, infants at low risk of SBI can be safely sent home when blood culture is negative at 24 hours (Pediatrics 130:e16, 2012).

Comments

  • Complex and evolving field. Not all diagnostic modalities are available in all settings
  • AAP Clinical Practice Guideline Pediatrics. 2021 Aug;148(2):e2021052228
  • Review:  Pediatrics. 2018 Dec;142(6);  Infect Dis Clin North Am 2015 Jul 16; Infect Dis Clin North Am 29:575, 2015.
  • Ceftriaxone is contraindicated in neonates if they require (or are expected to require) treatment with calcium-containing IV solutions, including continuous calcium-containing infusions such as total parenteral nutrition
