Meningitis, Bacterial, Preterm to Age < 1 month

Clinical Setting

  • Empiric therapy for meningitis in preterm and infants age < 1 mo
  • Presentation often subtle with nonspecific findings such as poor feeding or vomiting, lethargy, irritability
  • Fever or hypothermia in about 60%
  • Nuchal rigidity not usually present
  • Meningitis may be present up to 15% if infants with bacteremia

Etiologies

  • Group B Strep (Streptococcus agalactiae).
  • E. coli
  • Other Gram-negatives
  • S. pneumoniae, Enterococcus sp., other Gram-positives
  • Listeria sp.

Primary Regimens

  • Ampicillin 75-100 mg/kg IV q6h  + Cefotaxime (where available)[100-150 mg/kg/day div q8-12h (age 0-7 days) or 150-200 mg/kg/day div q6-8h (age 8-28 days)] + Gentamicin 2.5 mg/kg IV q8h or 5-7 mg/kg IV q24h
  • Ceftriaxone 100 mg/kg/day may be used in term infants without hyperbilirubinemia
  • Ceftazidime 100 mg/kg/day div q8h or Cefepime 100mg/kg/day div q8h may be used as alternatives to cefotaxime if ceftriaxone is contraindicated
  • Intraventricular therapy not recommended
  • Adjust therapy to specific organism once identified

Alternative Regimens

  • Ampicillin 75-100 mg/kg IV q6h + Ceftazidime 50 mg/kg IV q8h
  • Ampicillin 75-100 mg/kg IV q6h + Cefepime 50 mg/kg IV q8h
  • Ampicillin 75-100 mg/kg IV q6h + Ceftriaxone 50 mg/kg IV q12h (see Comments)
  • Ampicillin 75-100 mg/kg IV q6h + Gentamicin 2.5 mg/kg IV q8h or 5-7 mg/kg IV q24h
  • If there is a high risk of MRSA (e.g. presence of shunt or intraventricular reservoir), use Vancomycin + Cefotaxime. Alter the regimen after culture/sensitivity data are available (Lancet Infect Dis 10:32, 2010)

Comments

  • Primary and alternative regimens are active vs Group B strep, most coliforms, & Listeria. Some experts still prefer Ampicillin and Gentamicin for early onset disease (<7 days of life) especially in populations with higher rates of Group B strep and Listeria. However, with increased frequency of ampicillin resistant E. coli and intrinsically resistant Klebsiella this may not be optimal. In most populations, Listeria has become rare, but should be suspected when mother is ill
  • Citrobacter is a rare cause of neonatal meningitis but often leads to brain abscess. Imaging required. Outcomes are poor
  • Cefotaxime (where available) or alternative 3rd/4th generation cephalosporin should be included if E. coli or other gram negative organisms are suspected because of high rates of ampicillin resistance.
  • Ceftriaxone should be used with caution in neonates. It can displace bilirubin although the risk associated with this is unclear. Monitoring of bilirubin is important but may be used in infants with normal bilirubin. Cholestasis has been reported. Coadministration of ceftriaxone and IV solutions containing calcium can result in severe complications. Paediatr Drugs.2017 Feb;19(1):21-34
  • If premature infant with long nursery stay, Staph. aureus, enterococci, and resistant coliforms are potential pathogens.
