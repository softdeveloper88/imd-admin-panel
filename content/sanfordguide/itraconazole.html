Itraconazole
Tradename: Sporanox, Tolsura

Usage and Dosing

  • Itraconazole is used in the treatment of candidiasis, blastomycosis, histoplasmosis, and has in vitro activity against Aspergillus.
  • Formulations:
    • Itraconazole oral formulations are not interchangeable. Oral solution is preferred.
    • A new oral 65 mg capsule formulation (Tolsura) has improved bioavailability and reduced variability compared to the existing capsule formulation. It is indicated for blastomycosis, histoplasmosis, and aspergillosis, but not onychomycosis.
    • Other tablet and capsule formulations may be available outside the US.
    • The intravenous formulation is no longer available in the US.
  • Many authorities recommend measuring Itraconazole serum concentration after 2 weeks to ensure satisfactory absorption.
    • To obtain highest plasma concentration, Itraconazole capsules are given with food and acidic drinks (e.g., cola) while solution is taken in fasted state; under these conditions, the peak plasma concentration after a single 200 mg dose is approximately 0.3 μg/mL (capsules) and 0.54 μg/mL (solution).
    • Peak plasma concentrations after multiple doses of IV injection (200 mg IV q24h) compared to oral capsules (200 mg po q12h): 2.8 μg/mL (on day 7 of IV therapy) vs 2 μg/mL (on day 36 of oral therapy).
  • Itraconazole protein binding is over 99%, which explains its virtual absence of penetration into CSF (do not use to treat meningitis).
  • Black box warning: CHF, drug interactions.
  • Caution: numerous drug interactions. Itraconazole is a substrate of CYP3A4, and an inhibitor of CYP3A4 and P-glycoprotein. See chart below (not all-inclusive).

Adverse Effects

  • Most common Itraconazole adverse effects are dose-related nausea 10%, diarrhea 8%, vomiting 6%, and abdominal discomfort 5.7%. Allergic rash 8.6%, ↑ bilirubin 6%, edema 3.5%, and hepatitis 2.7% reported. ↑ doses may produce hypokalemia 8% and ↑ blood pressure 3.2%. Delirium and peripheral neuropathy reported.
  • Reported to produce impairment in cardiac function.
  • Severe liver failure requires transplant in patients receiving pulse treatment for onychomycosis: FDA reports 24 cases with 11 deaths out of 50 million people who received the drug prior to 2001.
  • Acquired apparent mineralocorticoid excess (e.g., hypokalemia, hypertension). Dose-dependent. Observed also with posaconazole but not fluconazole, voriconazole, or isavuconazole. Mechanism: inhibition of 11β-hydroxylase and 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 2 (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 2022;66(1):e0166821).
  • Other concern, as with Fluconazole and Ketoconazole, is drug-drug interactions. Some can be life-threatening.
