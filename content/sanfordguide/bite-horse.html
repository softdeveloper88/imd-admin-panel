Bite: Horse

Clinical Setting

  • Bite wound: horse. Pathogens may include normal oral flora of animal, organisms from recently consumed food remaining in mouth, or from skin of person bitten inoculated into wound.
  • Oral flora from horses include Actinobacillus spp., Pasteurella spp., and anaerobes among others.

Etiologies

  • Actinobacillus lignieresii, A. equili, A. suis
  • Pasteurella caballi, P. multocida
  • Neisseria spp.
  • Prevotella spp. and other anaerobes
  • Staphylococcus aureus and streptococci
  • Enterobacteriaceae
  • B. fragilis
  • Campylobacter ureolyticus
  • Yersinia spp.
  • Nipah virus /Hendra virus (Australia only)

Primary Regimens

  • Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg po bid

Alternative Regimens

  • Doxycycline 100 mg po bid

Comments

  • Horse bites may be associated with significant tissue injury. Wound irrigation and debridement useful (Plast Reconstruct Surg 125: 251e, 2010)
  • Administer tetanus and consider rabies prophylaxis as appropriate.
  • Bites caused by livestock, almost never require initiation of PEP.
    • Consultation with local public health is advised. U.S. State by State rabies points of contact  https://www.cdc.gov/rabies/resources/contacts.html 
  • Infections often polymicrobial; culturing wound can reveal target organisms on which to base definitive antimicrobial therapy if required.
  • Parenteral antibiotic therapy may be necessary for wounds that are already severely infected; consider blood cultures for severe wounds/admitted patients.
  • Case report of Streptococcus equi subsp zooepidemicus bacteremia,meningitis and brain abscess after horse bite (Lancet 376: 1194, 2010)
  • Case report of Staphylococcus hyicus subsp hyicus after donkey bite (Scan J Infect Dis 29: 95, 1997)
  • Microbiology of bite wound infections (Clin Microbiol Rev 24: 231, 2011)
