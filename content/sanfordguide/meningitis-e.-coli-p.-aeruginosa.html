Meningitis, Gram-negative Bacilli

Clinical Setting

  • Up to 1/3 of meningitis is due to gram-negative bacilli; risk factors include: head trauma, neurosurgery, presence of a CSF shunt or other neurosurgical device, and CSF leak
    • Mortality is 40-80 %
    • In immunocompromised patients, gram-negative bacteria can enter circulation via penetration by fecal contamination of occult Strongyloides stercoralis
  • Therapies suggested below assume a positive culture from CSF with report of in vitro susceptibility.
  • ID consultation is advised
  • Variety of factors influence choice of specific therapy: determination of in vitro susceptibility or resistance, degree of penetration of drug into the CSF (ideally want CSF levels 10x higher than the in vitro MIC), coexisting comorbidities, need for bactericidal activity due to absence of complement and opsonic activity in the CSF, and patient antibiotic allergies.

Etiologies

  • Escherichia coli
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Other coliforms and other gram-negative bacilli

Primary Regimens

  • Select regimen based on reported pathogen identification and in vitro susceptibility.
  • Initial adult regimen in patients with no evidence of significant antibiotic resistance:
    • (Ceftazidime 2 gm IV q8h or Cefepime 2 gm IV q8h)
      • Evidence that prolonged infusions, or continuous infusions, improve CSF antibiotic concentrations
        • Link to drug pages for ceftazidime and  cefepime for dosage details
      • Increasing use of adjunctive intraventricular (or intrathecal) aminoglycoside (most often Gentamicin). See Comments
    • Takes 2-4 days in adults and 3-8 days in neonates to "sterilize" the CSF.
  • Treatment duration is usually 21 days

Alternative Regimens

  • Options for infection with beta-lactam resistant bacteria, persistently positive cultures or antibiotic allergy.
    • TMP-SMX 5 mg/kg (TMP component) IV q6h
    • If resistance due to ESBL producing gram-negative organism:
      • Meropenem 2 gm IV q8h infused over 4 hrs (optimizes CSF penetration: Antimicrob Agts Chemother 2016; 60:6619; World Neurosurg 2017,98:525)
    • If isolate is resistant to all beta -lactam antibiotics:
      • Ciprofloxacin 400 mg IV q8–12h (q8h for Pseudomonas)
      • Moxifloxacin 400 mg IV q24h  (excellent CSF penetration but limited data on efficacy)
    • If patient has severe, IgE-mediated beta-lactam allergy:
      • Aztreonam 2 gm IV q6h

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Antimicrobial drug selection considerations:
    • Aztreonam:
      • Need to be sure of in vitro susceptibily as easily hydrolyzed by ESBL-producing bacteria.
      • Good CSF penetration.
    • TMP-SMX:
      • Penetrates the CSF and is active vs. many Salmonella, Enterobacter, Citrobacter and Klebsiella species.
      • Other troublesome less common pathogens are often susceptible: e.g., Acinetobacter calcoaceticus, Burkholderia cepacia, and Flavobacterium meningosepticum.
      • May have synergistic activity with gentamicin

Comments

  • Note:
    • Dexamethasone (steroid) therapy is not recommended.
    • Ceftolozane-tazobactam  not recommended due to low CSF ventricular drain concentrations after maximal IV dose of 3 gm IV q8h (Antimicrob Agt Chemother 2022.65: e01698-20).
    • Parenteral aminoglycoside (gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin) is not recommended.
  • CSF: Reculture CSF after 4-5 days of therapy; If culture is still positive, may need adjunctive intrathecal or intraventricular (preferred; requires placement of an Ommaya reservoir) antibiotic therapy:
    • Gentamicin into reservoir: 1-2 mg /day in children and 4-8 mg/day in adults
    • Monitor CSF gentamicin concentrations
    • If cultures remain positive, some have increased dose to 8-12 mg once daily
  • Although dexamethasone is recommended for pneumococcal and meningococcal meningitis, it is not recommended for meningitis due to gram-negative organisms as there are no studies to justify this approach.
  • Intrathecal/intraventricular therapy:
    • Used only for organisms resistant to aminoglycosides and sensitive only to polymyxins
    • Prefer Polymyxin B (Clin Infect DIs 59:88, 2014)
      • Complicated Colistin dosing: For rationale and dose calculation see Colistin.
    • Polymyxin B dosing:
      • Loading dose x one: 2.5 mg/kg IV over 2 hrs
      • Then, 12 hrs later, 1.5 mg/kg IV over 1 hr; repeat q12h
    • Ventriculitis is concomitant with meningitis in 70% of the patients; May make sense to place Omaya reservoir into lateral ventricle for access to both ventricles and subarachnoid spaces
    • Gentamicin intraventricular/intrathecal doses above.
      • Can use tobramycin; doses are the same as gentamicin.
      • If Amikacin is used, the daily dose is 30 mg
    • Colistin (Polymyxin E) and Polymyxin B
  • References:

    • For discussion of intraventricular therapy and doses, see  J Micro Immunol & Infection 47:204, 2014; Clin Micro & Infection 2016;22:66.
