Filariasis: Loa loa, Onchocerciasis

Clinical Setting

  • Loiasis, Loa loa or eyeworm disease. Conjunctival eyeworm migration is innocuous; transient migratory angioedema may occur. Subcutaneous migration causes localized subcutaneous nodules
    • Loiasis present only in West & Central Africa in a distribution that overlaps with that of onchocerciasis. Clinical reviews: BMC Infect Dis 20:63, 2020; Lancet Infect Dis 2024; 24: e165
  • Onchocerciasis, river blindness: significant onchodermatitis and inflammatory keratitis in Africans; temporary residents most commonly have transient onchodermatitis only.
    • Onchocerciasis present in sub-Saharan Africa, Yemen, and only 1 remaining focus on the Brazil/Venezuela border.
  • Important treatment note: Onchocerciasis and Loa loa are widely co-endemic.
    • Before treating Loa loa with DEC screen for onchocerciasis.
    • Before treating onchocerciasis with Ivermectin or Moxidectin, screen for Loa loa.
    • If coinfection, treat onchocerciasis first with Ivermectin or Moxidectin then DEC for Loa loa.
  • Moxidectin FDA approved in 2018 but not available for single patient treatment at present
    • Longer suppression of skin microfilaria compared to Ivermectin, so presumably less frequent treatment needed. Lancet 2018 Jan 17. pii: S0140-6736(17)32844-1.
    • Review of large clinical experience: Clin Infect Dis 2021 Aug 31;ciab751.

Etiologies

  • Loa loa
  • Onchocerca volvulus

Primary Regimens

  • Treatment depends on presence or absence of concomitant infection of Loa loa with onchocerciasis and/or lymphatic filariasis (W. bancrofti).
  • Monoinfection (in the absence of dual or triple infection):
    • Loiasis monoinfection
      • If < 2500 loa loa microfilaria/mL of blood:
        • Diethylcarbamazine (DEC): 9 mg/kg/day po in 3 divided doses x 21 days.
          • May start with 50, 150, then 300 mg in 3 divided doses on first 3 days if any microfilaremia.
          • Rule out onchocerciasis first, see comments.
          • Retreat with at least two additional 3 week courses according to clinical response; single course only curative in 50%.
        • CDC has strict criteria for DEC release under their IND.
      • If > 2500 loa loa microfilaria/ml: Refer to expert center for apheresis prior to DEC therapy;
        • Alternatively attempt to lower microfilaremia to <2,500/ml blood with Albendazole 200 mg bid X 3 weeks (repeat course may be necessary).
        • Alternatively, some experts start with 60 mg/day prednisone then very small doses of DEC increasing as tolerated.
      • Prophylaxis: Diethylcarbamazine (DEC) 300 mg po once/week for long-term residents of high-endemic areas; N Engl J Med 319:752, 1988.
    • Onchocerciasis, river blindness: monoinfection
      • Doxycycline 200 mg/day po x 6 weeks preceded by a single dose of ivermectin 1 week prior and then followed by single dose of [Ivermectin 150 μg/kg po or Moxidectin 8 mg po (age ≥12 years); repeat ivermectin or moxidectin every 3-6 months until asymptomatic; repeat annually after that for at least 3 years.
      • The safety of ivermectin in children <15kg has not been authorized by most national authorities.
      • WHO allows children who are at least 90 cm tall (equivalent to ~15 kg or ~2 yrs of age) to be treated with Ivermectin.
        • Systematic review (n=1,000 subjects) strongly suggest safety <15 kg (PLoS Negl Trop Dis 2021 Mar 17;15(3):e0009144)
        • Public health programs for mass drug administration for onchocerciasis do not use Ivermectin <5 yrs of age due to a relationship between microfilaria in skin and seizures. WHO guidance may change in near future.
      • DEC is contraindicated; can cause severe inflammatory reactions in the skin and eyes (Mazzotti reaction).
  • Dual infections:
    • Treat onchocerciasis first with Ivermectin or Moxidectin, wait 6-12 months and then treat the Loa loa with DEC.
    • In patients with >2,500 Loa loa microfilaria/ml of blood, treatment of onchocerciasis with Ivermectin or Moxidectin can result in facilitation of Loa loa entry into the CNS with severe encephalopathy and even death. See approach above for Loa loa monoinfection.
    • Either Loa loa or Onchocerciasis + Lymphatic filariasis: see Lymphatic filariasis.

Alternative Regimens

  • Loiasis monoinfection:
    • Albendazole 200 mg po bid x 21 days if still symptomatic after 2 courses of DEC .
    • May also be used if DEC unavailable but not well studied as primary therapy and much less effective as no activity against adult worms.
  • Onchocerciasis, river blindness:
    • No universally accepted alternative.
    • DEC is contraindicated

Comments

  • DEC including for prophylaxis of loiasis is available from the CDC Drug Service, see Antiparasitic Drugs, Sources.
  • Pediatric dosing for Doxycycline, Ivermectin and Albendazole same as adult dosing.
  • Suramin no longer to be considered for killing adult Onchocerca volvulus worms due to toxicity and success of combination therapy with Doxycycline and Ivermectin/Moxidectin
  • Ivermectin does not kill adults of Loa loa so should not be used.
  • DEC can accelerate the progression of blindness in patients with any onchocercal eye disease. DEC contraindicated in onchocerciasis.
  • Doxycycline has no effect against Loa loa; Ivermectin has no effect against adult worms
