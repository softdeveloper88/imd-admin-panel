Mastoiditis, Acute

Clinical Setting

  • Acute mastoiditis is usually a complication of acute otitis media, much more common in children than adults.
    • Antibiotics plus drainage of the middle ear are cornerstones of therapy and sufficient in most cases
  • Chronic otitis media may include cholesteatoma (keratoma) formation that predisposes to obstruction of ear drainage and predisposes to acute and/or chronic mastoiditis.
  • Look for suppurative complications: osteomyelitis, suppurative (septic) thrombophlebitis of the lateral venous sinus, epidural or subdural abscess, bacterial meningitis, contiguous brain abscess. 

Diagnosis

  • Suggestive clinical findings include tenderness, erythema and swelling over mastoid prominence, protrusion of the ear
  • Abnormalities (e.g., air cell opacification, bony destruction) on CT or MRI

Etiologies

  • If complication of 1st episode of acute otitis media:
    • Streptococcus pneumoniae (most common)
    • Streptococcus pyogenes
    • Hemophilus influenza
    • Less common, more likely to be seen in children treated with antibiotics prior to diagnosis of acute mastoiditis
      • Staphylococcus aureus, including MRSA
      • Fusobacterium necrophorum
      • Streptococcus intermedius
      • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • If secondary to chronic otitis media:
    • Staphylococcus aureus, including MRSA
    • Pseudomonas aeruginosa

Primary Regimens

  • ENT consultation strongly recommended to evaluate need for surgical debridement
  • Recommendations are for empiric therapy pending culture results
  • Obtain cultures of auditory canal or middle ear fluid (preferred)
  • 1st episode: medical management, uncomplicated disease (uncommon in adults, suspect complicated disease), no antibiotic treatment prior to diagnosis, pediatric dosing as follows:
    • Ampicillin-sulbactam  200 mg/kg/d IV in 4 divided doses, max dose 12 gm/day
    • Ceftriaxone 50-100 mg/kg IV in 2 divided doses, max dose 4 gm/day
  • Acute exacerbation of chronic otitis media/chronic mastoiditis, prior therapy before diagnosis of mastoiditis, complicated infection (e.g, subperiosteal abscess, osteomyelitis):
    • ENT surgical consultation/surgical debridement
    • Vancomycin 
      • Child: 60-80 mg/kg IV divided 2-4 times a day to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator); alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL
      • Adult: 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator); alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL
    • PLUS Cefepime
      • Child: 150 mg/kg/d IV in 3 divided doses, max dose 2 gm
      • Adult: 2 gm IV q8h
    • PLUS Metronidazole
      • Child: 22.5 mg/kg/d IV in 3 divided doses, max dose 750 mg
      • Adult: 750 mg IV q8h   

Alternative Regimens

  • 1st episode: medical management, uncomplicated disease 
    • Vancomycin as above OR Linezolid (Child: Age < 12 years, 30 mg/kg/d in 3 divided doses, max dose 600 mg per dose; Age > 12 years, 10 mg/kg q12h, max dose 600 mg per dose; Adult: 600 mg IV/po q12h)
    • PLUS Piperacillin-tazobactam
      • Child: 300 mg/kg/d of piperacillin component in 4 divided doses, max dose 16 gm/d
      • Adult: 4.5 gm IV q6h
  • Acute exacerbation of chronic otitis media/chronic mastoiditis, complicated infection (e.g, subperiosteal abscess, osteomyelitis), severe beta-lactam allergy
    • ENT surgical consultation/surgical debridement
    • Vancomycin or Linezolid as above 
      • PLUS Aztreonam (Child: 30 mg/kg IV q6h; Adult: 2 gm IV q6h)
      • PLUS Metronidazole (Child: 22.5 mg/kg/d IV in 3 divided doses, max dose 750 mg; Adult 750 mg IV q8h)    

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Duration of therapy
    • Guided by severity of disease, longer courses for more complicated disease
    • 7-10 days IV, then transition to oral therapy based on results of culture and susceptibility data to complete a 4-week course
    • Consider a longer course of therapy for more severe or complicated disease
  • De-escalate, modify regimen based on results of culture and susceptibility data
  • No need to include MRSA coverage if the PCR nasal swab is negative for MRSA

Comments

  • Unusual causes of mastoiditis, which is usually chronic include: Nocardia spp., Mycobacterium tuberculosis, Actinomyces.
