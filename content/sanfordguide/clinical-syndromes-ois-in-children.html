Clinical Syndromes, OIs in HIV+ Children

Introduction

  • Overview, prophylaxis and treatment recommendations in DHHS Guidelines for Opportunistic Infections in Children.
  • Adult OI guidelines are appropriate for adolescents who have sexual maturity rating (Tanner stage) 4 or 5
  • Many of these syndromes are rarely encountered in developed countries by may still be seen in children who present with advanced disease
  • Some OIs may be acquired from mother such as TB, Hepatitis C
  • Clinical syndromes and opportunistic infections (OIs) can differ in frequency and presentation in children with HIV.
  • In HIV-infected infants & children, disease progression is manifest by decrements in growth & delayed neurodevelopment as well as opportunistic infections as occur in adults (J Ped 128:58, 1996).
  • See disease specific pages for treatment of opportunistic infections
  • As in adults, beginning ART is critical part of treating all opportunistic infections and HIV related syndromes. For most OIs ART can be started as soon as patient tolerates. For cryptococcal meningitis, waiting 2 weeks may be safer
  • Vaccine preventable diseases may be more common. Prioritize appropriate vaccination

Syndromes and Opportunistic Infections

Central Nervous System

  • Encephalopathy. HIV encephalopathy is a syndrome that includes motor & cognitive dysfunction seen in patients with advanced HIV. ART has been shown to be beneficial in treating children with HIV encephalopathy.
    • Static course is common in infants and children, but not in adults. 25% children show cognitive & motor deficits. Most have head circumference in 10–25th percentile. Problems with verbal expression, attention deficits, hyperactivity. Mild ↑ reflexes in legs to spastic diplegia. IQ stable.
    • Plateau course is uncommon in infants and children. Infant's or child's gain of cognitive or motor skills plateaus. Motor deficits are common. IQ usually only 50–79.
    • Subacute progressive course is uncommon in infants and children, but common in adults as AIDS dementia. Gradual progressive decline in motor, language, adaptive function. Early, child is alert, wide-eyed, with a paucity of facial movements. Endstage: mute, dull-eyed, quadriparetic. CSF: mild pleocytosis, ↑ protein, may be + for HIV antibody & virus. CT: atrophy, progressive calcification in basal ganglia (most common in infants & young children).
  • Congenital CMV is more common in HIV-infected infants

Focal Brain Diseases

  • Toxoplasmosis is very rare in infants and children since it is most often due to reactivation. Common in adults.
  • Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML) (JC virus) is uncommon in infants & children since it is most often due to reactivation. Common in adults.

Endocrine Disorders

  • Failure to thrive & growth retardation is common in infants and children. 33/36 HIV+ children showed failure to thrive, not purely related to diarrhea & malnutrition. Known causes of growth failure are growth hormone deficiency, hypothyroidism, & glucocorticoid excess. 1/3 of HIV+ children have abnormal thyroid function (increased thyrotropin, increased TBG) which correlates with disease progression (J Ped 128:70, 1996).
  • Puberty may be delayed in perinatally-infected children.

Eye

  • Cytomegalovirus (CMV) retinitis is uncommon in infants and children. CMV chorioretinitis in 1.6% children vs. 10–20% in adults (Arch Ophthal 107:978, 1989). In children, it usually occurs with generalized CMV infection, viremia & multiple organ involvement. When present, ocular lesions are same as in adults.
  • Retinal depigmentation on zidovudine occurs in approximately 5% of infants and children on ART. Presents as asymptomatic peripheral retinal depigmentation (dosages >300 mg/M2/day). Does not present in adults.
  • HIV-associated "cotton wool" spots are rare in infants and children, but common in adults. Seen only in children >8–10 yrs, while seen in 60–70% of adults.

Gastrointestinal Tract

  • Kaposi's sarcoma in very rare in infants and children, although more prevalent in HIV-infected children in parts of Africa.
  • Dysphagia (Esophageal candidiasis) and odynophagia (CMV esophagitis) are uncommon in infants and children. When pain/difficulty swallowing occur, children more likely to refuse to eat. Both are common in adults.

Heart

  • Abnormal ECG changes (ventricular hypertrophy & non-specific ST-T changes) occur in 55–93% HIV+ children. 
  • Cardiomyopathy is common in infants and children, but uncommon in adults. (Indian J Pediatr. 2015 Mar;82(3):230-4) Left ventricular dysfunction 29–74% (most important cardiac change). 20% transient or chronic congestive failure.
    Unexpected cardiorespiratory arrests in 8/81 (JAMA 269:2869, 1993). Pericardial effusions & tamponade have been noted frequently in children (Ped Infect Dis J 15:819, 1996).

Hematologic

  • Hypergammaglobulinemia is common in infants and children, but uncommon in adults. By age 6 months, almost all untreated HIV+ children have increased gamma-globulins.
  • Protein S (coagulation inhibitor) is common in infants, children and adults. 19/26 children had ↓ levels, but risk of thrombosis low (Ped Infect Dis J 15:106, 1996). 

Hepatobiliary

  • Hepatobiliary complications are rare in children, but common in adults. Very few reports relating to children. Etiologies such as AIDS cholangiopathy, peliosis hepatis (bacillary angiomatosis) not reported. 2 cases of fatal hepatic necrosis associated with adenovirus reported (Rev Inf Dis 12:303, 1990).

Lung

  • M. tuberculosis is less common as presenting infection. Virtually all are primary infections. Clinical: fever, cough. X-ray: often focal infiltrates with hilar adenopathy, cavitation uncommon. TB meningitis and other disseminate MTb syndromes increased.
  • Lymphocytic interstitial pneumonitis (LIP) is common in infants and children, but very rare in adults.
    • LIP occurs in 40% of children with perinatally acquired HIV. HIV & EBV antigens have been demonstrated in lung tissue. Usually diagnosed in children >1 yr as compared with PJP which is most common in first year.
    • LIP has better prognosis than PJP. Median survival is ~5x shorter in children diagnosed with PJP than in children with LIP.
    • Clinical signs & symptoms: slowly progressive tachypnea, cough, wheezing, hypoxemia. Rales are infrequent. Clubbing of digits is characteristic. Generalized lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly & parotid swelling.
    • X-ray: diffuse reticulonodular infiltrates associated with hilar lymphadenopathy. Bacterial suprainfection is common.
    • Diagnosis by lung biopsy.
    • Treatment: steroids may be of some benefit.
  • Cryptococcosis is uncommon in children. Disseminated infection or localized process of the lungs. Intermittent fever is most common presenting manifestation. All pts have low CD4, history of previous OIs, & onset of cryptococcosis most commonly in 2nd decade of life (Ped Infect DIs J 15: 796, 1996).
  • Congestive heart failure is common in infants and children, but uncommon in adults.
  • Leiomyosarcoma is rare in infants and children, but increased in relative to HIV negative. It is very rare in adults. EBV demonstrated by PCR in tumors (N Engl J Med 332:12, 1995).

Renal

  • Nephropathy is common in untreated infants and children with advanced disesase. Nephropathy observed in 29% children with perinatal AIDS. In children may present with nephrotic syndrome with a course of 12–18 mos (N Engl J Med 321:625, 1989). Steroid therapy may be of value.

Sepsis

  • Sepsis is common in infants and children; uncommon in adults. 25% of symptomatic HIV+ children will have bacteremic episodes, most due to bacteremic pneumonia or bacteremia without a focus (Pediatric AIDS, Eds. P.A. Pizzo, C.M. Wilfert, Ch. 13, page 199, 1991).
  • Fungemia (nosocomially-acquired). Risk factors: central venous catheter (>90 days), prior antibiotic therapy (>3 different antibiotics, parenteral ↑ risk), parenteral hyperalimentation, hemodialysis, prolonged neutropenia, colonization by Candida species (Clin Infect DIs 23:515, 1996).
  • Disseminated Mycobacterium avium complex common in children with severe CD4 depletion

Skin

  • Impetigo is common in infants and children; uncommon in adults. Due to Staph. aureus or Group A strep. Clinical: areas of erythema with "honey crusting". May be widespread & evolve into "cellulitis". Increasing incidence of MRSA skin & soft tissue infections.

Other

  • Congenital syphilis in infants born to HIV infected persons and sexually acquired syphilis in adolescents are important infections
