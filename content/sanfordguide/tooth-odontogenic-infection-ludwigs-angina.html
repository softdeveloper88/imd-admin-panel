Tooth, Odontogenic Infection, Ludwig's Angina

Clinical Setting

  • Tooth infections result from dental caries (cavities) and periodontal disease (gingivitis and periodontitis). Collectively, as they arise from the teeth, such infections are referred to as odontogenic.
  • Periapical tooth infections can extend and cause a variety of infectious complications:
    • Ludwig's angina is a complication of an infected tooth and/or periodontal infection:
      • Rapidly progressive bilateral infection of the submandibular space leading to posterior displacement of the tongue with a choking (angina) sensation.
      • Usually occurs in adults with infection of the mandibular teeth.
      • Can occur after fracture of mandible or piercings of the frenulum or tongue.
    • Other complications include: odontogenic sinusitis, cervical necrotizing fasciitis, cavernous sinus thrombosis, brain abscess
    • Noma (also known as Cancrum oris) is a variant in the malnourished which manifests as severe necrotizing gingivitis
  • Most of the infections are polymicrobic; in general strict or facultative anaerobic bacteria predominate
  • Ludwig's Angina: Surgical drainage and removal of necrotic tissue are essential!
    • Risk of airway compromise is increased 10x in the absence of surgical drainage
    • Ref.: Med Princ Pract 2018;27:362

Etiologies

  • Polymicrobial:
    • Viridans group streptococci
    • Oral anaerobes:
      •  Peptoniphilus sp. (formerly Peptostreptococcus sp.)
      • Fusobacteria sp.
      • Prevotella  sp.
      • Actinomyces sp.
    • Streptococcus pyogenes
    • In immunocompromised patients, worry about Staph. aureus and aerobic gram-negative bacteria

Primary Regimens

  • Severe infection:
    • Protect the airway
    • Immunocompetent patient: Penicillin G 3 mU IV q6h + Metronidazole 500 mg IV q6h
      • If gram-positive cocci in clusters on gram stain, add Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL)
    • If empiric therapy in an immunocompromised patient: Vancomycin 1 gm IV q12h + Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h
  • Surgical debridement if abscess seen on CT scan or MRI

Alternative Regimens

  • Less severe infections:
    • Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg po bid
  • Severe infections immunocompetent or immunocompromised:
    • Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h
    • Meropenem 1 gm IV q8h
  • Penicillin allergy: Clindamycin 600 mg IV q6-8h

Comments

  • Complications: obstruction of the airway; spread to the mediastinum via the parapharyngeal space
