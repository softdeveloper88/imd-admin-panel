Conjunctivitis, Viral (Pink Eye)

Clinical Setting

  • Viral conjunctivitis or "pink eye" or "red eye".
  • Usually unilateral; highly contagious.

Etiologies

  • In adults: 80% caused by viruses; in children less than 20% caused  by viruses
    • Viral etiology suggested by: concomitant pharyngitis, preauricular lymphadenopathy and contact with a person with a red eye
  • Adenoviruses cause 65-90% of viral conjunctivitis and treatment is supportive                         
    • Types  8, 37, and  64 can cause severe epidemic keratoconjunctivitis. May involve the cornea and cause pain
  • Other potential viral etiologies:
    • Herpes simplex
    • Varicella zoster
    • Enterovirus
    • Measles, Mumps, Rubella, COVID-19, Ebola, and Mpox

Primary Regimens

  • No treatment.
  • If symptomatic, cold artificial tears may help.

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • Onset of ocular pain and photophobia in an adult suggests associated keratitis—but rare.
  • Reference on the red eye: N Engl J Med 2023; 389: 2363
  • Study in London showed steroid drops improved symptoms (Br J Ophth 95:1299, 2011); another study showed reduced duration of symptoms (9.4 days vs. 11.8 days; Current Eye Research 40:870, 2015) but steroids for the eye should only be prescribed by an ophthalmologist.
  • For review of eye infections, see New Engl J Med 2023; 389: 2363.
