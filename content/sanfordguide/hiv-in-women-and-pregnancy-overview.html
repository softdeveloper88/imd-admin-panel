HIV in Women and Pregnancy, Overview

Overview

  • Women represent half of persons with HIV/AIDS in the world. 
  • Heterosexual transmission is dominant mode of transmission worldwide. 
  • Transmission of HIV from men to women occurs more readily than from women to men. Risk factors for male-to-female transmission: genital ulcers, partner with advanced disease, other STDs, trauma.
  • Risk after several years of unprotected sex with same infected partner is 10–45%.
  • Despite these aspects, relatively less is written about women's issues. Women are generally under-represented in therapy trials, limiting ability to identify sex-specific issues.
  • Every year, approximately 5000 women with HIV give birth in the US
  • For patient assessment, see Initial Evaluation of the HIV-infected Adult. For treatment, see ART in Pregnancy. See also HIV in the Fetus and Newborn.

Clinical Manifestations

  • AIDS-defining diagnoses:
    • Disease progression similar in women and men (N Engl J Med 333:751, 1995).
    • Survival is related to access to care, which may be worse for women.
    • Viral load at high CD4 counts tends to be lower in women (Clin Infect Dis 35:313, 2002).
    • Gender difference narrows as CD4 drops.
  • Human papillomavirus (HPV):
    • HPV disease incidence increased. Cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN) more prevalent in multiple studies.  Prevalence increases with decreasing CD4 (Clin Infect Dis 38:737, 2004).
    • Aggressive course, with high rate of progression to cancer if immunosuppressed.
    • Pap smear recommended for HIV+ women. If initial Pap smear is negative, repeat in 6 mos. If both are negative,
      annual Pap smears adequate (CDC Guidelines). We recommend every 6 mos for those with CD4 <200.
      Colposcopy recommended for any suspicious lesions. There are no contraindications to standard treatment
      modalities for CIN. Risk of recurrence higher after treatment.
    • Although many women with HIV are already infected with some HPV genotypes, there may be benefit
      to use of HPV vaccine according to recommendations for HIV negative women.
  • Recurrent/refractory vaginal candidiasis:
    • May be early manifestation although poorly predictive of HIV infection (CD4 may be >500).
    • HIV diagnosis often missed because testing not offered.
  • Other conditions:
    • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) may be more severe, 7–17% require hospitalization; tubo-ovarian abscess more likely.
    • Menstrual disorders (41% HIV+ women had menstrual abnormalities vs. 24% in controls) include irregular
      periods, heavier or scantier periods, early menopausal symptoms, ↑ in premenstrual symptoms.
  • Adapted from Newman, MD, Global HIV/AIDS Medicine 2008.

Family Planning and Pregnancy

  • HIV testing is recommended for all women who are planning to become pregnant or who are pregnant
  • 85% of women living with HIV are in child-bearing years. Contraceptive and pre-conceptional planning are
    essential components of care. Hormonal contraceptives are generally safe in HIV+ women and HIV+ women can use all forms of hormonal contraception including implants and IUDs. (AIDS 29:2353 2015) However, only condoms protect against STIs and HIV transmission. Be aware of interactions between ARV's and hormonal contraception, most significant for ritonavir. Efavirenz may decrease the efficacy of contraceptive implants (e.g., etonogestrel/levonorgestrel)  For list of interactions see clinicalinfo.HIV.gov. 
  • For reviews on contraceptives for women living with HIV see Expert Opin Pharmacother. 2018 Jan;19(1):1-12. or AIDS. 2017 Apr 24;31(7):917-952
  • In ECHO trial (Lancet. 2019 Jul 27;394(10195):303) excellent contraceptive efficacy and similar rates of HIV acquisition for copper IUD,depot medroxyprogesterone acetate (DMPA-IM), and levonorgestrel implant suggesting all can be used. No differences were seen in disease progression by type of contraception. AIDS Res Hum Retroviruses. 2020 Aug;36(8):632

Pre-exposure Prophlyaxis (PreP)

  • PreP for HIV-negative women planning to become pregnant.
  • For those on daily oral PreP with TDF/FTC who are trying to become pregnant, DHHS guidelines recommend continuing daily PreP for 7-28 days after last HIV exposure
  • Efficacy studies for PreP with TAF/FTC for people with vaginal exposure have not been completed. DHHS does not recommend this regimen for PreP for women (or people with vaginas) including during pregnancy and postpartum
  • Limited data exist for injectable PreP (e.g. long acting carbotegravir) during pregnancy. Physicians are encouraged to enroll patients who become pregnant on long acting PreP in the Antiretroviral Pregnancy Registry 
  • See also PreP

Treatment Issues

  • Inadequate gender-specific data!
  • Theoretical issues
    • Baseline anemia (iron deficiency)
    • Low mean body weight, higher body fat, different hepatic metabolism compared to men
  • Menstrual dysfunction
    • Amenorrhea should be evaluated; start with pregnancy test
    • Premature menopause occurs frequently; consider short term hormone replacement therapy
  • Recommended treatment regimens currently identical for men & women
    • Emerging question regarding safety of Dolutegravir (and perhaps other InSTI agents) when used in first 6 - 8 weeks of pregnancy in low income setting.  Disproportionately higher rate of neural tube defects in infants born to women who took dolutegravir during 1st trimester. (N Engl J Med. 2019 Jul 22). Subsequent studies suggest rate of neural tube defects lower than original trial but higher than with other ART. For more information see section on counseling women trying to conceive and pregnant women in DHHS guidelines
    • Rates of some side effects different in women (increased rash and hepatitis on nevirapine with CD4 >250,
      increased GI side effects on Lopinavir/ritonavir).
    • Counsel about potential risk of Efavirenz and dolutegravir  in women who may become pregnant. Folic acid supplementation recommended
    • Lactic acidosis more common in women, associated with d4T & ddI.

HIV in Pregnancy

  • Pre-conception counseling
    • Regularly discuss reproductive plans with all persons of chid-bearing age
    • Provide education and support for women who are considering or planning to become pregnant.
    • This may influence decision on which regimen to start. Women ideally should achieve stable viral load suppression to <50 copies/mL before conceiving.
    • Recommend folate supplement or multivitamin. during preconception period and pregnancy
  • Counseling for HIV discordant couples wishing to conceive
    • Risk of transmission from a partner with sustained undetectable HIV viral load is extremely low
    • The use of PrEP by the HIV- partner can further decrease the risk and should be offered
    • For a complete discussion of options, see DHHS guidelines section on pre-conception counseling
  • Antepartum care
    • All sexually active women and all pregnant women should be offered HIV testing & counseling regardless of risk factors. Include HIV in routine pre-natal testing unless patient declines (opt-out testing).
    • Repeat HIV screening in the 3rd trimester for women with ongoing risk.
    • Expedited HIV testing during labor is recommended for women at high risk who were not retested during 3rd trimester, had a new STI, or were never tested
    • Quantitative measure of HIV RNA initially 1 month after starting ART, then every 3 months.
    • Obtain CD4 count and percent at outset & each trimester (some decrease in CD4 count in normal pregnancy).
    • Screening tests (HBsAg, RPR, chlamydia, gonorrhea) as in any pregnancy.
    • Discourage illicit alcohol use, drug use, smoking, unprotected sex with multiple partners.
    • Administer influenza, pneumococcal, Hep B, Hep A, SARS-Cov-2, and Tdap vaccines, as indicated (MMWR 60 (RR-2, RR41), 2011).
    • Assess need for OI prophylaxis.
  • For Treatment, see ART in Pregnancy.
