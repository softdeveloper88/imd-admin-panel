Surgical Prophylaxis, Ob-Gyn

Clinical Setting

  • Antimicrobial prophylaxis for obstetrical or gynecological (Ob-Gyn) surgery
    • Vaginal or abdominal hysterectomy
    • Cesarian section (C-section)
    • Surgical abortion
  • 2013 Clinical Practice Guidelines: Am J Health Syst Pharm 70:195, 2013.
  • See also, Prevention of Surgical Infections, Overview.

Etiologies

  • Normal vaginal flora: includes gram-positive, gram-negative aerobic and anaerobic bacteria
  • Not surprisingly, post-operative infections are often polymicrobial: E. coli or other aerobic gram-negative bacilli + enterococci + Bacteroides species + anaerobic streptococci

Primary Regimens

  • Vaginal or abdominal hysterectomy:
    • (Cefazolin 2 gm or Cefoxitin 2 gm or Cefotetan 2 gm) IV as a single dose within 60 minutes before incision OR
    • Ampicillin-sulbactam 3 gm IV as a single dose
  • Cesarean section:
    • Cefazolin 2 gm IV as a single dose if non-elective C-section, plus Azithromycin 500 mg IV as a single dose (see comment)
    • Post-operative prophylaxis in obese women: in addition to single dose Cefazolin, administer (Cephalexin 500 mg + Metronidazole 500 mg) q8h x 48 hrs following cesarean section (JAMA 2017; 318:1026; JAMA 2017;318:1012)
    • Second dose of Cefazolin 2 gm IV suggested in cases of excessive blood loss (>1500 ml) or extended duration of surgery (Am J Health Syst Pharm 70:195, 2013)
    • IF IgE-mediated beta-lactam allergy, see alternative regimen below
  • Surgical abortion (1st trimester):
    • Doxycycline 200 mg po, as 100 mg 1 hour before procedure

Alternative Regimens

  • For C-section:
    • If IgE -mediated beta-lactam allergy:
      • Clindamycin 900 mg IV + (Gentamicin or Tobramycin 5 mg/kg IV) as a single dose
      • If beta-lactam assoc. rash or uncertain allergy history, use Cefazolin prophylaxis as more efficacious
      • Ref: Obstet & Gyn 2018;132:948
  • For hysterectomy:
    • (Vancomycin + aminoglycoside)
    • (Vancomycin + Aztreonam)
    • (Clindamycin + Aztreonam)

Comments

  • Vaginal or abdominal hysterectomy and bacterial vaginosis: treat the bacterial vaginosis pre-op.
  • For hysterectomy, could use a fluoroquinolone for gram-negative coverage (with clindamycin or vancomycin) if local antibiograms suggest susceptibility of target pathogens.
  • Cesarean section for premature rupture of membranes or active labor:
    • Prophylaxis decreases risk of endometritis/wound infection. Traditional approach has been to administer antibiotics after the cord is clamped to avoid exposing infant to antibiotic. However, recent studies suggest that administering antibiotics before the skin incision results in fewer surgical site infections  (Am J Obstet Gynecol 199:301.e1, 2008; Am J Obstet Gynecol 199:310.e1, 2008) and endometritis (Am J Obstet Gynecol 196:455.e1, 2007).
    • In prospective randomized placebo controlled trial of non-elective C-sections, the addition of a single dose of azithromycin IV to cefazolin prophylaxis significantly reduced  the rates of endometritis and wound infection (N Engl J Med 2016;375:1231; New Engl J Med 2016;375:1284)
    • Questions have been raised about long-term impact of antibiotics on neonatal flora based on animal studies (Nature 488:621, 2012).
  • Surgical abortion:
    • Meta-analysis showed benefit of antibiotic prophylaxis in all risk groups, Obstet Gynecol 87:884, 1996.
