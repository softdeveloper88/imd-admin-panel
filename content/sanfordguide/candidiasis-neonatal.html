Candidiasis, Neonatal

Clinical Setting

  • Positive blood cultures, meningitis, urinary tract infection.
  • Clinical presentation of neonatal candidiasis may be subtle including respiratory deterioration, feeding intolerance, abdominal distension, temperature instability and hypotension.
  • Involvement of the CNS and eyes is more common in neonates with candidemia than in older patients
  • Risk factors include:
    • Extreme prematurity, low birth weight
    • Broad-spectrum antimicrobial
    • Colonization with Candida sp.
    • Central venous catheter
    • Mechanical ventilation
    • Gastrointestinal disease

Etiologies

  • Candida albicans
  • C. parapsilosis
  • Other non-albicans Candida sp.

Primary Regimens

  • Amphotericin B 1 mg/kg/day x 3 weeks
  • Fluconazole 12 mg/kg/day x 3 weeks

Alternative Regimens

  • Lipid-based Amphotericin B 3-5 mg/kg/day x 3 weeks
  • Micafungin 10 mg/kg/day x 3 weeks

Comments

  • Routine Fluconazole prophylaxis (3-6 mg/kg twice weekly x 4-6 weeks) recommended for premature and low birth weight infants (<1500 gm) in nurseries with high rates (incidence > 5-10%) of invasive candidiasis.
  • Lumbar puncture should be performed to rule out CNS disease; dilated retinal examination, and intravascular catheter removal are strongly recommended.
  • Lipid-based Amphotericin B should be used only if there is no renal involvement.
  • Echinocandins (Caspofungin, Micafungin, Anidulafungin) are considered 3rd line therapy in guidelines but appear non-inferior in recent trials (Pediatr Infect Dis J 38:42,2019). Higher doses of micafungin (e.g. 10-25 mg/kg/day may be needed to achieve adequate concentrations in brain tissue when meningoencephalitis cannot be reliably excluded (Clin Infect Dis 73:3225, 2021)
  • References: Clin Infect Dis 62:409, 2016 and Curr Opin Pediatr 26:151, 2014.
