HIV/AIDS, Anemia

Introduction

  • Causes of anemia in HIV-infected patients:
    • The renal source of erythropoietin is lost in renal failure patients with resultant chronic and often severe anemia.
    • Bone marrow production of erythrocytes is also impaired by: 
      • Drugs: e.g.,
        • Chemotherapy, anti-rejection drugs, and selected antivirals (e.g.  Ganciclovir)
        • Drugs used to treat other opportunistic infections: e.g., TMP/SMX, Flucytosine, Amphotericin B, and others).
      • Infections (e.g. disseminated viral, fungal, and mycobacterial infections)
      • Malignancy, especially if bone marrow involvement
      • Iron or B12 deficiency
    • Treatment:
      • First treat any reversible causes of anemia with standard methods: e.g., Iron supplements
      • Optimize antiretroviral therapy
      • IF possible discontinue potential drug(s) that may be contributing
      • If still anemic, consider starting an erythropoiesis-stimulating agent:
        • Exogenous erythropoietin can assist in repair of erythropoiesis. The table below provides dosing information for Erythroietin, Darbepoetin and Peginesatide.
  • Bone marrow production of polymorphonuclear leukocytes (granulocytes) is impaired by a wide variety of drugs, including antivirals, infections, and malignancies.
    • Selected common causes of neutropenia in HIV-infected patients:
      • Drugs: Ganciclovir/Valganciclovir, Foscarnet, TMP/SMX, Pyrimethamine, Acyclovir, Amphotericin B, 5-flucytosinie, Interferon alfa
      • Infections:  HIV, CMV, EBV, Disseminated mycobacterial infection, disseminated fungal infections
      • Malignancy: Lymphoma, Other malignancies with metastatic involvement of the bone marrow
    • Granulocyte colony stimulating factors (CSF) can help speed marrow recovery. The table provides dosing for Granulocyte CSF and Granulocyte-Monocyte CSF.

  • Need to confirm adequate iron stores prior to initiation of Rx (Fe / TIBC or Ferritin)
  • Erythropoietin is now FDA approved for treatment of anemia in HIV. Recall FDA advisory warning: increased risk of death, thromboembolic, and cardiovascular events with overuse;
    • Hence, monitor red blood cell levels (hemoglobin) and adjust dose to maintain  hemoglobin level of less than or equal to 11 gm/dL.
  • In treatment of chronic HCV, antibody to erythropoietin can develop and lead to red cell aplasia (Am J Gastroenterol 100:1415, 2005).
  • Erythropoietin Dose: 40,000 units sc once weekly equals efficacy of 3x/wk therapy; can increase to 60,000 units sc once weekly if response inadequate (AIDS Res Human Retroviruses 20:1037, 2004)
  • Darbepoetin(Long acting erythropoietin) Dose: 0.45 μg/kg IV or sc once weekly.
  • Peginesatide (Longest acting erythropoietin) Dose: 0.04 mg/kg IV or sc once per month.
  • Meta-analysis of use in dialysis pts: increased risk of stroke, increased BP, vascular access thrombosis (Ann Intern Med 153:23, 2010).
  • Granulocyte-CSF or G-CSF: Short acting 
  •    Filgrastim (Neupogen);lenograstim (Granocyte)
    • Biosimilars: Filgrastim-sndz (Zarxio) & Tbofilgastim (Tevagrastim, Granix)
  • Pegfilgrastim (Neulasta): Long-lasting pegylated form of G-CSF
  • Standard dose 5 μg/kg/day sc; lower doses may work in HIV patients, i.e., 1 μg/kg/day sc until ANC >1000 cells/dL, then 1–2x/wk. No effect on HIV replication. Long acting Pegylated G-CSF—pegfilgrastim: 6 mg sc once per chemotherapy cycle.
  • Granulocyte-monocyte-CSF or GMCSF, Sargramostim (Leukine)
  • Dose 5 μg/kg/day sc leads to increased PMNs. Trend toward increased CD4 count and slightly lower VL. Adverse effects: fever, myalgia, fatigue, malaise, headache, bone pain.
