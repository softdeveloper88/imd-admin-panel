Syphilis, Overview

Overview

  • Treponema pallidum is spirochete bacterium that cannot be cultivated in clinical laboratories.
  • There are 4 subspecies that are indistinguishable serologically (i.e. cross-reacting serologies) and morphologically.
    • Treponema pallidum pallidum, causes syphilis, a sexually transmitted infection seen world-wide that can also be acquired congenitally. Lancet 2017 389:1550 .
    • T. p. endemicum, causes bejel or endemic syphilis (Mediterranean and Africa)
    • T. p. carateum, causes pinta (Central and South America)
    • T. p. pertenue, causes yaws (humid tropical regions in South America, Africa, Asia and Oceania)
  • Reviews: 2021 CDC STD Guidelines MMWR 2021, 70 (RR-4):1; European 2020 Syphilis Guidelines and N Engl J Med. 2020;382:845
  • In most clinical settings, diagnostic screening depends upon using 2 serological tests, a nontreponemal and treponemal. Both are needed to minimize false positives and false negatives. 
  • Screening recommended every 6-12 months for MSM and/or individuals with high-risk sexual behavior (JAMA 2022;328:1243 and editorial JAMA 2022;328:1209).
    • Post-exposure prophylaxis: Open label randomized study in men that have sex with men (Lancet Infect Dis 2018;18:308).
      • Doxycycline 200 mg po x 1 dose within 72 hrs of exposure. 73% decrease in development of syphilis
  • Updated evidence and systematic review: higher rates of syphilis detection with screening every 3 months vs 12 months for MSM or patients who are HIV positive (JAMA 315:2321, 2016).
  • Penicillin is the mainstay of treatment. Penicillin Desensitization see Penicillin Allergy, Overview and Oral (Pen VK), Parenteral (Pen G).
  • Recent data suggesting that 3rd gen cephalosporins - Ceftriaxone (Lancet Inf Dis 21:1441, 2021) or Cefixime (Clin Inf Dis 73:907, 2021) - may be alternatives for CNS or early syphilis.
  • In some countries, benzathine Penicillin G is not available and shortages are increasingly common.
    • Consider high dose Amoxicillin 3 gm/day with probenecid (750 mg/day) po given tid in divided doses x 2 weeks (early syphilis) x 4 weeks (late latent or unknown duration) based on Japanese experience in HIV-infected individuals (efficacy 95.5%, 273/286 patients; Clin Infect Dis 61:177, 2015).
    • Another alternative is Amoxicillin 1.5 g/day is better tolerated than above Amoxicillin/Probenicid combination with similar efficacy (94.9%) Sex Transm Infect 98:173 2022
  • New "reverse sequence" paradigm for the serodiagnosis of syphilis: start with treponemal antibody rather than the VDRL (or RPR) (Clin Infect Dis 58:1116, 2014).

Diagnosis

  • Darkfield examinations and molecular tests (NAAT) for detecting T. pallidum directly from lesion exudate or tissue are the definitive methods for diagnosing early syphilis and congenital syphilis, but are not available in most clinical settings.
  • Presumptive diagnosis of syphilis requires use of two laboratory serologic tests: nontreptonemal (Venereal Disease Research Laboratory [VDRL] or rapid plasma reagin [RPR] test) and a treponemal test.
  • Treponemal test (T. pallidum passive particle agglutination [TP-PA] assay, various EIAs, chemiluminescence immunoassays [CIAs] and immunoblots, or rapid treponemal assays) usually positive for life (but 15-20% turn negative). Cannot be used to follow treatment. Occasional false positives.
  • Non treptonemal titers usually decline with treatment (expect at least four-fold), and over time, but can be positive in other conditions (false positives due to older age, other infections, autoimmune disease). 
  • Traditional: Start with RPR or VDRL and if positive confirm with treptonemal test. 
  • "Reverse sequence": Start with Treponemal EIA and if positive perform the RPR (N Engl J Med. 2020;382:845)
  • If serologies discordant: verify with specific (confirmatory) treponemal tests (use a different test than the first):  TP-PA, MHA-TP (Note: Due to lower sensitivity and specificity, FTA-ABS is NOT recommended Clin Infect Dis 2019;68:913). See flow chart in N Engl J Med. 2020;382:845
  • Review of lab testing: CDC Laboratory Recommendations for Syphilis Testing, United States, 2024 (MMWR 2024, 73 (RR-1):1-32)
    • Review of evidence for CDC guidelines: Clin Infect Dis 74:S127 2022
    • By adding a transcription-mediated amplification test to routine diagnostics, an additional 7% of syphilis cases were identified (Clin Infect Dis 2023; 77:1717).
