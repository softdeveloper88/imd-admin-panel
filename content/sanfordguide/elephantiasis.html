Filariasis, Lymphatic: Elephantiasis

Clinical Setting

  • Most of those infected are asymptomatic microfilaria carriers.
  • Transient lymphangitis, lymphedema, elephantiasis, can affect any limb or the breasts and can induce hydrocele in males.
  • Brugia malayi usually below the knee only, but can cause cold abscesses in affected lymph nodes
  • Tropical pulmonary eosinophilia with nocturnal asthma and bilateral interstitial infiltrates
  • Can be confused with podoconiosis (endemic nonfilarial elephantiasis), see Comment
  • Need to understand that individual drugs may act on only one life-cycle stage and not others
  • Need to determine if there is evidence of co-infection with either Loa loa or Onchocerca volvulus (river blindness).
  • Transmitted by different arthropod vectors in different parts of the world

Diagnosis

  • Stained blood film to detect microfilariae (take blood at night),
    • circulating antigen assay (not widely available)
  • Many serological assays detect only anti-filarial antibodies and do not differentiate between the 8 filarial species of humans.
  • Serology does not differentiate current vs past infection.

Etiologies

  • Wuchereria bancrofti
  • Brugia malayi
  • B. timori

Primary Regimens

  • Mono-infection including asymptomatic microfilaremia: no evidence of onchocerciasis or Loa loa.
    • Diethylcarbamazine (DEC): 6 mg/kg single-dose + Doxycycline 200 mg/day po x 6 weeks.
      • Treat adults and children >18 months of age with "tropical pulmonary eosinophilia" with DEC 6 mg/kg day for 21 days.
  • Dual infection with Onchocerciasis (river blindness)
    • DEC may kill onchocerciasis microfilaria in the skin and/or eye with severe inflammatory consequences.
    • First treat the onchocerciasis with Ivermectin 150 µg/kg po or Moxidectin 8 mg po as single dose; wait one month and then give DEC for lymphatic filariasis
      • Possible alternative to Ivermectin or Moxidectin in this situation: Doxycycline 200 mg po once daily x 4-6 weeks; then start DEC.
  • Dual infection with Loa loa
    • DEC drug of choice for both lymphatic filariasis and Loa loa; however, severe encephalopathy can occur if Loa loa microfilaria are present in the blood in concentrations of > 2,500 microfilaria/ml.
    • If < 2,500 Loa loa microfilaria/mL, DEC 9 mg/kg/day po in 3 divided doses x 21 days. 
      • May start with 50, 150, then 300 mg in 3 divided doses on first 3 days if any loa loa microfilaremia.
    • If > 2,500 Loa loa microfilaria/mL, refer to expert center for possible pre-treatment apheresis.
      • Or give Ivermectin 150 µg/kg po once followed by single dose of DEC 6 mg/kg
        • Alternatively, some experts start with 60 mg/day prednisone then very small doses of DEC, increasing as tolerated.
    • If > 20,000 Loa loa microfilaria/mL use Doxycycline alone to treat lymphatic filariasis infection.

Alternative Regimens

  • If DEC is not available or contraindicated, Doxycycline (200 mg/day for four to six weeks) can be used.
  • Diethylcarbamazine (DEC): 6 mg/kg single dose + Albendazole 400 mg single dose + Doxycycline 200 mg/day po x 6 weeks
  • Mass drug administration for population transmission control in endemic areas, (in the absence of onchocerciasis or loiasis), a three-drug combination regimen consisting of Ivermectin, Diethylcarbamazine, and Albendazole (single dose) or a two-drug combination regimen of Diethylcarbamazine plus Albendazole or Albendazole plus Ivermectin (administered once yearly for three years) should be implemented. (N Engl J Med 379:1801, 2018).
    • The safety of Ivermectin in children <15kg has not been authorized by most national authorities.
    • WHO allows children who are at least 90 cm tall (equivalent to ~15 kg or ~2 yrs of age) to be treated with Ivermectin.
      • Systematic review (n=1,000 subjects) strongly suggest safety <15 kg (PLoS Negl Trop Dis 2021 Mar 17;15(3):e0009144)

Comments

  • Refer patients to a certified lymphedema therapist if clinically indicated, in addition to anti parasitic therapy.
  • Six weeks of Doxycycline also improves mild/moderate lymphedema independent of active parasitic infection, Clin Infect Dis 55:621, 2012.
  • Ivermectin kills only the microfilariae, but not the adult worm; the adult worm is responsible for the pathology of lymphedema and hydrocele.
  • Doxycycline effect is on embryo development in adult worms with eventual adult worm death
  • DEC can cause irreversible eye damage if concomitant onchocerciasis in co-endemic areas of Africa; contraindicated in these cases.
  • DEC is available from the CDC Drug Service for proven infection.  CDC supplies only a single 6 mg/kg dose per patient.
  • Surgery for genital manifestations controversial World J Surg 39:2885, 2015
  • Moxidectin with a long half-life and already approved in onchocerciasis in advanced development for combination use with DEC, Albendazole, and/or Ivermectin.  PLoS Negl Trop Dis. 2023 Sep 18;17(9):e0011633. 
  • Podoconiosis (endemic nonfilarial elephantiasis): obstruction of lymphatics by fibrosis induced by minerals found in volcanic red clay. Same geographical distribution as filarial elephantiasis, N Engl J Med 366:1169, 2012; N Engl J Med 366:1200, 2012.
