Gastroenteritis, Campylobacter jejuni

Clinical Setting

  • Foodborne pathogen, common cause of sporadic gastroenteritis (Crit Rev Microbiol 39:57, 2013).
  • Poultry is the primary reservoir (Clin Microbiol Infect 2016 ; 22:103).
  • History of fever in majority of patients.
  • Often self-limited

Etiologies

  • Campylobacter jejuni

Primary Regimens

  • Azithromycin 500 mg po q24h x 3 days

Alternative Regimens

  • Erythromycin stearate 500 mg po qid x 5 days or Ciprofloxacin 500 mg po bid (Note: Ciprofloxacin resistance is increasing and may exceed 50% in some countries).

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Self-limited diarrhea in normal host and specific antimicrobial therapy not indicated except for severe infection.
  • Antimicrobial therapy recommended for immunocompromised patients, patients with severe or persistent symptoms or with extraintestinal infection.

Comments

  • Molecular-based detection methods suggest a significant proportion of culture-negative gastroenteritis is caused by Campylobacter spp. (Epidemiol Infect 140:684, 2012).
  • Association with Guillain-Barré syndrome (Mayo Clin Proc 2017; 92:467), other CNS syndromes (Neuroradiol J 2017; 30:65).
    • Evidence of preceeding Campylobacter infection in approximately 20-30% of patients with Guillain-Barré syndrome.
    • Risk is low (approximately 1/1000).
    • The organism may not be recoverable by stool culture at onset of Guillain-Barré syndrome.
  • Reactive arthritis may follow infection.
  • See also Traveler’s diarrhea.
  • Reference: Clin Microbiol Rev 28:687, 2015.
