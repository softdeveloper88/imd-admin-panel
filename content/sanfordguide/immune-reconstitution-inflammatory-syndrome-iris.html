Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome, IRIS

Overview

  • The use of antiretroviral therapy (ART) has led to dramatic declines in AIDS-associated opportunistic infections (JAMA 296:292, 2006; Clin Infect Dis 50:1, 2010) and other infections such as invasive pneumococcal disease (J Infect Dis 191:2038, 2005). As a result, there has been a significant reduction in AIDS-related mortality  with the use of ART (Ann Intern Med 135:17, 2001; Clin Infect Dis 44:599, 2007).
  • Nevertheless, reconstitution of immunity after initiation of ART can result in an inflammatory reaction in those with underlying infections or malignancies, resulting in the appearance or exacerbation of clinical symptoms from the underlying process. The term applied to such reactions is Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome (IRIS).

IRIS–In General

  • IRIS occurs in up to 25% of patients with an underlying opportunistic infection after initiation of ART (J Antimicrob Chemother 57:167, 2006).
  • Incidence and Severity. The incidence and severity of IRIS when ART is initiated after an AIDS-defining opportunistic infection (OI) can vary considerably with the OI or malignancy. Cumulative 1 year incidence of IRIS was 29% for Kaposi's sarcoma (KS), 16% for tuberculosis, 14% for Cryptococcus, 10% for MAI complex and 4% for Pneumocystis. Morbidity and mortality was highest for visceral KS (Clin Infect Dis 54:424, 2012).

Manifestations

  • General. The most frequent manifestations are dermatolological, particularly involving genital herpes and warts (Clin Infect Dis 42:418, 2006) or systemic reactions in patients with mycobacterial disease (Lancet ID 5:361, 2005). Criteria for diagnosis of IRIS are not absolute, but major criteria are discussed in CID 49:1424, 2009.
  • Link to Effect of Antiretroviral Therapy (ART) on Selected Opportunistic Infections (OIs).
  • IRIS in Opportunistic Infections: For detailed information on the risks of IRIS related to opportunistic infections after initiation of anti-retroviral therapy and advice regarding management see guidelines at clinicalinfo.HIV.gov.
    • Disseminated MAC. ART may unmask MAC infection or may cause paradoxical worsening of known disease. Massive mesenteric adenopathy with severe abdominal pain and thoracic adenopathy associated with pulmonary infiltrates and endobronchial proliferative lesions have also been described (J Infect Dis 179:329, 1999; J Infect Dis 180:76, 1999; AIDS 13:177, 1999). These patients are often systemically ill with fever, leukocytosis and malaise sufficient to require hospital admission (Lancet 351:252, 1998). We have seen one patient with disseminated MAC disease who developed a symptomatic brain abscess due to MAC after initiating ART. The resultant brisk granulomatous response and macrophage activation may lead to increased levels of 1,25 hydroxy vitamin D & hypercalcemia. One of the authors has observed 5 cases of hypercalcemia in this setting.
    • M. tuberculosis. IRIS is also a well-known complication of the initiation of antiretroviral therapy in patients infected with M. tuberculosis where it may be associated with a paradoxical worsening of treated TB (hectic fevers, lymphadenopathy, worsening chest film) or unmasking of previously undetected infection (J Infect Dis 196:S63, 2007). Corticosteroid therapy may be necessary in severe cases (Clin Infect Dis 48:101, 2009).
    • CMV. The development of symptomatic cytomegalovirus retinitis shortly after initiation of ART is likely a manifestation of the same phenomenon. Retinal injury from CMV IRIS may be permanent. When clinically feasible, delaying ART until 2 weeks of CMV therapy may decrease likelihood of IRIS effects (see DHHS website above).
    • Hepatitis. Flares in hepatitis in patients chronically infected with hepatitis B & C viruses are likely also the result of improvement in immune status (Lancet 349:996, 1997). Note that this syndrome must be distinguished from the mild hepatotoxicity associated with drugs used to treat ART.
    • PML. Initiation of ART may worsen PML or may unmask undetected infection. Corticosteroids have been used in some cases.
    • PJP. Symptoms of pneumocystis infection and radiographic findings may paradoxically worsen after initiation of ART.
    • Reference Ann Intern Med 133:447, 2000.
  • Neoplasms.
    • Exacerbation or initial manifestation of Kaposi sarcoma (HHV-8) is one of the most common IRIS syndromes encountered. Restoring immune competence by ART is useful, but directed chemotherapy may be necessary.
    • In a CFAR cohort of persons with HIV infection, 12% of lymphomas were judged to have been unmasked after initiation of ART (Clin Infect Dis 59:279, 2014).
  • Sarcoid or Autoimmune Disease. IRIS may also be seen in patients with underlying sarcoid or autoimmune disease, both of which appear to have immunopathology different from that of "classic" IRIS (Clin Infect DIs 48:101, 2009).

Clinical Course & Therapy

  • The symptoms of the immune reconstitution syndromes usually subside spontaneously with continued therapy for the underlying disease or with the use of nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory agents. Occasionally reactions may be severe enough to be life-threatening and may require corticosteroid therapy (Clin Infect Dis 38:1159, 2004). See comments under specific opportunistic infection (OI) in guidelines at clinicalinfo.HIV.gov.
