Peritonitis, Primary: Spontaneous, Bacterial

Clinical Setting

  • Primary spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP): patient with cirrhosis, ascites, fever, and abdominal tenderness
  • Microbial etiology:
    • Reports of the microbial etiology demonstrate a trend toward an increasing frequency of gram-positive bacteria and a concomitant increase in the resistance of isolated gram-negative bacilli
    •  Microorganisms isolated from ascitic fluid in 1333 patients  studied  between 1994 and 2012:
      • Gram-negative bacilli 55% (E.coli  33%, Other Enterobacteriaceae 11%, P. aeruginosa only 1%)
      • Gram-positive cocci 43% (Streptococcus sp. 15%, S. pneumoniae 3%, Staphylococcus sp 13%  but S. aureus only 5%, Enterococcus sp. 9%
      • Other, including anaerobic bacteria, fungi and others 2%
      • Source: Aliment Pharmacol Ther 2015; 41:1116
  • Diagnosis: Requires prompt paracentesis with fluid sent for:
    • WBC and differential. By definition, need PMN (neutrophil) absolute count of ≥ 250 cells/uL for a diagnosis of SBP
    • For culture and sensitivity, collect 20 mL of ascitic fluid and, with sterile technique, inoculate a minimum of two blood culture bottles (one aerobic and one anaerobic) with 10 mL of the ascitic fluid. Some suggest inoculation of two aerobic and two anaerobic bottles as the density of bacteria is low in SBP (Aliment Pharmacol Ther 2015;41:1116).
      • Other recommended ascitic fluid studies : Albumin, total protein, glucose, LDH, amylase, and bilirubin
  • See Comments below for risk factors

Primary Regimens

  • Treatment for community-acquired with low risk of MDR GNBs or Enterococci:
    • Cefotaxime 2 gm IV q8h (q4h, if life-threatening infection)(where available)
    • Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h
    • Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q24h
    • Ertapenem 1 gm IV q24h
    • If beta-lactam allergy, Ciprofloxacin 400 mg IV q12h
    • Add Albumin IV initially and on day 3 of therapy (see Alternative Regimens for dose)
    • Average treatment duration is 5 days but varies with severity of the infection
  • Prophylaxis (Primary Prevention) in patients with cirrhosis and UGI hemorrhage: See Cirrhosis/Variceal Bleeding page.
  • Prophylaxis (Secondary Prevention) in patients with chronic ascites until transplantation or liver function improves to a compensated state with resolution of ascites (Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews 2020, Issue 1. Art No.: CD013125):
    • Evaluated results of 29 randomized clinical trials which utilized 9 antibiotic regimens. There was no evidence of differences between any of the antibiotics and no intervention in terms of  the proportion of patients  who developed SBP; There was no difference between any of the antibiotics and no antibiotics in terms of patients with any adverse events. In short, with very low-certainty evidence, there is uncertainty as to whether antibiotic prophylaxis is beneficial.
    • NOTE: Cochrane study did not include trials in patients with possible transient bacteremia from breach in mucosa of GI tract :e.g., ruptured varices, ulcer disease
    • Nonetheless, commonly used prophylactic regimens include:
      • Norfloxacin 400 mg po once daily
      • Ciprofloxacin 500 mg po once daily

Alternative Regimens

  • Focus on nosocomial acquisition. Nosocomial with associated high risk of infection due to MDR gram-negative bacteria and/or Vancomycin resistant enterococci. Suggested regimen based on prospective randomized trial. Control arm was Ceftazidime wherein the failure rate was very high due to lack of efficacy vs. resistant bacteria (Hepatology 63:1299, 2016)
    • Meropenem 1 gm IV q8h plus
    • Daptomycin 6 mg/kg IV q24
      • Higher doses of Daptomycin may be considered if VRE (eg, 8-12 mg/kg q24h)
  • IV Albumin: 1.5 gm/kg at diagnosis and 1 gm/kg on day 3 to decrease frequency of renal impairment (Clin Gastroenterol Hepatol 11:123, 2013). Of interest, did not seem to benefit treatment of other sites of infection in cirrhotics (J Hepatol 62:822, 2015)

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Duration of therapy for primary SBT is unclear. Treat at least 5 days and perhaps longer if documented bacteremia or slow clinical response. Alternative is to trend serum procalcitonin level. Fall in procalcitonin may be slowed if there is associated hepatorenal syndrome slowing the renal clearance of procalcitonin.

Comments

  • In randomized controlled trial, there was no difference in SBP recurrence rates between Norfloxacin 400 mg once daily or Ciprofloxacin 750 mg po once a week: Am J Gastroenterol 2018; 113:1167.
  • Guidance from European Association for the Study of the Liver: J Hepatology 60:1310, 2014
  • Diagnosis of SBP: 30–40% of patients have negative cultures of blood and ascitic fluid with standard cultures; % positive cultures increases if 10 mL of patient's ascitic fluid is added to each blood culture bottle; use 2-4 bottles. JAMA 299:1166, 2008.
  • Studies of whether oral Rifaximin decreases SBP show discrepant results (J Clin Gastroenterol 46:709, 2012; PLoS One 9:e93909, 2014).
  • Risk  factors of interest:
    • Proton pump inhibitors identified as independent risk factor for SBP in retrospective, prospective, and meta-analysis studies. Mechanism speculative
    • Non-selective beta blockers associated with reduced risk of SBP. Mechanism unknown
    • References for risk factors and general review: Aliment Pharmacol Ther 2015; 41:1116
