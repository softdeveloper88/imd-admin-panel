Outpatient Parenteral Antimicrobial Therapy (OPAT)

Introduction

  • Outpatient Parenteral Antimicrobial Therapy (OPAT) is the administration of parenteral antimicrobial therapy in ≥2 doses on different days without intervening hospitalization.
  • In general, three models of OPAT delivery exist: home based, infusion center based, and skilled nursing facility based.
  • Selection of infusion device depends on drug stability, dosing regimen, cost, and patient preference.
  • Factors influencing stability include final drug concentration, diluent, temperature, pH, and type of infusion device.
    • Relevant temperatures for stability: 5°C (refrigerated). 25°C (room temperature), 37°C (body temperature).
    • An elastomeric pump will likely reach temperatures of 32-37°C since it is placed near the body.
  • IDSA guidelines: Clin Infect Dis 68:e1, 2019.
  • Review of carbapenem use in OPAT, including stability data: J Infect 2024;89:106299 (open access)

Drug Delivery Systems

  • Methods of drug delivery (Open Forum Infect Dis 2022;9:ofac525)
    • IV push
      • Rapid, convenient
      • Requires manual dexterity
    • Programmable ambulatory pump
      • Also known as continuous ambulatory delivery device (CADD)
      • Allows for intermittent, continuous, and tapered infusions
    • Elastomeric device (the "infusion ball")
      • Simple single-dose delivery system
      • No batteries, gravity not required for flow
      • Available in a variety of sizes (50 mL to 500 mL) and flow rates ranging from 5 mL/hr to 250 mL/hr
      • Medication stability can vary between different devices
    • Gravity infusion
      • Infusion controlled by roller clamp, not pump
      • Inexpensive
      • Can be complex for patients to learn
    • Stationary infusion pump
      • Heavy, cumbersome
      • Appropriate for certain care centers

Stability Data

  • Plastic containers and elastomeric devices
    • Detailed source: Ther Adv Infect Dis 2023;10:20499361231191877 (open access).
    • Drugs discussed: Ampicillin, ampicillin-sulbactam, cefazolin, cefepime, ceftazidime, ceftazidime-avibactam, ceftolozane-tazobactam, meropenem, nafcillin, oxacillin, piperacillin-tazobactam, vancomycin.
    • Plastic containers: Ethylvinyl acetate or polyvinyl chloride.
    • Elastomeric devices include AccuFlo, Accufuser, AutoDose, Easypump II, FOLfusor, Homepump Eclipse C-series, Intermate, MedFlo, MEDI-FLO, ReadMED, and SMARTeZ.
    • Data are provided for multiple diluents (NS, D5W, sterile water for injection) and various drug concentrations at 5°C, 25°C, and ≥30°C (if available).
  • Cefiderocol
    • Product labeling: Reconstituted drug stable in 100 mL D5W or NS for 6 hours at room temperature.
    • Elastomeric device: 12 gm/L stable for 24 hr at 32-37°C (Pharmaceutics 2023;15:2705).
  • Ceftaroline in an elastomeric device
    • 6 mg/mL in NS or D5W was stable for 144 hr at 4°C, 24 hr at 25°C, and 12 hr at 30°C. At 35°C, the drug was stable for 12 hr in NS and for 6 hr in D5W.
    • Reference: Eur J Hosp Pharm 2018;25(e2):e115.

Clinical Experience

  • Cefiderocol
    • A 31-yo male with NDM-producing K. pneumoniae otomastoiditis treated for 62 days with 6 gm/day of cefiderocol administered by 24-hr continuous infusion using an elastomeric device. Aggressive PK/PD target confirmed by TDM on days 17 and 45. Marked clinical improvement (avoiding need for surgery), no adverse effects reported. Note: concentration in the device was 25 gm/L (JAC Antimicrob Resist 2025;7:dlaf066).
  • Ceftolozane-tazobactam
    • In a retrospective case series, seven outpatients with P. aeruginosa were treated with ceftolozane-tazobactam by continuous infusion, 4.5 or 9 gm in 240 mL NS over 24 hours (infusion rate 10 mL/hr). Drug stability is 24 hours at room temperature, allowing the patients to present to the institution's outpatient infusion center for daily replacement of the medication cassette. 6/7 patients had symptom resolution, 3/3 microbiologic resolution (Open Forum Infect Dis 2020;7:ofaa014).
