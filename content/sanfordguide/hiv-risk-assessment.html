HIV Risk Assessment

Risk Assessment

Primary Care Considerations

  • HIV risk assessment is an essential component of primary care for all patients.(Summarized in N Engl J Med 384:2131, 2021)
  • When talking to patients about HIV, avoid using medical jargon (e.g., “intercourse”), vague terms (“sexually active”), group designations (“homosexual”), or judgmental terms (“promiscuous”).
  • Learn the language and terminology understood and used by your patients.
  • Question patient responses to the extent necessary to elicit behavior(s) that place the individual at higher risk for HIV acquisition and understand the extent of risk (for both acquisition and transmission).
  • Eliciting risk is particularly difficult but equally important in resource-limited areas of the world, especially when working through translators.
  • It is essential that the clinician be knowledgeable about cultural sensitivities, customs and traditions relative to HIV risk behavior.
  • See also, Recommendations for HIV Testing.
  • Assess whether the person is taking or has taken PrEP

Risk Reduction Counseling

  • Risk-reduction counseling works to reduce high-risk behavior (J AIDS 39:446, 2005; Ann Intern Med 149:497, 2008, U.S. Preventive Services Task Force).
  • HIV counseling & testing is a critical component of health care maintenance, see CDC guidance on risk behaviors.
  • Those individuals deemed at risk for acquiring HIV infection should be offered Pre-exposure Prophylaxis (PrEP)

Poverty and HIV

  • Poverty accounts for a large portion of the observed racial and ethnic disparities in the prevalence of HIV in urban areas of the US. 
  • 2.8% of heterosexuals living in 23 urban areas with annual income < $10,000 are infected compared to 0.4% infected who earned more than $50,000/yr. 
  • 1.5% of those with income between $10,000 and $19,999 were infected with HIV while 1.2% of those earning $20,000 - $49,999 were infected. 
  • The overall (urban and non-urban) rate of infection nationally is 0.1% (MMWR 60(31):1045, 2011).

Sexual Behaviors

  • Life-time risk of acquiring HIV in the U.S. is 1 in 64 adults.  Among MSM the risk is 1 in 6; for Black MSM, the risk is 1 in 2.

  • The CDC recommends antiretroviral therapy (ART) post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) within 24 - 36 hours (and certainly < 72 hrs) after high risk sexual exposure, injection-drug use, or other non-occupational exposure to HIV (CDC, 2025). PEP is not 100% effective; seroconversion is still detected in 1% of 702 exposed individuals.
  • PEP regimens updated (CDC Guidance, 2025) and typically consists of 2 nucleosides (TDF (or TAF) + FTC ) and a INSTI (e.g., Dolutegravir)
  • High-Risk Sexual Partner(s)
    • HIV-infected partners, especially those with increased levels of HIV-RNA 
    • Partners who are at risk but have not been HIV tested or do not disclose their HIV status to their partners 
    • Multiple partners: Unsafe sexual activity appears to be increasing among men who have sex with men (MSM) (Presence of mucosal ulceration or other STD in either partner 
  • Sexual Practices
    • High infection risk:
      • Unprotected anal receptive intercourse (“barebacking”)
      • Unprotected vaginal receptive intercourse
    • Infection risk documented:
      • Unprotected anal insertive intercourse
      • Unprotected vaginal insertive intercourse (risk may be higher during menses)
      • Unprotected oral receptive intercourse [HIV RNA levels in rectal secretions > serum with & without ART use 
      • Unprotected oral insertive intercourse rare 
    • Lower infection risk:
      • Any of the above with latex/vinyl condom (vaginal or penile) protection. Male condoms are 80–95% effective in ↓ risk of HIV infection (AmFAR Issue Brief #1, Jan. 2005); female condoms are 94–97% effective.
      • Cunnilingus, especially with rubber dam, microwaveable plastic food wrap or other water-impervious barrier.
      • Circumcision reduced HIV infection by 60% in a prospective randomized study in 3,274 men in South Africa RR; 0.40 (p<.001) (PLoS Med 2:e298, 2005). Two other studies confirmed 50-60% reduction and were stopped early in 2007 (Lancet 369:643, 2007, Ln 369:657, 2007; PLoS Med 4(7):e223, 2007).
    • Safer:
      • Deep kissing
      • Protected sex with HIV test negative partner
      • Mutual monogamy
      • Mutual masturbation
      • Masturbation or massage
      • Sex with HIV positive partner who is on successful ART (CD4 < 400) (Lancet 375:2092, 2010).
    • Safest:
      • Abstinence

  • Programs Aimed at Reducing Sexual Transmission

    • Treatment as Prevention (TasP) :  Decreasing HIV RNA (viral load) to sustained undetectable levels is associated with ~ zero transmission events. In the PARTNERS study, among MSM and heterosexual couples having > 77,000 condomless sex there were no cases of within-couple HIV transmission. The upper end of the 95% CI was 0.23 / 100 couple years of follow up (CYFU), meaning, at worst, one infection would occur per 433 years of condomless sex.  (JAMA 316:171, 2016)

    • Bottom line: HIV transmission is ~ zero risk when the seropositive partner is on ARV therapy and the viral load is <200 c/mL consistently.
    • PrEP (Pre-exposure Prophylaxis): Use of ART in uninfected high-risk individuals. iPrEx Study showed a 44% reduction in transmission of HIV over one year among high-risk gay men randomized to receive Tenofovir-FTC daily compared to placebo recipients. For those randomized to receive TDF-FTC and who had detectable drug levels, the relative risk reduction was 92% (N Engl J Med 363:2587, 2010). Using the overall rate of reduction, over 110 uninfected individuals would need to be treated to prevent one infection per year, at an estimated cost of > $1.8M/infection prevented/year (estimated risk ~ 2% / year). Cost effectiveness studies show, in selected very high risk populations of MSM, PrEP is borderline cost-effective (approx $60,000/QALY; Ann Int Med 156:541, 2012.
    • The major route of HIV spread worldwide remains heterosexual vaginal intercourse. Efforts to change sexual behavior by reducing number of sexual partners and using safe sex techniques (condoms) have met with varying degrees of success.
    • Voluntary counseling & testing (VCT): Prevention based on identifying infected persons & counseling them to prevent transmission to sexual partner(s). 
    • Counseling & controlling STD in female sex workers
    • Condom distribution campaigns
    • Prevention campaign focused on education about AIDS & promotion of safer sexual behavior
    • Concern regarding increased transmission among older heterosexual adults (>55 years old). Many older adults are sexually active (N Engl J Med 357:762, 2007), and with increased use of erectile dysfunction drugs, higher rates of STDs, including HIV, expected.

    Illicit Drug Use

    • Illicit drug use includes injection drug use (IDU) including intravenous injection and "skin popping" as well as smoking crack cocaine.
      • Assess injectable anabolic steroid use!
      • Assess sexual behaviors in all drug users!
      • In one study, infection rates in crack cocaine smoking women are as high as in men who had sex with men (41% vs. 43%).
      • Risk-reduction intervention can ↓ high risk sexual activity in crack cocaine users 
      • Buprenorphine is useful in reducing opioid-dependence, thus reducing HIV transmission 
    • Riskiest Practices
      • Sharing uncleaned needles, syringes, other paraphernalia (works), especially in “shooting galleries.” HIV DNA found on 85% of needles/syringes & 1/3–2/3 cottons, cookers, wash waters from shooting galleries.
      • Practicing “registering,” “booting,” or “back loading”
    • Less Risky Practices
      • Sharing cleaned needles, syringes, works. (Household bleach is effective, especially after washing & when contact time is greater than 5 minutes. It is important to rinse with water after bleach use.)
      • Drug paraphernalia used repeatedly but by single user
    • Least Risky Practices
      • Single use needles, syringes, works (needle exchange programs reduce HIV transmission, 
      • Sterile needles, syringes, works (needle/syringe exchange appears effective & has not ↑ drug use)

    Blood Product Infusion Recipient

    Parenteral transmission

      • Transfusion of infected blood 95 in 100
      • Needle sharing 1 in 150
      • Needle stick 1 in 300
      • Needle stick/AZT PEP 1 in 10,000
    • Highest Risk of HIV Transmission
      • Receipt of multiple units of blood products between 1978–1985
      • Receipt of blood products obtained from donors in countries where screening is unreliable or not done
    • Lesser Risk
      • HIV p24 antigen testing of all blood products (instituted 3/96) reduces the “window” by 6 days & ↓ infectious donations by 25%/yr to 1:600,000-1:880,000 units. 
      • RhoGAM & hepatitis B vaccine (serum-derived) have never been reported to transmit HIV-1.
      • Receipt of heterologous blood products in U.S. after 1985 (risk per unit 1:450,000 to 1:660,000 units or 1:28,000 after an average of 5.4 units). [This is because of a window (about 20 days) between infection & seroconversion (18–27 donations/yr are in this window).]
      • Receipt of donor-selected blood products in U.S. after 1985 (but no safer than random donors)
    • Safest
      • Receipt of autologous blood products
      • Receipt of genetically engineered blood product substitutes

    Other Risk Groups

    • Perinatal Infection: 1 in 4 live births if no ARV therapy; < 1% if mom on successful ARV therapy
    • Occupational Exposure (HHS/CDC Guidelines: Infect Control Hosp Epi 34: 875, 2014)
      • Deep parenteral inoculation (RR 16.8) via hollow needle of blood from source with high-titer viremia; seroconversion or advanced HIV disease (RR 7.8)
      • Parenteral inoculation of materials containing high titer virus in research laboratory setting
      • Failure to use ART after inoculation (RR 0.1 when used)
      • Note: Risk may be decreased by glove use, which removes >50% blood from exposure site in some studies but HIV-size microbes can pass through 1/3 of latex gloves tested. With double-glove use, blood-hand contacts ↓ from 71 to 32/100 procedures.
      • Small volume exposure via non-hollow needle
      • Mucosal exposure/non-intact skin exposure [risk is too low to be quantified in prospective studies; not zero but estimated to be at least a log (90%) lower than needlestick risk. Risk may be increased if large volume or prolonged contact occurs].
      • Cutaneous contact (intact skin)

      • Exposure to urine, saliva, sweat, tears

      • Highest Risk
      • Reduced Risk
      • Risk Not Identified
    • Donor Organ or Tissue Transplantation
      • Test potential donors for HIV (note window between infection & seroconversion).
      • Assess donors for risk factors: Report of transmission of HIV from HIV infected donor to recipient via kidney transplant (MMWR 60:297, 2011).

      • HIV-to-HIV (donor to recipient) transplants now approved via the HOPE Act.  Many transplant centers in the US now actively transplanting organs from HIV+ deceased donors into HIV+ recipients, dramatically increasing the number of transplants in HIV+ patients.

      • Evaluate risk/benefits
    • Artificial Insemination
      • HIV testing recommended but not legally required.
