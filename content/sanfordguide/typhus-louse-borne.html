Typhus, Louse-borne

Clinical Setting

  • Louse-borne rickettsial disease encountered in three settings:
    • Unhygienic conditions of war and crowding.
    • Relapsing disease years after the initial infection, i.e., Brill-Zinsser disease (see Comments).
    • Sylvatic cycle involving flying squirrels and their infected ticks and fleas.
      • Brill-Zinsser disease can occur after disease acquisition from flying squirrel: Clin Infect Dis 51:712, 2010
  • General ref.: Lancet Infect Dis 8:417, 2008.
  • Clinical Presentation:
    • Abrupt onset of fever, headache and malaise in patient carrying infected body lice (Pediculosis humanus) or infected head lice (Pediculosis capitis).
    • Presents with rash in 64% of patients; starts on trunk and spreads centrifugally to extremities.

Etiologies

  • Rickettsia prowazekii

Diagnosis

  • By serology: Emerg Infect Dis 15:1005, 2009. Contact state health departments or CDC.
  • PCR if available

Primary Regimens

  • Doxycycline 100 mg po/IV bid x 5 days; in one study a single 200 mg dose reported effective in 95% of the patients (See Comments)
  • Plus de-louse the patient's clothing.

Alternative Regimens

  • Chloramphenicol 500 mg po/IV qid x 5 days
  • Plus de-louse the patient's clothing.

Comments

  • Pediatrics. Use of tetracyclines in pediatric patients has historically been limited because of reports of permanent tooth discoloration in children age <8 years caused by incorporation of the drugs and their colored degradation products in enamel. Doxycycline binds less readily to calcium compared with other members of the tetracycline class, and recent comparative data suggest that doxycycline is not likely to cause visible teeth staining or enamel hypoplasia in children <8 years of age. Doxycycline can safely be administered for short durations (≤21 days) regardless of patient age (AAP Red Book 2018, Section 4; J Pediatr 166:1246, 2015)
  • Recrudescent louse-borne typhus is called Brill-Zinsser disease. It can occur 10-50 years after the primary infection. See Canadian Med Assn J 73:560, 1955 (relapse of louse-borne typhus acquired during Second World War).
  • Epidemics in flying squirrels (Emerg Inf Dis 9:1341, 2003), in homeless (Medicine 84:61, 2005) and in refugees (Lancet 352:353, 1998).
