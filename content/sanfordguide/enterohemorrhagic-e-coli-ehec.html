E. coli: Shiga toxin-producing (STEC)

Clinical Setting

  • Some strains of E. coli (63%) can produce Shiga toxin and are termed Shiga toxin-producing E. coli (STEC).
    • This is now the preferred name for Enterohemorrhagic E. coli (EHEC).
    •  It was initially thought that E. coli 0157:H7 was the only Shiga toxin-producing EHEC. It is now recognized that E. coli 0104:H4 and other strains account for upwards of 50% of STEC/EHEC infection.
  • STEC causes bloody diarrheal illness.
    • Patients are often afebrile. WBC may be elevated. Stools may initially be watery and non-bloody
    • Shiga toxin causes hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS): (hemolytic anemia, renal injury, thrombocytopenia).
      • HUS Complications include CNS damage, pancreatitis and other manifestations.
      • Toxin produces a microangiopathy with most severe damage to the renal glomerular tufts and the CNS
      • For management of HUS see: Arch Dis Child 2018;103: 285 and  N Engl J  Med. 2023. 389: 1402

Diagnosis

  • Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay for Shiga toxin antigen
  • Fecal DNA PCR probes for the genes of Shiga toxin 1 and 2 (included in commercial multiplex stool  assays)
  • Can screen stool for STEC 0157:H7 with sorbitol-MacConkey agar (neg. fermentation)
  • Reference: MMWR 58(RR12):1, 2009.

Etiologies

  • Shiga toxin producing E. coli (STEC). Synonyms for STEC:
    • Enterohemorrhagic E. coli (EHEC)
    • Verotoxin producing E. coli

Primary Regimens

  • Hydration.
    • Observational study (Pediatrics. 2005 Jun;115(6):e673-80) and meta-analysis (JAMA Pediatr 2017, 171:68) suggest that volume expansion in children with STEC infection can decrease risk of oligo-anuric HUS
  • Avoid anti-peristaltic drugs.
    • Look for evidence of HUS: low platelet count, renal injury, CNS signs and symptoms
  • Antibiotic therapy controversial, see Antimicrobial Stewardship

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Pros and Cons of antibiotic therapy (Curr Opin Gastroenterol 2022; 38:30)
    • Con
      • In children under age 10, precipitation of, or worsening of, HUS when given empiric TMP/SMX, beta-lactam, Metronidazole, or fluoroquinolone for diarrheal illness. One case of HUS for every 4 children treated: Clin Infect Dis 55:33, 2012. With these drugs, increased odds of precipitating HUS (Clin Infect Dis 2016; 62:1259)
      •  In a meta-analysis Clin Infect Dis. 2016;62:1251, odds ratio for risk of HUS with antibiotic exposure for EHEC was 2.24 (CI 1.45-3.46)
      • HUS is bad disease: 10% mortality. 50% of patients have some degree of permanent renal and/or CNS damage: Clin Infect Dis 38:1298, 2004.
    • Pro
      • In uncontrolled study, antibiotic treatment of STEC outbreak, antibiotic recipients had shorter period of stool excretion of the pathogen, fewer seizures and decreased mortality: Brit Med J 345:e4565, 2012.
  • Conclusion with less than perfect data:
    • If started on antibiotic for episode of non-bloody diarrhea and lab subsequently identifies STEC, discontinue antibiotic;
    • If bloody diarrhea and afebrile patient, especially if a child <10 years old, do not start empiric antibiotic;
    • If, for whatever reason, plan is to start an empiric antibiotic, Azithromycin may be the least likely to precipitate HUS: JAMA 307:1046, 2012.
    • If patient has positive blood cultures, Azithromycin 500 mg IV/po for 3 or more days depending on clinical response is suggested

Comments

  • In 2011, there was a large STEC outbreak due to 0104:H4 STEC, with many cases of HUS.
    • Infection was associated with consumption of sprouts (N Engl J Med 365:1763, 2011).
      • Strain was unusual: an enteroaggregative strain of E. coli that had acquired prophage mediating Shiga toxin 2 (N Engl J Med 365:709, 2011; N Engl J Med 365:718, 2011).
      • Approximately 20-25% of infected patients developed HUS, most of whom were adults (N Engl J Med 365:1763, 2011; N Engl J Med 365:1771, 2011; Lancet Infect Dis 11:671, 2011).
      • Abdominal pain and bloody diarrhea were each noted in 88% of patients (N Engl J Med 365:1771, 2011).
  • Eculizumab has evolved into an important therapy of HUS
    • Eculizumab therapy blocks the alternative complement pathway and mitigates the inflammatory response to HUS shiga toxin 2 (Arch Dis Child 2018;103: 285)
    • Use of eculizumab increases risk of meningococcal infection; some use  azithromycin to prevent meningococcal infection during eculizumab therapy
  •  0104:H4 STEC is very different from 0157:H7  STEC 
    • It is a Shiga toxin-producing enteroaggregative E. coli.
    • In 15 patients with prolonged carriage of the organism, treatment with 3 days of azithromycin cleared organisms from the stool without inducing HUS (JAMA 307:1046, 2012).
