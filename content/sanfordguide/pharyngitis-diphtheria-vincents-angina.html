Diphtheria

Clinical Setting

  • Presentation:
    • Sore throat, cervical lymphadenopathy, low grade fever, inadequate immunization history for diphtheria
    • Exam: Erythema progresses to white exudate and then adherent gray pseudomembrane
      • Membrane due to necrosis of mucosal cells by diphtheria toxin
      • Toxin can also cause damage to myocaridium, kidneys, and central nervous system
    • Membranous pharyngitis due to Diphtheria may occur in immunized individuals.
      • Antibiotics therapy decreases toxin production and reduces spread of organisms.
  • Respiratory droplet isolation indicated
  • Diphtheria toxin:
    • Leads to adherent gray membrane in nasal passages, pharynx, and laryngotracheal  bronchial tree
      • Toxin can also cause myocarditis (22%) of patients
      • Toxin can cause neuropathy (soft palate, cranial nerves)
    • To obtain equine diphtheria antitoxin from CDC, +1 404-639-2880. More information at CDC Diphtheria pages.
  • In short:
    • Toxigenic C. diphtheriae primarily causes respiratory track inflammation and necrosis. Treatment is antitoxin and usually parenteral macrolide antibiotic therapy
    • Non-toxigenic strains of C. diphtheriae, and other invasive Corynebacteriae species, most often manifest as cutaneous ulcers, do not need anti-toxin, and may respond to oral macrolide therapy
  • See comment  section for data on  cutaneous ulcers due to non-toxigenic diphtheria

Diagnosis

  • Culture of pharynx and nose (alert laboratory as special media needed)
  • PCR can detect organism and/or C. diphtheriae toxin. CDC can help: phone +1 404-639-1231

Etiologies

  • Corynebacterium diphtheriae, toxigenic strains (spread is human to human)
    • Toxigenic strains; spread is human to human
    • Non-toxigenic strains are a rare cause of serious disease, e.g., bacteremia, endocarditis, osteomyelitis and septic arthritis (MMWR. 2024; 73:405)
  • Other Corynebacterium species are rarely toxigenic (spread is animal to human)
    • Corynebacteriuim ulcerans
    • Corynebacterium pseudotuberculosis

Primary Regimens

  • Place patient in respiratory droplet isolation; Continue isolation until two consecutive cultures, taken at least 24 hrs apart, are negative
  • Carefully and constantly monitor adequacy of airway. Some patients may need intubation.
  • Treatment includes antibiotics, antitoxin, and maintenance of patient airway.
  • Antibiotic Therapy:
    • Due to increasing resistance to penicillins, prefer a macrolide as first choice (Genome Med. 2020;12:107)
      • Macrolides
        • Azithromycin: (IV/po)
          • Child: 10-12 mg/kg once daily (Max. 500 mg/day)
          • Adult: 500 mg IV/po once daily
        •  Erythromycin: Child and Adult
          • 40-50 mg/kg/day (max. of 2 gm/day ) IV/po in 4 divided doses
          • If IV, infuse each over 60 minutes
    • Penicillins: Use only if no available macrolides and confirmed in vitro susceptibility
      • Child:
        • Aqueous Penicillin G 100,000-300000 units/kg/day IV, divided q6h, (max.of 1  million units per dose) or
        • Procaine penicillin G  50,000 units/kg/day IM once daily ( max. of  1.2 million units/ day) or
        • Pen V-K  10-15 mg/kg/dose (max of 500 mg per dose) po 4 times /day
      • Adult:
        • Aqueous Penicillin G 25,000 units/kg IV q6h (max 1 million units/dose)) or
        • Penicillin G procaine 1.2 million units IM once daily
          • Can switch to Penicillin VK 500 mg po without food qid when patient is able
  • Diphtheria Antitoxin
    • In USA obtain from the CDC; telephone consultation at: 1-404-639-2889
    • See WHO Clinical Management Guideline, 2 Feb 2024.
    • No longer a need for sensitivity testing. If suspected or confirmed sysmptomatic  diphtheria administer single dose of antitoxin:
      • Antitoxin IV therapy. Dose depends on stage of illness:
        • < 48 hours: 20,000-40,000 International units
        • Presence of  nasopharyngeal gray membranes: 40,000-60,000 International units
        • > 3 days and "bull neck": 80,000-120,000 International units. Infuse IV over 60 minutes
  • Close contacts.
    • Identify, culture, and manage close contacts. Some will need prophylactic antibiotic:
      • Treat contacts with Azithromycin 500 mg po once daily x 7-10 days or Erythomycin (dosage above)
      • If no available macrolide, can use Procaine Penicillin G IM: 600,000 units if age < 6 yrs, 1.2 million units if age ≥ 6 yrs.
  • Follow up cultures after 1-2 days of therapy and again 2 weeks post end of therapy to document eradication of organism from the airway.
  • Post therapy, patient will need immunization with diphtheria toxoid vaccine.

Alternative Regimens

  • No clinical data with other drugs

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Duration of therapy: typically 14 days
  • C. diphtheriae is susceptible in vitro to clarithromycin, azithromycin, fluoroquinolones, clindamycin, TMP/SMX, vancomycin and linezolid. If strain resistant to macrolides and penicillin, vancomycin or linezolid are reasonable alternatives
  • Report of fatal toxigenic C. diphtheriae due to resistance to all beta-lactams to include inducible resistance to carbapenems: Clin Infect Dis 2020; doi.org/10.1093/cid/ciaa1147

Comments

  • Azithromycin is better tolerated (both po and IV) than Erythromycin. Erythromycin IV is associated with phlebitis and po with nausea and diarrhea.
  • Ensure adequate airway. Obtain EKG & cardiac enzymes.
  • Cutaneous diphtheria
    • Chronic shallow ulcer
    • Presence of dirty gray membrane
    • Diagnosis by culture or PCR
    • Corynebacteria  strains that cause cutaneous ulcers are usually  non-toxigenic, and no antitoxin  is needed
  • Contact prophylaxis
    • Identify, culture, and consider antimicrobial prophylaxis of close contacts
    • Immunize if vaccine status  is not current or known
    • Post-cultures: treat contacts with: Azithromycin 500 mg po once daily x 7-10 days
  • Disease typically no longer contagious after 48h of therapy.
