Malaria, Uncomplicated, P. ovale, P. malariae, P. knowlesi

Clinical Setting

  • Treatment of uncomplicated malaria due to P. ovale, P. malariae, P. knowlesi
  • In the US, malaria is uncommon in usual tourist travelers
  • Most cases are seen in immigrants, immigrants returning home to visit friends and relatives, and long-stay temporary residents of endemic areas who do not adhere to long-term prophylaxis.
  • Less than 10% of malaria seen in US is due to P. ovale, P. malariae, P. knowlesi combined
  • Dual infections with more than 1 Plasmodium species may occur
  • For uncomplicated malaria the oral ACT Artemether/lumefantrine is recommended as first choice by the US CDC for all species of malaria unless assuredly chloroquine sensitive.
    • P. falciparum is rarely chloroquine sensitive and other species uncommonly chloroquine resistant.
  • Consolidated WHO guidelines GRADE give a strong recommendation to preferentially treat adults and children with all of uncomplicated P. vivax, P. ovale, P. malariae or P. knowlesimalaria with either an oral ACT (including all pregnant women including in the first trimester)
    • However, chloroquine remains an option unless there is known chloroquine resistance in which case an ACT should be used.

  • Algorithm for diagnosis and treatment of malaria in the US  https://www.cdc.gov/malaria/hcp/clinical-guidance/diagnosis-treatment.html

  • P. knowlesi (mostly Malaysia, Thailand, Philippines) can cause severe malaria, treat as for severe malaria due to P. falciparum.  Trends Parasitol 2017; 33:242.

Diagnosis

  • Approved rapid diagnostic test: Binax NOW.  Many hospitals will do RDT only and not smear.
    • 50% sensitivity for low non-faciparum parasitemias which is the usual case.
    • High specificity; treat a positive test.
    • Need microscopy to speciate.
    • Can stay positive for over a month after successful treatment.
  • Blood film read by properly trained personnel.
  • PCR is available from commercial labs and is the gold standard.
  • Serology of limited value.

Etiologies

  • Plasmodium ovale
    • P. ovale curtisi and P. ovale walikeri are 2 defined strains with any clear biologic or therapeutic implications yet to be defined.
  • Plasmodium malariae
  • Plasmodium knowlesi (monkey malaria that can infect humans)

Primary Regimens

  • P. ovale, P. malariae, P. knowlesi
  • Adult:
    • Chloroquine phosphate 1 gm (600 mg base) po, then 0.5 gm in 6 hrs, then 0.5 gm daily x 2 days. Total: 2500 mg (Entire course is 5 x 500 mg tablets)
    • Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil and generics) 800 mg salt (620 mg base) po, followed by 400 mg salt (=310 mg base) po at 6, 24, and 48 hours.  Total: 2000 mg salt (=1,550 mg salt) 
    • Artemether-lumefantrine 4 tablets (20 mg/120 mg tabs) as a single dose, then 4 tablets again after 8 hours, then 4 tablets every 12 hours for 2 days (take with food).  
      • ACT not mentioned by CDC for  P. ovale, P. malariae, P. knowlesi
      • Can use all 3 drugs above in all trimesters of pregnancy 
    • PLUS (for all 3 drugs above)
      • For P. ovale only: add Primaquine phosphate 52.6 mg (30 mg base = 2 tablets) po once daily x 14d.
        • Total dose of primaquine in patients ≥70 kg should be adjusted to a total dose of 6 mg/kg, given in daily doses of 30 mg/day times the number of days to complete the total dose.
        • WHO: no longer recommends less than 14 days of primaquine as a viable option. 
        • See Comments section for Primaquine use in pregnancy
  • Pediatrics
    • Chloroquine phosphate 10 mg/kg of base po; then 5 mg/kg of base at 6, 24, 48 hrs.
      • Total: 25 mg/kg base (never exceed adult dose) 
    • Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil and generics) 10 mg base/kg po immediately, followed by 5 mg base/kg po at 6, 24, and 48 hours.
      • Total: 25 mg base/kg     
    • Artemether-lumefantrine (20/120 mg tablets)(Wt-based dosing in table below)
      WtDose

      25 kg to < 35 kg3 tablets as a single dose, then 3 tablets again after 8 hrs, then 3 tablets q12h for 2d

    • PLUS for P.ovale only;
      • Primaquine phosphate 0.5 mg/kg po base once daily x 14 days.

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • Avoid same drug for treatment for chemoprophylaxis failures with that same drug.
  • Obesity (WHO caution): As the evidence for an association between dose, pharmacokinetics and treatment outcome in overweight or large adults is limited, and alternative dosing options have not been assessed in treatment trials
    • In the absence of data, treatment providers should attempt to follow up the treatment outcomes of large adults whenever possible.
  • Primaquine  is added to P. ovale treatment to eradicate latent parasites in liver.
    •  Screen for G6PD deficiency before starting Primaquine; if mildly G6PD deficient, dose Primaquine as 45 mg po once weekly x 8 weeks. Note: rare severe reactions.
  • WHO Guidelines: Tafenoquine not recognized for radical cure of P. ovale due to inavailability of quantitative G6PD testing in most endemic settings.
  • P. ovale relapses in returned travelers seen in 2.8% receiving PQ vs 10.0% in patients not receiving PQ (not statistically different).
    • Supportive evidence for primaquine use in P. ovale remains limited. Clin Infect Dis 2021 Jul 3:ciab610. doi: 10.1093/cid/ciab610.
  • New (2024) WHO classification system for G6PD deficiency (Bull World Health Organ 2024;102:615)
  • Artemisinin combination therapy (ACT) in the US. Only Artemether/lumefantrine is FDA-approved.
    • Coartem is not widely stocked by hospital or retail pharmacies in the US.
      • Generally available from regional drug distribution warehouses with up to 24-hour delay in initiating therapy for a medical emergency.
        • Do not delay therapy in order to obtain Coartem if another 1st line agent is more readily available.
    • Call 1 855-COARTEM for expedited availability information.
  • ACT combinations are not FDA-approved for P. ovale, P. malariae, P. knowlesi
  • An ACT dihydroartemisinin/piperaquine (Eurartesim) is licensed and available in Europe.
    • artesunate/mefloquine, artesunate/amodiaquine, artesunate+sulfadoxine/pyrimethamine are additional ACTS recommended by WHO and available in some settings.
  • Pregnancy:
    • Avoid primaquine in pregnancy.
      • Pregnant patients with P. ovale infections should be maintained on chloroquine prophylaxis (300 mg base once/wk) for the duration of their pregnancy.
        • After delivery, pregnant patients who do not have G6PD deficiency should be treated with primaquine or tafenoquine.
    • Artemether-lumefantrine now recommended in all trimesters by CDC as drug of choice for all species.  
    • Doxycycline or Tetracycline may be used if benefits outweigh risks.
  • Chloroquine has a long half-life and prolongs QTc intervals.  Minor overdosages or too many doses over several days may be toxic or fatal.
  • In Southeast Asia, beware of P. knowlesi. It looks like P. malariae, but behaves like P. falciparum (Clin Infect Dis 46:165, 2008) and can cause severe malaria.
