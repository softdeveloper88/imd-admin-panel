Tuberculosis, Multi-Drug Resistant

Clinical Setting

  • Multidrug-resistant Mycobacterium tuberculosis (MDR-TB): Expert consultation strongly advised.
    • Treatment regimens for pulmonary disease and extrapulmonary disease and extrapulmonary osteoarticular, CNS, and disseminated disease.
  • MDR-TB defined as resistance to both Isoniazid and Rifampin.
  • TB caused by a strain with rifampin monoresistance
  • XDR-TB
    • Defined by CDC as TB caused by MDR strains (i.e., resistant to isoniazid and rifampin) that are also resistant to any fluoroquinolone plus resistant to at least one of three injectable second-line drugs (i.e., amikacin, kanamycin, or capreomycin).

    • Defined by WHO as TB caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis strains that are resistant to isoniazid, rifampicin, any fluoroquinolone, and either bedaquiline or linezolid.

  • See WHO MDR treatment guidelines and Curry Center Summary of MDR treatment guidelines.
  • ATS/CDC/ERS/IDSA Guidelines (Am J Respir Crit Care Med. 2019;200:e93-e142 and American Journal of Respiratory and Critical Care Medicine, Volume 211, Issue 1 (January 2025) 

Etiologies

  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis

Primary Regimens

  • Four-drug regimen (Bedaquiline + Pretomanid + Linezolid + Moxifloxacin), taken with food, administered for 6 months (See Comments) (American Journal of Respiratory and Critical Care Medicine, Volume 211, Issue 1 (January 2025).
    • Bedaquiline 400 mg po once daily for 2 weeks, then 200 mg 3 times per week, with at least 48 hours between doses, for 24 weeks plus
    • Pretomanid 200 mg po once daily for 26 weeks plus
    • Linezolid 600 mg po once daily  for 26 weeks (600 mg dose preferred and can be reduced to 300 mg to mitigate linezolid toxicity) plus
    • Moxifloxacin 400 mg po once daily for 26 weeks
  • For patients intolerant of fluoroquinolone or a fluoroquinolone-resistant strain, use the three-drug regimen of Bedaquiline + Pretomanid + Linezolid
  • Extend either regimen to 9 months (39 weeks) if evidence for delayed response to treatment as assessed by > 8 weeks to culture conversion, persistent culture positivity, clinical response to treatment, and other underlying clinical factors or modifications based on adverse events
  • Caveats: The ZeNix  (N Eng J Med 2022; 387:810) and PRACTECAL (N Engl J Med 2022;387:2331) trials on which these recommendations are based did not enroll pregnant or breast-feeding women, CNS disease (See Comments), osteoartciular disease, disseminated disease and children < 13-14 years of age, known resistance to any drug within the regimen, or prior therapy (>2-4 weeks) with any drug within the regimen.

    • Fourth option: 

      • WHO suggests the use of a 6-month regimen composed of bedaquiline, delamanid, linezolid, levofloxacin, and clofazimine in patients with or without fluoroquinolone resistance (conditional recommendation, very low certainty of evidence) based on results of the BEAT trial (NCT04062201) in which this regimen showed high treatment success and is composed of medicines used widely in all patient groups. The trial's evidence suggests that this regimen may be effectively and safely used in eligible patients with rifampin-resistant TB and pre-XDR-TB regardless of HIV status. The available evidence included children, adolescents, pregnant and breastfeeding women, flagging the possible use of the regimen in these population groups. Thus, the evidence provided by the study will support new recommendations for the programmatic use of the regimen in many population groups.

Comments

  • Expert consultation strongly advised.

  • Treatment of MDR/rifampin-resistant TB meningitis/CNS disease is best guided by results of drug susceptibility testing of the infecting strain and by the ability of drugs to cross the blood–brain barrier. Levofloxacin and moxifloxacin, ethionamide or prothionamide, cycloserine, linezolid and imipenem–cilastatin cross the blood-brain barrier well. High-dose isoniazid and pyrazinamide can also reach therapeutic levels in the CSF and may be useful if the strains are susceptible. P-aminosalicylic acid , ethambutol, amikacin and streptomycin do not penetrate the CNS well. There are few data on the CNS penetration of clofazimine, bedaquiline or delamanid. In addition, cerebrospinal fluid concentrations may not mirror concentrations in the meninges or brain.

  • Adverse events

    • Elevations in hepatic enzymes; toxicity due to linezolid including myelossuppression, peripheral neuropathy and optic neuropathy.
    • QTc prolongation may occur; frequent monitoring is required; discontinue the combination regimen if significant ventricular arrhythmia develops or for QTcF (QT interval correction by Fridericia's formula) prolongation of greater than 500 ms on repeat ECGs).
      • Levofloxacin, Moxifloxacin, Bedaquiline, Delamanid, Pretomanid and Clofazimine cause QTc prolongation and caution is required when these are used in combination 
      • ECG should be obtained at baseline, 2, 12 and 24 weeks of Bedaquiline therapy to monitor the QTc interval; monitor ECG weekly if concurrent use of other drugs that prolong QTc (e.g., fluoroquinolone, macrolide).
      • Monitor serum levels of potassium, magnesium, and calcium.
      • Discontinue Bedaquiline or other suspected offending agent for clinically significant ventricular arrhythmia or a QTc interval of > 500 ms (confirmed by repeat ECG).
  • Patients receiving Cycloserine should receive pyridoxine, 50 mg/day for Cycloserine 250 mg, 100 mg/day for 500 mg and 150 mg/day for 750 mg.
  • Surgical resection may be appropriate for localized disease caused by MDR or XDR strains.
