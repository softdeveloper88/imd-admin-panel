DDx: Sexually Transmitted Diseases, HIV

Overview

  • Common selected HIV/AIDS-associated sexually transmitted diseases (STDs).
  • Presented by Clinical Syndrome
  • For treatment options, see Topics Links below.
  • General Reference: MMWR 70(4):1- 135,  2021 (update).
  • Patients with frequent STIs, use of doxycycline PEP (200 mg orally once after each sexual exposure) is effective (NEJM 388:1296, 2023)

Differential Diagnosis

Cervicitis, Urethritis

  • Etiologies: N. gonorrhoea and/or C. trachomatis. High frequency of dual infection.
  • Males: Painful urination and purulent urethral discharge; gram stain sensitive (>95%) and specific (>99%).
  • Females: May not have cervical discharge. Gram stain less reliable.
  • For both males and females: NAAT on urine, urethral swab and/or cervical swab for both N. gonorrhoea and C. trachomatis.
  • Treatment: Cervicitis, Urethritis, Chlamydia
  • Treatment: Urethritis, Recurrent

Genital Ulcers

  • Four Etiologies
    • Treponema pallidum: Painless ulceration
    • Chancroid (H. ducreyi). Discrete outbreaks; cofactor for HIV transmission; 10% of patients coinfected with syphilis or HSV
    • Granuloma inguinale, Klebsiella granulomatis. Large and destructive but little pain. Rare in the US.
    • Lymphogranuloma venereum, C. trachomatis. C. trachomatis serovars L1, L2 or L3. NAAT test for C. trachomatis will be positive.
  • Primary Syphilis
    • Painless ulcer
    • Often missed, especially if on cervix or in pharynx
    • Diagnosis: darkfield exam.  Serology negative with primary disease.
  • Chancroid:
    • Painful genital ulcer + tender suppurative lymphadenopathy. Do darkfield to rule out syphilis. Even with special culture media, culture sensitivity is <80%. Retest for syphilis 3 months after treatment. All sex partners of patients with chancroid should be examined and treated if they have evidence of disease or have had sex with index patient within the last 10 days.
    • Treatment: Chancroid
  • Granuloma inguinale:
    • Etiology: Klebsiella granulomatis
    • Painless progressive ulcerative lesions without regional lymphadenopathy. Ulcers bleed easily. Hard to culture; rely on visualization of Donovan bodies on biopsy.
    • Treatment: Granuloma inguinale
  • Lymphogranuloma venereum:
    • Unilateral inguinal/femoral lymphadenopathy. Self-limited papule/small ulcer at site of inoculation. Rectal exposure leads to proctitis (rectal discharge, pain, constipation, fever and/or tenesmus) (Dis Colon Rectum 52:507, 2009). Can result in fistulas or stricture.
    • Diagnosis: Based on serology; biopsy contraindicated because sinus tracts can develop.
    • Treatment: Lymphogranuloma venereum

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)
  • Etiologies:
    • Acute: N. gonorrhea and C. trachomatis – especially the 1st episode
    • Acute or recurrent: vaginal flora (anaerobes, enteric gram-neg bacilli, Streptococcus
      agalactiae). Ref: MMWR 59 (RR-12):63,2010.
  • Any combination of endometritis, salpingitis, tubo-ovarian abscess and pelvic peritonitis. Early diagnosis and treatment important to avoid scarring of upper genital tract with sequelae (infertility, dyspareunia).
  • Diagnosis: Tender with cervical motion or uterine palpation or adnexal bimanual exam. Majority have mucopurulent cervical discharge. Urine nucleic acid amplification test for N. gonorrhoea and C. trachomatis.
  • Treatment: Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

Perihepatitis

  • Fitzhugh Curtis Syndrome
  • Etiologies: N. gonorrhea, C. trachomatis
  • Right upper quadrant pain. Do NAAT for N. gonorrhoea and C. trachomatis on urine and/or cervical swab.
  • Treatment: Fitzhugh Curtis Syndrome

Proctitis, Proctocolitis

  • Etiologies:
    • Proctitis: N. gonorrhoea, C. trachomatis, to include LGV, T. pallidum & HSV
    • Proctocolitis: Campylobacter, Shigella, E. histolytica & rarely LGV
    • In AIDS patients (CD4 < 200): add CMV, MAI, cryptosporidium, microsporidia and isospora
  • Proctitis: Anorectal pain, tenesmus and/or rectal discharge.
  • Proctocolitis: Proctitis plus diarrhea and abdominal cramps.
  • Try to make a specific diagnosis: Urine NAAT for N. gonorrhoea & C. trachomatis, stool C&S, stool ova & parasites, antigen detection for cryptosporidia, whole blood CMV-PCR.
  • Treatment: Gonococcal Proctitis

Venereal Warts

  • Etiology: Human papillomavirus (HPV)—multiple types, but type 6 & 11 most common worry about oncogenic types.
  • Diagnosis is usually by appearance: flat, papular or pedunculated growth (condylomas) on genital mucosa. Can cause warts and/or malignancy at a variety of sites: cervix, vagina, vulva, penis, anus, oropharynx.
  • Treatment: Human papillomavirus (HPV)
