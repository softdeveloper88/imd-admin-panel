Mucormycosis

Clinical Setting

  • Mucormycosis (formerly called Zygomycosis) is infection caused by mold of the order Mucorales.
  • Risk factors: hematological malignancy, hematopoietic stem cell transplant, solid organ transplantation, diabetes mellitus with ketoacidosis, iron chelation therapy and iron overload states, and corticosteroid use.
  • Clinical presentations: most common are pulmonary and rhinocerebral disease although cutaneous, disseminated, and gastrointestinal have been described.

Diagnosis

  • Diagnose by culture of tissue or stain: pathology shows wide ribbon-like, non-septated hyphae that are right angle branching.

Etiologies

  • Mucor sp.
  • Rhizopus sp.
  • Rhizomucor sp.
  • Cunninghamella sp.
  • Lichtheimia sp. (formerly called Absidia sp.)

Primary Regimens

  • Liposomal Amphotericin B 5-10 mg/kg IV daily
  • Surgical debridement of infected tissue whenever possible

Alternative Regimens

  • Amphotericin B deoxycholate 1 mg/kg IV daily
  • Azole therapy is often used as step-down once disease is (1) controlled and (2) susceptibility confirmed at reference lab.
    • Posaconazole (NOTE: different dosing delayed release tabs/IV vs. suspension, better levels achieved with delayed-release tabs)
      • Delayed-release tabs 300 mg po bid x 2 doses and then 300 mg po daily OR
      • Suspension 200 mg qid, then 400 mg po bid after stabilization of disease OR
      • Posaconazole IV 300 mg IV over 90 minutes bid x 1 day, then 300 mg IV daily
    • Isavuconazonium sulfate (prodrug of Isavuconazole) 372 mg po/IV q8h x 6 doses and then 372 mg po/IV daily

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Duration of therapy:
    • Resolution of clinical signs and symptoms of infection AND resolution or stabilization of radiographic abnormalities and resolution of underlying immunosuppression (consider secondary prophylaxis)

Comments

  • Keys to successful therapy:
    • Early diagnosis and initiation of antifungal therapy
    • Surgical debridement of involved tissue whenever possible
    • Reduction of immunosuppressive therapy, when possible
  • Oral therapy is often used as step-down once disease is (1)controlled and (2)susceptibility confirmed at reference lab.  Options include posaconazole and isavuconazole as below.
    • Posaconazole:
      • Evidence: Clinical efficacy has been demonstrated in Posaconazole salvage protocols with > 60% response rate (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 50:126, 2006; Clin Infect Dis 42:e61, 2006).  Activity demonstrated in vitro although higher MICs are seen with certain species (Rhizomucor sp. and Rhizopus sp.). Consider obtaining speciation +/- susceptibility testing to guide therapy (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 51:2587, 2007).  Treatment has not been studied in a RCT.
      • Measurement of serum concentrations advisable, better serum levels achieved with delayed-release tabs; attempt to achieve trough concentrations of > 1.0 μg/mL for treatment of disease (J Antimicrob Chemother 69:1162, 2014).
    • Isavuconazonium sulfate (prodrug of Isavuconazole):
      • Evidence: Approved for treatment of invasive mucor infection based a multicenter open-label single-arm study (the VITAL study) that included 37 patients with proven or probable mucormycosis (Lancet Infect Dis 16:828, 2016)
  • Combination therapy is not routinely recommended as it has not been supported in any study to date. Adjunctive Deferasirox therapy to liposomal Amphotericin B failed to demonstrate benefit (J Antimicrob Chemother. 67:715, 2012).  There has not been evidence of combination therapy with amphotericin B products, echinocandins, or azoles.
  • Review articles: Clin Infect Dis 54(S1):S23, 2012; Clin Infect Dis 54:1629, 2012; Lancet Infect Dis 11:301, 2011.
