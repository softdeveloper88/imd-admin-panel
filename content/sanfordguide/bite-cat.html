Bite: Cat

Clinical Setting

  • Bite wound from cat.
  • 80% get infected. Culture and treat empirically. Pasteurella multocida infection develops within 24 hours.
  • Observe for Osteomyelitis.

Etiologies

  • Pasteurella multocida
  • Staphylococcus aureus
  • Capnocytophaga spp.
  • Bartonella henselae
  • oral Anaerobes

Primary Regimens

  • Amox-Clav (875/125 mg po bid or 500/125 mg po tid)

Alternative Regimens

  • Cefuroxime axetil 500 mg po q12h or Doxycycline 100 mg po bid or
    • Prophylaxis for bites not clinically infected and at risk (near joints, involving danger spaces of hands, face, genital area)
  • If culture is positive for only P. multocida, can switch to Penicillin G IV or Penicillin VK po.

Comments

  • P. multocida is resistant to Dicloxacillin, Cephalexin and Clindamycin. In vitro sensitivity to fluoroquinolones. Also susceptible to ceftaroline; ceftaroline active vs. MRSA and Pasteurella sp.
  • Many strains appear susceptible to Azithromycin but no clinical data.
  • Prophylaxis for bites not clinically infected and at risk (near joints, involving danger spaces of hands, face, genital area), deep puncture, immunocompromised host
  • Reference: Clin Infect Dis. 2014; 59:147
  • Rabies considerations for penetrating dog bites:
    • If healthy appearing observe for 10 days, PEP only if animal develops clinical signs of rabies
    • Immediate PEP if animal suspected rabid
    • Escaped; consult local public health authority
  • See also, Cat-scratch disease.
