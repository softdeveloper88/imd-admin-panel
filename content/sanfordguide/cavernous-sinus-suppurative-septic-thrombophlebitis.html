Cavernous Sinus Suppurative (Septic) Thrombophlebitis

Clinical Setting

  • Sinus infection precipitated by contiguous infection in the sphenoid or ethmoid sinuses.
  • Veins of the face and eyes drain via the cavernous sinuses; hence, spread to the cavernous sinus or infections of the skin on the face, nose, tonsils, orbits, and soft palate.
  • Cranial nerves III, IV, V, and VI pass through the lateral wall of the cavernous sinuses and damage thereto can occur as a consequence of infection.
  • Often presents with headache, unilateral and/or bilateral eye edema, and then cranial nerve palsies.
  • Obtain specimen for culture if patient to have adjunctive surgery

Diagnosis

  • Neuro-imaging: CT or MRI

Etiologies

  • Depends on primary site of infection
  • Staph. aureus (70% of patients)
  • Streptococcus sp.
  • H. influenzae
  • Aspergillus sp. or other fungi including mucor (less common)
  • Rarely anaerobic bacteria, gram-negative bacteria

Primary Regimens

  • Heparin is used, but no definitive evidence of efficacy
  • Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL) + Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q12h; Cefepime 2 gm IV q8h
  • If dental source with attendant increased risk of anaerobic infection, some add Metronidazole 500 mg IV q8h
  • If fungal etiology, see specific organism.
  • Continue heparin and antibiotic until afebrile for 48 hrs and WBC normal and then switch to coumadin for several weeks.

Alternative Regimens

  • Heparin is used, but no definitive data on efficacy
  • (Daptomycin 8-12 mg/kg IV q24hNAI or Linezolid 600 mg IV q12h) + Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q12h
  • If presumed dental source, add Metronidazole 500 mg IV q8h
  • Consider surgical drainage if sphenoid sinusitis.
  • Continue heparin and antibiotic until afebrile for 48 hrs and WBC normal and then switch to coumadin for several weeks.

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Typical duration of antimicrobial therapy is 3-4 weeks, with the longer duration for those with S. aureus infection.

Comments

  • Thrombosis may impair penetration of antibiotic hence the higher dose of Ceftriaxone as well as protecting the central nervous system.
  • Reference: Neurosurg Clin N Am. 2024; 35:305
