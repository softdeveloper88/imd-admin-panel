UTI, Complicated or Catheter-related

Clinical Setting

  • Complicated urinary tract infections (UTI): obstruction, reflux, azotemia, transplant.
  • Foley catheter-related infection. For catheter management see Comment below.
  • Important to rule out obstruction.

Etiologies

  • Enterobacteriaceae
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Enterococcus sp.
  • Staphylococcus aureus (rare), Clin Infect Dis 42:46, 2006

Primary Regimens

  • Empiric therapy:
    • Ciprofloxacin (500 mg po bid or extended release 1000 mg po q24h or 400 mg IV q12h)
    • Levofloxacin 750 mg po/IV q24h
    • If patient with systemic illness or high risk for multi-drug resistant organisms, consider alternative regimens below for empiric therapy
  • Specific therapy: based on bacterial speciation and drug susceptibility testing

Alternative Regimens

  • Nitrofurantoin (Macrobid) 100 mg po bid (if without signs or symptoms of systemic infection - fever, flank pain)
  • TMP-SMX-DS 1 tab bid (If local prevalence of resistance to TMP/SMX is < 20%)
  • If high risk for multi-drug resistant infection or systemic illness (see also Pyelonephritis):
    • Ertapenem 1gm IV q24h
    • Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h
    • Cefepime 2 gm IV q12h
    • Ceftazidime-avibactam 2.5 gm IV q8h
    • Plazomicin 15 mg/kg IV q24h

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • For tiered approach to prevent catheter-associated UTI : Ann Int Med 2019;171:S30
  • Duration of therapy: Recommended treatment in 7-14 days. If signs or symptoms of upper tract infection (fever, flank pain) treat for 14 days. Treat for 7 days for catheter-associated UTI if prompt resolution of symptoms, 10-14 days if delayed response; a 3-day course of therapy may be consider for women < 65 years of age (Clin Infect Dis 50:625, 2010). 

Comments

  • Replacing catheters: For patients with concomitant chronic necessary  Foley catheter, results of a prospective hospital based study indicates no benefit to replacing the bladder catheter (J Am Geriatr Soc 2018; 66:1779).
  • Special considerations:
    • Cephalosporins and Ertapenem are not predictably active vs. Enterococcus sp. or P. aeruginosa.
    • Ciprofloxacin is approved in children (age 1-17 yrs) as alternative but is not a first choice due to increased incidence of joint adverse effects.
    • Pediatrics dose: Ciprofloxacin 6-10 mg/kg (400 mg max) IV q8h or 10-20 mg/kg (750 mg max) po q12h. 
    • See N Engl J Med 365:239, 2011 for recent review of diagnosis and treatment of pyelonephritis in children.
  • Reference: Clin Infect Dis 50:625, 2010 for IDSA guidelines.
