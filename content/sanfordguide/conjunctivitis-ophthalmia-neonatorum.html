Conjunctivitis, Newborn

Clinical Setting

  • Conjunctivitis in the newborn: ophthalmia neonatorum. All doses are pediatric.
  • Eye discharge may be due to obstruction of nasolacrimal duct, chemical irritation, or infection
  • Usually due to maternal infection see CDC 2021 STI guidelines MMWR Recomm Rep 70 (RR-4):1 2021
  • Untreated can lead to visual loss, especially gonococcal infection
  • Age at onset suggests etiology.
    • Onset 1st day: Chemical reaction due to silver nitrate prophylaxis (no longer recommended, see Primary Regimens)
    • Onset 1-5 days: gonococcal infection (often very purulent)
    • Onset 5-14 days: chlamydial infection (pneumonia occasionally may be present)
    • Onset 2-16 days: Herpes simplex infection
  • Gram stain can be very helpful

Etiologies

  • Neisseria gonorrheae (disseminated infection can occur)
  • Chlamydia trachomatis
  • Herpes simplex (Types 1, 2)
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa

Diagnosis

  • N. gonorrheae: Gram stain shows gram-negative diplococci. Culture on selective media.
  • C. trachomatis: Nucleic antigen detection or antigen detection of conjuctival scraping.
  • Herpes simplex: PCR (preferred) or viral culture of conjunctiva. Direct fluorescent antigen rapid but less sensitive.
  • Rarely: Staph aureus, E. coli

Primary Regimens

  • Any child with suspected HSV eye involvement should be evaluated for systemic and CNS disease with PCR of CSF and serum and LFTs. See Herpes, neonatal
  • Prophylaxis, silver nitrate 1%: no longer recommended.
  • Treatment:
    • Acyclovir 60 mg/kg/day IV divided into 3 daily doses recommended for neonates with HSV infections
    • Topical antiviral therapy (e.g., Trifluridine or Vidarabine) under the direction of an ophthalmologist.
    • Onset day 1: Therapy not usually indicated
    • Onset 2-5 days (N. gonorrheae): Ceftriaxone 25-50 mg/kg IV x 1 dose (not to exceed 125 mg). Hospitalization usually indicated. Evaluate and treat for Chlamydia
    • Onset 3-10 days (C. trachomatis): Erythromycin base or ethyl succinate syrup 50 mg/kg/day div q6h x 14 days OR Azithromycin suspension 20 mg/kg po q24h x 3 days; oral therapy preferred to topical therapy
    • Onset 2-16 days (H. simplex):
  • Prophylaxis:
    • Not generally effective against C. trachomatis but can prevent gonococcal ophthalmitis
    • Erythromycin 0.5% ointment x 1 application
    • Tetracycline 1% ointment x 1 application

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Infants born to women with untreated Chlamydia are at high risk but prophylactic treatment is not recommended. (See Am Acad Ped Red Book 2018). Monitor closely. If followup is unlikely, treat as if infected
  • Treat asymptomatic infants of mothers with active cervical gonococcal infection at delivery

Comments

  • For gonococcal infection:
    • Treat mother and her sexual partners.
    • Often extremely purulent in infant.
    • Topical treatment is inadequate.
    • Treat neonate for concomitant C. trachomatis.
  • For chlamydial infection:
    • Treat mother & sexual partner
  • For H. simplex (Types 1, 2):
    • See also, Keratitis
  • For a general reference, see N Engl J Med 343:345, 2000 or CDC 2021 STI guidelines MMWR Recomm Rep 70 (RR-4):1 2021
