Cyclosporiasis

Clinical Setting

  • Watery diarrhea (can be profuse and protracted or remitting, relapsing), often accompanied by other gastrointestinal symptoms (e.g., anorexia, weight loss, nausea, abdominal cramps, flatulence) with or without low-grade fever.
  • A prodromal illness with nonspecific flu-like symptoms may precede the onset of diarrhea.
  • Fatigue maybe prolonged.
  • May follow a relapsing remitting pattern.
  • Uncommon extraintestinal manifestations in HIV and immunocompromised:  biliary diseases, Guillain-Barre, reactive arthritis, ocular inflammation.
  • Marked summertime seasonality worldwide, including annual outbreaks in U.S. & Canada (imported produce especially basil, cilantro, salad ingredients, raspberries). See MMWR Morb Mortal Wkly Rep. 2018 Oct 5;67(39):1101 and Foods 2020, 9:1703
  • May be asymptomatic in local persons especially children in highly endemic areas.
  • Only multiplies in external environment not within human host.

Diagnosis

  • May not be detected by routine O and P exam. Modified acid fast stain, autoflourescence with UV microscope or PCR may be necessary for diagnosis.
  • Clinical significance of Cyclospora detected only by PCR (includled in most commercial multiplex stool panels) and not by stool AFB stain is not clear.

Etiologies

  • Cyclospora cayetanensis (Clin Microbiol Rev 23:218, 2010; Lancet Infect Dis 19:e226, 2019)

Primary Regimens

  • Immunocompetent patient: TMP-SMX-DS 1 tab po bid x 7–10 days
    • Pediatric ≥2 mos-18 yrs: 8 – 10 mg/kg TMP and 40 – 50 mg/kg SMX per day, po divided bid X  7 – 10 days.
  • Immunocompromised patient (HIV+): TMP-SMX-DS 1 tab po qid x 3-4 weeks; after primary therapy, highly immunocompromised patients may require long-term suppression with TMP-SMX-DS 1 tab 3x/week

Alternative Regimens

  • No effective alternatives have been identified among other usual anti-protozoal drugs
  • If sulfa-allergic: Ciprofloxacin 500 mg po bid x 7 days (results are inconsistent and based on a single study)
  • Limited anecdotal experiences suggest that the following drugs are ineffective: albendazole, trimethoprim (when used as a single agent), azithromycin, nalidixic acid, tinidazole, metronidazole, quinacrine, tetracycline, doxycycline, and diloxanide furoate. 

Comments

  • No animal reservoir
  • Not killed by chlorine or iodine
