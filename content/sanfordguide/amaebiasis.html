Amebiasis, Entamoeba histolytica

Clinical Setting

  • Transmission is fecal oral including exposure to fecal matter during sexual contact.
  • Variety of clinical disease states:
    • Asymptomatic cyst passer; cysts do not invade or cause symptoms
      • Unknown stimulus for ex-cysting to become invasive trophozoites in intestine
    • Manifestations:
      • Diarrhea or dysentery, mild or moderate disease
      • Severe intestinal disease, hepatic abscess or other extraintestinal disease. Serology is positive with extraintestinal disease.
      • Invasive intestinal amebiasis (dysentery, colitis, appendicitis, toxic megacolon, amebomas)
      • Invasive extraintestinal amebiasis (liver abscess, peritonitis, pleuropulmonary abscess, cutaneous and genital amebic lesions)
      • Colitis can mimic ulcerative colitis; ameboma can mimic adenocarcinoma of colon.
  • Overview: Open Forum Infect Dis 5:ofy161, 2018.

Diagnosis

  • Stool microscopy will not differentiate E. histolytica cysts from non-pathogenic E. dispar or E. bangladeshi or E. moshkovskii (associated with diarrhea) unless E. histolytica is observed with ingested red blood cells (erythrophagocytosis)
  • Can differentiate E. histolytica from other amebas by stool antigen or preferably by stool PCR.
  • Serology (EIA) conversion may be delayed 7-10 days, so re-test if initial test negative soon after symptom onset. 
    • Serology remains positive indefinitely so if asymptomatic may indicate past infection in persons from highly endemic areas. 
    • Serology negative if no invasive intestinal or hepatic disease.
  • Hepatic abscess:  Antigen testing and/or PCR on aspirated material may be helpful (if available) with PCR being more sensitive than antigen-based assays.
    • Empiric therapy rather than aspiration for diagnosis is preferred in most cases of clinically suspect amebic abscess if other causes unlikely or ruled out.

Etiologies

  • Entamoeba histolytica

Primary Regimens

  • Asymptomatic cyst passer:
    • Paromomycin 25-35 mg/kg/day po in 3 divided doses x 7 days
    • Iodoquinol 650 mg po tid x 20 days
  • Diarrhea/mild dysentery:
    • (Metronidazole 500–750 mg po tid x 7-10 days) or (Tinidazole 2 gm po daily x 3 days), followed by
    • Either [Paromomycin 25-35 mg/kg/day po divided in 3 doses x 7 days] or [Iodoquinol 650 mg po tid x 20 days] to clear intestinal cysts.
  • Severe intestinal disease, hepatic abscess or other extraintestinal disease:
    • (Metronidazole 750 mg IV or po tid x 10 days) or (Tinidazole 2 gm 1x/day po x 5 days) followed by 
    • Either [Paromomycin 25-35 mg/kg/day po divided in 3 doses x 7 days] or [Iodoquinol 650 mg po tid x 20 days] to clear intestinal cysts.
      • Oral metronidazole well-absorbed so preferred unless a reason oral agents cannot be given.
    • Percutaneous hepatic abscess drainage (not aspiration) may be warranted  signs of imminent rupture, no clinical response within five days, or uncertainty about the diagnosis
      • Patients with either a large left-lobe abscess (>10 cm) should generally be given a trial of therapy first rather than drainage
    • Aspiration for uncommonly occurring pleural or cardiac effusions.
  • Pregnancy:
    • Paromomycin safe in pregnancy
    • For severe disease in pregnancy use Metronidazole.
      • Human studies suggest (but cannot definitely establish) absence of risk. No evidence of toxicity in animals.
      • ACOG allows use in this setting in pregnancy.
    • Chloroquine (600 mg base daily for two days, followed by 300 mg base daily for three weeks) is an acceptable alternative agent to metronidazole for treatment of liver abscess but less effective

Alternative Regimens

  • Asymptomatic cyst passer:
    • Diloxanide furoate (Furamide) 500 mg po tid x 10 days
      • Not easily available in most settings
  • Nitazoxanide (500 mg twice daily for 10 days)(effective in a small case series).

Comments

  • List of sources for obtaining Diloxanide, Iodoquinol, Paromomycin.
  • Secnidazole, Ornidazole available in some countries for amebiasis but secnidazole only FDA approved for vaginosis.
  • No benefit to cyst aspiration in lesions <10 cm.
  • Nitazoxanide 500 mg po bid x 3 days may be effective (J Infect Dis 184:381, 2001 & Tran R Soc Trop Med & Hyg 101:1025, 2007).
