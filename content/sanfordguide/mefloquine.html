Usage and Dosing

  • Mefloquine is an antimalarial agent. It has significant neuropsychiatric and possible vestibular side-effects; do not use for self-treatment.
  • For treatment of P. falciparum malaria Mefloquine may be life-saving if no first-line therapies such as Atovaquone-Proguanil, Artemether-Lumefantrine, or Quinine/Doxycycline are immediately available.
  • For malaria chemoprophylaxis, restrict use to situations where Atovaquone-Proguanil, Doxycycline, Tafenoquine, or Chloroquine (useful only in certain areas), is not practical or economic.
  • Mefloquine compared to other options for malaria chemoprophylaxis (Cochrane Database Syst Rev. 2017 Oct 30;10:CD006491).
  • Uncommonly prescribed for short-stay travel, practical for long-stays due to weekly dosing, especially in children.
  • Long term effects of antimalarials - assessment (National Academy of Sciences, https://doi.org/10.17226/25688).

Adult Dose

  • Prophylaxis:  250 mg (228 mg base) 1 tab po once/week, for 2-3 weeks before, every week during and for 4 weeks after travel.
    • Start 3 weeks ahead, if feasible, to assess for intolerance
  • Treatment: Alternative drug: 1250 mg x 1 or 750 mg, then 500 mg in 6–8 hr
  • Note:
    • US formulation: 250 mg tablet = 228 mg base
    • Other countries: 275 mg tablet = 250 mg base

      Adverse Effects

      • Mefloquine has an FDA "black box" warning, the highest level of warning possible, stating that neuropsychiatric and vestibular adverse reactions may persist indefinitely even after discontinuation of drug.
        • A written Medication Guide and wallet card with this boxed warning must be given to all patients by the dispensing pharmacist.
      • Side-effects in roughly 3%. Minor: headache, irritability, insomnia, weakness, diarrhea. Toxic psychosis, seizures can occur.
      • Rare: Prolonged QT interval and toxic epidermal necrolysis (Lancet 349:101, 1997). Do not use with quinine, quinidine, or halofantrine
      • Pregnancy risk category B, main option for prophylaxis in pregnancy. Safety emphasized: Malar J 13:75, 2014.
      • Large US military experience but many limitations of the available database (Am J Trop Med Hyg. 96:159, 2017).
