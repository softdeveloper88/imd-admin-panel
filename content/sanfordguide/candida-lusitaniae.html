Candida lusitaniae

Clinical Setting

  • Candidemia, disseminated candidiasis: non-neutropenic, neutropenic patients
  • Positive blood culture for yeast, suspected catheter-related bloodstream infection
  • Candida lusitaniae is usually resistant to Amphotericin B

Classification

  • Candida lusitaniae (recently renamed to Clavispora lusitaniae): an uncommon cause of invasive candida infections

Primary Regimens

  • Empiric Therapy:
    • Caspofungin 70 mg IV loading dose, then 50 mg IV qd
    • Micafungin 100 mg IV qd
    • Anidulafungin 200 mg IV loading dose, then 100 mg IV qd
  • Directed Therapy:
    • Fluconazole 800 mg (12 mg/kg) loading dose, then 400 mg IV/po qd once blood cultures have cleared and clinically stable

Alternative Regimens

  • Empiric Therapy:
    • Voriconazole 6 mg/kg bid x 2 doses, then 4 mg/kg bid
    • Posaconazole delayed-release 300 mg (3 x 100 mg tabs) po/IV 1 day, then 300 mg po /IV daily

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Duration of therapy for candidemia without persistent fungemia or metastatic complications is for 2 weeks after documented clearance of Candida from the bloodstream, resolution of symptoms attributable to candidemia, and resolution of neutropenia.
  •  Antifungals
    • Echinocandins (Caspofungin, Micafungin, Anidulafungin): echinocandins compared to polyenes or an azole was associated with better survival and is considered the first-line drug of choice by some (Clin Infect Dis 54:1110, 2012).  
    • Voriconazole:  Little advantage over Fluconazole (more drug-drug interactions) in fluconazole susceptible isolates
    • Isavuconazole: Not non-inferior to caspofungin during treatment for candidemia (Clin Infect Dis 68:1981, 2019).

Comments

  • Repeat blood cultures to confirm clearance of fungemia.
  • Evaluation for ophthalmic involvement:
    • Non-neutropenic patients: perform funduscopic examination within first week of therapy to exclude ophthalmic involvement; ocular findings, which usually resolve with systemic therapy alone, occur in ~15% of patients (Clin Infect Dis 53:262, 2011) and endophthalmitis is uncommon (<2%).
    • Neutropenic patients: perform funduscopic examination after recovery of white count as signs of ophthalmic involvement may not be seen during neutropenia.
    • Treatment of ocular disease depends on degree of involvement, chorioretinitis vs. endophthalmitis.
  • IDSA treatment guidelines: Clin Infect Dis 62:409, 2016.
