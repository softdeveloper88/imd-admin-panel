Photosensitivity Reaction

Overview

  • Drug-induced photosensitivity is a common cutaneous drug reaction caused by the interaction of a drug with ultraviolet radiation. UVA radiation (wavelength 315-400 nm) is most commonly implicated. Classified as either phototoxicity or photoallergy.
  • Phototoxicity:
    • More common.
    • Subtypes: hyperpigmentation and dyschromia, pellagra-like reaction, photo-onycholysis, eruptive telangiectasia, pseudoporphyria.
    • Direct tissue injury.
    • Dose-dependent.
    • Onset 30 min to 24 hours after sun exposure. Resembles an exaggerated sunburn.
  • Photoallergy:
    • Subtypes: lichenoid reaction, photodistributed erythema multiforme, subacute or chronic cutaneous lupus erythematosus,
    • Type IV hypersensitivity.
    • Requires previous exposure to the photosensitizing agent.
    • Appears as a delayed eczematous reaction.
  • The following drugs (listed alphabetically) are known to cause photosensitivity in some individuals. Note that photosensitivity lasts for several days after the last dose of the drug, at least for tetracyclines.
  • There is no intent to indicate relative frequency or severity of reactions in the list below.
  • References: Drug Saf 2019; 42:827, Front Allergy 2022;3:876695.

Specific Drugs

  • Antibacterial drugs
    • Cefotaxime: Manifested as photodistributed telangiectasia (Br J Dermatol 2000;143:674).
    • Ceftazidime: Increased susceptibility to sunburn observed (Lancet 1993;341(8854):1221).
    • Fluoroquinolones
      • The worst offenders have a halogen atom (e.g., fluorine, chlorine, bromine) at C-8 of the quinolone nucleus (Lomefloxacin, Sparfloxacin).
      • Recent data suggest that certain other molecular features, such as a large polar group at N-1, can mitigate the effect of the C-8 halogen. Thus, Delafloxacin seems to be relatively non-phototoxic despite having a chlorine atom at C-8.
      • After Sparfloxacin, the rank of phototoxic potential among other fluoroquinolones is Ciprofloxacin > Gemifloxacin > Levofloxacin > Norfloxacin > Ofloxacin.
      • Gatifloxacin and Moxifloxacin have not been linked to phototoxic events to date (Photochem Photobiol Sci 17:773, 2018).
    • Tetracyclines
      • Reported with Tetracycline and Doxycycline, uncommon with Minocycline.
      • Photosensitivity possibly more likely with Sarecycline than with other tetracyclines (J Am Acad Dermatol 2022;87:1150).
    • Trimethoprim: Alone and in combination with Sulfamethoxazole.
  • Antifungal drugs
    • Azoles:
      • Reported with Voriconazole, Itraconazole, Ketoconazole (but not Fluconazole).
      • Report of Posaconazole-induced phototoxic reaction, confirmed by skin biopsy (Photodermatol Photoimmunol Photomed 2024;40:e12949).
    • Flucytosine: Two reports.
    • Griseofulvin: Not thought to be a potent photosensitizer, although a few old reports exist.
    • Rezafungin: Listed in manufacturer's prescribing information.
    • Terbinafine: Two reports (Acta Dermatovenerol Croat 2024;32:113).
  • Antimycobacterial drugs
    • Isoniazid: Confirmed both by rechallenge and photopatch testing.
    • Pyrazinamide: Confirmed by rechallenge.
  • Antiparasitic drugs
    • Atovaquone-Proguanil: recent report, confirmed by photopatch testing. 
    • Chloroquine (rare reports).
    • Dapsone: Rare, onset slow (weeks to months). Case report and review of the literature: Photodermatol Photoimmunol Photomed 2023;39:407.
    • Pyrimethamine: One report (Br Med J 1974;2(5918):556).
    • Quinine (may cross-react with quinidine).
  • Antiretroviral drugs
    • Efavirenz: Three reports.
    • Saquinavir: One report.
    • Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate: One report, confirmed by histopathology and photopatch testing.
  • Antiviral drugs (non-HIV)
    • No reports.
