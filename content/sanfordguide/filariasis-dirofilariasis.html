Filariasis, Dirofilariasis

Clinical Setting

  • Dirofilariasis is caused by larvae of natural filarias of animals accidentally infecting humans.
  • Transmitted by mosquitoes.
  • Dirofilaria immitis is the most common in North America but is present worldwide except for Africa.
    • In humans, larvae lodge and die in pulmonary vessels causing coin lesions and mimic malignancy. Eosinophilia is rare.
  • Dirofilaria repens has emerged throughout Europe and is the most common worldwide outside of North America
    • D. repens may cause subcutaneous nodules or severe ocular lesions leading to disastrous surgical interventions for presumed tumor (Clin Microbiol Rev 25:507, 2012).
    • In humans, migratory subcutaneous adult worms (including on face) and blood microfilaremia can occur (Emerg Infect Dis 24:1761, 2018 and N Engl J Med. 2018, 378:e35).
  • Other species migrate to conjunctiva, subcutaneous tissue, scrotum, breasts, extremities (Trends Parasitol 2024 Mar;40(3):257).

Diagnosis

  • Serology rarely available and not helpful. Non-sensitive and non-specific.
  • Histology of extracted worm will confirm Dirofilaria 
    • No need to identify to species level using genomic analysis

Etiologies

  • Dirofilaria immitis (dog heartworm)
  • Dirofilaria repens (dog and sometimes cat)
  • Uncommon in humans
    • D. tenius (raccoon)
    • D. ursi (bear)

    • D. striata (felines)

    • D. spectans (otter)

    • Dirofilaria sp. genotype Hong Kong

      • D. hongkongensis not acceptable as a separate species (India)

Primary Regimens

  • No proven antiparasitic therapy
  • Surgical removal is usually necessary

Alternative Regimens

  • None
