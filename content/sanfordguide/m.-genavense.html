Mycobacterium genavense

Clinical Setting

  • Present in water and GI tract of healthy individuals, so important to distinguish colonization from true infection if isolated from a non-sterile site (e.g., respiratory tract).
  • Disseminated infection in HIV and non-HIV immunocompromised hosts.
  • Fever, abdominal pain, lymphadenopathy , bone marrow involvement are common presenting features
  • Fastidious organism:
    • Growth in BACTEC vials slow (mean 42 days)
    • Subcultures grow only on Middlebrook 7H11 agar containing 2 µg/mL mycobactin J—growth still insufficient for in vitro sensitivity testing, which is generally not done.

Etiologies

  • Mycobacterium genavense

Primary Regimens

  • Three-drug regimen such as: [Clarithromycin 500 mg po bid or Azithromycin 250-500 mg po once daily] + Ethambutol 15 mg/kg po once daily + [Rifabutin 300 mg po once daily or Rifampin 600 mg once daily]

Alternative Regimens

  • Clofazimine 100 mg po once daily OR Amikacin 15-25 mg/kg IV three times weekly OR Moxifloxacin 400 mg po once daily are alternatives to above agents or may be added for non-responders

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Treat for 12 months post conversion of cultures to negative; longer duration and potentially lifelong therapy may be required for those with continuing immunocompromise.

Comments

  • Macrolide containing regimens appear to be most effective.
  • For review see Infect Dis (Lond). 2018;50:329-339 and Lancet Infect Dis 2022; 22: e178-90.
