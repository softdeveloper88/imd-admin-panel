Pericarditis, Bacterial

Clinical Setting

  • Acute febrile illness with signs and symptoms of pericarditis, with or without tamponade, including fever, chest pain, shortness of breath, signs of elevated venous pressure, tachycardia, hypotension.
  • Echocardiographic signs of pericardial effusion.
  • Purulent pericardial fluid.
  • Recommendations are for empiric therapy while culture and Gram strain results are pending.

Diagnosis

  • Urgent pericardiocentesis to establish the diagnosis.
    • Send pericardial fluid for bacterial, fungal and mycobacterial cultures, obtain cultures of blood and send other specimens for culture as clinically indicated. 

Etiologies

  • Staph. aureus (MSSA, MRSA)
  • Strep. pneumoniae
  • Streptococci
  • Neisseria meningitidis
  • Enterobacteriaceae

Primary Regimens

  • Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL) + (Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q24h or Cefepime 2 gm IV q12h)

Alternative Regimens

  • Vancomycin as above or Daptomycin 10 mg/kg IV q24h + Ciprofloxacin 750 mg po bid or 400 mg IV bid or Aztreonam 2 gm IV q6h

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Empiric use of vancomycin is recommended if local prevalence of MRSA is high; use Nafcillin, Oxacillin, or Cefazolin for confirmed MSSA infection.
  • Modify regimen and narrow coverage based on results of culture and susceptibility tests.

Comments

  • Drainage required in most cases; immediate drainage indicated if there is evidence of tamponade (see Eur Heart J 34:1186, 2013 for management of pericardial effusion).
  • Chronic restrictive pericarditis occurs in 20-30% of cases (Curr Opin Cardiol 27:308, 2012); this complication may be prevented by early intrapericardial fibrinolysis (Crit Care 15:220, 2011).
  • Review article: JAMA 314:1498, 2015. 
  • See Eur Heart J 2015 ; 36:2921 for European guidelines.
