Bedaquiline
Tradename: Sirturo

Usage and Dosing

  • Bedaquiline is used for treatment of multidrug-resistant (MDR) pulmonary M. tuberculosis (defined as resistant to at least Isoniazid and Rifampin), or extensively drug resistant (XDR) pulmonary M. tuberculosis (defined as MDR + resistance to any fluoroquinolone and an injectable aminoglycoside): see N Engl J Med 371:723, 2014 and Clin Infect Dis 60:188, 2015. 
  • Bedaquiline should be used ONLY as one component of a multi-drug regimen that includes at least 2 or more other active agents.
  • Provisional CDC Guidelines: Use can also be considered for persons with extrapulmonary TB, children, pregnant women, or persons with HIV (MMWR  62(RR-09):1, 2013).
  • Caution: numerous drug interactions as a CYP3A4 substrate (see chart below, not all-inclusive).
  • Bedaquiline terminal half-life is about 5.5 months. Hence, resistance could occur if Bedaquiline is the only circulating drug. Consider, if clinically reasonable, stopping Bedaquiline 4-5 months before discontinuation of other drugs in a multidrug treatment regimen.
  • Avoid alcohol during therapy due to potential aggravation of liver toxic potential of Bedaquiline.
  • Emergence of resistance has been reported (Clin Infect Dis 2018; 66:1625). 
  • A 20 mg/mL syrup (stable for 15 days at room temp and 30 ºC) or a 20 mg/mL sugar-free suspension (stable for 30 days at room temp and 30 ºC) is easily prepared using 100 mg tablets and common, affordable ingredients. Convenient alternative for children and patients with dysphagia in settings where the 20 mg dispersible tablets are not available  (Int J Tuberc Lung Dis 2023;27:189).

Adult Dose

  • Directly observed therapy (DOT): 400 mg once daily for 2 weeks, then 200 mg 3x/week for 24 weeks, taken with food and always used in combination with other active anti-TB medications.

Adverse Effects

  • NOTE: Bedaquiline in clinical trials was administered as one component of a multiple drug regimen, so side effects were common, yet difficult to assign to a particular drug.
  • Most common side effects are nausea, vomiting, arthralgia, headache, hyperuricemia.
  • Imbalance in mortality in phase IIb C208 stage 2 trial (10/79 for Bedaquiline versus 2/81 for placebo), but deaths were deemed unrelated to study drug or attributable to tuberculosis.
  • Moderate QTc increases (average of 10-16 ms over the 24 weeks of therapy):
    • ECG should be obtained at baseline, 2, 12 and 24 weeks of Bedaquiline therapy to monitor the QTc interval; monitor ECG weekly if concurrent use of other drugs that prolong QTc (e.g., fluoroquinolone, macrolide).
    • Monitor serum levels of potassium, magnesium, and calcium.
    • Reference: Clin Infect Dis 2021;73:2083
    • Discontinue Bedaquiline for clinically significant ventricular arrhythmia or a QTc interval of > 500 ms (confirmed by repeat ECG).
    • If coadministered with other agents that may prolong QTc, frequent ECG assessment is recommended.
    • If possible, concomitant administration of Bedaquiline with fluoroquinolone antibiotics that have a potential for significant QT prolongation (Gatifloxacin, Moxifloxacin, and Sparfloxacin) should be avoided.
  • Elevated transaminases. Monitor liver-associated enzymes at baseline and at least monthly throughout therapy.
  • Potential risks of pancreatitis, myopathy, myocardial injury, severe hepatotoxicity.

    Comments

    • CDC guidelines: MMWR 62(RR-09):1-12, 2013.
    • Bedaquiline, a diarylquinoline, inhibits mycobacterial ATP synthase, a novel mechanism of action:
      • Does not exhibit cross-resistance to other currently available TB drugs.
      • Always use in combination with other anti-TB medications to prevent selection of resistant mutants.
    • No drug-drug PK interactions with Isoniazid, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol, Kanamycin, Ofloxacin, Cycloserine.
    • Consultation with an expert in management of drug-resistant TB strongly advised.
    • Review of kinetics and drug-drug interactions: J Antimicrob Chemother 69: 2310, 2014.
    • Other references:  Lancet 380:986, 2012; Antimicrob Agents Chemother 56:3271, 2012; N Engl J Med 360:2397, 2009; N Engl J Med 352:933, 2005 & 371: 723, 2014.
