Prostatitis, Bacterial, Chronic

Clinical Setting

  • Low grade fever, urinary frequency, dysuria, urgency, perineal discomfort.
  • Chronic infection and positive urine or prostatic fluid cultures.
  • See comments section for management of chronic pain syndrome or chronic prostatitis without infection.
  • Reference: Clin Infect Dis 50: 1641, 2010.

Etiologies

  • Enterobacteriaceae (80%)
  • Enterococci (15%)
  • P. aeruginosa
  • Burkholderia pseudomallei (uncommon; usually in acute prostatitis)

Primary Regimens

  • Ciprofloxacin 500 mg po bid  x 4 weeks or
  • Levofloxacin 750 mg po q24h x 4 weeks (see Comment re higher dose than approved)

Alternative Regimens

  • TMP-SMX-DS 1 tab po bid x 1–3 months
  • Azithromycin (if C. Trachomatis suspected)

Comments

  • Levofloxacin: FDA approved dose is 500 mg; editors prefer 750 mg dose because of higher drug levels and reports of treatment failures at the lower dose.
  • Urine culture is important; for best results collect urine after prostatic massage.
  • With treatment failures consider infected prostatic calculi.
  • Management of chronic prostatitis:  Reviewed Canadian Urol Assoc J (6 Suppl 3): S161, 2018
    • Confirm diagnosis
    • Multimodal therapeutic approach
    • Use the "5 A's"
      • Avoid dietary or physical activities (e.g., bike riding) that exacerbate symptoms
      • Antibiotics (if 4 week Rx fails, no further antimicrobials should be used unless confirmed UTI)
      • Alpha-blockers:  Part of a multimodal treatment plan
      • Anti-inflammatory agents: Part of a multimodal treatment plan
      • 5-alpha reductase inhibitors: Only in men > 50 years and enlarged prostate on exam
  • Management of chronic pain syndrome, chronic prostatitis without infection (Chronic Prostate Pain Syndrome (CPPS): Reviewed  Am J Mens Health: 14, 2020)
    • Most common prostatic syndrome.
    • Patient presents symptoms of prostatitis but negative cultures and no cells in prostatic secretions.
    • Etiology unknown.
    • Antimicrobial therapy not recommended.
  • Case reports that Fosfomycin penetrates prostate and success with 3 gm po once daily x 12-16 weeks (Clin Infect Dis 61: 1141, 2015). Another 15 patients responded to 3 gm po q48-72 hrs x 6 weeks (Antimicrob Ag Chemother 60:1854, 2016).  Fosfomycin may be an oral option if no other alternatives (e.g., ESBL-producing organism, as in these cases).
  • Excellent Review: BJU International 116:509, 2015.
