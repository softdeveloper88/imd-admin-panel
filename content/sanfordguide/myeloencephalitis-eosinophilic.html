Gnathostoma spinigerum

Clinical Setting

  • Transmitted by ingesting undercooked and raw infected freshwater fish, though they can also become infected by eating raw or undercooked infected freshwater eels, frogs, birds, and reptiles. 
  • Sushi in US and Europe usually made with saltwater fish, gnathostomiasis most commonly acquired in SE Asia but emerging especially in Latin America and also Africa.
  • Cutaneous larva migrans syndrome with urticaria and with or without cutaneous hemorrhagic necrosis (most common).
  • Meningitis/meningoencephalitis with eosinophilic CSF pleocytosisl with Asian species.
    • Intracerebral hemorrhage if larval invasion of blood vessels.
    • Patients with neurologic symptoms should be carefully monitored and plans for prompt intervention should be put into place.
  • Migration to other tissues (visceral larva migrans), can result in cough, hematuria, and ocular involvement.
  • Other causes of eosinophilic meningitis include Angiostrongylus cantonensis and Baylisascaris procyonis.

Diagnosis

  • Serology done in reference centers in Asia and Europe
  • CT scan for large intaparencymal hemorrhage but subdural and subarachnoid hemorrhage and larval tracks best seen by susceptibility-weighted MRI.

Etiologies

  • Gnathostoma spinigerum (cutaneous ± CNS disease)
  • Other Gnathostoma sp. in Asia including G. hispidum (cutaneous disease only)
  • G. binucleatum in Latin America (cutaneous disease only)

Primary Regimens

  • Cutaneous disease:
    • Albendazole 400 mg po bid x 21 days
    • Ivermectin 200 μg/kg/day po x 2 days
  • CNS disease: Supportive care, monitor for cerebral hemorrhage.
  • Pediatric:
    • Treatment: Children >15 kg can use Ivermectin 200 ug/ po as a single dose
      • Limited data suggests no safety issues <15 kg: PLoS Negl Trop Dis 15:e0009144, 2021)
      • WHO allows Albendazole use as single dose therapy in children 1 year of age and older and this is acknowledged in CDC materials.
      • CDC states children aged > 6 yrs may receive an adult dose.
  • Pregnancy:
    • WHO allows use of Albendazole in 2nd and 3rd trimesters.
      • Remains FDA pregnancy risk category C.
      • Large systematic analyses show no harm in 1st trimester Albendazole (Intl J Parasitol 2019 Jun;49(7):541)

Alternative Regimens

  • CNS or ocular disease: Steroids used in case reports; Albendazole or Ivermectin therapy has shown both benefit and harm in case reports (Emerg Infect Dis 17:1174, 2011).
  • Comprehensive review of therapy (Waterborne Parasitol 2023 Sep 7:33:e00207)

Comments

  • Comprehensive review of clinical trials (Food Waterborne Parasitol 2023 Sep 7:33:e00207).
  • For cutaneous disease unresponsive to one agent, the other one often works.
  • Late relapses occur (up to 26 months), monitor patients for at least 1 year.  Can re-treat with the same or the other drug.
  • Albendazole will stimulate the outward migration of the larva and make it more accessible to excisional biopsy.
  • Ivermectin may cause a temporary increase of cutaneous symptoms.
  • Albendazole 200 mg tablets available from Expert Compounding Pharmacy (see Antiparasitic Drugs, Sources).  
  • Generic Albendazole (Albenza) marketed by Amneal is very costly and is difficult for retail pharmacies to access.
