Peritonitis, Dialysis (CAPD) Associated

Clinical Setting

  • Infectious complication of chronic ambulatory peritoneal dialysis (CAPD).
  • Presents as abdominal pain, cloudy effluent and dialysate WBC of >100 cells/μL (over 50% neutrophils). Fever in half of the patients.
    • For diagnosis: concentrate several hundred mL of removed dialysis fluid by centrifugation. Gram stain concentrate and then inject into aerobic/anaerobic blood culture bottles.
    • A positive Gram stain will help guide initial therapy.
    • Rapid point of care test of dialysis effluent available outside of US:
      • Periflex lateral flow antigen detection device by Mologic Ltd
        • Detects neutrophil matrix metalloproteinase-8 and elevated amounts of IL-6 within minutes
        • Ref.: Kidney Int 2020; 97:1253
  • If culture shows S. epidermidis, good chance of “saving” the dialysis catheter. Indications for catheter removal:
    • If multiple Gram-negative bacilli are cultured, consider catheter-induced bowel perforation and the need for catheter removal.
    • Relapse with same  microorganism(s) within one month
    • Failure of clinical response within 5 days
    • Fungal peritonitis
    • Infection of catheter skin exit site and/or catheter subcutaneous tunnel
  • Infection almost always limited to abdominal cavity; complicating bacteremia is rare.

Etiologies

  • Gram positive cocci (45%)
  • Gram negative bacilli (15%)
  • Mixture (1%)
  • Fungi (2%)
  • M. tuberculosis (0.1%)

Primary Regimens

  • Usually treat by adding drugs to dialysis fluid; if bacteremia documented/likely, treat via IV route.
  • See Intraperitoneal Drug Dosing for specific dose recommendations
    • For gram-positive cocci: Vancomycin added to the dialysis fluid.
    • For gram-negative bacilli: Cefepime or Ceftazidime, Carbapenem, Aztreonam, Ciprofloxacin or an Aminoglycoside (Gentamicin).
    • Add an antifungal only if yeast seen on Gram-stain.
  • Start with continuous therapy (drug in all dialysis bags) until culture results available. Then, if specific therapy with a beta-lactam, continuous therapy; if specific therapy with an aminoglycoside, intermittent therapy. Two strategies for treatment: intermittent dialysis or continuous dialysis.
  • Do not co-administer the same drug IP (intraperitoneal) and systemically (either oral or IV therapy)
  • See Comment for prophylactic anti-fungal therapy

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • Guidelines: Perit Dial Int 2022; 42(2):110.
  • General Review: N Engl J Med 2021;385: 1786
  • If polymicrobic gram-negative flora is cultured, consider possibility of catheter-induced bowel perforation, and/or concomitant underlying GI pathology (e.g., dead bowel).
  • Treatment Guideline (Perit Dial Int 2016;36:481) suggests anti-fungal prophylaxis with either nystatin po or fluconazole (IV/po)  concomitant with antibacterials that are used for any purpose, not only for just CAPD-catheter associated peritonitis
    • Limited supporting data
    • With concern for selection of resistant Candida species, drug-drug interactions if fluconazole used, and uncertain effects on intestinal microbiota,  the Guide is reluctant  to recommend routine anti-fungal prophylaxis every time an antibiotic is administered to a CAPD patient
