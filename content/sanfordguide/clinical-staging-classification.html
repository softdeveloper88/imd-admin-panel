HIV Clinical Staging, Classification

Clinical Staging of HIV/AIDS

  • There are two widely-recognized systems for staging of HIV/AIDS: U.S. CDC classification and WHO clinical staging.
  • These staging systems were established in the 1980s, prior to the widespread availability and use of potent antiretroviral therapy, and were used mostly to track the evolving epidemic.  The use of the staging systems in the current era is mostly anachronistic.

U.S. CDC Classification System

  • The CDC classification system emphasizes the importance of CD4 lymphocyte testing in clinical management of HIV infected persons. The system is based on 3 ranges of CD4 counts & 3 clinical categories giving a matrix of 9 exclusive categories. This system is less valuable in clinical decision-making today because of the availability of tests to quantify levels of viral RNA.
  • CDC Criteria for HIV infection:
    • Persons 13 years or older with a positive combination HIV antibody - antigen test with a confirmatory HIV RNA test.
  • CDC Classification System:
Clinical Category 
A
  • Asymptomatic HIV infection
  • Persistent generalized lymphadenopathy (PGL) (Nodes in 2 or more extrainguinal sites, at least 1 cm in diameter for ≥3 mos)
  • Acute (primary) HIV illness
B
  • Symptomatic, but not Category A or C conditions. Examples include but not limited to:
    • Bacillary angiomatosis
    • Candidiasis, vulvovaginal: persistent >1 month, poorly responsive to therapy
    • Candidiasis, oropharyngeal
    • Cervical dysplasia, severe or carcinoma in situ
    • Constitutional symptoms, e.g., fever (38.5°) or diarrhea lasting >1 month
  • The above must be attributed to HIV infection or have a clinical course or management complicated by HIV.
C
  • Candidiasis: esophageal, trachea, bronchi
  • Coccidioidomycosis, extrapulmonary
  • Cryptococcosis, extrapulmonary
  • Cervical cancer, invasive
  • Cryptosporidiosis, chronic intestinal (>1 month)
  • CMV retinitis, or CMV in other than liver, spleen, nodes
  • HIV encephalopathy
  • Herpes simplex with mucocutaneous ulcer >1 month, bronchitis, pneumonia
  • Histoplasmosis: disseminated, extrapulmonary
  • Isosporiasis, chronic, >1 month
  • Kaposi's sarcoma
  • Lymphoma: Burkitt's, immunoblastic, primary in brain
  • M. avium or M. kansasii, extrapulmonary
  • M. tuberculosis, pulmonary or extrapulmonary
  • Pneumocystis jiroveci pneumonia
  • Pneumonia, recurrent (≥2 episodes in 1 year)
  • Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy
  • Salmonella bacteremia, recurrent
  • Toxoplasmosis, cerebral
  • Wasting syndrome due to HIV
  • Clinical category combined with CD4 counts gives the CDC classification
  • Note: Classifications A3, B3 and C (all) require reporting as AIDS.

WHO Clinical Staging System

  • The World Health Organization uses the following clinical staging system for HIV/AIDS.
WHO StageClinical Symptoms
1
  • Normal level of activity
  • No clinical symptoms
  • May have persistent generalized lymphadenopathy (PGL)
  • Performance scale 1
2
  • Symptomatic, but normal level of activity
  • Weight loss <10%
  • Minor skin rash
  • Herpes zoster
  • Recurrent oral ulcerations
  • Angular cheilitis
  • Fungal nail infections
  • Recurrent upper respiratory infection
  • Performance scale 2
3
  • Bedridden <50% of the day during the last month
  • Weight loss >10%
  • Chronic diarrhea >1 month
  • Recurrent fevers >1 month
  • Oral thrush
  • Oral hairy leukoplakia
  • Severe presumed bacterial infections
  • Acute necrotizing ulcerative stomatitis, gingivitis, or periodontitis
  • Pulmonary tuberculosis
  • Unexplained anemia (< 8 gm/dL), neutropenia (< 500 cells/μl),
    and/or chronic thrombocytopenia (< 50,000/μl)
  • Performance scale 3
4
  • Bedridden >50% of the day during the last month
  • Cryptococcal meningitis
  • Toxoplasmosis of the brain
  • Pneumocystis jiroveci pneumonia
  • Chronic Herpes simplex infection
  • Esophageal Candidiasis
  • Extrapulmonary Tuberculosis
  • Disseminated non-TB mycobacterial disease
  • Lymphoma
  • Progressive Multifocal Leukoencephalopathy
  • Isosporiasis
  • Cytomegalovirus disease
  • Disseminated endemic mycoses (Histoplasmosis, Penicilliosis,
    Coccidiomycosis)
  • Salmonellosis
  • Invasive cervical carcinoma
  • Visceral leishmaniasis
  • Kaposi sarcoma
  • Dementia
  • Performance scale 4
