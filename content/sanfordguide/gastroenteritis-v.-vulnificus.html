Vibrio vulnificus: Cellulitis, Sepsis

Clinical Setting

  • Associated with exposure to contaminated sea water and/or consumption of contaminated seafood.
  • Gastroenteritis with nausea, vomiting, diarrhea.
    • In healthy persons: self-limited, antibiotics generally not needed.
    • In immunocompromised persons or those with liver disease: antibiotics recommended to prevent systemic complications (See also Vibrio vulnificus).
  • Immunocompromised persons and those with underlying liver disease, especially hemochromatosis, may have life-threatening infection with skin lesions, (cellulitis, which may include hemorrhagic bullae), bacteremia, and necrotizing fasciitis: antimicrobial therapy is indicated.
  • Review comparing different Vibrio infections (Nat Rev Dis Primers 2018;4:8).
  • General references with images of skin lesions (N Engl J Med 2018;379:375; N Engl J Med 2016;375:1780).

Etiologies

  • Vibrio vulnificus

Primary Regimens

  • Severe disease:
    • (Ceftazidime 1 gm IV q8h or Ceftriaxone 2 gm IV q24h) + (Ciprofloxacin 750 mg po bid or 400 mg IV q12h or Doxycycline 100 mg po/IV bid)

Alternative Regimens

  • Ciprofloxacin 750 mg po bid or 400 mg IV q12h
  • Levofloxacin 750 mg po/IV once daily

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Resistance patterns vary widely geographically.
  • Resistance to Ampicillin and Doxycycline is common regardless of location.

Comments

  • See CDC recommendations for treatment here.
  • May require adjunctive surgical debridement, including amputation.
  • Review: Microorganisms. 2025; 13:128.
