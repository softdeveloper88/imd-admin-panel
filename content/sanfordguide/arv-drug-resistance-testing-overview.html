ARV Drug Resistance Testing, Overview

Overview

Genotype and Phenotype Resistance Testing

  • Resistance Guidelines, see Clin Infect Dis 2019, 68:177. 
  • Genotypic testing is the preferred resistance testing method to guide therapy in treatment-naive patients (Updated Genotypic Mutations):
    • At time of initial evaluation; if antiretroviral therapy (ART) is delayed, repeat testing prior to initiation of ART.
    • Patients who fail ART if HIV RNA >500 - 1000 copies/mL.
    • Pregnancy (HIV+): test prior to initiating ART or when HIV RNA >500 - 1000 copies/mL.
    • Look for mutations in the reverse transcriptase and protease genes. If considering treatment with integrase inhibitor, look for integrase gene mutations.
    • Acute HIV infection: at time of diagnosis.
    • Chronic HIV:
  • Genotypic resistance testing is NOT recommended:
    • If > 4 weeks after stopping ART.
    • For patients with HIV RNA <500 copies/mL.
  • Phenotypic resistance testing is recommended:
    • In addition to genotypic testing if complex drug resistance mutation patterns are present, especially if genotypic resistance to protease inhibitors.

Genotype vs. Phenotype Resistance Testing

  • Genotype Resistance Testing
    • Identifies the specific mutation
    • Results are available in 1-2 weeks
    • Cost $300-500
    • Suggested use either pre-ART or to analyze failure on initial ART regimen
  • Phenotype Resistance Testing
    • Does not identify specific mutation
    • Results are available in 2-3 weeks
    • Cost $800-1500
    • Suggested use for known or complex resistance mutation patterns.

Low Frequency Resistance Mutations

  • Standard PCR sequencing fails to detect low frequency drug resistance mutations.
  • With ultra-sensitive methods, low frequency resistance mutations in approximately 10% of patients with increased risk of treatment failure (Hazard Ratio 2.3)–especially to NNRTIs (Hazard Ratio 2.6).

Results Analysis

  • Does resistance testing predict virologic response? Using genotype & phenotype testing to guide treatment results in:
    • Extra 0.5-0.6 log10 copies/mL (approx. 30,000 copies/mL or 3-fold) reduction in viral load
    • Extra 10-20% of patients with viral loads below 200-500 copies/mL
    • Improved long-term virologic outcome in treatment-experienced patients 
    • Fair ability to predict phenotype from genotype 
  • References: https://hivdb.stanford.edu/ and IAS-USA Resistance Mutation Panel

Comments

  • Genotype resistance testing detects mutations in resistance-associated target proteins. Updated figures at IAS-USA
  • With “ultra-deep” sequencing, increasing % of treatment-naïve patients harbor resistant virus (JAMA 305:1327, 2011). Expert advice on interpretation of test results improves virologic response.
  • Use PCR to amplify HIV protease & reverse transcriptase genes; some labs do not detect mutations in the envelope gene or integrase gene. Sequence genes, report mutations found, mutations pattern used to predict response to antiretrovirals. See http://hivdb.stanford.edu for current mutation patterns. Selected mutations (or combinations) may decrease replication capacity of HIV clinical isolates.
    • “Replication capacity” means number of progeny produced per round of infection
      per unit of time.
    • “Fitness” means relative reproductive success of various subtypes of HIV.
    • “Virulence” means ability to destroy CD4 lymphocytes or impair immune system function.
