Mycobacterium leprae, Leprosy

Clinical Setting

  • Mycobacterium leprae is a mycobacterium that causes leprosy. There are two main forms:
    • Paucibacillary (intermediate, tuberculoid, borderline tuberculoid)
    • Multibacillary (borderline, borderline-lepromatous, lepromatous)
  • Erythema nodosum leprosum (ENL)
    • New painful, tender, erythematous nodules on therapy due to antigen-antibody complex deposition in patients with high bacillary loads
    • Seen in lepromatous and borderline-lepromatous cases
    • Systemic symptoms common
  • Reversal reactions are increased erythema, warmth, edema, ulceration of preexisting lesions of skin and nerves
    • Typically seen in patients with borderline tuberculoid, borderline, and borderline-lepromatous leprosy
    • Systemic symptoms uncommon

Etiologies

  • Mycobacterium leprae
  • Mycobacterium lepromatosis

Primary Regimens

  • WHO treatment recommendations, for resource limited settings (see WHO guidelines for pediatric dosing):
    • Paucibacillary (intermediate, tuberculoid, borderline tuberculoid) (see WHO guidelines for pediatric dosing):
      • (Dapsone 100 mg once daily + Rifampin 600 mg once monthly + Clofazimine 300 mg one monthly and 50 mg once daily) po x 6 months
    • Multibacillary (borderline, borderline-lepromatous, lepromatous):
      • Same as above x 12 months
    • Preventive treatment for contacts of leprosy patients after excluding leprosy and TB disease
      • Rifampin 600 mg single dose for age 15 years and older (see WHO guidelines for pediatric dosing)
  • US National Hansen's Disease Programs treatment recommendations (see NHDP guidelines for pediatric dosing and treatment of pregnant patients):
    • Paucibacillary (intermediate, tuberculoid, borderline tuberculoid
      • Moxifloxacin 400 mg + Rifampin 600 mg +  Minocycline 100 mg  po  all administered one monthly for 12 months
    • Multibacillary (borderline, borderline-lepromatous, lepromatous):
      • Moxifloxacin 400 mg + Rifampin 600 mg +  Minocycline 100 mg  po  all administered one monthly for 24 months
    • Preventive treatment for contacts of leprosy patients:
      • Not recommended currently, but under study: see Lancet Glob Health. 2024;12:e1017; PLoS Negl Trop Dis . 2024;18:e0012446; and BMC Infect Dis. 2024; 24:226.
  • Treatment of Reversal Reaction (Cell-mediated immune reaction, Type 1 reaction):
    • Mild: symptomatic treatment with non-steroidal anti-inflammatory agents
    • Severe:
      • Prednisone 60-80 mg po daily x 2 weeks, tapered to 40-60 mg po daily and then gradually tapered until eliminated over 2-6 months (use with monthly instead of daily rifampin dosing).
        • Methotrexate 7.5 mg to 20 mg once weekly may be effective as a corticosteroid-sparing agent (Travel Med Infect Dis. Sep-Oct 2020;37:101670).
  • Treatment of Erythema nodosum leprosum (ENL) (Antigen-antibody immune-complex mediated, Type 2 reaction)
    • ENL is due to heavy antigen load and immune complexes and is not necessarily a post-treatment reaction only.  Can occur pre-treatment or many years post-treatment.
    • Mild: symptomatic treatment with non-steroidal anti-inflammatory agents
    • Severe:
      • Prednisone 40-80 mg po daily tapered to lowest dose needed to control the reaction; should be used for treatment of neutritis (use with monthly instead of daily rifampin dosing).
        • Methotrexate 7.5 mg to 20 mg once weekly may be effective as a corticosteroid-sparing agent (Travel Med Infect Dis. Sep-Oct 2020;37:101670).
      • Thalidomide 300 mg po in divided doses, tapered to 100-200 mg daily within 2 weeks, then 50-200 mg within 2 weeks, then 50-200 mg daily as needed to control the reaction
        • Contraindicated in pregnancy: Women of child bearing age must use two forms of birth control and take a monthly pregnancy test while on therapy; rifampin interaction with birth control pills render these less effective and 2 barrier methods should be used.
      • Combinations of the above agents may be used 
    • See Indian Dermatol Online J. 2020; 11:482 for review of other treatment options.

Alternative Regimens

  • WHO treatment recommendations
    • Rifampin resistance
      • ([Ofloxacin 400 mg once daily OR Levofloxacin 500 mg once daily OR Moxifloxacin 400 mg once daily] + Minocycline 100 mg once daily +  Clofazimine 50 mg once daily) x 6 months then ([Ofloxacin 400 mg once daily or Levofloxacin 500 mg once daily or Moxifloxacin 400 mg once daily OR Minocycline 100 mg once daily] + Clofazimine 50 mg once daily) x 18 months OR
      • ([Ofloxacin 400 mg once daily or Levofloxacin 500 mg once daily or Moxifloxacin 400 mg once daily] + Clarithromycin 500 mg once daily + Clofazimine 50 mg once daily) x 6 months then ([Ofloxacin 400 mg once daily or Levofloxacin 500 mg once daily or Moxifloxacin 400 mg once daily] +  Clofazimine 50 mg once daily) x 18 months
    • Rifampin and ofloxacin resistance
      • (Clarithromycin 500 mg + Minocycline 100 mg + Clofazimine 50 mg once daily) x 6 months then ([Clarithromycin 500 mg OR Minocycline 100 mg] + Clofazimine 50 mg once daily) x 18 months

Comments

  • Proof-of-concept study bedaquiline monotherapy cleared M. leprae from skin lesions (assayed by inoculation of tissue homogenates into footpads of mice) bby 4 weeks with improvement in the appearance of skin lesions by 7 weeks (N Engl J Med 2024; 391:2212). 
  • Thalidomide is available in the U.S. at 1-800-4-CELGENE. Although thalidomide is effective, WHO no longer recommends its use because of potential toxicity (J Infect Dis 193:1743, 2006), however the majority of leprosy experts feel thalidomide remains drug of choice for ENL under strict supervision.
