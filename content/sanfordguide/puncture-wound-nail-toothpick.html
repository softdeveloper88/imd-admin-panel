Puncture Wound, Foot

Clinical Setting

  • Puncture wound to foot from sharp pointed object, e.g., nail or a toothpick
  • Osteomyelitis evolves in only 1–2% of plantar puncture wounds.
  • Risk factors for infection
    • Diabetes
    • Deep puncture wound
    • Retained foreign body
    • Wound contamination with organic matter

Etiologies

  • P. aeruginosa (more common in non-diabetics than diabetics; children)
  • Staph. aureus, Streptococcus sp., mixed flora (particularly in diabetics)

Primary Regimens

  • Cleanse wound
  • Consider X-ray or ultrasound to exclude presence of foreign body
  • Tetanus booster (if due; other tetanus prevention steps as appropriate)
  • Observe for evidence of infection, treat based on culture results (see Comments)

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • Role of prophylactic antibiotics unclear and these are not routinely recommended, but may be considered for those with risk factors for infection.
  • Reference:  J Am Podiatr Med Assoc. 2020 Nov 2;20-206.
  • Outcomes of puncture wound infections in diabetics and non-diabetics: diabetics do worse (J Foot Ankle Surg. 2019; 58:1064-1066).
