Malaria, Overview

Overview

  • In the US, malaria is uncommon in usual tourist travelers.
    • Most cases are seen in immigrants, immigrants returning home to visit friends and relatives, and long-stay temporary residents of endemic areas who do not adhere to long-term prophylaxis.
  • Over 90% of malaria seen in US is due to P. falciparum or P. vivax
    • Dual infections may occur

  • CDC algorithm for diagnosis and treatment of malaria in the US. 

  • For general information on malaria prophylaxis and treatment:

    • CDC Malaria website

Prophylaxis

  • Drugs plus personal protection: screens, nets, 30–35% DEET skin repellent (avoid 95% products in children), picaridin skin repellent, permethrin spray on clothing and mosquito nets.
    • Review of insect repellents: JAMA 2016;316:766.
  • Main drugs: Atovaquone/proguanil, Doxycycline, Mefloquine, Tafenoquine (Arakoda in US; Kodatef in Australia).
    • Chloroquine only effective in very limited areas of the world; do not use mefloquine in SE Asia
    • Tafenoquine requires quantitative G6PD testing and use criteria that are different than for Primaquine.

Diagnosis

  • Usually by microscopy
  • CDC offers species confirmation and drug resistance testing. Instructions here.
  • Alternative Rapid antigen detection test (Binax NOW) (Clin Infect Dis 49:908, 2009; Clin Infect Dis 54:1637, 2012) used in many hospital laboratories, not approved as a point of care test.
    • Original trials: >96% sensitive for clinically significant P. falciparum parasitemias.
    • Caution: A negative RDT (US: Binax; HRP based) no longer reliably rules out P. falciparum infection due to the increasing global prevalence of HRP2/3 deletion mutants.
      • A negative RDT should be quickly followed by a malaria blood film. Malar J 2022 21:26, 2022. 
    • 50% sensitivity for low non-faciparum parasitemias which is the usual case.
    • High specificity; treat a positive test.
    • Need microscopy to speciate.
    • Can stay positive for over a month after successful treatment.
    • PCR is the gold-standard, gives accurate speciation and offered by commercial reference labs.

Treatment

  • Artemether-Lumefantrine is the only artemisinin combination therapy (ACT) that is FDA approved.
    • Not widely stocked by hospital or retail pharmacies in the US.
      • Generally available from regional drug distribution warehouses with up to 24-hour delay in initiating therapy for a medical emergency.
        • Do not delay therapy in order to obtain Coartem if another 1st line agent is more readily available.
      • Call 1 855-COARTEM for expedited availability information
    • For uncomplicated malaria the oral ACT Artemether/lumefantrine is recommended as first choice by the US CDC for all species of malaria unless assuredly chloroquine sensitive.
      • P. falciparum is rarely chloroquine sensitive and other species uncommonly chloroquine resistant.
    • Consolidated WHO guidelines GRADE give a strong recommendation to preferentially treat adults and children with all of uncomplicated P. vivax, P. ovale, P. malariae or P. knowlesi malaria with either an oral ACT (including all pregnant women including in the first trimester)
      • However, chloroquine remains an option unless there is known chloroquine resistance in which case an ACT should be used.
    • Artemisinin resistance is present in the Greater Mekong subregion of Asia Antimicrob Agents Chemother 2021; AAC0112121 and in Eritrea, Rwanda and Uganda.
      • Artemisinin resistance alone rarely leads to treatment failure but resistance to both the artemisinin derivative and the partner ACT drug (Mekong region only) does.
      • Do not use ACT for malaria from the Mekong region
  • FDA approved Artesunate for Injection (intravenous artesunate; Amivas), is now commercially available in the US through major distributors (Cardinal Health, Americsource Bergen, and McKesson).  
    • See Amivas website for distributors' 24/7 emergency numbers; all voice messages will receive a response in <30 minutes.
    • Wholesale cost is $5,000 USD per vial; the average 80 kg person requires 6 vials for the first day and then 2 vials per day for each subsequent day that parasitemia is greater than 1%.
    • CDC no longer distributes no-cost antimalarial drugs.
  • Pregnancy:  
    • Artemether-lumefantrine can now be used in all trimesters in pregnancy per WHO and CDC evidence review and policy.  See WHO analysis.

  • Pediatrics:

    • IV artesunate can be used in infants, children

    • The AAP now recommends that Doxycycline can safely be administered for short durations (≤21 days) regardless of age.
    • CDC still prefers clindamycin for children <8  yrs over doxycycline
  • IV quinidine gluconate no longer produced or available in the US.
