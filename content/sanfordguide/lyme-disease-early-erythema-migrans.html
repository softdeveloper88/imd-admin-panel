Erythema migrans, Lyme Disease, Early

Clinical Setting

  • Erythema migrans skin lesions (70-80% of patients). Early manifestation of Lyme disease, a spirochetal tick borne illness. See Lyme Disease, Overview for other stages and syndromes.
  • Most common in the US between April and September in the Northeast, Middle Atlantic and North Central states. Rarely in Northern California and Oregon.
  • Skin lesions are usually homogeneous, not target-like, see Ann Intern Med 136:421, 2002.

Diagnosis

  • For details on diagnostic methods see: Lyme Disease, Diagnosis and Emerging Infect Dis 22:1169, 2016.
  • Antibody tests often negative in early Lyme Disease; takes 4-6 weeks to develop antibody.
  • When one or more typical skin lesion of erythema migrans is present, no diagnostic testing is recommended
  • Antibody testing indicated if:
    • Exposure to endemic area
    • Risk factor(s) for tick exposure
    • Signs of late disease (e.g., meningitis, nerve palsy, carditis, arthritis)
  • Two-step testing of blood for specific antibodies
    • Enzyme immunoassay (EIA) or immunofluorescence assay (IFA) followed by Western Blot (WB): if EIA or IFA is negative, no further testing is recommended.
    • Western blot diagnostic criteria (to be performed if EIA or IFA is positive):
  • IgM antibody—Need 2 of 3 positive of kilodaltons (KD): 23, 39, 41
  • IgG antibody—Need 5 of 10 positive of KD: 18, 21, 28, 30, 39, 41, 45, 58, 66, 93
  • Sensitivity of testing for IgM or IgG antibody is suboptimal during the first 2 weeks of disease
  • Second generation antibody assay: Antibody to C6; in high prevalence areas, need to look for co-infection with Babesia microti and/or Anaplasma phagocytophilum
    • Less specific than full two-step test described above but, positive earlier in the infection
    • Helpful in discriminating true vs false positive IgM immunoblot assay
    • Better at detection of European Lyme disease due to B. garinii or B. afzellii
    • Not yet part of official guidelines. References: Clin Infect Dis 57:333, 2013; Clin Infect Dis 57:341, 2013.
  • May be able to detect organism by PCR on CSF or joint fluid.
  • Babesia diagnosis: visible on blood smears, serologic testing, or PCR
  • Anaplasma diagnosis: sensitivity of smears is poor, diagnosis by serology or PCR.

Etiologies

  • Borrelia burgdorferi spirochetes are among 5 Borrelia spp isolated from humans suspected of tick-borne illness in US (Clin Infect Dis 66: 1864, 2018)
    • Borrelia miyamotoi less common than B burgdorferi in surveys from New England and New York (Emerg Infect Dis 20: 1183, 2014)
    • Borrelia mayonii identified in Minnesota, Wisconsin, North Dakota; high spirochetemia, diffuse rash (Lancet Infect Dis 16: 556, 2016)
  • There are 5 or more species known to cause disease in Europe. Clinical symptoms vary.
  • Ixodes deer tick is the vector. Image is nymphal stage tick.

Primary Regimens

  • See Comments regarding treatment considerations if dual infection, e.g., Lyme, anaplasma, babesia
  • Adult:
    • Doxycycline 100 mg po bid x 10 days
    • Amoxicillin 500 mg po tid x 14 days 
    • Cefuroxime axetil 500 mg po bid x 14 days
    • Cephalexin is NOT effective
  • Pediatric:
    • Doxycycline (see comment above) 4.4 mg/kg/day divided q12h (max of 200 mg per day).
      • Note: Considered safe regardless of age for up to 21 days (AAP Red Book 2018, Section 4; J Pediatr 166:1246, 2015)
    • (Amoxicillin 50 mg/kg/day po in 3 divided doses or Cefuroxime axetil 30 mg/kg/day po in 2 divided doses) x 14-21 days.
      • Note: Cefuroxime suspension not available in the US, may be available in other markets.
    • Azithromycin 10 mg/kg per day po x 7-10 days

Alternative Regimens

  • Azithromycin 500 mg po once daily x 7 days
  • Pregnancy: Amoxicillin 500 mg po tid x 14 days; avoid Doxycycline in pregnancy

Comments

  • Dual infection:
    • Doxycycline would also treat anaplasma (but not babesia) if dual infection present
    • Amoxicillin and Cefuroxime axetil would not be effective against concomitant Babesiosis or Granulocytic anaplasmosis.
  • 2020 Clinical practice guidelines (Neurology 96:262, 2021) and (Clin Infect DIs 72:e1-e48, 2021)
  • Review in JAMA 315:1767 & 2461, 2016.
  • High rate of clinical failure reported with Azithromycin and Erythromycin, Drugs 57:157, 1999.
