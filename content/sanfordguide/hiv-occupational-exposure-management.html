HIV, Occupational Exposure

Clinical Setting

  • Initiate HIV post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) as soon as possible after exposure (within a few hours).
    • Otherwise, do not delay therapy pending virologic or serologic confirmation of source patient status.
    • PEP unlikely to be effective and not recommended more than 72 hours after exposure.
    • If status of source patient is unknown, and rapid HIV testing is available and can be performed stat, it is reasonable to await results of this test before starting PEP;

Initiation of PEP - Factors

  • The decision to initiate PEP is a clinical judgment made in concert with the exposed individual and is based on three factors listed below (See PEP Algorithm).
    • Type of exposure
        • Potentially infectious substances include: blood, unfixed tissues, CSF; semen and vaginal secretions (neither has not been implicated in occupational transmission of HIV); synovial, pleural, peritoneal, ascitic, and amniotic fluids; other visibly bloody fluids.
        • Fluids of low or negligible risk for transmission, unless visibly bloody include: urine, sweat, vomitus, stool, saliva, nasal secretions, tears, and sputum: PEP is not indicated.
        • Exposure of intact skin: regardless of whether the substance is potentially infectious or not, and regardless of the HIV status of the source patient: PEP is not indicated.
        • Exposure of mucous membranes (e.g., blood splash to the eye) or non-intact skin (e.g., abraded skin, open wound, dermatitis) or percutaneously (e.g., as a consequence of a needle stick, scalpel, or other sharps injury or cut): PEP may be indicated.
        • Human bites breaking skin could theoretically transmit HIV, particularly if oral blood is present, although these have not been implicated in occupational transmission of HIV.
    • Likelihood that source patient in HIV infected
        • If the exposure constitutes a risk of HIV transmission as described above and the source patient is known positive for HIV, then PEP should be instituted immediately, within hours of exposure but up to 72 hours after exposure may still provide benefit.
        • If exposure constitutes a risk of HIV transmission, and the HIV status is unknown, but patient is likely to be HIV infected or there is a reasonable suspicion for infection based on HIV risk factors, then PEP should be initiated pending confirmation of the source patient’s HIV status.
          • If a rapid HIV test of the source patient can be performed, it is reasonable to withhold therapy pending results of this test and initiating PEP if the test is positive.
          • If rapid testing cannot be performed, PEP should be initiated pending results of source patient testing and discontinued if the test returns negative.
          • NOTE: Antibody testing is sufficient to rule out HIV infection, unless the source patient has suspected acute retroviral syndrome, in which case HIV viral load testing is recommended.
        • If the source is unknown or the source is known but status and risk cannot be determined, the decision to initiate PEP should be made on a case-by-case basis in consultation with an expert (PEPline at http://www.nccc.ucsf.edu)(1-888-448-4911), guided by the severity of the exposure and epidemiologic likelihood of HIV exposure.
    • Adverse effects and potential for drug interactions with PEP regimen
        • Doses of some agents may need to be adjusted based on renal function.
        • Newer agents are better tolerated and should allow a higher proportion of exposed healthcare providers to complete the prescribed four-week course of therapy.
        • See specific ARV agents for information about drug interactions, also the package insert and on-line at http://hivinsite.ucsf.edu.
        • Breast feeding and pregnancy are not contraindications to PEP.

Preferred Regimens

  • Descovy (Emtricitabine (FTC) 200 mg + Tenofovir alafenamide fumarate (TAF) 25 mg) (Descovy is not recommended if CrCl < 30 ml/min or for use in pregnancy) po once daily + (Raltegravir 1200 mg (two 600 mg tabs) po once daily OR Dolutegravir 50 mg po once daily)
    • See Comments below concerning use of Descovy [TAF + FTC] rather than Truvada [Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF) + FTC].
  • In pregnancy or woman of child-bearing age not using contraception: Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF) 300 mg once daily + Emtricitabine (FTC) 200 mg once daily +  (Raltegravir 1200 mg once daily OR [Darunavir 800 mg po once daily + Ritonavir 100 mg po once daily]) 
  • For creatinine clearance < 30 ml/min: Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF) 300 mg q72-96h + Emtricitabine (FTC) 200 mg q72-96h + (Dolutegravir 50 mg po once daily OR Raltegravir 1200 po once daily) 
  • Duration of therapy is 28 days

Alternative Regimens

  • Truvada [Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF) 300 mg q72-96h + Emtricitabine (FTC)] 200 mg q72-96h + (Dolutegravir 50 mg po once daily OR Raltegravir 1200 po once daily) 
  • Descovy po once daily + Darunavir 800 mg po once daily + Ritonavir 100 mg po once daily OR
  • Descovy po once daily + Prezcobix (Darunavir 800 mg + Cobicistat 150 mg) one tab once daily OR
  • Descovy po once daily + Lopinavir-Ritonavir 800 mg/200 mg po once daily
  • For creatinine clearance < 30 ml/min, includes pregnancy: renally adjusted doses of Zidovudine + Lamivudine + Darunavir 800 mg po once daily + Ritonavir 100 mg po once daily
  • Duration of therapy is 28 days

Comments

  • U.S. Public Health Service guidelines for the management of occupational exposures to HIV and recommendations for postexposure prophylaxis here.

  • Guide authors prefer Descovy (Emtricitabine [FTC] 200 mg + Tenofovir alafenamide fumarate [TAF] 25 mg) over Truvada (Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate [TDF] 300 mg + Emtricitabine [FTC] 200 mg) given lower potential for renal injury, ability to be used in individuals with some degree of renal impairment, and lower potential osteomalacia for TAF compared to TDF.
    • Note: There are no direct clinical trial data supporting this recommendation and the likelihood of such a clinical trial be done is low owing to the infrequent number of exposures that lead to infection (1/ 300 with no prophylaxis; less than 1 / 1,000,000 per exposure if prophylaxis is used properly) and the required size of a study to definitively compare TDF to TAF. Descovy not recommended if CrCl is < 30 mL/min and is not approved for use in pregnant women.
    • In pregnancy use TDF/FTC (Truvada).
  • Not recommended: Didanosine, Nelfinavir, Tipranavir, Stavudine (d4T) and Nevirapine (contraindicated)
  • Use only in consultation with an expert: Abacavir, Efavirenz, Enfuvirtide, Maraviroc
  • If transmission of drug resistant virus is suspected, the regimen should be appropriately modified in consultation with an expert to include agents to which it is likely to be susceptible.
  • Follow-up:
    • Complete blood count, renal and hepatic panels recommended at baseline and repeated at 2 weeks and 4 weeks.
    • Pregnancy test at baseline.
    • HIV antibody testing to monitor seroconversion should be performed at baseline, 6 weeks, 12 weeks, and 6 months post-exposure. If a 4th generation p24 antigen-HIV antibody test is used, testing may be terminated at 4 months.
    • Extended follow-up for 12 months is recommended if HCV conversion occurred upon exposure to an HIV-HCV co-infected patient
