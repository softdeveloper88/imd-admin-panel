Malaria, Uncomplicated, P. vivax

Clinical Setting

  • Treatment of uncomplicated malaria due to P. vivax
  • In the US, malaria is uncommon in usual tourist travelers, most cases are seen in immigrants, immigrants returning home to visit friends and relatives, and long-stay temporary residents of endemic areas who do not adhere to long-term prophylaxis.
  • Over 90% of malaria seen in US is due to P. falciparum or P. vivax
    • Dual infections may occur

  • CDC algorithm for diagnosis and treatment of malaria in the US.

  • Chloroquine-sensitive in almost all areas
  • Chloroquine-resistant (Papua New Guinea, Indonesia)
    • Sporadic low-grade resistance in some other countries does not change approach, treat as chloroquine-sensitive
  • For uncomplicated malaria the oral ACT Artemether/lumefantrine is recommended as first choice by the US CDC for all species of malaria unless assuredly chloroquine sensitive.
    • P. falciparum is rarely chloroquine sensitive and other species uncommonly chloroquine resistant.
  • Consolidated WHO guidelines GRADE give a strong recommendation to preferentially treat adults and children with all of uncomplicated P. vivax, P. ovale, P. malariae or P. knowlesi malaria with either an oral ACT (including all pregnant women including in the first trimester)
    • However, chloroquine remains an option unless there is known chloroquine resistance in which case an ACT should be used.
  • P. vivax can cause severe malaria, treat as for severe malaria due to P. falciparum.

Diagnosis

  • Approved rapid diagnostic test: Binax NOW.  Many hospitals will do RDT only and not smear.
  • 50% sensitivity for low non-faciparum parasitemias which is the usual case.
  • High specificity; treat a positive test.
  • Need microscopy to speciate.
  • Can stay positive for over a month after successful treatment. 
  • Blood film read by properly trained personnel.
  • PCR is available from commercial labs and is the gold standard.
  • Serology of limited value.

Etiologies

  • Plasmodium vivax

        Comments

        • Avoid same drug for treatment for chemoprophylaxis failures with that same drug.
        • Obesity (WHO caution): As the evidence for an association between dose, pharmacokinetics and treatment outcome in overweight or large adults is limited, and alternative dosing options have not been assessed in treatment trials
          • In the absence of data, treatment providers should attempt to follow up the treatment outcomes of large adults whenever possible.
        •  Pregnancy:
          • Artemether-lumefantrine now recommended in all trimesters by WHO and CDC.
          • Avoid primaquine or tafenoquine in pregnancy.
            • Pregnant patients with P. vivax infections should be maintained on chloroquine prophylaxis (300 mg base once/wk) for the duration of their pregnancy.
            • After delivery, pregnant patients who do not have G6PD deficiency should be treated with primaquine or tafenoquine.
        •  Primaquine
          • Primaquine or tafenoquine (TQ) is added to P. vivax treatment to eradicate latent parasites in liver. Screen for G6PD deficiency before starting Primaquine; if mildly G6PD deficient, dose Primaquine as 45 mg po once weekly (WHO dose is 0.75 mg/kg) x 8 weeks.
            • Note: rare severe reactions.
          • WHO: no longer recommends less than 14 days of primaquine as a viable option. 
        • Tafenoquine
          • TQ should be co-administered with chloroquine only.
            • This is a change from the original Krintafel labeling that allowed for use in combination with other antimalarials (artemether-lumefantrine, atovaquone-proguanil, quinine plus doxycycline).
          • Tafenoquine succinate
            • Requires >70% normal G6PD activity on quantitative testing for use; do not use rapid or qualitative tests.
            • Not approved for use in persons under age 16 years
            • G6PD test results difficult to interpret.  See review J Travel Med. 26: pii: taz023, 2019. Different G6PD criteria compared to primaquine.
              • Point of care rapid tests for G6PD are in advanced development PLoS Negl Trop Dis. 2023 Oct 12;17(10):e0011652.
              • New (2024) WHO classification system for G6PC deficiency (Bull World Health Organ 2024;102:615)
            • Tafenoquine FDA approved in 2018. Arakoda (U.S) and Kodatef (Australia) are now commercially available.
              • Use 3 X 100 mg tablets once for radical cure of P. vivax
            • WHO Guidelines: Tafenoquine not recognized for radical cure of P. vivax due to inavailability of quantitative G6PD testing in most endemic settings.
        • Artemisinin combination therapy (ACT) in the US. Only Artemether/lumefantrine is FDA-approved.
          • Not widely stocked by hospital or retail pharmacies in the US.
            • Generally available from regional drug distribution warehouses with up to 24-hour delay in initiating therapy for a medical emergency.
              • Do not delay therapy in order to obtain Coartem if another 1st line agent is more readily available.
            • Call 1 855-COARTEM for expedited availability information.
        • An ACT dihydroartemisinin/piperaquine (Eurartesim) is licensed and available in Europe.
          • Artesunate/mefloquine effective against resistant P. vivax (Clin Infect Dis 62:1403, 2016)
          • limited data on pyronaridine/artesunate in P. vivax infection though should work well.

        • Chloroquine has a long half-life and prolongs QTc intervals. Minor overdosages or too many doses over several days may be toxic or fatal.
