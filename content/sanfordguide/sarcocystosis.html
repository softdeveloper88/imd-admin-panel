Sarcocystosis

Clinical Setting

  • There are 200 known species of sarcocystis, most in zoonotic cycles and as a common livestock pathogen.
  • Some cause mild GI disease in humans, rare muscular disease in species where humans are accidental dead-end intermediate hosts after ingestion of material contaminated with snake feces.
  • Fever, myalgia, headache mimicking other tropical disease occur but may be distinguished by a pattern with multiple episodes of relapse and remission in the initial phases (PLoS Negl Trop Dis 8:e2876, 2014).
  • A small proportion with heavy infection develop myositis with elevated CPK and eosinophilia after several weeks (Clin Infect Dis 59:1401, 2014).
  • Most human cases from Malaysia but case reports from other parts of Asia and Africa J Neurovirol 2018; 24:376. 
  • 2000-2019 surveillance in travelers (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2023;109:327). 

Diagnosis

  • Presumptive diagnosis of muscular sarcocystosis can be made in the setting of compatible epidemiology, muscular inflammation, laboratory abnormalities, and negative testing for other causes of myositis.

Etiologies

  • Sarcocystis nesbitti (implicated in muscular disease; restricted to Malaysia)
  • Sarcocystis hominis and Sarcocystis suihominis (human gastrointestinal disease)

Primary Regimens

  • Muscular disease: TMP-SMX 160/800 mg 2 tabs po bid x 14 days.
    • Anecdotal reports and case series only, e.g., Clin Infect Dis 60:329, 2015.

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • Gastrointestinal disease is self-limiting with no treatment required.
  • Anecdotal reports of steroid benefit in muscular disease but many patients without obvious inflammatory component.
  • Exhaustive review Clin Microbiol Rev. 28:295, 2015.
