Pneumonia, Mycoplasma

Clinical Setting

  • Mycoplasma pneumoniae is an atypical pneumonia pathogen.
    • Presents with fever, headache, myalgias, dry cough, bilateral infiltrates on chest radiograph, occasionally accompanied by pharyngitis, sinus congestion, and otitis media.
  • Extrapulmonary manifestations:
    • Cold agglutinins (JAMA 2018;319:1377), hemolytic anemia
    • CNS complications: meningo-encephalitis, transverse myelitis, Guillain-Barre
    • Skin:  Urticaria, erythema nodosum, erythema multiforme, Stephen-Johnson syndrome
    • Other: arthritis, glomerulonephritis.
  • See also acute bronchitis.

Diagnosis

  • Gold standard is PCR of sputum or oropharyngeal swab. Four-fold rise in specific antibody titer is the previous gold standard
  • See Ped infect Dis J 2018;37:1192

Etiologies

  • Mycoplasma pneumoniae

Primary Regimens

  • Remember that Mycoplasma sp. are cell wall deficient and hence are resistant to all beta-lactam antibiotics
  • See comments below referable to doxycycline use in children:
    • Doxycycline 100 mg IV/po bid x 7-10 days
    • If Doxycycline not available, Minocycline 200 mg po/IV x 1 dose, then 100 mg po/IV bid

Alternative Regimens

  • Azithromycin 500 mg po on day 1 and then 250 mg po once daily for 4 days (see Comments about increasing problem of macrolide resistance)
  • Levofloxacin 750 mg po/IV x 5 days

Comments

  • Use of tetracyclines in pediatric patients has historically been limited because of reports of permanent tooth discoloration in children <8 years of age caused by incorporation of the drugs and their colored degradation products in enamel.
    • Doxycycline can safely be administered for durations (≤21 days) regardless of age (AAP Red Book 2018, Section 4; J Pediatr 166:1246, 2015). Doxycycline binds less readily to calcium compared with other members of the tetracycline class, and recent comparative data suggest that doxycycline is not likely to cause visible teeth staining or enamel hypoplasia in children <8 years of age.
  • Macrolide resistance:
    • Increasing recognition of in vitro resistance, especially in Asia. US National surveillance study indicates macrolide resistance in 7.5% of isolates. Isolates remain susceptible to tetracyclines and fluoroquinolones: J Clin Micro 2019; 57: e00968-19
      • Clinical failures with macrolide therapy of in Pediatric Community-Acquired Pneumonia (Emerg Infect Dis. 2020;26:1382).
      • Clinical disease from resistant strains, at least in China, maybe intrinsically more severe. Because infections are mainly in children, treatment with a fluoroquinolone is a limited option.
    • Doxycycline preferred over Azithromycin only because of increasing number of reports of macrolide resistant strains of mycoplasma and better outcomes with tetracyclines (Infect Dis Clin No Am. 2019; 33:1087 and BMC Infect Dis. 2021;21: 1003).
  • May occasionally cause severe pneumonia and acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS).
  • Clindamycin and beta-lactams are not effective
