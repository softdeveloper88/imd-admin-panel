Blastocystis hominis

Clinical Setting

  • Common and ubiquitous stramenopiles protists (not a conventional parasite) of highly disputable clinical significance.
    • Recent paradigm shift (Trends Parasitol 2024 Sep;40(9):775) based on metagenomic stool analysis.
      • More common in the bowel of healthy persons than unhealthy.
        • Found in stool of 5% of general population in developed countries and in 40% in developing countries (Trends Parasitol 34:369, 2018).
    • 9 subtypes with different environmental niches, host specificities and life cycles, sub-type 3 may be most pathogenic.
    • Life cycle in humans undefined.
  • Positive stool PCR (n=5,333 specimens) for B. hominis was not associated with any of the measured clinical outcomes (Clin Microbiol Infect 2023 Sep 7:S1198-743X(23)00421-4)
  • Blastocystis is relatively rare in patients with inflammatory bowel disease, and its role in irritable bowel syndrome is still controversial.

Etiologies

  • Blastocystis hominis

Primary Regimens

  • Need to treat is now especially dubious
  • Therapy may treat concomitant or other co-pathogens.
  • RCT showing no treatment benefit (J Travel Med 2023 Feb 18;30(1):taac143)

Alternative Regimens

  • Few studies; many agents variably treat detectable organisms; none is reliable or preferred.
    • Metronidazole (1.5 gm po 1x/day or 750 mg po tid) x 10 days
    • Iodoquinol 650 mg po tid x 20 days
    • TMP-SMX-DS, 1 tab bid x 7 days
    • Nitazoxanide 500 mg po bid x 3 days
      • Children 4 – 11 yrs: 200 mg po bid x 3d;  1 – 3 yrs: 100 mg bid x 3d

Comments

  • Many clinical laboratories in the U.S. do not report B. hominis when they see it.
