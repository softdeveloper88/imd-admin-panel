Trypanosomiasis, West African

Clinical Setting

  • West African Trypanosomiasis: West African Sleeping Sickness
    • Early disease, Stage 1 (blood or lymphatic) is often subacute or mild compared to T.b. rhodesiense.
    • Late disease, Stage 2 (encephalitis): gradual-onset dysregulation of sleep-wake cycle, various motor disorders, and neuropsychiatric disorders. Somnolence often mistaken for depression.
  • In the US seen in immigrants, rare in travelers.
  • Non-endemic cases 2011-2020:  PLoS Negl Trop Dis. 2022 Nov 7;16(11):e0010885. 
  • Transmitted by Glossina sp. (tsetse fly) with humans as the main reservoir
  • Chancre early after infection (infrequent), posterior cervical adenopathy later.
  • CSF examination needed to stage.  ≥5 WBC, parasites, parasite specific IgM, or parasite DNA by PCR indicate Stage 2. 
    • >100 WBC indicate stage 2, a severity not eligible for fexinidazole therapy.

Etiologies

  • Trypanosoma brucei gambiense

Diagnosis

  • Stained blood smears (after concentration or using buffy coat).
  • Unstained wet preps of blood (motility).
  • Lymph node or chancre aspirates, and CSF for direct examination.
  • Serology and rapid card agglutination test have variable performance.
  • Rapid tests, serology, and PCR not available from US CDC; available in European centers.

Primary Regimens

  • Stage 1 or Stage 2 patients who are  ≥ 6 yrs and body weight ≥ 20 kg presenting with <100 WBC/µL in CSF: 
  • Fexinidazole 
    • Wt ≥ 35 kg: Loading dose 1800 mg (3 tablets) once/day for 4 days then 1200 mg (2 tablets) once/day for 6 days
    • Wt ≥ 20 and < 35 kg:  Loading dose 1200 mg (2 tablets) once/day for 4 days then 600 mg (1 tablet) once/day for 6 days
      • First-choice treatment for g-HAT based on oral administration and tolerability. 
        • See 2024 WHO Guidelines but clinical criteria and dosing identical as for East African trypanosomiasis.
          • In affected areas Fexinidazole is available only to government programs (Lancet 391:144, 2018).  
          • In the US, FDA-approved but must be obtained directly from Sanofi.  See Antiparasitic Drugs, Sources

          • Discussion of WHO Guidelines (Lancet Infect Dis. 2024 Oct 7:S1473-3099(24)00581-4)

        • Patient must be able to swallow the 600 mg tablets after an appropriate intake of food as well as on completing the full 10-day treatment schedule. 
          • FDA suggests avoiding use if eGFR < 30 mL/min.
        • Inpatient therapy for entire oral course.
        • If CSF cell number not known do not use fexinidazole
        • Pregnancy: can use fexinidazole in 2nd and 3rd trimester.
        • Pediatric data: Lancet Glob Health. 2022 Nov;10(11):e1665
  • Severe Stage 2 patients with more than 100 WBC/µL in CSF
    • Eflornithine 400 mg/kg/day IV divided q12h x 7 days + Nifurtimox 15 mg/kg/day po, divided q8h x 10 days (abbreviated NECT) non-inferior to 14-day standard Eflornithine therapy (Lancet 374:56, 2009; Clin Infect Dis 56:195, 2013).
  • Children aged < 6 years or body weight < 20 kg
    • Stage 1: Pentamidine 4 mg/kg IV X 7 days
    • Stage 2: NECT (as above)

Alternative Regimens

  • Age ≥ 6 yrs and body weight ≥ 20 kg
    • Stage 1: Pentamidine 4 mg/kg IV X 7 days
    • Stage 2 (<100 WBC CSF):  Pentamidine or NECT
    • Stage 2 (>100 WBC CSF):  Fexinidazole as above
  • Age <6 yrs or <20 kg: 
    • Stage 1: NECT
    • Stage 2: Eflornithine 400 mg/kg/day IV divided q12h x 7 days
  • NECT may have lower fatality and fewer relapses but oral fexinidazole much higher adherence (Cochrane Database System Rev 2021 Dec 9;12(12):CD015374)

Comments

  • In resource poor settings patients with trypanosomes in blood or body fluids can be managed without an LP if:
    • low index of suspicion of severe disease (>100 WBC in CSF) and
    • high confidence that the patient will have appropriate follow-up to detect relapse early
    • Patients who reject or do not tolerate fexinidazole may need a LP to decide between pentamidine and
      NECT.
  • There is no test of cure for African trypanosomiasis.
    • After treatment, patients should be closely followed q6 mos for 24 months for relapse.
      • Recurrence of symptoms will require examination of body fluids, including CSF, to detect the presence of trypanosomes.
      • Relapse: Any tryps founds in any fluid
        • or >5 WBCs in CSF at > 5-9 mos post treatment or >20 WBCs at 10-24 mos post treatment
  • Eflornithine may be obtained from the WHO or from the CDC Drug Service (at no charge for US physicians). See Antiparasitic Drugs, Sources.
    • No data on safety of eflornithine in pregnancy or lactation
  • Nifurtimox (Lampit, Bayer) FDA approved August 2020 only for T. cruzi but now commercially available so clinicians can use off-label for NECT.
  • Phase II/III study a single 960 mg dose of an oral treatment, acoziborole produced an 18-month treatment success rate of 95% in 167 patients with late-stage and 100% in 41 patients with early-stage gambiense sleeping sickness. Lancet Infect Dis 2023 Apr;23(4):463-470.
    • Being fast-tracked by WHO for use in control programs (single-dose, oral)
