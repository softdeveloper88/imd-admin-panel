Meningitis: Catheter, Shunt Related

Clinical Setting

  • Empiric therapy, pending culture results
    • Device-related meningitis.
    • Ventriculitis/meningitis caused by infected ventriculo-peritoneal (atrial) shunt, lumbar catheter, external ventricular drain, Ommaya reservoir, other CSF drainage device.
  • Signs and symptoms: new headache, change in mental status, fever, tenderness and/or erythema over shunt site.
  • Laboratory findings:  positive blood culture with no evidence source of infection; abnormal CSF with pleocytosis, low glucose, and/or high protein (CSF can be normal, however); positive CSF culture is the gold standard.
    • If infection is suspected on clinical grounds and cultures are negative, hold cultures for 10 days to identify slow growing organisms (e.g., Cutibacterium(formerly Propionibacterium) acnes) 
  • Most infections occur within a month of device placement.
  • Recurrent infections are not uncommon.
  • IDSA Guidelines:  Clin Infect Dis 64:e34, 2017. 

Etiologies

  • Coagulase-negative staphylococci
  • Cutibacterium (Formerly Propionibacterium) acnes
  • Staph. aureus
  • Gram-negative bacilli
  • Diphtheroids
  • Candida species

Primary Regimens

  • Empirical: Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL) + (Cefepime 2 gm IV q8h or Ceftazidime 2 gm IV q8h)
  • See Comments for definitive therapy.

Alternative Regimens

  • Vancomycin as above  + Meropenem 2 gm IV q8h administered over 3 hours
  • Serious beta-lactam allergy: Vancomycin + (Ciprofloxacin 400 mg IV q8h or Aztreonam 2 gm IV q6h)

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Narrow coverage based on pathogen and susceptibility test results (see Comments for definitive therapy recommendations).

Comments

  • Two-stage shunt removal is the optimal treatment for shunt-dependent patients (Clin Infect Dis 2017 Apr 15;64(8):989-997)

  • Usual care
    • Removal of the infected device whenever possible and placement of external ventricular drain, if needed.
    • Repeated cultures of CSF from the external drain to document that these convert to negative.
    • Duration of therapy: 10-14 days (shorter duration for patients with P. acnes, coagulase-negative staphylococci, or diphtheroids and no or minimal CSF abnormalities) after last positive CSF culture.
    • Shunt reimplantation:
      • Coagulase-negative staphylococci, diphtheroids, or P. acnes
        • No CSF abnormailites:  day 3 after externalization if CSF cultures are negative at 48h 
        • CSF abnormalities present: 7-10 days after the last positive CSF culture.
      • S. aureus or Gram-negative organism: 10 days after last positive CSF culture.
  • Intraventricular therapy should be considered if the shunt cannot be removed or cultures fail to clear with systemic therapy.
    • Intraventricular antibiotic dosing (lower dose for slit ventricles, intermediate dose for normal size ventricles, higher dose for enlarged ventricles):
      • Amikacin 30 mg
      • Gentamicin 4-8 mg in adults, 1-2 mg in infants, children
      • Polymyxin E (Colistin) 10 mg
      • Tobramycin 5-20 mg
      • Vancomycin 5-20 mg
      • Quinupristin-dalfopristin 2-5 mg
      • Daptomycin 5 mg (see Ann Pharmacother 48:1376, 2014).
    • Frequency of administration depends on drainage output
      • < 50 ml/24h: every 3rd day
      • 50-100 ml/24h: every second day
      • 100-150 ml/day: once daily
      • 150-200 ml/24h: increase dose of vancomycin by 5 mg, gentamicin by 1 mg
      • 200-250 ml/24h: increase dose of vancomycin by 5 mg, gentamicin by 1 mg
