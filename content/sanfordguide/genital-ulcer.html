Genital Ulcer

Clinical Setting

  • Usually sexually transmitted. Includes genital, anal and perianal ulcers. See CDC guidelines 2021 MMWR Recomm Rep 70 (RR-4):1 2021
  • Most commonly HSV or syphilis.
  • May also present as oral ulcers (syphilis, herpes, and LGV) 
  • Painful:
    • Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV): usually multiple vesicles, but can be single nonvesicular ulcer.
    • Chancroid (Haemophilus ducreyi): classically, deep with ragged edges
    • Monkeypox: papular lesions on skin, but may present as painful lesions in oral or genital/anal regions
  • Painless:
    • Syphilis (Treponema pallidum): usually single, well-circumscribed, indurated ulcer; rectal syphilis presenting as proctitis can be painful
    • Lymphogranuloma venereum (with painful lymphadenopathy; Chlamydia trachomatis)
    • Granuloma inguinale (Klebsiella granulomatis or Donovanosis): friable, single or multiple nodular lesions.
  • Regional Lymphadenopathy common
  • May be accompanied by dysuria

Etiologies

  • Bacterial:
    • Haemophilus ducreyi (Chancroid)
    • Treponema pallidum (Syphilis)
    • Chlamydia trachomatis (LGV, Serovars L1, L2, L3)
    • Klebsiella granulomatis (Donovanosis)
  • Viral: Herpes simplex virus I or II or monkeypox
  • Rare: primary HIV, tuberculosis, leishmaniasis, amebiasis
  • Non-infectious (often autoimmune): aphthae, Behcet’s syndrome, malignancy, Crohn's disease, psoriasis trauma, yeast, carcinoma, fixed drug eruption
  • Up to 25% may not have a definitive laboratory diagnosis. 

Diagnosis

  • All patients with genital ulcers should be evaluated for syphilis, HSV.
    • On occasion, more than one pathogen is present.
    • Clinical presentation may not be reliable for definitive diagnosis
    • Granuloma inguinale needs biopsy
  • Syphilis serology tests and darkfield examination from lesion exudate or tissue, or NAAT if available
  • NAAT or culture for genital herpes type 1 or 2
  • NAAT for monkeypox
  • Serologic testing for type specific HSV antibody. 
  • Where chancroid is prevalent, NAAT or culture for Hemophilus ducreyi

Comments

  • As with all STIs, all patients with genital ulcers should be tested for HIV.
  • Often a clinical diagnosis with empiric treatment as diagnostic testing may not be available
  • Empiric treatment for syphilis (esp MSM) or Herpes if consistent clinical presentation should be considered prior to laboratory diagnosis.
  • Sexual partners of patients should be referred for evaluation or treatment depending on the likely etiology.
