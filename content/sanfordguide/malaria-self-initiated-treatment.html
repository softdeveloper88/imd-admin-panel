Malaria, Self-initiated Treatment

Clinical Setting

  • Self-initiated treatment of malaria to be used only if malaria is laboratory diagnosed while overseas and reliable medication is not accessible.
    • To be administered under the supervision of a local physician or remotely by the home physician
    • Avoids exposure to counterfeit medications
  • May be carried by those at high risk who refuse continuous prophylaxis (often travelers to lower risk destinations) or those who will take sub-optimal prophylaxis for reasons of cost and there is an unreliable drug supply at destination.
  • In instances when no medical care is readily available, the medication can be self-administered presumptively, but that this is only a temporary measure on the way to a proper medical setting.
  • Adequate evidence for travelers ability to properly self-diagnose using malaria Rapid Diagnostic Tests (RDT) supplied in travel kits is lacking.

Etiologies

  • Plasmodia sp.

  • None

Comments

  • Atovaquone-proguanil:
    • Do not use if on atovaquone/proguanil prophylaxis
    • Do not use if creatinine clearance <30 mL/min
    • Do not use if pregnant
  • Artemether-lumefantrine sold as Riamet in Europe, Coartem in U.S. and elsewhere:
    • Do not use if on mefloquine prophylaxis
    • Not widely stocked by hospital or retail pharmacies in the US.
      • Generally available from regional drug distribution warehouses with up to 24-hour delay in initiating therapy for a medical emergency.
        • Do not delay therapy in order to obtain Coartem if another 1st line agent is more readily available.
      • Call 1 855-COARTEM for expedited availability information.
    • Safe in pregnancy
  • Artemisinin resistance is present in the Greater Mekong subregion of Asia Antimicrob Agents Chemother 2021; AAC0112121 and in Eritrea, Rwanda and Uganda.
    • Artemisinin resistance alone rarely leads to treatment failure but resistance to both the artemisinin derivative and the partner ACT drug (Mekong region only) does.
    • Do not use self-initiated ACT in the Mekong region but ACT remains efficacious in Africa.
  • Mefloquine, quinine too many adverse effects for self-treatment.
  • Dihydroartemisin-piperaquine (Eurartesim) available in several European countries and generically in some malaria-endemic countries.
