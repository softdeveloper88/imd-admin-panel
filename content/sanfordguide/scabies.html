Scabies

Clinical Setting

  • Mite infestation of the skin that causes intense itching.
  • Adult Female lays eggs in outer layer of skin; eggs hatch in 3 - 4 days becoming adult mites in 1 - 2 weeks.  Skin hypersensitivity in 4 - 6 weeks (clinical manifestations).  Symptoms occur much earlier (~ 1 - 2 days) with reinfection.
  • Transmission requires ~15 min of direct skin to skin contact or via fomites (linens, clothing) when heavy infestation is present (crusted scabs)
  • Typical distribution:
    • Immunocompetent patient: Sides and webs of fingers, flexor aspects of wrist, periumbilical, male genitalia, extensor surface of knees.
    • Immunocompromised patient (HIV/AIDS): can involve entire skin and cause "crusting" (Norwegian) scabies.
  • Diagnosis is based on history and distribution of skin lesions. Sometimes can see mite or eggs from scrapings of burrows, palms, sole creases, and web spaces.
  • Course and management:
    • Immunocompetent patient: Pruritus may persist for 2 weeks after mites are gone.
    • Immunocompromised, HIV/AIDS, HTLV-infected patients: Extensive, crusted Norwegian scabies can mimic psoriasis. Not pruritic. Highly contagious condition—isolate the patient!
  • Outbreaks in nursing homes (Lancet Infect Dis 18:894, 2018)
  • Review:  JAMA 332(14): 1189, 2024

Etiologies

  • Sarcoptes scabiei (mite)

Primary Regimens

  • Immunocompetent
    • Permethrin 5% cream (ELIMITE). Apply to entire skin from chin down to and including toes. Also, under fingernails and toenails. May require 30 gm. Leave on 8–14 hours. Repeat in 1-2 weeks. Safe for children age > 2 months.
      • Reapply to hands after handwashing.
    • Alternative is Ivermectin (below)
  • Norwegian or crusted  scabies in immunocompromised patients, HIV/AIDS:
    • Ivermectin 200 μg/kg/dose po with food
      • Depending on severity, take in three doses (days 1, 2, and 8), in five doses (approx. days 1, 2, 8, 9 and 15), or in seven doses (approx. days 1, 2, 8, 9,15, 22 and 29)
      • CDC suggests concomitant use of topical agent (Permethrin as above)
      • The safety of ivermectin in children <15kg has not been authorized by most national authorities.
      • WHO allows children who are at least 90 cm tall (equivalent to ~15 kg or ~2 yrs of age) to be treated with ivermectin.
        • Systematic review (n=1,000 subjects) strongly suggest safety <15 kg (PLoS Negl Trop Dis 2021 Mar 17;15(3):e0009144) 
      • Unsafe in pregnancy

Alternative Regimens

  • Immunocompetent: Ivermectin 200 µg/kg/ dose po with food on day 1, then a second dose one week later
    • Safety of ivermectin in children weighing less than 15 kg and in pregnant women is not known
  • Lindane 1% applied in thin layer from neck down and washed off after 8 hours. Due to toxicity, should only be used if patient cannot tolerate other regimens or other regimens have failed. Not recommended for children <10 years of age.

Comments

  • See 2015 CDC STD Guidelines:  MMWR 64(RR-3):1, 2015. Also see: CDC information resources on scabies.
  • Antihistamines may help reduce itching.
  • Secondary streptococcal infections can occur.
  • Identify and treat all persons (e.g. staff, relatives, patients, etc.) having prolonged, direct skin-to-skin contact with an infested person before he/she was treated.
  • Offer treatment to household members (e.g. spouses, children, etc.) of staff who are receiving scabies treatment.
  • Avoid skin-to-skin contact with person with scabies for at least 8 hours after application of scabicide treatment
  • Wash and dry linens; decontaminate clothing.
  • Infected individuals generally can return to work the day after receiving a dose of treatment with permethrin or ivermectin; however, symptomatic persons who provide hands-on care to any patient may need to use disposable gloves for several days after treatment until sure they are no longer infested.
