Schistosomiasis

Clinical Setting

  • Schistosomiasis; trematode infection.
  • Larvae penetrate skin after release from snail hosts in freshwater bodies only.
  • Acute schistosomiasis; toxemic often febrile illness with cough and eosinophilia 4-8 weeks post-infection.
    • Clinically may mimic malaria, typhoid, or other systemic tropical infections, eosinophilia generally points to schistosomiasis
  • Travelers and temporary residents lightly infected with no chronic disease.
    • See Diagnosis for screening recommendations
    • Uncommon CNS involvement including spinal cord.
    • Almost all travel related cases are from Africa
  • Chronic schistosomiasis; liver, bladder, cervical, uterine, tube-ovarian, intestinal disease. Cerebral lesions with S. japonicum.
    • Female genital schistosomiasis (cervical, uterine, tubo-ovarian) likely the most neglected gynecologic condition and HIV/AIDS transmission cofactor across sub-Saharan Africa (PLoS Negl Trop Dis 13: e0007025, 2019).

Diagnosis

  • Light infection detected by serology (ELISA), eggs not usually present in stool or urine.
  • All returnees from Africa with history of any freshwater skin exposure should be screened with serology.
  • Speciation requires Western blot from CDC.
  • Point-of-care circulating anodic antigen test available in some endemic areas for monitoring
  • Review of developments in diagnosis: Lancet Microbe 2024 ;5(4):e366.

Etiologies

  • Schistosoma mansoni (hepatointestinal), found in South America, Caribbean, Africa
    • S. intercalatum and S. gueenensis  (central west Africa) uncommon variants of S. mansoni
  • Schistosoma japonicum (hepatointestinal), found in Asia
    • S. mekongi (Southeast Asia), Schistosoma malayi (Peninsular Malaysia), uncommon variant of S. japonicum; 
  • Schistosoma haematobium (genitourinary), found in Africa

Primary Regimens

  • S. mansoni, S. hematobium, S. intercalatum
    • Praziquantel 40 mg/kg po in 1 or 2 doses the same day.
      • Presence of viable eggs 6 to 12 wks after initial therapy warrants repeat treatment
  • S. japonicum, S. mekongi
    • Praziquantel 60 mg/kg po in 2 or 3 doses the same day.
  • Acute toxemic schistosomiasis (Katayama fever)
    • Short course of high-dose Prednisone until 48 hrs after symptom resolution with or without Praziquantel 40-60 mg/kg po in 2 or 3 doses the same day x 3-6 days.
    • Repeat Praziquantel in 4-6 wks (≤ 3 days of steroids) when most worms are mature (Clin Microbiol Infect 16:225, 2010) and a final course of Praziquantel alone at least 12 wks after infection to eliminate late maturing worms.
  • Myeloradiculopathy due to neuroschistosomiasis
    • Praziquantel 40-60 mg/kg once + prednisone daily x 6 months (Clin Infect Dis 39:1618, 2004) but Praziquantel should be delayed for several days after start of prednisone.
  • Note: Immune response in lightly infected travelers may be insufficient and repeat treatment after 1-2 months may be undertaken empirically even with no residual eggs.
  • Insufficient data to allow antibody titers to be followed as test of cure and eosinophil counts may increase post-treatment as worms die.

Alternative Regimens

  • Older drugs oxamniquine and metrifonate are no longer used.

Comments

  • Praziquantel ineffective as immediate post-exposure prophylaxis as primarily effective against adult worms which mature adults 8-12 weeks after infection.
    • many travelers and expatriates empirically take post-exposure prophylaxis with PZQ at 8-12 wks; no controlled trials
  • Praziquantel resistance is not clinically important PLoS Negl Trop Dis 15:e0009189, 2021.
  • Some clinicians use 60 mg/kg praziquantel for all species for travelers and temporary residents not expecting re-exposure or empiric repeat treatment with standard dose PZQ at 4 wks
    • Review of literature on non-standard praziquantel doses (J Travel Med.16. pii: taz050, 2019).
  • Acute schistosomiasis is an immune-mediated reaction to onset of egg laying 4-6 weeks after infectious exposure in fresh water.
    • Praziquantel effective only against mature adult worms; steroids are primary therapy to control reaction. Praziquantel alone may exacerbate acute disease.
    • Steroids decrease serum Praziquantel levels by half
  • No therapeutic advantage or adverse effect reduction when the 40 mg/kg total dose for urinary schistosomiasis is split in 2 (Cochrane Database Syst Rev 8:CD000053, 2014).
  • Chronic disease in immigrants, including liver fibrosis, obstructive uropathy, bladder cancer, and cervical lesions may not warrant drug treatment if no eggs present.
  • Praziquantel can be used in children as young as 1 year of age but tablets large and hard to swallow.
  • Praziquantel is a pregnancy category B drug.
    • WHO recommends use in pregnant women after 1st trimester.
      • 1st trimester: Consider treatment risk in lactating women compared to the risk of disease progression if untreated.
  • Praziquantel is excreted in low concentrations in breast milk.
    •  Consider treatment risk in lactating women compared to the risk of disease progression if untreated.
