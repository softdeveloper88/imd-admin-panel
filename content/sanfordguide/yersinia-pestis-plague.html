Yersinia pestis, Plague

Clinical Setting

  • Plague (Yersinia pestis) is transmitted by the bite of infected fleas or in the case of pneumonic plague, by inhalation of droplets.
  • There are 3 clinical presentations of plague:
    • Bubonic plague: most common, 80-95% of cases.  An acute painful non-fluctuant lymphadenitis. Bubo comes from the Greek for "groin".
    • Septicemic or Bacteremic plague: 10-20% of plague patients. Occurs with or without bubonic plague. Early in the course, G-I symptoms occur; late in the course, hypotension, DIC, and multiorgan failure occur.
    • Pneumonic plague : haematogenous seeding or inhalation of infected droplets. Rare with a mortality rate  of 60% or higher.

Classification

  • Yersinia pestis, Gram-negative bacilli (may have closed safety pin appearance)

Primary Regimens

  • Initiate contact isolation; plus droplet isolation if respiratory symptoms
  • Streptomycin, Gentamicin,  Ciprofloxacin, and  Doxycycline are drugs of choice
  • Dual therapy with two distinct classes of antimicrobials (e.g., Ciprofloxacin + Gentamicin) should be used for initial treatment of patients with severe pneumonic or septicemic plague and patients infected after intentional release of Yersinia pestis.
  • Comprehensive reviews of antibacterial therapy options:  MMWR. Recommendations and Reports,  July 16, 2021 / 70(3);1–27.

Alternative Regimens

  • See above.

Prevention

  • In the past vaccination has been thought to protect against plague in the long term,
  • No currently approved licensed vaccine is available
  • WHO does not recommend immunisation with old generation (killed whole cell) plague vaccines
  • A systematic review was unable to quantify the efficacy of older vaccines nor their long-term safety (Curr Res Immunol 2023 Nov 1;4:100072). 

Comments

  • In a mass casualty situation, may have to treat with oral drugs.
  • Isolate patients with plague pneumonia during the first 48 hours of treatment.
  • Notify Clinical Microbiology of possible plague to avoid occupational exposure.
  • References: StatPearls; 2022 Jan. 2022 Aug 8 and  Clin Infect Dis 2020;70(suppl1): S1-S60.
