Scedosporium apiospermum

Clinical Setting

  • Scedosporium apiospermum is ubiquitous in the environment and infection occurs most commonly through inhalation or direct inoculation of skin.
  • Syndrome is referred to as Madura Foot
  • Immunocompetent hosts most often have cutaneous disease.
  • Immunocompromised hosts can have a wide spectrum of infection ranging from pulmonary colonization to invasive disease in lung, skin, and disseminated disease that can include CNS and bone infections.

Etiologies

  • Scedosporium apiospermum

Primary Regimens

  • Voriconazole 6 mg/kg IV q12h on day 1, then 4 mg/kg IV q12h

Alternative Regimens

  • MICs are higher to azoles other than voriconazole, others may not be as effective:
    • Posaconazole (NOTE: different dosing delayed release tabs/IV vs. suspension)
      • Delayed-release tabs 300 mg po bid x 2 doses and then 300 mg po daily
      • Suspension 200 mg qid, then 400 mg po bid after stabilization of disease
      • Posaconazole IV 300 mg IV over 90 minutes bid x 1 day, then 300 mg IV daily

Comments

  • Surgical debridement should be considered in most cases since these infections can be difficult to eradicate with antifungal agents alone.
  • Scedosporium apiospermum is resistant to Amphotericin B
  • Combination therapy: Synergy with voriconazole and terbinafine and echinocandins have been reported in vitro although clinical data is limited (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 56:2635, 2012). 
  • Consider antifungal susceptibility testing for infections with these organisms.
  • ESCMID treatment guidelines and other review: Clin Microbiol Infect 20 (Suppl 3):27, 2014; Clin Microbiol Rev 21:157, 2008.
