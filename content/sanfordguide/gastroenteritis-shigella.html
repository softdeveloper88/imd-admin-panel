Gastroenteritis, Shigella

Clinical Setting

  • Shigellosis: acute bacterial diarrhea, often severe.
  • Cause of traveler's diarrhea
  • Fever and history of bloody stools in about half of patients.
  • Cause of hemolytic uremia syndrome.
  • Often associated with sexual transmission in men who have sex with men
  • See also, Shigella sp.

Etiologies

  • Shigella sp.

Primary Regimens

  • Reserve therapy for immunocompromised patients or those with severe or complicated disease.
  • Confirm in vitro susceptibility: specific therapy based on in vitro susceptibility results.
    • Adult:
      • Ciprofloxacin 750 mg po once daily (or bid) x 3 days or Levofloxacin 500 mg po once daily x 3 days;
      • If severely ill, can use Ciprofloxacin or Levofloxacin IV or Ceftriaxone 1-2 gm IV once daily x 5 days.
    • Pediatric:
      • Azithromycin 10 mg/kg/day (max dose 1 gm) once daily x 3 days.
      • For severe disease: Ceftriaxone 50–75 mg/kg/day x 2–5 days or Ciprofloxacin suspension 10 mg/kg bid x 5 days.
  • If patient is immunocompromised (adult or child) treat for 7-10 days.
  • Antimicrobial resistant strains more common in international travelers, immunocompromised individuals and MSM. 

Alternative Regimens

  • Azithromycin 500 mg po once daily x 3 days (adults)
  • TMP-SMX 160/800 mg po bid for 5 days (only if isolate is confirmed to be susceptible).

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Shigellosis is a self-limited infection, lasting 5-7 days.  Treatment shortens course of illness by only a day or two, promotes resistance, and may prolong shedding.
  • Reserve therapy for more severely ill patients or for use in an outbreak setting if so advised by public health officials.
  • Isolates with ciprofloxacin MICs of 0.12 µg/mL to 1.0 µg/mL may be reported as susceptible but these concentrations are associated with presence of fluoroquinolone resistance genes.  Confirm MIC and avoid  fluoroquinolones if the ciprofloxacin MIC is  > 0.12 μg/mL even if the laboratory reports the isolate as susceptible.
  • Outbreaks of Shigella flexneri resistant to azithromycin have been reported in MSM Lancet Infect Dis. 15:913, 2015. 
  • Outbreaks of MDR/XDR Shigelli sonnei resistant to multiple antibiotics have been reported in retirement facilities Clin Inf Dis 74:455 2022 and amongst MSM Lancet ID 2022 S1473-3099(22)00370-X.
  • Increasing risk of antibiotic resistance to ceftriaxone, azithromycin, or fluoroquinolones for Shigella infection especially in MSM: Emerg Infect Dis, 22:1613, 2016. Lancet Infect Dis. 15:913, 2015. Lancet ID 2022, S1473-3099(22)00370-X. 

Comments

  • CDC advisory about resistance and CDC resistance threat report monitor increasing Shigella antimicrobial resistance.
  • It takes at least 48 hrs of effective therapy before stool cultures become negative.
  • Resistance to fluoroquinolones increasing and more strains are resistant to Azithromycin, agent of choice for treating infections due to multiple-drug resistant strains (Emerg Infect Dis 22:1083, 2016). 
  • Current empiric recommendations for traveler's diarrhea are for treatment as a single dose, continuing for 3 days if symptoms not improved.
