Peritonitis: Rupture, Perforation, Abscess

Clinical Setting

  • Contamination of peritoneal cavity by bowel flora due to bowel perforation, ruptured appendix, ruptured diverticula, ischemic bowel, leaking surgical anastomosis, intraabdominal abscess or other like conditions.
  • Mild/Moderate peritonitis:
    • Inpatient parenteral therapy.
    • Examples: Focal peri-appendiceal peritonitis, peri-diverticular abscess.
  • Severe peritonitis, often with septic shock:
    • Life-threatening infection, patient in ICU.
    • Consequence of a wide range of intra-abdominal processes resulting in spillage of intestinal content into the peritoneum: e.g., ruptured appendix, vascular insufficiency with necrotic bowel, surgical inadvertent enterotomy.

  • Surgical consultation is indicated in virtually all patients. Need to control the source of the peritonitis. See Comments for rationale behind treatment regimens. General Guideline references: IDSA Treatment Guideline: Clin Infect Dis 50:133, 2010; Chinese Society of Surgical Infections. Clin Infect Dis.2020; 71 (S4): S337.

Etiologies

  • Enterobacteriaceae: e.g., E.coli, Klebsiella species. Other aerobic gram-negative bacilli
  • Anaerobic Bacteria:
    • Bacteroides species (anaerobic gram-negative bacilli) and other anaerobic bacteria
    • Anaerobic gram-positive bacilli to include Eggerthella lenta (Clin Infect Dis 2018;67:221) notable for:
      • High associated mortality
      • In vitro resistance to Piperacillin-tazobactam; susceptible to Metronidazole and carbapenems
  • E. faecalis, E. faecium
  • P. aeruginosa  (uncommon, 3-15 %)
  • Most often, within a few hrs of infection, dominant flora is a combination of an aerobic gram-negative bacillus, e.g., E. coli plus an anaerobic gram-negative bacillus, B. fragilis. 
  • Rarely Actinomyces can lead to a protracted infection with sinus tract formation
  • Role of Candida species is unclear when fecal flora contaminant the abdomen. A prospective randomized placebo-controlled multicenter trial found no benefit to adding preemptive antifungal therapy to prevent invasive candidiasis after GI surgery for intra-abdominal infections (Clin Infect DIs 61:1671, 2015)

Primary Regimens

  • Mild/Moderate Peritonitis; inpatient; requires parenteral therapy:
    • Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h
    • Ertapenem 1 gm IV q24h
    • Moxifloxacin 400 mg IV q24h
  • Severe Disease (diffuse peritonitis or septic shock): Patient is in ICU; requires parenteral therapy:
    • Meropenem 1 gm IV q8h or Imipenem 500 mg to 1 gm IV q6h or Doripenem 500 mg IV q8h (1-hr infusion)
  • Duration of therapy is variable and clinical trial data, especially for severe disease is sparse:
    • Mild/Moderate peritonitis: clinical trial found comparable clinical outcomes in patients treated for 4 days vs those treated until vital signs and GI continuity had returned (mean of 8 days). All patients had "source control" (N Engl J Med 372:1996, 2015; N Engl J Med 372:2062, 2015).
    • Severe peritonitis: need source control and resolution of fever, leukocytosis and ileus. Some centers continue antibiotics until the  procalcitonin serum concentration is < 0.25 mg/ml or has decreased by 90% from its peak concentration.

Alternative Regimens

  • Mild/Moderate Disease:
    • (Ciprofloxacin 400 mg IV q12h or Levofloxacin 750 mg IV q24h) + Metronidazole 1 gm IV q12h
    • Cefepime 2 gm q12h + Metronidazole 1 gm IV q12h
    • Eravacycline 1 mg/kg IV over 60 min q12h
  • Severe Disease:
    • Metronidazole 1 gm IV q12h + (Ciprofloxacin 400 mg IV q8h or Levofloxacin 750 mg IV q24h)
    • (Ceftolozane-tazobactam 1.5 gm IV + Metronidazole 500 mg IV (1-hr infusion)) q8h
      • Evidence of decreased efficacy if CrCl between 30-50 ml/min (Clin Infect Dis 60: 1462, 2015)
    • Ceftazidime-avibactam 2.5 gm IV over 2 hrs + Metronidazole 500 mg IV over 60 minutes; repeat combination every 8 hrs.
      • Evidence of decreased efficacy if CrCl between 30-50 ml/min (Clin Infect Dis 62:1380, 2016) OR
    • Aztreonam 1 gm IV q8h or 2 gm IV q6h + Metronidazole 1 gm IV q24h
    • Eravacycline 1 mg/kg IV over 60 min q12h x4-14 days.
      • Based on randomized controlled phase 3 study demonstrating non-inferiority vs Ertapenem or Meropenem (see Clin Infect Dis 2019;69: 921)
  • See Primary Regimens for duration of therapy

Comments

  • General principal for empiric therapy of peritonitis due to bacterial flora of the GI tract
    • Select drug regimen that includes drugs with predictable activity vs both aerobic and anaerobic gram negative bacteria
      • Drugs active vs only aerobic gram-negative bacteria:
        • Aminoglycosides
        • 2nd, 3rd, and 4th generation cephalosporins and Aztreonam
        • Antipseudomonal penicillins: e.g., piperacillin without tazobactam (rarely used or available)
        • Ciprofloxacin and Levofloxacin
      • Drugs active vs only anaerobic gram negative bacilli and some anaerobic gram-positive bacilli:
        • Metronidazole: see comment
        • Ceftolozane-tazobactam and Ceftazidime-avibactam have some, but incomplete activity vs anaerobes and hence need concomitant Metronidazole
        • Drugs no longer recommended as having predictable activity vs anaerobes:
          • Cefoxitin and Cefotetan (Clin Infect Dis 2014;59:698)
      • Drugs active vs both aerobic and anaerobic gram-negative bacilli:
        • Piperacillin-tazobactam. In RCT, Piperacillin-tazobactam failed vs Meropenem in patients with infections due to ceftriaxone-resistant E. coli or K. pneumoniae (JAMA 2018, 320:979 & 984).
        • Ampicillin-sulbactam (Clin Infect Dis 2010;50:133) is no longer a recommended option for empiric therapy due to increasing resistance of E. coli and other Enterobacterales; resistance in up to 45% of clinical  E. coli urine isolates (Current Urology. 2022;16:94)
        • Carbapenems: meropenem, imipenem, doripenem
        • Moxifloxacin:  limited experience with severe disease. Maybe OK for mild peritonitis
        • Eravacycline: Patients with severe infections were excluded from FDA registration trials (Clin Infect Dis 2019;69: 921)
  • Common areas of uncertainty and questions:
    • Need for empiric therapy for MRSA? No clear need for peritonitis
    • Need for empiric therapy for enterococci?  Usually not necessary. Exception maybe patient at risk of endocarditis due to known valvular heart disease
    • Empiric therapy of Candida species?
      • No need to treat empirically(preemptively) or for Candida found as part of mixed culture from patient operated on  for perforated viscus (e.g., perforated appendix or diverticulitis).
      • Prospective randomized placebo-controlled trial found no benefit of preemptive echinocandin therapy in preventing invasive candidiasis in high-risk surgical patients with intra-abdominal infections (Clin Infect Dis 61:1671, 2015).
      • Treat for Candida if:
        • Pure culture of Candida species from abdominal cavity or blood
        • Concomitant Presence of acute necrotizing pancreatitis
        • Presence of positive fungal biomarker, e.g., serum beta-D-glucan, pending culture results in patient not responding to antibacterial therapy
  • Metronidazole regimens:
    • Wide variation of metronidazole dose and dose frequency; No prospective controlled trials
    • Interest in once daily dosing of metronidazole because:
      • Long serum half life: 6-10 hrs
      • Low plasma protein binding: <1- to 20%
      • Concentration-dependent killing and long post-antibiotic effect.
      • Hydroxy metabolite has antibacterial activity
      • PK/PD study in human volunteers found similar AUC antibacterial target concentrations between 500 mg IV q8h and 1500 mg IV once daily (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 2004;48: 4597)
      • Observational reports of efficacy of once daily dosing in pediatric patients with acute appendicitis: J Ped Infect Dis Soc 2017; 6:57
