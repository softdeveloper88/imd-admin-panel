Primaquine phosphate

Usage and Dosing

  • Drug of choice for the eradication of the hepatic stage of malaria parasites.
    • Tafenoquine, a long-acting analogue with single-dose therapy, is FDA approved but tafenoquine can only be used in combination with chloroquine and not other primary blood stage treatments.
      • Primaquine has less toxicity in G6PD-deficient persons.
    • Screen for G6PD deficiency prior to primaquine therapy. G6PD reports are complex to understand. 
      • Point of care rapid tests for G6PD are in advanced development PLoS Negl Trop Dis. 2023 Oct 12;17(10):e0011652.
      • New (2024) WHO classification system for G6PD deficiency (Bull World Health Organ 2024;102:615)
    • P. ovale relapses in returned travelers seen in 2.8% receiving PQ vs 10.0% in patients not receiving PQ (not statistically different).
      • Supportive evidence for primaquine use in P. ovale remains limited. Clin Infect Dis 2021 Jul 3:ciab610. doi: 10.1093/cid/ciab610.
  • Effective against blood stages of P. vivax and P. falciparum outside Africa but no longer a good alternative for malaria prophylaxis.

Adult Dose

  • Note: Doses are given as the base: 15 mg base = 26.3 mg of Primaquine phosphate.  26.3 mg tablets often erroneously mistaken as the recommended 30 mg base dosing and only 1 tablet per day rather than 2 given.
  • Can take with meals to lessen GI side effects
To prevent relapse of P. vivax and P. ovale malaria

30 mg base (2 tablets) po once daily x 14 days.  Total dose of primaquine in patients ≥70 kg should be adjusted to a total dose of 6 mg/kg, given in daily doses of 30 mg/day times the number of days to complete the total dose.

 If documented mild (type 3) G6PD deficiency, 45 mg base po once weekly x 8 weeks.  

WHO: no longer recommends less than 14 days of primaquine as a viable option. 

Gametocidal activity in P. falciparum only

WHO recommends a single dose of 0.25 mg/kg primaquine (with ACT treatment for P.f. cases) in low transmission P. falciparum areas to reduce ongoing transmission. G6PD testing is not required. Exclude pregnant women,  age < 6 mos and women breastfeeding infants aged < 6 mos.

For primary prophylaxis of P. vivax and outside of Africa P. falciparum

30 mg base (2 tablets) po once daily from 1 day before until 7 days after exposure

Alternative therapy: For Pneumocystis pneumonia (PJP)15 - 30 mg of base po once daily x 21 days
with Clindamycin 600 mg IV or po q8h x 21 days

Adverse Effects

  • In G6PD deficient patients, can cause hemolytic anemia with hemoglobinuria, especially persons of African, Asian origin. Methemoglobinemia can occur.
  • Nausea/abdominal pain if patient fasting (Clin Infect Dis 39:1336, 2004).
  • Point-of-care G6PD test cards becoming more widely available.
