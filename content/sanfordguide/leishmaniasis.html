Leishmaniasis

Clinical Setting

  • Three clinical forms; Cutaneous, mucosal (Espundia), or visceral (Kala-Azar) leishmaniasis.
  • Cutaneous leishmaniasis treatment is determined by severity of disease
    • Mild Disease: 4 or fewer lesions (1 cm or more), none ≥ 5 cm in diameter; no lesions in cosmetically sensitive areas, no lesions over joints or genitalia; may observe.
    • Complex Disease: > 4 lesions and/or one over 5 cm in diameter and/or lesion(s) over joints or in cosmetically sensitive area(s), or failure of previous treatment, or substantial local or lymphatic nodules, or large regional lymphadenopathy.
    • Leishmania in travelers frequently responds to observation or local therapy (Clin Infect Dis 57:370, 2013).
    • L. brasiliensis cutaneous disease requires treatment due to risk of mucosal dissemination.
      • Concept challenged, see International Health 4:153, 2012.
  • Mucosal disease (L. brasiliensis or less commonly other Viannia genus species; new world only)
    • palate, oropharynx including vocal cords; essentially never seen in travelers.
  • Visceral disease (VL) presents with insidious onset but prolonged fever, weight loss, and prominent splenomegaly.
    • GI involvement with HIV infection.
    • Mostly in Brazil, India, Ethiopia, South Sudan, and Sudan. 
  • Unusual syndromes: post kala-azar dermal leishmaniasis (Clin Infect Dis 2018; 67:667), leishmaniasis recidivans, diffuse CL, disseminated CL, and bubonic leishmaniasis.
  • Large European experience in travelers: Euro Surveill 2022 Jan;27(4):2002028.
  • Drugs have widely varying effects against different species even from same geographic area.

Diagnosis

  • Cutaneous: Sample full depth punch biopsy from raised edge of ulcer.
    • PCR mandatory to speciate; culture and histology also available. Culture for sporotrichosis in highly endemic areas.
    • Rapid diagnosis often possible using needle aspirate, slit skin smear, brushings, or scraping of lesion edge with giemsa staining.
    • Serology not useful. Leishmanin skin test used in several countries.  
  • Mucosal:  Biopsy most active lesion for histology and PCR.
  • Serology not useful.
  • Visceral:  Bone marrow aspirate for histology, culture, PCR or serology.  
    • Splenic aspirate of historical significance, do not do.
    • K39 rapid test discontinued at present.

Etiologies

  • L. brasiliensis (Cutaneous & Mucosal, Latin America)
    • High risk in Manu Park, Peru and Madidi Park, Bolivia
  • L. donovani (Visceral, India, Africa)
  • L. infantum (Visceral, Mediterranean)
  • L. chagasi (Visceral, Western Hemisphere)
    • (L. infantum and L. chagasi are the same organism)
  • L. major (Cutaneous, Old World)
  • L. tropica (Cutaneous, Old World)
  • L. aethiopica (Cutaneous, Old World)
  • L. peruviana (Cutaneous, New World)
  • L. mexicana (Cutaneous, New World)
  • L. amazonensis (Cutaneous, New World)
  • L. guyanensis (Cutaneous, New World)
  • L. panamensis (Cutaneous, New World)
  • Approximate distribution maps: Clin Infect Dis 63:e202, 2016

Primary Regimens

  • Cutaneous: Mild Disease
    • Observation only for spontaneous healing preferred in most cases.
    • Paromomycin ointment bid x 10 days, rest 10 days, then 10 more days. 
      • Topical paromomycin/gentamicin in Aquaphilic (proprietary to US military) has shown some success in Panama and Bolivia (Clin Infect Dis. 68:844, 2019)
        • No more effective than paromomycin ointment alone in another trial in Panama (PLoS Negl Trop Dis 2019;13: e0007253).
      • Good effect in travelers with old world CL. PLoS Negl Trop Dis. 2023 Aug 10;17(8):e0011492.
    • Heat therapy, see www.thermosurgery.com; Mil Med 177:345, 2012.
      • Data only for L. tropica, L. major, L. mexicana.
        • One time treatment, painful.
    • Intralesional (IL) antimony. Meglumine antimoniate (Glucantime) up to 20 mg/kg intralesionally weekly x 5-10 weeks.  Use undiluted drug.
      • For mild disease and can combine with cryotherapy 5-8 sessions until healing. 
      • PAHO Guidelines prefer intralesional dosing if few lesions.
      • IL not advocated by CDC; commonly used in Europe and South America. 
      • Data on IL dosing in old world CL (PLoS Negl Trop Dis 2022 Jul 8;16(7):e0010569).
      • Review of consideration for IL dosing. Clin Infect Dis 2023;77:583.
    • Cryotherapy: freeze up to 3 times with liquid nitrogen (alone or together with intralesional antimony)
    • Laser therapy
    • Debride necrotic tissue before any local therapy instituted.
  • Cutaneous: Complex Disease
    • Meglumine antimoniate (Glucantime) 20 mg/kg/day IV/IM x 20 days
    • Liposomal Amphotericin B 3 mg/kg  IV once daily days 1-5 and 10 (J Am Acad Dermatol 68:284, 2013) or daily on days 1-7.
    • Systematic review of use of short course liposomal Amphotericin B for CL and ML. Open Forum Infect Dis. 2023 Jul 11;10(7):ofad348. 
    • With HIV consider treating as for L. donovani with either (but not both) Liposomal Amphotericin B or Miltefosine or meglumine antimoniate 20 mg/kg IV or IM daily for 28 days
  • Cutaneous: L. braziliensis of any severity
    • Meglumine antimoniate (Glucantime)] 20 mg/kg/day IV/IM x 20 days
    • Liposomal Amphotericin B  3 mg/kg  IV once daily days 1-5 and days 14, 21 or days 1-5 and 10 (J Am Acad Dermatol 68:284, 2013).
  • Mucosal
    • Liposomal Amphotericin B (regimens vary) with total cumulative dose of 20-60 mg/kg (Trans R Soc Trop Med Hyg 108:176, 2014) OR
    • Meglumine antimoniate (Glucantime) 20 mg/kg/day IV or IM x 28 days OR
    • Amphotericin B 0.5-1 mg/kg IV daily or qod to total dose of 20-45 mg/kg.
  • Visceral
    • Immunocompetent: Liposomal Amphotericin B 3 mg/kg  IV once daily days 1-5 and days 14, 21 (need 21 mg/kg total dose).  
      • Shorter courses of combination therapy for VL have been unsuccessful.  Lancet Inf Dis 2024;1:e36-46. 
      • East Africa (high resistance):  Combination of Meglumine antimoniate (Glucantime) and Paromomycin for 17 days first line for public health.
        • Can use high dose Liposomal Amphotericin B (Ambisome) 40 mg/kg total dose.
    • HIV/AIDS: 
      • L. donovani: Liposomal Amphotericin B (up to 30 mg/kg cumulative, at 5 mg/kg on days 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11) + Miltefosine (100 mg/day po X 14 days in Asia and most places and 28d for East Africa) 
      • L. infantum/chagasi: Liposomal Amphotericin B (total dose of 20–60 mg/kg via either
        • 3–5 mg/kg IV daily, or
        • Interrupted schedule, such as 4 mg/kg IV on Days 1–5, 10, 17, 24, 31.
        • this schedule (no miltefosine) is an alternate for L. Donovani
      • Secondary prophylaxis (CD4 <200) with Liposomal Amphotericin B (3-5 mg/kg) or Amphotericin B (1 mg/kg) q2-4 wks until CD4 >350.
        • Consider stopping secondary prophylaxis when the CD4 count is >350 cells/mm, viral load undetectable for 6 months, and asymptomatic.
          • Alternate Therapy
            • Amphotericin B lipid complex 3 mg/kg every 21 days, or
            • Pentavalent antimony (meglumine antimoniate) 20 mg/kg IV or IM every 4 weeks or
            • Pentamidine isethionate 4 mg/kg (maximum of 300 mg) IV every 2–4 week
      • Reference: WHO Guideline 7 Jun 2022 and NIH Guidelines for HIV OIs.
  • Note:  Glucantime dosing is in mg pentavalent antimony. Glucantime:  available as 1.5g/5ml solution for injection, with 0.4050g of antimony per 5ml ampoule (eg. 75 mg of meglumine antimoniate contains 20 mg of antimony).

Alternative Regimens

  • Cutaneous: Complex Disease
    • Fluconazole 200 mg po daily x 6 weeks. Data for L. major only.
    • Ketoconazole 600 mg po daily for 30 days. Data for L. mexicana, L. panamensis, and L. major.
    • Miltefosine  Wt >45 kg 50 mg po tid; Wt 30-44 kg 50 mg po bid for 28 days.
      • Not FDA approved under 12 years of age or wt < 30 kg. 
        • 2.5 mg/kg/day (up to 150 mg maximum) in other countries. For L. braziliensis, L. guyanensis, L. panamensis only.
    • Amphotericin B 0.5-1 mg/kg IV daily or qod to total dose of 15-30 mg/kg
  • Mucosal
    • Efficacy of oral Miltefosine is variable.
    • Miltefosine Wt >45 kg 50 mg po tid; Wt 30-44 kg 50 mg po bid for 28 days. 
  • Visceral
    • Miltefosine  Wt >45 kg 50 mg po tid; Wt 30-44 kg 50 mg po bid for 28 days.
      • Not FDA approved under 12 years of age or wt < 30 kg.  2.5 mg/kg/day (up to 150 mg maximum) in other countries.
        • Resistance to monotherapy in India and parts of southern Africa. 
      • Only FDA approved for L. donovani not L. infantum. Limited data in HIV patients, don't use as mono therapy.
    • Standard Amphotericin B 1 mg/kg IV daily  to a total of 1.5-2.0 gm (can use in HIV) or qod x 8 weeks (to total of 15-20 mg/kg)
    • Meglumine antimoniate (Glucantime)] 20 mg/kg/day IV or IM x 28 days (unless exposure was in Indian subcontinent)
    • Liposomal Amphotericin B 10 mg/kg IV on days 1 and 2) for children with L. infantum in Europe
    • Miltefosine plus paromomycin for refractory VL in East Africa  (Clin Infect Dis 2022 Sep 27;ciac643)
    • L. infantum/chagasi with HIV
      • Amphotericin B deoxycholate* 0.5–1.0 mg/kg IV daily total dose of 1.5–2.0 grams OR
      • Meglumine antimoniate) 20 mg/kg IV or IM daily for 28 days. 

Comments

  • Follow clinical response, cutaneous lesion(s) should be >50% healed by 4-6 weeks or no signs of healing by end of treatment indicates failure and referral to an expert for further treatment.
    • No need for re-biopsy in follow-up, parasite DNA and dead organisms persist even with successful therapy
  • Definitive and detailed expert consensus guidelines for US/Canadian setting (Clin Infect Dis 63:e202, 2016).  
    • Few RCTs to guide approach for any species and guidelines don't specify treatment of choice for cutaneous disease,
      • all possibilities above are listed as alternatives that must be individualized.
  • Glucantime (Meglumine antimoniate) 
    • Available from Sanofi after obtaining an individual IND from FDA.  See Antiparasitic Drugs, Sources
      • Stibogluconate sodium (Pentostam, GSK) has been discontinued by GSK worldwide and is no longer available from CDC. 
        • Many generic pentavalent antimonial preparations of variable quality widely distributed in the developing world.
  • Short course Miltefosine (14d.) for new world cutaneous leishmaniasis when combined with thermotherapy (PLOS Neglected Tropical Diseases 2022; 16(3):e0010238.)
  • Late relapses after liposomal amphotericin treatment of cutaneous or mucocutaneous disease (PLoS Negl Trop Dis 11:e0006094, 2017).
  • Experimental surrogate markers of latent visceral leishmaniasis more than a decade after return from Iraq in asymptomatic US veterans, no parasitologic proof of active infection (Clin Infect Dis 68:2036,2019)
  • Miltefosine FDA approved for cutaneous, mucocutaneous, and visceral disease.
    • Must be ordered directly from Profounda, Inc. 407-270-7790. www.impavido.com.
    • Licensed in Europe, Central and South America.
  • Topical Paromomycin ointment; (paromomycin sulfate 15% in methylbenzethonium chloride 12% quite irritating and efficacy not clear) available from compounding pharmacies;
    • Leishcutan (Teva) available from Victoria Pharmacy (see Antiparasitic Drugs, Sources).
    • Topical treatment indicated only when low potential for mucosal spread.
  • Consider treating VL and Mucosal disease in pregnancy if benefit outweighs risk. 
    • Liposomal amphotericin B is the first choice for therapy of VL in pregnancy
      • concerns about toxicity and lack of experience with use of pentavalent antimony in human pregnancy.
