ART in Pregnancy

Treatment Goals, Risk Factors

  • Treatment of HIV in pregnancy requires attention to 2 complementary goals (clinicalinfo.HIV.org).
    • Provide optimal treatment to the woman and reduce VL to < 50 cps/mL.
    • Prevent transmission to the infant without drug toxicity.
    • Eliminating mother-to-child (MTCT) is an achievable goal
  • Risk factors for mother-to-child transmission (MTCT)
    • Maternal viral load (outset & at delivery are independent predictors (J Infect Dis 183:539, 2001)
    • Maternal CD4 count (risk of transmission increased 3-fold if CD4 <400)
    • Lack of ART (independent of other factors) (J Infect Dis 183:539, 2001)
    • Prolonged rupture of membranes (rate doubled if >4 hours) (N Engl J Med 334:1617, 1996)
    • Mode of delivery (natural vs. C-section)
    • Breastfeeding in the absence of ART (additional 10–14% transmission) (JAMA 282:744, 1999)
    • Shorter duration of antepartum ART (AIDS 22:973, 2008)
  • General principles of ART in pregnancy
    • Preventing MTCT should be integrated with obstetrical & HIV medical care for the mother. The mother should be informed & involved in decisions.
    • ART should be started as early in pregnancy as possible, regardless of HIV viral load or CD4 count
    • DHHS preferred regimens are based on efficacy, tolerability, PK in pregnancy and risk of toxicity to mother and fetus
    • Resistance testing recommended before beginning ART and if on therapy, but detectable viral load
    • Long-term safety of ART for infant exposed in utero is not fully known. Generally safe, although conflicting data on mitochondrial toxicity; myocardial function from ZDV (J Am Coll Cardio 57:76, 2011)
    • DHHS now lists dolutegravir as a preferred regimen because of the other benefits.
    • Preliminary data from trial in Botswana reported increased rates of neural tube defects in women taking dolutegravir at the time of conception. Subsequent studies have shown that the risk is quite small, and thought to be restricted to the first 4 weeks after conception. The risk of neural tube deficit in women with adequate folic acid supplementation is not known. 
    • Some data suggest increased rate of prematurity, low birth weight with PI use during pregnancy (AIDS 2018 Jan 14;32(2):243-252); given clear benefits, PIs should not be withheld if otherwise indicated. Optimal dosing in pregnancy has not been adequately studied for all agents
    • PI levels fall in third trimester (lopinavir/ritonavir, atazanavir). Avoid unboosted PI and avoid once daily dosing of DRV/RTV and LPV//RTV. Consider obtaining levels for select patients with complex resistance.
  • Initial evaluation of women who are pregnant and not currently on HIV therapy
    • Similar to non pregnant patients
    • Review resistance testing in conjunction with antiretroviral history
    • CD4 monitoring for those with CD4 counts > 300 is similar to non pregnant patients
    • Viral load monitoring should be more frequent. Monitor 2-4 weeks after initiating or changing therapy, and at least every 3-4 months thereafter. Viral load should be assessed at 36 weeks gestation or ~4 weeks prior to a planned delivery. 

Considerations for Regimens

  • Combination therapy with a preferred NRTI backbone (2 NRTIs) + a potent third agent is recommended (see clinicalinfo.HIV.gov). See also, ART in Treatment Naive Adults. Combination formulations improve convenience and compliance
  • Preferred NRTI BackboneTDF +  3TC or FTC, TAF + 3TC or FTC or ABC* + 3TC
    Preferred 3rd AgentsDarunavir/r**, or Dolutegravir,***(preferred agent if presenting with acute HIV or late in pregnancy)
    Alternative NRTI AgentsZDV + 3TC
     Alternative 3rd AgentsAtazanavir/r or Raltegravir, or Bictegravir, or EFV, or Rilpivirine
    • *Abacavir should only be used in women who test negative for HLA-B*5701
    • ** Darunavir/r preferred for patients with prior use of carbotegravir for PreP
    • ***Observational studies in Botswana, where folate supplementation is not routinely used, reported small increase in neural tube defects when dolutegravir taken at time of conception. Most recent data from Tsepamo study report neural tube defect in 0.3% on dolutegravir vs 0.1% on non-dolutegravir. N Engl J Med 381:827, 2019. All pregnant women and women trying to conceive should received folic acid to reduce risk of neural tube defects although whether this reduces risk associated with dolutegravir is unknown. This information should be discussed. see DHHS counseling guideline 
    • There are insufficient data to recommend TAF or bictegravir in pregnancy
  • ART is recommended for all pregnant women. Treatment should be started as early in pregnancy as possible
    • Transmission in cohorts 0.5–2.0% with 3-drug ART
    • Regimen selection should be individualized based on resistance, side effect profile, convenience, likelihood of adherence, potential drug interactions, and maternal toxicity.
    • Pregnant women receiving and tolerating an ARV regimen that is suppressing viral replication should continue the regimen;
      • if already on dolutegravir and in first trimester, counsel about risk of neural tube defect (see above). For many women, the window of risk may have already passed by the time they seek prenatal care.
    • If on atazanavir/cobicistat-, darunavir/cobicistat-, or elvitegravir/cobicistat-containing regimen, consider changing because of risk of low drug exposure and treatment failure later in pregnancy
    • If not fully suppressed on current regimen, obtain resistance testing to guide in selection of at least 2 new active drugs 
    • ZDV & 3TC are frequently used nucleosides based on experience, but is considered an alternative. ZDV requires twice daily dosing. The use of d4T & ddI should be avoided; combination (D4T + ddI) is contraindicated.
    • Tenofovir associated with decreased in growth and bone abnormalities in pregnant monkeys, but no evidence to date on humans. TDF + FTC is one of preferred ART regimens and regimen of choice for Hepatitis B co-infection.
    • WHO recommends 2 NRTI backbone (ZDV + 3TC) and an NNRTI (nevirapine or efavirenz).
    • Package insert continues to recommend avoiding EFV in 1st trimester but human data are reassuring. AIDS 28 Suppl 2:S123 2014 

Specific Situations

  • For pregnant women who have been on combination ART and have sustained viral suppression and HIV RNA < 1000 copies/mL during late pregnancy and near delivery: Continue oral combination ART during labor. Therapy can be continued preoperatively with sips of water even if caesarian section is planned. Intravenous zidovudine is not required.

  • For pregnant women who have received antepartum combination ART but have suboptimal viral suppression near deliveryA >1000 copies/mL). Women who have HIV RNA > 1000 copies/mL should be offered scheduled C-section at 38 weeks. Intravenous zidovudine should be given along with the other oral ART. Additional antiretroviral prophylaxis for the neonate should be considered - see below.

  • Women in labor who have not received combination ART during pregnancy: All HIV-infected women who present in labor who were not on therapy and those with a positive rapid HIV test should be immediately started on intravenous zidovudine. Additional antiretroviral prophylaxis should be given to the neonate.

  • Women in labor with unknown HIV status: Expedited HIV testing with results within 24 hours should be available in all obstetrical units. All women should undergo expedited antigen/antibody test. If results will be delayed, rapid HIV test should be done. If test is positive, begin antiviral prophylaxis for infant and send HIV-1/HIV-2 antibody  differentiation assay.  If recent infection or acute HIV suspected, send NAT

ART: Infants Postpartum

  • All infants born to an HIV-positive mother should receive postpartum prophylaxis. Selection of ARV regimen now based on maternal and infant risk factors.
  • Prophylactic ARV regimen should be started as soon as possible, preferable within 6-12 hours of delivery
  • Low risk infants (infants of women with consistent viral suppression during pregnancy, at least 10 weeks of antiretroviral therapy, HIV VL < 50 copies/mL, and no concerns about maternal adherence) A 2-week regimen of ZDV can be used.
  • Infants whose mothers do not meet all the above criteria but have viral load < 50 copies/mL at delivery should receive 4-6 weeks of ZDV (see table 8F for dose based on gestational age) 
  • Infants at higher risk of HIV infection should receive a multidrug ARV regimen: This is now referred to as presumptive antiretroviral therapy. These are infants who:
    • Have not received antepartum or intrapartum ARV drugs or
    • Have received only intrapartum ARV drugs or
    • Have received antepartum ARV drugs but without viral suppression to < 50 copies/mL near delivery or
    • Have primary or acute HIV infection during pregnancy or
    • Have primary or acute HIV infection during breastfeeding.
  • Infants born to women with unknown status or whose preliminary test is positive should be started on multidrug prophylactic ARV or presumptive ARV based on assessment of risk. ARV can be stopped if mother can be determined to be HIV negative.
  • Infants with HIV infection (positive NAT) ARV should be initiated
  • Consider testing for additional infections based on parental history: Syphilis, Hepatitis C. Some would screen all infants born to HIV infected birth parent for CMV with urine or saliva PCR
  • Providers with questions about ARV management of perinatal HIV exposure should consult the National Perinatal HIV Hotline (1-888-448-8765)
  • Multidrug presumptive ARV regimens:
    • Zidovudine, 3TC and daily Nevirapine treatment dose for 6 weeks or
    • Zidovudine, 3TC, and Raltegravir for 6 weeks
  • Raltegravir (Approximately 1.5 mg/kg per dose; see wt band dosing) was safe and achieved adequate levels in term neonates in IMPAACT P1110. Raltegravir dosing needs to be increased at 1 week (Approximately 3 mg/kg per dose; see wt band dosing) and 4 weeks of age (Approximately 6 mg/kg per dose; see wt band dosing). No dosing information is available for infants born before 37 weeks EGA or weighing <2 kg.
  • Prophylaxis for infants born to women with antiretroviral resistance must be individualized. Expert Consultation Recommended (National (US) Perinatal Hotline 1-888-448-8765)
  • If an infant on antiretroviral prophylaxis is found to be HIV infected by HIV RNA or DNA PCR, prophylaxis should be stopped immediately and treatment should be initiated with combination ART

Infant Breastfeeding

  • Based on recent data, women with sustained viral suppression may consider breastfeeding. 
  • Achieving and maintaining viral suppression through ART during pregnancy and postpartum decreases breastfeeding transmission risk to less than 1%, but not zero

  • For additional information on appropriate monitoring of mother and infants during breastfeeding see HHS Perinatal Guidelines

Intrapartum care

  • Pregnant people with unknown HIV status or those at increased risk who were not retested in the third trimester should have expedited Ag/Ab testing for HIV
  • Intravenous zidovudine is not required for women on ART who have HIV RNA <50 copies/mL and good adherence.
  • Intravenous zidovudine should be administered during labor to all pregnant patients with RNA ≥ 1000 copies and considered for those with RNA >50 but <1000 copies
  • Elective C-section before the onset of labor reduces transmission by 50% for women on no therapy or ZDV monotherapy (N Engl J MedM 340:977, 1999). However, in recent cohorts on 3-drug therapy with suppressed HIV RNA, no apparent additional benefit of C-section was observed (AIDS 22:973, 2008).
  • Considerations
    • This should be discussed with the patient & they should be involved in the decision.
    • Elective C-section (before 38 weeks) should be considered if Maternal viral load >1000 at delivery despite ART. Possible benefit if >50 but <1000 copies.
    • Unknown viral load at delivery: Mother received less than 3-drug therapy; mother presents late in pregnancy; obstetrical indications or maternal preference.
    • Elective C-section is not cost-effective in resource-poor settings
  • Group B Strep prophylaxis recommended for C-section.
  • AROM, fetal scalp electrodes, operative delivery (forceps, vacuum extractor) and episiotomy should be avoided unless clear obstetric indications exist.

Pneumocycstis Pneumonia Prophylaxis

  • Recommended for women with CD4 count <200 or on prophylaxis. PJP during pregnancy can be more severe. See Pneumocystis pneumonia (PJP), Adult.
  • TMP-SMX may be used, although use in last trimester may be associated with ↑ bilirubin. Risk of kernicterus unknown but very small. TMP-SMX reduced maternal & infant mortality among mothers with CD4 < 200 in resource poor setting. 1st trimester exposure might be related to small increase in birth defect rate.
  • Dapsone: no known adverse effects, although experience limited.
  • Aerosolized Pentamidine. Little systemic absorption, although less effective in advanced disease. Effect of ventilation changes due to pregnancy on distribution is unknown. Can be used in 1st trimester.
