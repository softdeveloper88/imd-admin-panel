Cryptosporidiosis

Clinical Setting

  • Gastrointestinal infection caused by Cryptosporidium, especially in HIV/AIDS.
    • Sexual transmission in men who have sex with men.
  • Self-limited watery diarrhea in immunocompetent adults, not usually requiring treatment but symptoms may persist for weeks to months.
  • Refractory diarrhea with malabsorption with AIDS or severe immunocompromised.
    • Cryptosporidium infections have also been found in other organs including biliary tree, the lungs, and possibly conjunctiva.
  • Refractory to chlorination, transmission in swimming pools.
  • In general, treatment is unsatisfactory, see general review: Lancet Infect Dis 15:85, 2015.
  • Responds to treatment of underlying immunosuppression.
  • For symptoms tincture of opium may be better than loperamide, may try lactose-free diet

Diagnosis

  • Acid-fast staining required for microscopy.
  • Stool antigen, immunofluorescense or PCR commercially available.
  • Due to intermittent excretion of oocysts, suggested that three stool specimens, collected on separate days, be tested.
  • ERCP may be needed to diagnose biliary disease (HIV patients)
    • sphincterotomy, stenting, or both may be of benefit

Etiologies

  • C. hominis (infects only humans)
  • C. parvum (bovine/ovine origin infectious to humans)
  • C. meleagridis
  • Cryptosporidium occultus
  • At least 14 other zoonotic species; some infect humans

Primary Regimens

  • Immunocompetent/HIV negative:
    • Nitazoxanide tablets 500 mg po bid x 3 days; may repeat multiple courses 
      • retest stool at 7 days if still symptomatic 
      • 1-3 years: 100 mg oral suspension po bid X 3d
      • 4-11 years 200 mg oral suspension po bid X 3d
  • Immunocompromised/HIV positive:
    • Effective antiretroviral therapy is the best treatment.
    • May try Nitazoxanide 500 to 1000 mg po bid x 14 days plus optimized ART (single small study).
      • Not licensed for use in immunocompromised

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Paromomycin (or Paromomycin/Azithromycin) generally considered ineffective even in high doses (500 mg po qid x 14-21 days) but some clinicians will still try while instituting ART.

Comments

  • Immunocompetent adults clinical cure 72–88% and parasitologic cure 60–75% after first Nitazoxanide course
  • C. hominis but not C. parvum associated with non-intestinal sequelae, mainly reactive arthropathy (Clin Infect Dis 39:504, 2004).
  • Review: Curr Trop Med Rep 2:181, 2015
  • Patients with HIV may have biliary tract involvement, requiring ERCP for diagnosis. They may also benefit from sphincterotomy, stenting, or both.
  • CDC HIV OI Guidelines for cryptosporidiosis
