Pneumonia, Legionella sp.

Clinical Setting

  • Legionella sp. cause both community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) and hospital-acquired pneumonia (HAP).
    • Despite cough, many patients produce small amounts of mucoid non-purulent sputum
  • Risk factors: Immunocompromised patient, smoking, co-morbidities.
  • Associated clinical findings (although somewhat nonspecific):
    • Diarrhea, other gastrointestinal symptoms
    • Confusion
    • Relative bradycardia
    • Hyponatremia
    • Elevated hepatic enzymes
    • Elevated BUN and creatinine
    • Elevated ferritin levels. Range of peak levels: 591-5990 (Clin Infect Dis 46:1789, 2008)

Diagnosis

  • Difficult to detect in culture (need special media). Patient's often do not produce much sputum
  • Legionella urinary antigen reliable (100% specific) in detection of L. pneumophila, serotype 1, which causes ~80% of the cases in the USA.
    • Sensitivity 70-80%, hence if urine antigen is negative and clinical suspicion is high, suggest Legionella PCR of respiratory specimen
    • Legionella PCR of respiratory specimen detects all clinically relevant Legionella species and serotypes
      • Sensitivity of 0.83-0.98 and specificity of  0.90-0.94 (positive likelihood ratios of 8.3-15, negative likelihood ratios of 0.02-0.2) (J Med Microbiol 2017; 66:485).
      • Best to test sputum or bronchoalveolar lavage; can use nasopharyngeal swabs but the sensitivity is lower.

Etiologies

  • Legionella pneumophilia
  • Other Legionella sp.

Primary Regimens

  • Azithromycin 500 mg IV/po q24h 
  • Levofloxacin 750 mg IV/po q24h or Moxifloxacin 400 mg IV/po q24 

Alternative Regimens

  • Doxycycline 100 mg IV/po bid 

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Initial intravenous therapy in hospitalized patient with switch to oral once patient has stabilized.
  • Duration of therapy 7-10 days for immunocompetent host; longer duration (14-21 days) in patients with severe disease or immunocompromised (Clin Chest Med 2017; 38:45-58).
  • No proven benefit of Rifampin combination therapy (and drug interactions are a major issue in many patients) or combination of Azithromycin + fluoroquinolone

Comments

  • Macrolides and fluoroquinolones are probably equally effective (Clin Infect Dis 2021;72:1979).
  • Reference on diagnosis and treatment: Infect Dis Ther. 2022; 11:973-986.
