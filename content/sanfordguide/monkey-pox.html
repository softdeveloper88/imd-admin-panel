Mpox, Monkeypox

Clinical Setting

  • Zoonotic pathogen with presentation similar to smallpox except lympadenopathy occurs.
    • WHO and CDC have adopted the synonym mpox as the name for the disease caused by monkeypox virus.
  • Outbreaks
    • Clade Ia (previously Congo Basin Clade)
    • Clade Ib
      • Cause of ongoing 2023-2024 outbreak in eastern DRC and neighboring countries
        • >30,000 cases; just 1 US case to date
    • Clade II (previously West African Clade)
      • Clade IIb cause of 2022 global outbreak and almost all current cases outside of Africa
      • 100,000 cases of Clade IIb in 122 total countries, including 115 countries where mpox was not previously reported. 
        • ~10 cases/wk in US during 2024
  • Transmission
    • Transmission mostly from direct person-to-person contact. Skin-skin contact; contact with contaminated clothing, bed sheets; respiratory transmission possible (but much less likely). Transmission reported via needle stick with a skin lesion-contaminated sharp or tattooing / body piercing.
    • Incubation period most frequently ~12 days;   All persons are infectious with onset of illness, but some people can transmit the virus to others up to four days prior to onset of signs or symptoms.
    • Quarantine no longer recommended after exposure; rather, monitor for symptoms. Vaccinate after exposure if possible; Smallpox/mpox vaccination within 4 days of exposure appears to be protective (See Mpox Vaccines)
    • Isolation at home is recommended for people with Mpox from the time of onset of illness through the time the rash has fully resolved.
    • Transmission in health care settings occurs but is uncommon.  Use contact precautions and N95 masks. 
  • Symptoms
    • Prodromal symptoms not universal (42% did not have prodrome) and disseminated skin lesion seen in 20% to 45% of patients; isolated genital lesions have been confused with typical STI
    • Disease symptoms present with prodromal fever, headache,  and cough, followed by adenopathy and a vesiculopapular rash that pustulates, umbilicates and crusts on the head, trunk, groin, and extremities.  All lesions usually synchronous. Macules may develop on palms and soles (similar to syphilis).  Periungual, oral, and conjunctival lesions also reported.
    • Pain in the area of lesions (e.g., genital, anal region) is a hallmark of infection
    • Low index of suspicion; err on the side of testing (PCR of lesion via swab; see Diagnosis below) 
  • Indications for Treatment with Antiviral Agent (Tecovirimat)
    • Patients with severe disease (e.g. hemorrhagic disease, confluent lesions, sepsis, encephalitis or other conditions requiring hospitalization).
    • Patients who are at high risk of severe disease, including those who are:
      • Immunocompromising conditions (e.g. HIV/AIDS, leukemia, lymphoma, generalized malignancy, solid organ transplant, therapy with alkylating agents, antimetabolites, radiation, tumor necrosis factor inhibitors, high-dose corticosteroids, being a recipient of hematopoietic stem cell transplant <24 months post-transplant or >24 months but with graft-versus-host disease or disease relapse, or having autoimmune disease with immunodeficiency as a clinical component)
      • Pediatric populations, particularly patients younger than 8 years of age
      • Pregnant or breastfeeding women
      • People with history or presence of atopic dermatitis, people with other active exfoliative skin conditions (e.g. eczema, burns, impetigo, varicella zoster infection, herpes simplex virus infection, severe acne, severe diaper dermatitis with extensive areas of denuded skin, psoriasis or Darier disease [keratosis follicularis])
      • People with one or more complication (e.g. secondary bacterial infection, gastroenteritis with severe nausea/vomiting, diarrhea, or dehydration; bronchopneumonia, concurrent disease or other comorbidities)
    • For severe manifestations in immunocompromised (HIV or otherwise): consider early treatment with Tecovirimat.
    • Patients with aberrant infection involving accidental implantation in eyes, mouth, or other anatomic areas where Monkeypox virus infection might constitute a special hazard (e.g. the genitals or anus) 

Diagnosis

  • PCR of lesions (use swabs, not sharps, to collect specimen from active skin lesions).  Anorectral or oral swabs can also be used as sites for specimen collection. PCR can remain positive until crusted lesion falls off; individuals considered to be infectious until crusted lesions dissipate

Etiologies

  • Monkeypox virus, an orthopox virus

Primary Regimens

  • Tecovirimat (TPOXX) 600 mg (three 200 mg capsules) twice daily x 14 days (see indications for treatment above).  IV Formulation available; see drug page for details).  

Alternative Regimens

  • Cidofovir (Vistide) is active in vitro; commercially available for CMV retinitis only. Generally recommended for treatment (though no clinical trial data)
  • Vaccinia immune globulin (VIGIV; US FDA-approved for vaccinia vaccination complication treatment).  VIGIV may be considered for prophylactic use in severely immunocompromised persons with exposure to monkeypox. 

Prevention

  • Vaccination for PEP with Jynneos (nonreplicating vaccinia vaccine; Bavarian Nordic A/S; branded as Imvanex in Europe and the UK and as Imvamune in Canada) is indicated mostly for high risk exposures as defined by CDC.
  • See Mpox (Monkeypox), Vaccines

Comments

  • Majority of infections occur in endemic areas of Central Africa. Current outbreaks in Nigeria, DRC, and Central African Republic.  West African (mostly in Nigeria) strain appears less virulent than Congo strain; lower case-fatality rate.
    • Prior outbreaks. US (2003): Source of viral pathogen was likely imported Gambian giant rats, MMWR 52:642, 2003. Also noted in Funisciurus squirrel in the DRC. Endemic areas increasing outbreaks since 2010 due to waning effect of smallpox vaccines ceased in the 1970's. 2016-2021 >10 cases imported to UK, Israel, Singapore US from Nigeria. Summary of outbreaks in UK (Lancet ID, 2022), including 2021 first ever transmission (household and nosocomial) outside Africa. 
  • Antiviral Agents
    • Requests to the US CDC EOC for tecovirimat, cidofovir, and VIGIV can be made by state health departments only.
    • Tecovirimat:  See package insert (pages 12 and 13) for in vivo monkey data. 
    • Cidofovir is active in vitro and in mouse model, Antimicrob Agents Chemother 46:1329, 2002; Antiviral Res 57:13, 2003.
    • Brincidofovir (FDA approved for smallpox) does not appear to work well for mpox and is not available from the CDC or commercially. Retrospective review in Lancet ID 2022
  • Reviews: Clin Infect Dis 58:260, 2014; N Engl J Med 2022;387:1783 (with pictures)
