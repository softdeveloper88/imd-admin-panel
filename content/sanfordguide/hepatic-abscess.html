Hepatic Abscess, Empiric Therapy

Clinical Setting

  • Fever, right upper quadrant tenderness.
  • Single or multiple abscesses on abdominal ultrasound or CT.
  • Serological tests for amebiasis should be done on all patients.
    • Suspect amebic liver abscess if bacterial cultures of the abscess fluid are negative, particularly in those who are residents in or travelers to an endemic region.
  • Percutaneous drainage for suspected or proven bacterial abscess.
  • In cases of pyogenic abscess, 50% have identifiable GI source or underlying biliary tract disease.
  • Liver abscess can be one manifestation of the highly virulent Klebsiella pneumoniae with mucoid phenotype (serotypes K1 and K2, especially in Asia): Lancet Infect Dis 12:881, 2012.

Etiologies

  • Enterobacteriaceae (especially Klebsiella sp.)
  • Bacteroides sp.
  • Enterococcus sp.
  • Entamoeba histolytica (see Comments)
  • Yersinia enterocolitica (rare)
  • Fusobacterium necrophorum (Lemierre's)
  • Klebsiella pneumoniae with highly mucoid phenotype
  • Bartonella henselae or quintana in AIDS patients (see Peliosis hepatitis or Bacillary angiomatosis)
  • Echinococcus species

Primary Regimens

  • Empiric therapy pending determination of bacterial vs. amoebic liver abscess (empiric Metronidazole for latter):
    • (Metronidazole 30-40 mg/kg/day in 3 divided doses IV q8h or 500 mg po q6-8h) + (Ceftriaxone 1-2 gm IV q24h or Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 gm IV (over 4 hr) q8h)
    • (Metronidazole 30-40 mg/kg/day in 3 divided doses IV q8h or 500 mg po q6-8h) + (Ciprofloxacin 400 mg IV q12h 750 mg po or Levofloxacin 750 mg po/IV q24h)
  • See Stewardship below for comment on duration of therapy

Alternative Regimens

  • Empiric therapy: (Metronidazole 30-40 mg/kg/day in 3 divided doses IV q8h or 500 mg po q6-8h) + Ertapenem 1 gm q24h

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • For anaerobic or mixed infections piperacillin-tazobactam, ertapenem (or other carbapenem) are sufficiently active alone: discontinue metronidazole.
  • Oral step-down reasonable once patient is stabilized.
  • Tailor the regimen based on culture data but given that pyogenic liver abscesses are often polymicrobial consider using a regimen that covers both gram-negative enterics and anaerobes.  

  • Duration of therapy depends on the specific pathogen and whether patient is immunocompetent or immunocompromised. Without large controlled trials, it is reasonable to treat pyogenic abscesses based on clinical response, until source control is achieved and biomarkers (e.g., procalcitonin, CRP, WBC and differential) have normalized. See Amebiasis, Entamoeba Histolytica for treatment of that infection.

Comments

  • Review: Gastroenterol Clin North Am. 2020;49:361-377.
  • If amoeba serology is positive, see Amebiasis, Entamoeba Histolytica
  • If bacterial etiology: CT-guided percutaneous (preferred) or surgical drainage should be performed whenever feasible.
  • Hemochromatosis is associated with Y. enterocolitica liver abscess; regimens listed above are effective for Yersinia infection.
  • General reference on intraabdominal infections (IDSA guidelines): Clin Infect Dis 50:133, 2010.
  • Klebsiella pneumoniae serotypes K1 and K2 associated with endophthalmitis (Am J Emerg Med 32:1300e3, 2014).
