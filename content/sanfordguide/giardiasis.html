Giardia duodenalis (G. intestinalis, G. lamblia)

Clinical Setting

  • Giardia duodenalis is now the accepted species name by CDC and ASM but synonymous with G. lamblia and G. intestinalis.
  • Giardiasis is caused by a diplomonad flagellated protozoan that contaminates food and water.
  • Can cause a slow-onset acute diarrhea or a chronic form with abdominal pain, loose stools, bloating, and malabsorption with weight loss.
  • Incubation typically >1 week.  Slow onset of symptoms differentiates from bacterial travelers diarrhea which has an explosive onset.
  • If patients are refractory, consider underlying hypogammaglobulinemia, IgA deficiency, or HIV.

Diagnosis

  • Diagnosis usually with stool antigen detection tests, stool EIA, stool IF testing (DFA), but commercial stool multiplex PCR  panels most sensitive. BMJ 355:i5369, 2016
  • May also detect on duodenal sampling
  • No role for serology
  • Axenic culture possible in research labs
    • No specific genetic markers for resistance, no in vitro susceptibility tests available

Etiologies

  • Giardia duodenalis
    • Also known as Giardia lamblia and Giardia intestinalis

Primary Regimens

  • Adult: Tinidazole 2 gm po x single dose (J Antimicrob Chemother 2018, 73:596)
  • Pediatric (Age ≥3 years): Tinidazole 50 mg/kg po, single dose (maximum dose 2 gm)

Alternative Regimens

  • Metronidazole 250 mg po tid x 5-7 days (high frequency of GI side-effects)
    • Pediatric: 15 mg/kg orally divided three times per day for 5 to 7 days (maximum 250 mg per dose).  Only drug with data in children under 1  year of age, so preferred.
  • Nitazoxanide 500 mg po bid x 3 days
    • Pediatric:
      • Age 1-3 years: 100 mg suspension po bid x 3 days
      • Age 4-11 years: 200 mg suspension po bid x 3 days
  • Quinacrine 100 mg po tid x 5 days
    • Pediatric:  2 mg/kg orally three times per day for 5 days (maximum 100 mg per dose
  • Albendazole 400 mg po once daily with food x 5 days
  • Paromomycin 10 mg/kg po tid x 5-10 days
  • Furazolidone 100 mg po qid x 7 days
  • If patient immunodeficient or refractory, may try Quinacrine 100 mg po tid x 5 days first and consider combination therapy (J Travel Med 2021 Aug 5, J Travel Med 2022 Jan 17;29(1):taab120)
    • Albendazole 400 mg once daily + Metronidazole 250 mg tid x 5 days
    • (Metronidazole 750 mg po + Quinacrine 100 mg po)—both 3x/day x 3 weeks (Clin Infect Dis 33:22, 2001)
      • Less data to support
    • Paromomycin 10 mg/kg po tid plus Metronidazole 750 mg po tid x 3 weeks
    • Nitazoxanide 500 mg po bid x 3 days with a second agent
      • Retreatment with a nitroimidazole

Comments

  • No RCTs on treatment of refractory disease done in last 25 years. Curr Opin Infect Dis 33:355, 2020.
  • If documented recurrence (symptoms may persist after inability to detect organisms in stool X 3), re-treat with drug from different class; quinacrine may be best J Infect Dis 215:946, 2017 Emerg Infect Dis. 20:1742, 2014. or else combination of tinidazole and albendazole.
    • Quinacrine: Expert Compounding Pharmacy (Lake Balboa, California) (800) 247-9767; +1-818-988-7979.
  • Recurrence of symptoms may be due to re-infection, resistant organisms, or lactose intolerance.
    • Consider lactose-free diet to differentiate treatment failure from continuing continuing diarrhea due to ongoing lactose intolerance.
    • Wait for 2 weeks after treatment completion to re-test for persistent organism
    • Initiate immunodeficiency workup (including serum immunoglobulins) after repeated failures.
  • Metronidazole not teratogenic in several meta-analyses; consider for pregnant women with severe giardiasis that is non-responsive to paromomycin (Obstet Gynecol Surv 60:386, 2005).
  • Nitazoxanide reference, Clin Infect Dis 40:1173, 2005.
  • Genetic heterogeneity (Assemblages A-H likely will become separate species, only A & B infect humans) accounting for differing drug sensitivity patterns (J Clin Invest 123:2346, 2013) and (Int J Parasitol 2021 Dec;51(13-14):1099)
