Avian Influenza

Clinical Setting

  • Avian influenza strains are classified as high pathogenicity (HPAI) or low pathogenicity (LPAI) based on their virulence in chickens. Some strains of avian influenza have infected humans in recent years. The majority of human infections with avian influenza have been due to H5N1 and H7N9. H5N1 has been recognized to cause human infections since since 1997.
  • Recently, a worldwide epizootic of HPAI H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b has caused enormous mortality in birds. An ongoing epizootic in cattle is spreading in North America.
  • Recent infections with HPAI H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b have been associated with contact with infected cattle or birds.
  • In contrast to earlier clades, infections with HPAI H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b have generally been mild. Conjuctivitis has been prominent.

Etiologies

  • H5N1
  • H7N9
  • H9N2
  • H5N7
  • H5N6
  • H5N8

Diagnosis

  • As of the end of 2024, no commercial assays specifically identify H5N1
  • H5N1 infection should be suspected in persons exposed to infected cattle, raw milk, or sick or dead birds
  • Some assays may test positive influenza A but negative for H1 or H3. These may represent avian influenza
  • If avian influenza is suspected, contact local or state health department to arrange testing (see CDC guidance)

Primary Regimens

  • Virtually all avian influenza tested to date is sensitive to neuraminidase inhibitors such as Oseltamivir and the cap dependent endonuclease inhibitor Baloxavir
  • Oseltamivir 75 mg po bid is recommended for suspected avian influenza. https://www.cdc.gov/bird-flu/hcp/novel-av-treatment-guidance/
  • CDC currently recommends post exposure prophylaxis of asymptomatic close contacts of persons with avian influenza https://www.cdc.gov/bird-flu/php/novel-av-chemoprophylaxis-guidance/index.html
    • Oseltamivir 75 mg po bid for 5 days is exposure was limited. Continue for 10 days if exposure is ongoing

Alternative Regimens
