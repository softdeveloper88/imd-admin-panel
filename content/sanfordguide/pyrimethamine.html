Pyrimethamine
Tradename: Daraprim

Usage and Dosing

  • Pyrimethamine is an antifolate drug used in the combination therapy of toxoplasmosis, as an alternative for Pneumocystis prophylaxis, and as an alternative for Cystoisospora infections.
  • Mechanism of action: inhibition of dihydrofolate reductase, inhibiting the conversion of dihydrofolate to tetrahydrofolate.
  • Co-administration of leucovorin (folinic acid) recommended to prevent bone marrow toxicities (10-25 mg q24h).
  • Obtaining the drug:
    • Most toxoplasmosis treatment experts strongly recommend pyrimethamine, but the drug can be very difficult to obtain in the US.
    • Prices range from 7 cents to $750 per tablet world-wide, with $1 per tablet in most countries outside the US.
    • Vyera (formerly Turing) declared bankruptcy. Distribution now via Tilde Sciences (www.daraprimdirect.com).
    • Compounding pharmacy alternatives may be available, but current availability uncertain.
    • Experts at National Collaborative Chicago-based Congenital Toxoplasmosis Study (+1 312-513-6355) or rmcleod@uchicago.edu may be able to assist with sourcing.
  • Meta-analysis suggests that TMP-SMX is a reasonable alternative for treatment of CNS toxoplasmosis if unable to obtain pyrimethamine (HIV Med 2017;18:115).
  • Used in combination with sulfadoxine for intermittent preventive treatment of malaria in pregnancy (IPTp). See Fansidar page for dosing details.

Adult Dose

  • Usual dose: 25-75 mg po q24h.
    • A loading dose is administered in some situations.
    • Once-weekly dosing is also used.
  • See appropriate syndrome page for specific dosing recommendations:
    • Cystoisospora belli
    • Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia prophylaxis (in combination with dapsone)
    • Toxoplasmosis: See Toxoplasmosis, Overview. Choose the appropriate topic link on the page.

    Adverse Effects

    • Hypersensitivity reactions (e.g., rash, Stevens-Johnson syndrome, toxic epidermal necrolysis, erythema multiforme, anaphylaxis).
    • GI (anorexia, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea).
    • Hematologic: megaloblastic anemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia, pancytopenia.
    • Atrophic glossitis.
    • Hyperphenylalaninemia.
    • Photosensitivity.
    • Pyrimethamine + sulfadiazine can cause mental changes due to carnitine deficiency (Am J Med 1993;95:112).
