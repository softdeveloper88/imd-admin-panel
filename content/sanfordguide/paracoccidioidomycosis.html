Paracoccidioidomycosis

Clinical Setting

  • Paracoccidioidomycosis, also referred to as South American blastomycosis.
  • Exposure in rain-forest regions of Central and South America.
  • Systemic infection involving lungs, skin (ulcers), mucous membranes, lymph nodes, bone, internal organs.
  • Diagnosis confirmed by isolation of the fungus in culture.
  • If mucosal lesions, "pilot-wheel" pattern in KOH stained scrapings more sensitive than culture

Etiologies

  • Paracoccidioides brasiliensis

Primary Regimens

  • Mild to moderate disease:
    • Itraconazole 200 mg po daily for 6-9 months for mild and for 12-18 months for moderate disease
      • <30 kg: 5-10 mg/kg po daily for 12-18 months
  • Severe disease:
    • Amphotericin B 0.7-1 mg/kg IV daily to a cumulative total of 30 mg/kg followed by Itraconazole 200 mg po daily for at least 12 months

Alternative Regimens

  • Liposomal Amphotericin B 3-5 mg/kg IV daily followed by oral therapy
  • TMP-SMX 800/160 mg bid or tid x 30 days, then 400/80 mg/day indefinitely (up to 3-5 years)
  • Voriconazole 6 mg/kg po bid x 2 doses, then 4 mg/kg po bid (Clin Infect Dis 45:1462, 2007).

Comments

  • Time to clinical cure is shorter with itraconazole when compared to TMP-SMX (PLoS Negl Trop Dis: 17, e2793, 2014).
  • Improvement in >90% patients on Itraconzaole or Ketoconazole.
  • Itraconazole or TMP-SMX suppression is life-long in HIV-positive patients.
  • High rate of relapse, all patients need frequent follow-up for years.
  • Some patients with inflammatory CNS lesions may benefit from corticosteroid therapy (see guidelines below)
