Fusariosis

Clinical Setting

  • Fusariosis is the fourth most common cause of invasive fungal infection (after candida, aspergillus, and mucor) in hematopoietic stem cell transplant (HCT) recipients (Clin Infect Dis 50:1091, 2010).
  • In one study of HCT recipients, metastatic skin lesions were present in 75% of patients and blood cultures were positive in 44% of patients (Clin Infect Dis 38:1237, 2004).
  • Fusarium can also cause keratitis in normal hosts.

Etiologies

  • Fusarium solani
  • F. oxysporum
  • F. verticillioides
  • F. moniliforme

Primary Regimens

  • Severe infection/immunocompromised: Lipid-based Amphotericin B 5 mg/kg IV daily plus Voriconazole 6 mg/kg po/IV q12h x 1 day, then 4 mg/kg po/IV q12h until speciation/susceptibility are available (species have variable susceptibility)

Alternative Regimens

  • Voriconazole 6 mg/kg po/IV q12h x 1 day, then 4 mg/kg po/IV q12h (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 54:4446, 2010)
  • Lipid-based Amphotericin B 5 mg/kg IV daily
  • Amphotericin B 1 mg/kg IV daily
  • Posaconazole (NOTE: different dosing delayed release tabs/IV vs. suspension, better levels achieved with delayed-release tabs)
    • Delayed-release tabs 300 mg po bid x 2 doses and then 300 mg po daily OR
    • Suspension 200 mg qid, then 400 mg po bid after stabilization of disease OR
    • Intravenous: 300 mg IV over 90 minutes bid x 1 day, then 300 mg IV daily

Comments

  • Speciation and antifungal susceptibility testing of infecting isolate should be obtained. Although correlation between antifungal susceptibility and outcomes is not well established, it is recommended by most experts to guide treatment.
  • Role of combination therapy for definitive therapy not well defined (Mycoses 50:227, 2007).
  • Consider surgical debridement and reduction in immunosuppression when possible.
  • Breakthrough fusarium infection have been reported during Posaconazole prophylaxis (J Clin Microbiol 49:1674, 2011).
  • Review on invasive fusariosis: Clin Microbiol Rev. 2023
