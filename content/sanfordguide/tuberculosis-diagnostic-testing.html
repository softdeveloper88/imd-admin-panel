M. tuberculosis, Diagnostic Testing

Diagnostic Testing

  • Diagnostic tests for Mycobacterium tuberculosis infections.
  • CDC and WHO recommend that either a tuberculin skin test (TST) or interferon-gamma release assay (IGRA) can be used to test for LTBI in persons > 5 years of age (Clin Infect Dis. 2017; 64:e1-e33)
    • CDC recommends IGRA over TST in most circumstances the exception being children age < 5 years (although some experts recommend an age < 3 years)
    • Neither TST nor IGRA can distinguish latent from active TB.
  • New recommendations from the World Health Organization aim to speed up detection and improve treatment outcomes for MDR-TB through use of a novel rapid diagnostic test and a shorter, cheaper treatment regimen. Click here for details.
  • As of 2018 National TB Molecular Surveillance Center offers whole genome on M. tuberculosis isolates
  • WHO guidelines for latent tuberculosis guidelines (2018).

Tuberculin Skin Test (TST)

  • Tuberculin skin test (TST) is the same as PPD
    • ≥5 mm induration:
      • HIV-positive
      • Immunosuppressed
      • Prednisone ≥ 15 mg/day
      • Healed tuberculosis on chest x-ray
      • Recent close contact with TB infected patient
    • ≥10 mm induration:
      • Foreign-born, countries with high prevalence of M. tuberculosis
      • IV illicit drug users
      • Low income
      • Long-term care facility residents
      • Chronic illness
      • Silicosis
    • ≥15 mm induration:
      • Otherwise healthy
    • Criteria for positive TST after 5 tuberculin units (intermediate PPD) read at 48–72 hours:
  • Two-step testing (retesting with second TST in 1-3 weeks for initial negative test) is useful in adults who are going to be retested periodically, such as health care workers or nursing home residents.  This approach reduces likelihood that a boosted reaction to a subsequent TST will be misinterpreted as a recent infection.
  • BCG Vaccine received as a child:
    • If TST ≥10 mm induration, and patient is from a country with a high prevalence of M. tuberculosis, the test result should be attributed to M. tuberculosis.
    • In areas of low prevalence, TST reactions of ≤10 mm and up to 18 mm are more likely to be from BCG than TB (Clin Infect Dis 40:211, 2005).
    • Prior BCG vaccination may result in booster effect in 2-stage TST.
  • Routine anergy testing not recommended.

Other Diagnostic Tests

  • Latent Tuberculosis Infection (LTBI) Interferon Gamma Release Assays (IGRAs) (for review see JAMA 308:241, 2012): T-SPOT.TB (Oxford Immunotec) and QuantiFERON-TB Gold  and QuantiFERON-TB Gold Plus (Qiagen)
    • IGRAs preferred in BCG-vaccinated populations (assay antigens are not cross-reactive with BCG) and for single-encounter visits.
    • May be used in any setting where TST is indicated, although use caution in children < 5 years old and immunocompromised as data as limited in these populations (See Am J Respir Crit Care Med 188:422, 2013 for review of IGRA vs TST in immunocompromised patients). 
    • Sensitivity ~80-90%
    • NOTE:  Low risk individuals have higher false positive conversion rates with IGRAs as compared to TST. Review of many reasons for false positives: J Clin Micro 54:845, 2016
  • Rapid (24-hr) diagnostic tests for active M. tuberculosis infection
    • Both of the above tests have sensitivities and specificities > 95% in sputum samples that are AFB-positive. In negative smears, specificity remains > 95%, but sensitivity is 40-77% (MMWR 58:7, 2009; Clin Infect Dis 49:46, 2009).
    • Variety of platforms and tests now available for diagnosis of active TB (Mol Biol Rep. 2020; 47:4065-4075 and Chonnam Med J. 2018; 54:1-9) including loop-mediated isothermal amplification (LAMP), line probe assay (LPA), GeneXpert, and pyrosequencing (Clin Infect Dis 2019; 69:668-75) and whole genome sequencing. Some of the more commonly used tests are described below. 
    • Enhanced Amplified Mycobacterium Tuberculosis Direct (E-MTD) Test (Gen-Probe): amplifies and detects M. tuberculosis ribosomal RNA (rRNA) in respiratory specimens. FDA approved for use in smear-positive and smear-negative samples.
    • AMPLICOR Mycobacterium Tuberculosis Test (Roche Diagnostics): amplifies and detects M. tuberculosis rRNA gene in respiratory specimens. FDA approved for smear-positive samples.
    • COBAS TaqMan MTB Test: real-time PCR test targeting rRNA gene for detection of M. tuberculosis in respiratory specimens (not available in the U.S.) with performance characteristics similar to AMPLICOR (J Clin Micro 51:3225, 2013).
    • Cepheid Xpert MTB/RIF: amplifies and detects the MTB rpoB gene; identifies MTB and detects rifampin resistance (N Engl J Med 363:1005, 2010; MMWR 62:821, 2013) in respiratory specimens.
      • For smear-positive and smear-negative samples;
      • For discontinuation of TB isolation if 1-2 sputum specimens test negative.
      • 98% for a single sputum specimen, smear-positive culture-positive cases.
      • ~70% for a single sputum specimen, smear-negative culture-positive cases; 90% if 3 samples tested.
      • 65% for 2 nasopharyngeal aspirates  in children (Clin Infect Dis 55:1088, 2012); similar results with  gastric lavage aspirates (Lancet Infect Dis 13:36, 2013).
      • Recommended for diagnosis of TB meningitis, extrapulmonary diseases (except pleural disease, for which it is relatively insensitive).
      • FDA approved
      • Sensitivity
      • Specificity of 99%.
      • Correctly identifies resistance or susceptibility to rifampin in 98% of cases.
      • Cepheid Xpert MTB/RIF Ultra (Xpert Ultra) (not yet available in the US) (Lancet Infect Dis 2018 Jan;18(1):76-84).
        • Higher sensitivity than Xpert MTB/RIF for detection of M. tuberculosis in smear-negative specimens, pediatric patients, HIV-infected patients, and in extra-pulmonary disease. 
        • Slightly lower specificity than Xpert MTB/RIF.
        • Recommended by WHO as initial diagnostic test for suspected tuberculous meningitis (Lancet Infect Dis. 2018 Jan;18(1):68-75.)
  • Molecular testing for Multiple Drug Resistant MTB
    • CDC offers a Molecular Detection of Drug Resistance (MDDR) service that can rapidly identify drug resistance, including multidrug-resistant, Mycobacterium tuberculosis complex.

    • Pyrosequencing (PSQ) is a real-time DNA sequencing for rapid detection of mutations associated with MDR-TB (J Clin Tuberc Other Mycobact Dis. 2021; 24:100254).
