Exserohilum rostratum

 

Clinical Setting

  • The presence of a fungus known as Exserohilum rostratum (a dematiaceous fungus and cause of phaeohyphomycosis) in unopened medication vials of preservative-free methylprednisolone acetate (80mg/mL) from New England Compounding Center (NECC) caused an outbreak in 2012. These contaminated steroid injections were linked to a multistate outbreak of fungal meningitis, epidural abscesses, and joint infections resulting in over 700 infections and nearly 50 deaths. CDC estimates that ~14,000 patients may have received injections with medication from the implicated lots of methylprednisolone (MMWR 61:839, 2012).
  • Exserohilum rostratum was the predominant organism recovered although a handful of infections due to Aspergillus fumigatus have also been identified.
  • Symptoms of infection were often slow and typically appeared 1 to 4 weeks following injection, but longer and shorter incubation periods were reported.

Etiologies

  • Exserohilum rostratum

Primary Regimens

  • Voriconazole 6mg/kg IV/po bid
  • See Comment

Alternative Regimens

  • Liposomal Amphotericin B 7.5 mg/kg IV daily

Comments

  • Consider adding Liposomal Amphotericin B (at dosing above) in cases of severe disease or disease refractory to therapy with Voriconazole.
  • CDC does not recommend empiric antifungal therapy for symptomatic patients who have normal cerebrospinal fluid laboratory examination. These patients should be closely monitored and re-evaluated for progression of symptoms.
  • For invasive fungal infections treated with Voriconazole, better outcomes and fewer adverse effects when trough serum concentrations adjusted to 1.0-5.5 mg/l (Clin Infect Dis 55:1080, 2012).
  • Descriptive publications: N Engl J Med 367:2194, 2012; N Engl J Med 368:2495, 2013; Clin Infect Dis 57:853, 2013; and Ann Intern Med 158:154, 2013.
