Cutaneous larva migrans

Clinical Setting

  • Cutaneous penetration by larva of dog or cat hookworm causing very characteristic pruritic, serpiginous, subcutaneous migratory track.
    • Atypical presentations: folliculitis, bullous lesions
    • Can occur on any body part that contacts ground or contaminated surface
  • Also called “hookworm-related creeping eruption" (there are other causes of creeping eruption).
  • Bacterial superinfection may occur and requires antibacterial therapy if clinically indicated.
  • Diagnosed clinically, no serology available.
  • Occasionally A. caninum larvae may migrate to the human intestine, causing eosinophilic enteritis and larvae have also been implicated as a cause of diffuse unilateral subacute neuroretinitis.

Prevention

  • Travelers to tropical and subtropical climates, especially where beach exposures are likely, should be advised to wear closed shoes and use protective mats or other coverings to prevent direct skin contact (includes genital areas) with sand or soil.

Etiologies

  • Ancylostoma braziliense
  • Ancylostoma caninum
  • Ancylostoma ceylanicum
  • Uncinaria stenocephala

Primary Regimens

  • Albendazole 400 mg po bid x 3-7 days 
    • Pediatrics
      • WHO allows Albendazole use in children 1 year of age and older and this is acknowledged in CDC materials.
        • CDC states children aged >2 years: 400 mg per day by mouth for 3 days
          • Do not use in children <2 yrs
    • Pregnancy:
      • WHO allows use of Albendazole in 2nd and 3rd trimesters. Remains FDA pregnancy risk category C; do not use in pregnancy.
        • Large systematic analyses show no harm in 1st trimester Albendazole (Intl J Parasitol 2019 Jun;49(7):541)
  • Ivermectin 200 μg/kg po qd x 1–2 days (not in children <15 kg)
    • Pediatrics:
      • Children >15 kg can use Ivermectin 200 ug/ po as a single dose
        • Limited data suggests no safety issues <15 kg: PLoS Negl Trop Dis 15:e0009144, 2021.

Alternative Regimens

  • None

Comments

  • Albendazole 92-100% effective but multiple doses may be needed. Lancet Infect Dis 8:302, 2008.
  • For failures can repeat albendazole or try ivermectin, multiple courses sometimes necessary.
  • Larvae will die and symptoms resolve in about 6 weeks even with no treatment
  • Topical thiabendazole effective but not widely available and requires tid application for 7 days.
  • Cryotherapy obsolete (dermatologists still do) and potentially scarring; difficult to localize the larvae which are in front of visual track in skin.
  • Albendazole 200 mg tablets available from Expert Compounding Pharmacy (see Antiparasitic Drugs, Sources)
  • Generic Albendazole (Albenza) marketed by Amneal is very costly and is difficult for retail pharmacies to access
