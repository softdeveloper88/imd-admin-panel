Aspergillosis, Invasive

Clinical Setting

  • Invasive pulmonary infection most common, dissemination can occur to CNS, skin, GI tract, etc.
  • Risk groups: Prolonged neutropenia (acute leukemia, myelodysplastic syndromes), allogeneic stem cell transplant (graft-versus-host disease), solid organ transplant, chronic granulomatous disease, high-dose corticosteroids.
  • See Endocarditis, Aspergillus for specific recommendations for this infection.

Diagnosis

  • Typical x-ray/CT lung lesions include halo sign, cavitation, or macronodules (Clin Infect Dis 52:1144, 2011). Initiation of antifungal therapy based on halo signs on CT is associated with better response to treatment and improved outcome.
  • Vascular interruption on CT pulmonary angiogram plus contiguous focal lung lesion in a patient with suspected invasive aspergillosis supports the diagnosis; absence of this finding may also be useful in excluding the disease (Clin Infect Dis 54:610, 2012).
  • Serum: Sensitivity of 78% (95% CI of 61-89%) and specificity of 81% (95% CI, 72-88%)  (Cochrane Database Syst Rev 2008:CD007394).
  • BAL fluid: Enhanced sensitivity over serum but reduced specificity (Curr Opin Pulm Med 17:167, 2011; Chest 138:817, 2010).
  • Improved manufacturing processes have eliminated previous false positive tests that occured with patients receiving Piperacillin-tazobactam and Amoxicillin-clavulanate.
  • Also false positives from cross-reactivity with other fungi (Fusarium sp., Histoplasma capsulatum, Blastomyces dermatitidis, and even some blood product conditioning fluids: Clin Infect Dis 55:e22, 2012.
    • Imaging
    • Galactomannan antigen (Summary reference: JAMA 2017;318:1175)
    • Beta-D-glucan assay
      • Polysaccharide in cell wall of aspergillus and other fungi
      • Detected in serum with chromogenic quantitative enzyme immunoassay
      • High frequency of false positives; low sensitivity and specificity: J Antimicrob Chemother 71:913, 2016.

Etiologies

  • Aspergillus fumigatus most common, but rarely A. niger, A. flavus, A. terreus can cause disease 

Primary Regimens

  • Voriconazole 6 mg/kg IV/po q12h on day 1; then 4 mg/kg IV/po q12h
  • Isavuconazonium sulfate (prodrug of Isavuconazole) 372 mg po/IV q8h x 6 doses and then 372 mg po/IV daily

Alternative Regimens

  • Posaconazole (NOTE: different dosing delayed release tabs/IV vs. suspension, better levels achieved with delayed-release tabs)
    • Delayed-release tabs 300 mg po bid x 2 doses and then 300 mg po daily
    • Suspension 200 mg qid, then 400 mg po bid after stabilization of disease
    • Posaconazole IV 300 mg IV over 90 minutes bid x 1 day, then 300 mg IV daily
  • Liposomal Amphotericin B (L-AmB) 3-5 mg/kg/day IV
  • Amphotericin B lipid complex (ABLC) 5 mg/kg/day IV

Comments

  • Voriconazole:
    • Monitor serum concentrations to achieve target range of 1.0–5.5 mg/L (trough on day 4): associated with improved response rates and reduced adverse effects (Clin Infect Dis 55:1080, 2012).
    • Administration of IV voriconazole in patients with renal dysfunction was not associated with worsening renal function with up to 7 days of treatment; IV voriconazole may be less toxic than previously thought (Clin Infect Dis 54:913, 2012).
    • In retrospective study of 196 pts, 37 (19%) harbored voriconazole-resistant infection which correlated with an increase in mortality. Hence, need for early testing for in vitro susceptibility (Clin Infect Dis 2019;68:1463).
  • Isavuconazole (prodrug isavuconazonium sulfate):
    • A randomized control trial of Isavuconazole vs. Voriconazole for invasive aspergillosis demonstrated that Isavuconazole is non-inferior to voriconazole for the treatment of invasive aspergillosis.  However, isavuconazole was associated with fewer side effects overall, (specifically skin, visual, and hepatobiliary)(Lancet 387:760, 2016).
  • Posaconazole:  
    • A randomized control trial of Posaconazole vs. Voriconazole for invasive aspergillosis demonstrated that Posaconazole is non-inferior to Voriconazole for the treatment of invasive aspergillosis.  However, Posaconazole was associated with fewer treatment-related side effects overall (Lancet 397:499 2021).
  • Liposomal Amphotericin B (L-AMB) or Amphotericin B lipid-complex (ABLC):
    • Preferred regimen when patient is intolerant to voriconazole
    • 10 mg/kg no more efficacious but more toxic than 3 mg/kg doses of L-AMB (Clin Infect Dis 44:1289, 2007).
  • Caspofungin:
    • Approximately 50% response rate in IPA, not recommended for use as monotherapy
  • Combination therapy:
    • A randomized control trial of Voriconazole plus Anidulafungin vs. Voriconazole alone showed a trend towards reduced mortality in all patients with invasive aspergillosis in the combination therapy arm (Ann Intern Med 162:81, 2015). Subgroup of patients with invasive aspergillosis whose diagnosis was established by radiographic findings and GM positivity had lower mortality with combination therapy. Combination therapy should be strongly considered although further data is needed to determine which patients would benefit the most. Some experts would recommend addition of echinocandin to amphotericin-based regimen or other azoles as well.
  • When to obtain antifungal susceptibility testing?  (1) Extensive prior patient antifungal exposure, (2) High rates of local azole resistant Aspergillus fumigatus as seen in some areas in Europe (Emerg Infect Dis 25: 176, 2019), (3) Lack of response to treatment
  • Are any aspergillus species commonly resistant to any antifungals? A. terreus is often Amphotericin resistant while A. lentulus, and Neosartorya udagawae may be azole resistant
