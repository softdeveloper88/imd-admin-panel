Candidiasis, Candidemia or Disseminated

Clinical Setting

  • Candidemia, disseminated candidiasis: non-neutropenic, neutropenic patients
  • Positive blood culture for yeast, suspected catheter-related bloodstream infection.
  • Cell wall polysaccharide of Candida and other fungi (see Diagnosis)
    • Suspected dissemination (e.g., fever, multiple colonized or infected sites, even if blood cultures are negative).
      • Risk factors include previous exposure to broad-spectrum antibiotics, diabetes or hyperglycemia, total parenteral nutrition, ICU patient, abdominal surgery.

    Diagnosis

    • Presence can be detected in serum with a chromogenic quantitative enzyme immunoassay
    • Problem is poor sensitivity and specificity of the assay. Many false positives. Comprehensive review: J Clin Microbiol 51:3478, 2013.
      • A single positive test of limited value; need multiple positives and compatible clinical syndrome;
      • There is value in monitoring quantitative levels during therapy of invasive candidiasis.
    • Blood cultures are insensitive (positive in <50% of cases).
    • Beta-D-glucan

    Etiologies

    • Candida albicans
    • Candida glabrata
    • Candida krusei
    • Candida tropicalis
    • Candida parapsilosis
    • Candida lusitaniae
    • Candida auris (see Comments)

    Primary Regimens

    • Empiric Therapy (see Comments):
      • Caspofungin 70 mg IV loading dose, then 50 mg IV qd
      • Micafungin 100 mg IV qd
      • Anidulafungin 200 mg IV loading dose, then 100 mg IV qd
      • Rezafungin 400 mg x 1 loading dose, then 200 mg IV once weekly
    • Directed Therapy (species identified)
      • Fluconazole 800 mg (12 mg/kg) loading dose, then 400 mg IV/po qd once blood cultures have cleared and clinically stable
      • An echinocandin should be used for treatment of C. glabrata unless susceptibility to Fluconazole or Voriconazole has been confirmed and once blood cultures have cleared and clinically stable
      • An echinocandin, as above, with step down to Voriconazole 6 mg/kg bid x 2 doses, then 4 mg/kg bid once blood cultures have cleared and clinically stable

    Alternative Regimens

    • Empiric Therapy Non-Neutropenic Patients:
      • Fluconazole 800 mg (12 mg/kg) loading dose, then 400 mg IV/po qd (in cases of mild-to-moderate illness and no prior azole therapy)
      • Lipid-based Amphotericin B 3-5 mg/kg IV qd
      • Amphotericin B 0.7 mg/kg IV qd
      • Voriconazole 6 mg/kg bid x 2 doses, then 4 mg/kg bid
      • Duration: 2 weeks after last positive blood culture; systemic therapy should be extended to 4-6 weeks for eye involvement.
    • Empiric Therapy Neutropenic Patients:
      • Lipid-based Amphotericin B 3-5 mg/kg IV qd
      • Amphotericin B 0.7 mg/kg IV qd
      • Voriconazole 6 mg/kg bid x 2 doses, then 4 mg/kg bid
      • Fluconazole 800 mg (12 mg/kg) loading dose, then 400 mg IV/po qd

    Comments

    • Other important considerations:
      • Remove and replace at a new site; catheter retention is associated with higher mortality (Clin Infect Dis 54:1110, 2012).
      • For fungal septic thrombophlebitis: catheter removal and incision and drainage and resection of the vein, as needed, are recommended.
      • Repeat blood cultures to confirm clearance of fungemia.
      • Catheter-related infection:
      • Evaluation for ophthalmic involvement:
        • Ocular involvement occurs in 1.8% of patients with candidemia although in a subgroup analysis of Asian countries rates were 3.6% (Clin Infect Dis 76: 1738, 2023).  
        • Routine ophthalmology consult to obtain a dilated eye exam in patients with candidemia is recommended by the IDSA (Clin Infect Dis 62:409, 2016) although some debate exists (JAMA Ophthalmol 137; 698, 2019).  When decision is made to do an exam, in non-neutropenic patients: perform funduscopic examination within first week of therapy to exclude ophthalmic involvement; ocular findings, which usually resolve with systemic therapy alone, occur in ~15% of patients (Clin Infect Dis 53:262, 2011) and endophthalmitis is uncommon (<2%). Neutropenic patients: perform funduscopic examination after recovery of white count as signs of ophthalmic involvement may not be seen during neutropenia. Treatment of ocular disease depends on degree of involvement, chorioretinitis vs. endophthalmitis.
    • Antifungals
      • Echinocandin compared to polyenes or an azole was associated with better survival and is considered the first-line drug of choice by some (Clin Infect Dis 54:1110, 2012).
      • An echinocandin should be used for treatment of C. glabrata unless susceptibility to Fluconazole or Voriconazole has been confirmed.
      • Echinocandins (Caspofungin, Micafungin, Anidulafungin)
        • An echinocandin is preferred empiric therapy in centers with high prevalence of non-albicans Candida sp.
        • Rezafungin, a long-acting echinocandin, is non-inferior to caspofungin in the treatment for candidemia and invasive candidiasis in persons age ≥18 years (Lancet 401:49, 2023)
      • Fluconazole
        • Not recommended for treatment of documented C. krusei.
        • Not recommended for treatment of documented C. glabrata without confirmation of susceptibility.
      • Voriconazole
        • Little advantage over Fluconazole (more drug-drug interactions) except for oral step-down therapy of C. krusei or Voriconazole-susceptible C. glabrata.
      • Isavuconazole
        • Isavuconazole was not non-inferior to caspofungin during treatment for candidemia (Clin Infect Dis, 2019;68:1981)

    • Recommended duration of therapy for candidemia without persistent fungemia or metastatic complications is for 2 weeks after documentation of negative cultures of Candida from the bloodstream, resolution of symptoms attributable to candidemia, and resolution of neutropenia.

    • Candida auris is a multi-drug resistant species of Candida known to cause severe disease in the health-care setting.
      • Cases have been identified in the US but larger outbreaks seen internationally (Clin Infect Dis 64:134, 2017).
      • Be aware that isolates can be misidentified as Candida haemuloni or Saccharomyces cerevisiae and require molecular testing for definitive identification. CDC Clinical Update on Candida auris.
      • Often multidrug resistant:
        • Most isolates resistant to Fluconazole
        • Some are resistant to Amphotericin B
        • Only small proportion resistant to echinocandins
        • Review: Clin Infect Dis 2018;66:306
    • Candida lusitaniae is often resistant to Amphotericin
    • IDSA treatment guidelines: Clin Infect Dis 62:409, 2016.
