Bacteremia, Staph. aureus

Clinical Setting

  • Blood culture positive for Staphylococcus aureus.
  • Most common sources of bacteremia:
    • Skin and soft tissue infections
    • Intravascular catheters
    • Bone and joint infections
    • Pneumonia
    • Endocarditis
    • Source not obvious in a quarter of cases.
  • Sepsis syndrome and septic shock are common, mortality rate of 10-20%.
  • Recommendations are for IV therapy.
    • Role of oral step-down therapy is not well defined, but this is an evolving area (see Open Forum Infect Dis. 2020;7:ofaa151 and  Antimicrobial Stewardship section below).
    • See Comments for discussion of potential role of oral therapy for persons who inject drugs (PWID) unable to complete standard-of-care IV therapy.

Etiologies

  • Methicillin-susceptible Staphylococcus aureus (MSSA)
  • Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA)

Primary Regimens

  • MSSA
    • (Nafcillin or Oxacillin) 2 gm IV q4h (preferred for initial therapy)
    • Cefazolin 2 gm IV q8h (see Comments)
  • MRSA
    • Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator); alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL
    • Daptomycin 8-12 mg/kg q24h (higher than FDA-approved dosing of 6 mg/kg q24h)
  • For duration of therapy, see Stewardship.

Alternative Regimens

  • MSSA (for patients who cannot be treated with a beta-lactam)
    • Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator); alternative is trough of 15-20 μg/mL
    • Daptomycin 8-12 mg/kg IV q24h
  • MRSA
    • Ceftobiprole 500 mg (as active drug) IV infused over 2h q6h on days 1-8, then q8h thereafter (See Comments)
      • Non inferior to daptomycin in randomized, double-blind, double dummy trial: N Engl J Med 2023; 389:1390
    • See Comments for other options
    • For treatment failure options, See Comments

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Expert infectious diseases consultation improves outcome.
  • Duration of therapy: typically 2 weeks for uncomplicated bacteremia (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 57:1150, 2013) or 4-6 weeks for complicated bacteremia
    • Uncomplicated or low risk bacteremia, no consensus definition but the following are reasonable
      • Sterile blood cultures within 2-3 days of initiation of antimicrobial therapy (the strongest predictor of complicated infection)
      • Presence of an identifiable and easily removable focus of infection
      • Prompt removal of the primary focus of infection
      • No echocardiographic or clinical signs of endocarditis
      • No osteomyelitis
      • No hematogenous secondary foci of infection
      • No pre-existing cardiac valvular abnormalities predisposing to endocarditis (e.g., prosthetic valve, rheumatic heart disease, bicuspid aortic valve)
      • Note: community-acquired bacteremia is associated with a higher risk of complicated infection compared to hospital-acquired bacteremia due to a higher rate of associated endocarditis
    • Complicated bacteremia: failure to meet all of the above criteria
      • 4-6 weeks recommended for treatment of endocarditis or bacteremia complicated by slower resolution or presence of metastatic infection
      • At least 6-8 weeks of therapy for osteomyelitis
  • Oral step-down therapy
    • The role of oral step-down therapy for treatment of uncomplicated bacteremia is evolving. Evidence in support of this approach is based on low quality, observational studies and one small, open-label, randomized, non-inferiority study.  Oral therapy may be an option in highly selected patients who have a readily identified focus of infection with good source control, no evidence of metastatic foci, and prompt clearance of blood cultures (i.e., with 24-48 hours) of starting therapy.
    • Observational data suggest that oral step-down therapy following initial therapy with a parenteral anti-staphylococcal agent may be as effective as standard parenteral therapy in selected patients with uncomplicated bacteremia (e.g., good source control, prompt conversion of blood cultures to negative, no metastatic focus of infection, no endovascular infection).  Agents that have been most commonly used include:
      •  Linezolid (Int J Antimicrob Agents. 2021;57:106329, Clin Infect Dis. 2019;69:381, J Antimicrob Chemother. 2005; 56:923),
      • TMP-SMX (Int J Infect Dis. 2021; 102:554, J Antimicrob Chemother. 2019; 74: 489),
      • Fluoroquinolone-rifampin combinations (Open Forum Infect Dis. 2020;7:ofaa151), and
      • Oral anti-staphylococcal beta-lactams (MSSA only, Antimicrob Agents Chemother. 2020;64:e02345).
    • SABATO (Lancet Infect Dis. 2024;24:523), an open-label randomized trial of partial oral therapy (7 days IV, then 7 days PO) (n=108) versus 14 days IV therapy (n=105) for uncomplicated bacteremia (88% central or peripheral line-associated  infection or skin and soft tissue infection), found oral switch therapy to be non-inferior to all IV therapy.  The composite primary endpoint (defined as complications related to S. aureus bloodstream infection within 90 days, defined as relapsing S aureus bloodstream infection, deep-seated infection with S. aureus, or death attributable to S. aureus bloodstream infection) occurred in 13% of patients in the oral switch group and 12% of the intravenous group. 90-day all cause mortality was 14.4% in the oral switch group and 11.0% in the intravenous group. Only 213 patients (4.2%) of 5063 subjects screened for eligibility were enrolled.  Caveats include early termination of the study at 50% of the planned enrollment due to slow recruitment, an increase in the non-inferiority margin from a planned 5% to 10%, and under-representation of MRSA (only 16 of 213 cases). Protocol oral agents were TMP/SMX 160/800 mg po q12h for MRSA or MSSA (58% of patients), Clindamycin 600 mg q8h for MSSA (32% of patients), and Linezolid 600 mg q12h for MRSA (9% of patients). 

Comments

  • Vancomycin appropriate for empiric therapy pending results of susceptibility tests (Clin Infect Dis 61:361, 2015).
  • No benefit of routine use of combination therapy with Rifampin (Lancet. 2018; 391:668-678) or beta-lactam/vancomycin combination therapy for MRSA (randomized trial terminated by Data Safety Monitoring Board for safety concerns and lack of benefit: JAMA. 2020;323:527-537).
  • TMP-SMX not recommended for initial therapy based on results of randomized controlled trial that failed to show non-inferiority to vancomycin (BMJ 14:350, 2015).
  • Obtain follow-up blood cultures to document clearance; positive blood culture after 3-4 days of appropriate therapy is a strong predictor of a complicated bacteremia.
  • Identify and remove primary and secondary foci of infection to reduce the chance of treatment failure or relapse.
  • Echocardiography recommended to rule out endocarditis (JAMA 312:1330, 2014).
  • Persistent bacteremia suggests endovascular infection or poor source control: should prompt a search for and drainage or surgical debridement of other foci of infection.
    • Emerging interest in use of F18-fluoro-deoxyglucose-positron emission tomography/computed tomography (FDG-PET/CT)  to detect missed foci of infection: Clin Infect Dis 2021; 73:e3859
  • For injections drug users with complicated S. aureus bacteremia:
    • If the patient does not desire standard of care IV antibiotic therapy, discharging the patient with oral antibiotics with outpatient support was associated with significantly better outcomes than discharging with no antibiotics and is a reasonable option (Clin Infect Dis. 2023;76:487).
  • MSSA treatment options
    • Nafcillin (or other anti-staphylococcal penicillin) OR
    • Cefazolin, found to be therapeutically comparable to an anti-staphylococcal penicillin for definitive therapy( See Clin Infect Dis. 2017; 65:100 and BMC Infect Dis. 2018; 18:508).
      • Cefazolin treatment of MSSA bacteremia caused by strains demonstrating a cefazolin inoculum effect (MIC ≥16 µg/mL at standard 107 CFU/mL), presumably due to hydrolysis of Cefazolin, may be associated with worse outcome (Open Forum Infect Dis. 2018 May 23;5(6):ofy123). Whether treatment failure is a direct result of hydrolysis of Cefazolin or due to some other factor was not determined in this study. A study of osteoarticular infections in children (Antimicrob Agents Chemother 2020;64:e00703-20) found that the CzIE was independently associated with worse outcome and treatment failure irrespective of which antibiotic was used, suggesting that the CzIE phenotype may be a marker for strain-dependent virulence factors and not true, cefazolin-specific treatment failure.
    • Treatment failures higher with Ceftriaxone than with Cefazolin in a retrospective cohort study (Open Forum Infect Dis. 2018; 5:ofy089). A metaanalysis of 12 retrospective cohort studies found no difference in outcomes compared to standard-of-care agents but patients receiving standard-of-care agents were sicker had more severe disease and endovascular infections (Antibiotics 2022; 11:375). Ceftriaxone is neither a 1st nor a 2nd line therapy and should be avoided in patients with endocarditis, more serious infections, and complicated/high risk S. aureus bacteremia.
  • Other MRSA treatment options
    • Ceftaroline 600 mg IV q8h (not an approved indication): Retrospective study comparing ceftaroline to daptomycin for MRSA bacteremia found similar outcomes (Open Forum Infect Dis. 2021 Dec 23;9(3):ofab606).
    • Telavancin 10 mg/kg IV q24h (not an FDA approved indication)

  • Treatment failure due to prolonged bacteremia (e.g., >4 days on therapy, but cut-off not well established), more common with vancomycin (median duration of positive blood cultures ~7 days in endocarditis), MRSA infection, endocarditis or other endovascular infection, poor source control.

    • Consider use of combination therapy (Am J Health Syst 2024; Sep 4:zxae252).
      • Daptomycin 8-12 mg/kg IV q24h + Ceftaroline 600 mg IV q8h) appears effective against MRSA strains as salvage therapy for persistent MRSA bacteremia, even if the isolate is non-susceptible to Daptomycin (Clin Ther. 2014; 36:1303; J Clin Pharm Ther. 2018: 43:614)
      • Anecdotal data suggests that the addition of Ertapenem 1 g IV q24h to Cefazolin is an effective salvage regimen for patients with persistent MSSA bacteremia (Antimicrob Agents Chemother. 2022; 66:e0216621).
    • Ceftaroline 600 mg q8h IV appears to be an effective as salvage therapy (Antimicrob Agents Chemother. 2017; 61(2). pii: e02015-16)
    • Daptomycin 8-12 mg/kg IV q24h if poor response to vancomycin (confirm in vitro susceptibility as some VISA strains are daptomycin non-susceptible (MIC >1 μg/mL).
    • Telavancin 10 mg/kg q24h IV (Infect Dis [Lond] 47:379, 2015).
