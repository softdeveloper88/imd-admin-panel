HIV and Travel Considerations

Introduction

  • The likelihood of developing an illness during a 3-week vacation in a tropical area is about 50% (Ann Intern Med 158:456, 2013).
  • Illnesses are likely to be more serious &/or become chronic in the HIV+ patient
  • Persons may be refused entry to country. ARVs may be confiscated.
  • Medical care may be difficult to obtain.
  • Uncontrolled HIV and transplant patients at increased risk for fungal infections (Clin Infect Dis 56: 861, 2013).

Recommendations

Vaccinations

  • Educate on possible decreased immunogenicity of many vaccines
  • See HIV+ travel immunizations and HIV+ routine immunizations
  • Hepatitis A and B Vaccines recommended
  • Meningococcal quadrivalent (ACYW-135) recommended
  • SARS-CoV-2 vaccine recommended
  • Avoid live vaccine antigens if possible
    • Varicella, zoster, typhoid Ty21a, intranasal influenza
    • Measles highly indicated if CD4 >200
    • Yellow fever
      • Almost always give if CD4 <500; but never if <200
      • Extra dose recommended if previously vaccinated while HIV+
    • Assure most recent influenza vaccine has been administered (regardless of time of year).

Malaria

  • Malaria prophylaxis for those on ART
    • lack of published data on malaria prophylaxis regimens with ART
    • clinically relevant concerns have yet to be shown
    • Potential interactions at www.hiv-druginteractions.org.
    • Mefloquine is the most likely prophylactic antimalarial to have potential interactions, and Doxycycline and chloroquine the least likely.
      • Avoid Mefloquine if possible
    • Protease inhibitors are the most likely anti-retrovirals to have interactions and NRTIs (nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitor), and strand-transfer integrate inhibitors (STII) the least likely.
    • the extremely cautious patient on a protease-inhibitor or on a NNRTI might want to avoid atovaquone/proguanil if possible.
  • Prophylaxis considerations for 3 common ART regimens
    • INSTI/NRTI combination drugs—no discussion with patient necessary just give anti-malarials (usually atovaquone/proguanil) per routine.
    • Emtricitabine/Tenofovir/Efavirenz (Atripla)
      • Mefloquine and Doxycycline appear acceptable.
      • Concurrent use of Efavirenz and proguanil may decrease plasma concentrations of the active metabolite cycloguanil and possibly lower the efficacy of Atovaquone-Proguanil (moderate interaction)
      • Explain to the patient the theoretical lower efficacy of Atripla/Atovaquone-Proguanil if there are contraindications to Mefloquine or Doxycycline.
    • Lopinavir/Ritonavir + Tenofovir/Emtricatabine (Kaletra/Truvada).
      • Avoid chloroquine and mefloquine due to possible QT prolongation
      • Ritonavir may lower the atovaquone level (minor interaction).
  • Antimalaria treatment regimens including artemisinins and quinine are much more likely to have safety or efficacy problems, and patients on ARVs need to seek care by an experienced specialist should any possibility of malaria arise.

ARVs

  • If the traveler is known to be HIV+, take account of legal and/or public health restrictions on travel for persons with HIV infection (e.g., ability to transport ARV medications into the country). If traveler is on a Ritonavir based regimen, make sure he/she is taking a tablet form of the drug in order to avoid a need for refrigeration. Assess the immune status (CD4 cell count) in infected persons.

Precautionary Measures

  • Review planned itinerary & activities in light of the patient's immune status & review the added risks for travel, especially to developing or tropical countries. In some instances, it may be prudent to recommend a change in itinerary or activities because of serious risks that cannot be eliminated or reduced.
  • Consider packing treatments for malaria and travelers diarrhea and intense food/water precautions
  • Recommend the following measures to reduce exposure to pathogens:
    • Assiduously avoid food & beverages that may be contaminated, especially raw or undercooked shellfish, fish, meat, or eggs; raw, unpeeled fruits & vegetables; tap water & ice; as well as unpasteurized milk & milk products (cheese).
    • Insist on eating only well-cooked foods & on drinking only very hot or bottled beverages.
    • Reduce contact with vectors, for example, by using insect repellent & avoiding outdoor exposure at dusk or other times & places of increased insect activity.
    • Frequent hand washing, sanitize with alcohol based hand cleanser gels.
  • Urge the patient to obtain prompt evaluation of symptoms of illness & early treatment of infection. Where possible, identify a physician knowledgeable about HIV infection at the destination prior to departure. Arrange for continuation of medical management during travel (for example, prophylaxis for pneumocystis pneumonia).
