Uveitis

Clinical Setting

  • Uveitis is inflammation of the uvea (iris, ciliary body, choroid) or retina
  • Presenting symptoms include red eye, eye pain, decreased vision, floaters.
  • Defined by where inflammation in the eye occurs
    • Anterior (iritis, iridocyclitis)
      • autoimmune causes include sarcoidosis, Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada syndrome, Behcet disease, reactive arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis.
      • Most common type of uveitis
      • Usually not infectious.
    • Intermediate (pars planaris)
    • Posterior (choroiditis, retinitis, chorioretinitis)
    • Panuveitis
  • Most are noninfectious (idiopathic or autoimmune)
  • Acute retinal necrosis is a medical emergency

Etiologies

  • Depends on the anatomic location. 
  • Infectious etiologies depend upon region of the world, more common in "tropical" regions--travel history a must!
  • Anterior 
    • Most idiopathic or auto-immune.
    • Herpes simplex (HSV) reactivation is the most common infectious cause
    • Herpes zoster (VZV)
    • CMV (immunocompromised)
    • syphilis
    • Mycobacterium tuberculosis (TB)
    • Lyme disease
    • Mycobacterium leprae (leprosy)
  • Intermediate (pars planaris)
    • rare
    • Lyme disease
    • Leptospirosis
    • syphilis
    • sarcoidosis, multiple sclerosis
  • Posterior (choroiditis, retinitis, chorioretinitis)
    • Often infectious
    • World-wide, the most common infectious etiology of posterior uveitis is Toxoplasma gondii 
    • CMV
    • Acute retinal necrosis (HSV, VZV, CMV if immunocompromised)
    • Toxocara
    • syphilis
    • Candida 
  • Panuveitis
    • syphilis
    • tuberculosis
    • Candida endophthamitis
  • Viruses including Chikungungya, Ebola, Zika, Dengue, West Nile associated with different types of uveitis, and symptoms may occur months after initial acute illness
  • Other bacterial causes include Brucella, Bartonella henselae, leptospirosis 

Diagnosis

  • Clinical presentation/exam, location in the eye, travel history important
  • Syphilis is diagnosed presumptively if serologies consistent
  • Toxoplasma often diagnosed based upon exam and positive serologies.
  • Sampling of anterior chamber or vitreous with positive PCR for HSV, VZV, CMV or T. gondii is diagnostic, though not sensitive. 

Primary Regimens

  • Depends on the etiology (see above)
  • Acute retinal necrosis is a medical emergency and should be treated with antivirals that cover HSV and VZV and possibly CMV (if immunocompromised).
  • Management with an ophthamology specialist is recommended.
  • Syphilis should be treated as for CNS disease.

Comments

  • Worldwide, toxoplasmosis is the most common cause of posterior uveitis. More common in South and Central America due to more virulent genotypes.
  • Given high prevalence in the general population, serologies for HSV, VZV, CMV, T. gondii or other pathogens under consideration may not be helpful. Negative serologies are helpful to exclude diagnoses.
