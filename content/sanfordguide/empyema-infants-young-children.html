Empyema, Infants/Children

Clinical Setting

  • Parapneumonic empyema in infants and children age < 5 years.
  • Pneumonia with parapneumonic effusion plus positive pleural fluid culture or pleural fluid WBC > 50,000 μL.

Etiologies

  • Strep. pneumoniae
  • Staph. aureus (MSSA and MRSA)
  • Strep. pyogenes
  • H. influenzae (rare in vaccinated population)

Primary Regimens

  • Catheter or chest tube drainage (± fibrinolytics – see below) or video-assisted thorascopic surgery (VATS).
  • Ceftriaxone 100 mg/kg IV q24h or Cefotaxime 100 mg/kg IV q8h (where available outside the US) 
  • If MRSA,  Vancomycin 60- 80 mg/kg IV divided 3-4 times a day to achieve preferred target AUC24 400-600 μg/mL x hr (see vancomycin AUC dosing calculator; alternative is trough of 10-15 μg/mL but AUC targeting is preferred) ± (Cefotaxime or Ceftriaxone)(if H. influenzae suspected). PCR studies show MRSA is rare in culture negative empyema (Pediatr Infect Dis J 30:289, 2011).

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • If organism is known, tailor antibiotics appropriately.
  • If cultures are negative, Staph aureus is unlikely based on PCR studies and therapy can be narrowed Pediatr Infect Dis. 2011 Apr;30(4):289 
    Acta Paediatr. 2023 Jul;112(7):1555
  • Optimal duration of treatment unclear; some experts treat 7-10 days after resolution of fever in drained effusion, others 4 weeks after drainage.
  • After initial response, completion with high dose oral therapy as effective as prolonged outpatient IV therapy. Hosp Pediatr. 2015 Dec;5(12):605-12.

Comments

  • See IDSA treatment guidelines:  Clin Infect Dis 53:e25, 2011.
  • Ceftriaxone appropriate for term infants without hyperbilirubinemia
  • Vancomycin: traditional dosing of 45-60 mg/kg/day frequently does not achieve target AUC in term infants and older children with normal renal function. Use of AUC24  closer to 400 μg/mL adequate for most non-CNS infections. Clin Infect Dis Jul 13 2020
  • Ceftriaxone is contraindicated in neonates if they require (or are expected to require) treatment with calcium-containing IV solutions, including continuous calcium-containing infusions such as total parenteral nutrition
  • Indications for diagnostic thoracentesis:
    • Moderate (> 10 mm rim of fluid or opacification of 1/4 to 1/2 of hemithorax), particularly if there is respiratory compromise or worsening of condition with IV antibiotics alone.
    • Large effusion (opacification of > half of hemithorax).
  • Indications for chest tube drainage or VATS:
    • Empyema (positive fluid culture of > 50,000 μL WBCs in pleural fluid, septations on ultrasound or CT scan).
    • Large effusion.
  • Chest-tube drainage + fibrinolytics (regimens below) or VATs recommended if fluid is loculated:
    • Tissue plasminogen activator, 4 mg in 40 mL normal saline once daily per chest tube with 1-hour dwell time for 3 doses. Alternative 0.1 mg/kg in 30 mL normal saline with 1 hour dwell time 3 x daily for total 9 doses.
    • Urokinase 10,000 U in 10 mL normal saline (< 1 year old) or 40,000 U in 40 mL normal saline (> 1 year old) with 4-hour dwell time repeated every 12 hours for 6 doses.
  • PCR based studies increase yield - especially S. pneumoniae . Staph aureus empyema usually culture positive even with some pretreatment. Pediatr Infect Dis J 30(4):289-94, 2011
