Meningitis, Listeria monocytogenes

Clinical Setting

  • Culture of CSF positive for Listeria monocytogenes.
  • Gram-stain (~30% are positive):small, sometimes pleomorphic Gram-positive rods.
  • Risk factors: Age > 65 years or < 30 days, pregnancy, alcoholism, cirrhosis, or immunocompromised host.
  • Often the presentation is less acute than with other bacterial pathogens.

Etiologies

  • Listeria monocytogenes

Primary Regimens

  • Adults: Ampicillin 2 gm IV q4h (See Comments regarding use of gentamicin)
  • Infants: Ampicillin 300-400 mg/kg/day divided q6h or TMP-SMX 20 mg/kg/day (based on TMP component)  divided q6-12h (See Comments regarding use of gentamicin)

Alternative Regimens

  • TMP-SMX 20 mg/kg/day (based on TMP component)  divided q6–12h
  • Meropenem 2 gm IV q8h
  • Linezolid 600 mg IV q12h listed as an option in ESCMID guidelines (Clin Microbiol Infect 2016; 22 Suppl 3:S37).

Antimicrobial Stewardship

  • Duration of therapy 3 weeks for meningitis, 4-6 for cerebritis or brain abscess.

Comments

  • Although ampicillin-gentamicin combination therapy has been commonly recommended,  data supporting this recommendation are weak at best and based almost entirely on in vitro and animal data. The optimal dose, if there is one, has never been established, and there are no clinical data demonstrating better outcomes with the combination compared to ampicillin alone, which has been reported to be effective in small case series. Given the known nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity (potentially irreversible) of gentamicin, particularly at higher doses, and the unproven benefit, gentamicin combination therapy is not routinely recommended.
  • See Clin Infect Dis 43:1233, 2006
