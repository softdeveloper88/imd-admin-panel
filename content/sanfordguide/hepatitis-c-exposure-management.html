HCV Exposure Management

Clinical Setting

  • Health care workers (HCW). Needlestick injury: exposure to Hepatitis C virus (HCV)
  • Summarized in MMWR 69:1, 2020
  • General steps in management:
    • Wash clean wounds/flush mucous membranes immediately (use of caustic agents or squeezing the wound is discouraged; data lacking regarding antiseptics).
    • Assess risk by doing the following:
      • Characterize exposure
      • Determine/evaluate source of exposure by medical history, risk behavior and testing for HBV, HCV, HIV
        • Ideally check source with HCV RNA testing within 48 hours of exposure 
        • If not immediately available, check source with HCV Ab test and if positive, reflex to HCV RNA testing
      • Evaluate and test exposed individual for HBV, HCV and HIV.
  • Specific steps:
    • Determine HCV status for both exposed person &, if possible, exposure source (index patients).  
      • If source + or unknown and exposed person negative:  
        • Re-check follow-up HCV testing for HCV RNA 3 - 6 weeks post-exposure (detectable in blood in 1-3 weeks)
        • If remains neg, re-check at 6 months (this coincides with repeat HBV testing) with anti-HCV Antibody, and if positive, reflex to HCV RNA
        • A positive HCV RNA test should be referred for treatment with DAA Rx as per Acute HCV Infection guidelines
    • NO Post-Exposure prophylaxis is indicted

Etiologies

  • Hepatitis C virus (HCV)

Primary Regimens

  • No recommended post-exposure prophylaxis; immune serum globulin not effective.
  • Persons who have documented HCV RNA viremia should be treated with a course of targeted direct acting agents after determination of the HCV genotype (see Comments)

Alternative Regimens

  • Treatment should be initiated during the time of confirmed acute infection (see Acute HCV Infection).  Those situations / individuals include:
    • HCV transmission prevention, e.g., a surgeon, a person with ongoing intravenous drug use, or an HIV-positive man who engages in sex with other men.
    • Mitigation of clinical consequences, e.g., a patient with cirrhosis who is acutely superinfected with HCV.
  • In such instances, treatment is the same as for Chronic Hepatitis C (see Hepatitis C, treatment)

Comments

  • Primary (immediate) post-exposure prophylaxis not recommended.  Rather, the HCW should be monitored for evidence of long-term infection with serial HCV RNA determinations.
  • Case-control study suggested risk factors for occupational HCV transmission include percutaneous exposure to needle that had been in artery or vein, deep injury, male sex of health care worker and was more likely when source viral load > 6 log10 copies/mL.
