Strongyloidiasis

Clinical Setting

  • An intestinal roundworm (nematode).
  • Infections in immunocompetent patients can manifest as asymptomatic eosinophilia
  • Infection in immunocompromised patients can cause disseminated disease and septic shock.
  • The life cycle of S. stercoralis can be completed without leaving the human host. 
    • The adults live in the small intestine; the eggs develop into larvae that can penetrate the perianal skin and complete the cycle of autoinfection.
  • In patients with depressed cell-mediated immunity, uncontrolled autoinfection can result in a severe hyperinfection syndrome.
    • Corticosteroids and HTLV-1 infection frequent causes of hyperinfection
  • Immunocompetent patients may have mild GI symptoms; larval lung migration can cause dyspnea or hemoptysis.
  • In chronic low-level auto-infection, dermatologic manifestations include recurrent serpiginous urticarial rashes on the buttocks and waist areas (larva current syndrome) especially. Larva current may advance as rapidly as 10 cm/hr.
  • Hyperinfection can result in multi-organ symptoms, including meningitis, and can proceed to septic shock.
  • General review: Nat Rev Dis Primers 2024 Jan 25;10(1):6.

Diagnosis

  • Detection of larva in stool.  
    • Stool O & P, sputum O & P in suspected hyperinfection;
      • Special culture techniques including Baermann concentration, filter paper culture, or agar plate culture often required.
    • Direct duodenal sampling may increase sensitivity.  String-test (Enterotest) discouraged and is a potential bio-hazard. 
    • If persistent post-treatment symptoms repeat O & P in 4 wks
  • Serology helpful in temporary visitors to endemic areas but has broad cross-reactivity.
    • Positive IgG does not distinguish current from past infection although titers decrease with effective treatment.
    • Frequent false positives and negatives from commercial reference labs
      • CDC lab reports often are equivocal
  • Consider screening persons:
    • Who are on or about to begin corticosteroid therapy or other immunosuppressants
    • Known to have HTLV-1 infection
    • With leukemias/ lymphomas
    • Being considered for organ transplantation
      • no evidence for empiric ivermectin; treat only positive screens
    • With persistent peripheral or unexplained eosinophilia

Etiologies

  • Strongyloides stercoralis

Primary Regimens

  • Ivermectin 200 μg/kg/day po x 1-2 days for asymptomatic or intestinal disease. 
    • Single dose may be adequate Lancet Infect Dis 19; 1181, 2019.
    • For disseminated disease with larvae in stool and sputum, repeat treatment every 15 days while stools positive and then 1 more treatment cycle.
  • For hyperinfection with sepsis or meningitis in severely immunocompromised hosts: Ivermectin 200 µg/kg per day orally until stool and/or sputum exams are negative for 2 weeks.
    • If oral and/or rectal administrations are not possible, IND exemptions from FDA for the veterinary subcutaneous formulation of ivermectin are possible.
  • The safety of ivermectin in children <15kg has not been authorized by most national authorities.
  • WHO allows children who are at least 90 cm tall (equivalent to ~15 kg or ~2 yrs of age) to be treated with ivermectin.
    • Systematic review (n=1,000 subjects) strongly suggest safety <15 kg  (PLoS Negl Trop Dis 2021 Mar 17;15(3):e0009144)

Alternative Regimens

  • Albendazole 400 mg po bid x 7 days for asymptomatic or intestinal disease but not as effective as Ivermectin. Cochrane Database Syst Rev. 2016 Jan 18;(1):CD007745 .

Comments

  • Beware of disseminated disease in COVID-19 patients treated with high-dose dexamethasone.
    • Consider screening patients with high epidemiologic risk at outset of steroid therapy.
